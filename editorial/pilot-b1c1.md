# Chapter 1: The Bandits at Sarn Ford

*Nénimë 6, 2965*

Winter had not yet let go of Bree the night Celenneth came in out of the dark. She put her shoulder to the door of the Prancing Pony until it gave, and the wind came in with her before the latch caught — cold across a room already thick with heat and roast fat and the noise of people who had somewhere to belong. She stood in it a moment, hood up, and let the warmth work through the seams of her armour.

Orlen was at his table by the hearth, laughing at something she would never hear. She looked at him the way she had taught herself to look at anything she wanted and could not keep: once, then not again. She was always arriving or leaving. Bree was a place other people lived.

She lowered her hood, and Barliman Butterbur found her before she had gone three steps.

"Celenneth! Back again? In and out like the wind itself." A few heads turned. She let herself smile — small, but real; she spent them more freely here than on the road.

"I can't seem to stay away, Barliman."

He set a tankard down, foam sliding over the rim. "You've the look of a long road on you. Sit. It'll keep an hour."

But she had stopped listening. Two tables over, men had dropped their voices, and dropped voices carried farther than they knew. *Brigands. Along the Baranduin. Third party this month.* It was not the words that turned her in her seat but the fear beneath them.

She crossed to them, and her presence was enough to still the table. "Forgive me — I couldn't help but hear. These men on the Baranduin. How many, and how often?"

They gave her what they had: travellers waylaid at the fords, shapes in the reeds at dusk, a merchant bound for Sarn Ford who never arrived. By the end her mind was made. Orlen would keep. The road would not.

She pushed the tankard back, drunk from once. "I'll need those provisions after all. There's no rest in it tonight."

Butterbur sighed, already reaching. "Off again. Mind yourself, girl. The roads are dark this year."

⁂

She made good time down from Bree — the Greenway, then off it, cutting the corner of the Barrow-downs where the grass lay grey and the air went still.[^r-shortcut] By the time the land broke toward Sarn Ford the sun was down, and a ruined tower stood black across a ravine.

Smoke gave the camp away, a thin thread of it above an abandoned robbers' den. She went toward it low and quiet, and saw the woman before the woman saw her: a figure slipping into a cave by the fire, dressed in the cast-off finery of Tharbad, a long way from wherever she had started.

A lookout, Celenneth judged — someone who could carry word ahead. She closed the distance without sound and took her from behind, an arm across the throat, her free hand reaching for the rope. But desperate is quick. The woman twisted a hand loose before the knot could bite, and the fight slipped Celenneth's control in a breath.

There was a knife between them, or would be. She made the choice the road makes when it leaves no other, and drove the blade up beneath the ribs, and clamped a hand over the woman's mouth to keep the cry from carrying. The body went slack by degrees, and then it was only weight.

She lowered it down with more care than the killing had allowed. It had not been a battle — no honour spent, none earned, only a life ended because keeping it might have cost her own. Kneeling in the sudden quiet, she knew that a thing done in the dark for one's own sake does not stay there. It goes in with you.[^s-kill]

She cleaned the blade on the grass and stood. No time to mourn a stranger.

⁂

The cave gave onto the ravine, and the ravine onto a bridge — old Dúnedain stonework broken at the span, and beside it a newer crossing lashed from rope and plank. She had barely set foot to it when a shape came out of the dark on the far side: a man, wild-eyed, already sawing at the ropes.

She ran. The bridge slewed under her boots, the ropes went, and she threw herself the last stride into open air.[^r-bridge] Her feet found stone as the crossing folded into the dark below.

The man stared as if she had broken a law of the world, then lost his nerve and bolted into the passage beyond. She followed by torchlight into a chamber choked with rubble and old water, oak doors across the far wall, the trickle of water somewhere doubling until it sounded like whispering.

He came at her out of the pantry with an axe and a cry. She turned the first swing on her buckler — the shock of it drove up into her shoulder — and gave it back: a thrust turned aside, a second that scored the wall, then the opening, and her sword into his chest to the hilt.[^r-axe] She dragged it free and let him fall. There was no time to search him. She hauled him to the ravine's lip, gave him to the dark, and did not watch him land.

⁂

Beyond the doors lay a pillared hall gone to flood, and the dread came off it like cold off stone. Worn carvings lined the walls, figures past reading; across the black water a stair rose half out of the flood. She was ankle-deep and crossing when a dwarf broke from cover — small, dark, quick — and was gone down a side passage before she could call out. Then a bell tolled, deep and slow, up through the pillars and the water and into her teeth.

The water answered it. Two shapes rose streaming from the flood, grey and gaunt and wrong, and came for her.[^r-ghouls] Her arrow, thrown off by the water, cracked against a pillar. Then they were on her — one set its teeth to her arm and found only leather; she wrenched clear, turned a raking hand on the buckler, cut and missed. The second caught her sword-arm in fingers like wet bone and bore in, jaws working at the seam of her helm.

Her torch was gone, guttering in the shallows — and the sight of it dying saved her, for both creatures flinched even from that. She strained toward it against the grip, got her fist to the handle, and swung the flame up into the thing's face. It loosed her with a shriek, and its fellow with it, and both slid back beneath the water and did not rise.

She stood shaking until the flood lay flat. Only then did she see the sepulchres ranged across the hall, some sealed, some broken open onto nothing. She searched them and found only cold and echo. Whatever the place kept, it would not give to her. The bridge was gone behind her; there was only forward. She climbed the drowned stair.

⁂

She came up into another country. Ruins ran to the edge of sight, drowned now in mist — a grey unbroken sea of it, the low red sun going down beyond. Nothing stood higher than a few yards; the rest had been thrown down, as though a single great hand had closed on the city and let go. One tower still kept its shape, its walls black with scrawled marks.

She read them when she was near enough. The letters were Elvish, cut in an old twisted hand, but the words were the Black Speech of Mordor, and they were a curse — the ruin laid on this hold by the Witch-king of Angmar in his fury at the Dúnedain who had defied him, bound to hold as long as the torment of one Lord Arveleg endured. She did not know the name. She did not need it to understand what she stood in: a stronghold of her own people, taken and fouled and left to rot around its grief. The knowing pressed in past the armour and closed on something beneath her ribs.[^s-curse] She made herself turn from the wall and breathe. She had read enough to blunt the worst of it — but only the worst.

She found a hollow beneath a fallen arch, out of the wind, and set the day down there a while.[^m-rest] When she rose her muscles had stiffened with cold. Searching, she came on a rusted gate half-buried in rubble, and behind it, impossibly, green: an orchard run wild, an herbalist's garden still keeping faith with a gardener centuries dead. She knelt and found what she knew, a healing herb and enough of it, and pressed it to the ghouls' bite — but the ache in her ran deeper than any leaf could reach.[^m-herbs] She gathered the rest and told herself it was something.

⁂

The tower door groaned open on a round room walled with books gone soft with the years. One had held: black leather, titleless, marked with a sigil she half-knew. She took it; it felt like something that had waited to be taken.

A sound came from above — a shuffle, a shifting of weight. Her hand went to her sword. As she started up the winding stair the mist below seemed to gather, and a figure rose out of it: a woman worn nearly to smoke, her hair drifting white as cobweb, her eyes lit from within.

"Why have you come? There is nothing here. Only death. Leave, or stay and share my torment."

Celenneth held still — the markings, the name half-remembered. "Lady Elwin," she said, because she believed it. "Who is it you keep this place against?"

The wraith's sorrow went to fury in an instant, and it came at her like blown fog. She gave ground, reached for her bow, found the thing already on her where footing meant nothing. So she cut, and the blade passed clean through where a head should be, and the wraith screamed — a sound like winter forced through a gap in a door, and the cold of it went into her marrow and told her, in a voice like her own, to lie down and give up.[^r-wraith] She cut again. On the second stroke the spirit came apart and was gone.

She stood shivering long after, the despair still guttering in her. She reached past it — for the ones counting on her return, whether or not they rode at her side — and took hold, and pulled herself back up into herself.[^m-rally] The worst of the cold lifted. The shadow of the place did not.

⁂

At the top she found the reason for all of it. A corpse sat chained upright to a rotting throne, mummified to leather and bone, and driven through its skull, nailed into it, a crown — a blue gem burning cold in the metal. She saw how the nails were set, and understood: not honour paid a king but its mockery.

She bowed her head to the ruined thing, because it had once been a lord of her people, and asked its pardon. Then she set her hands to the crown and worked it free. The air changed; a presence gathered at her back, and she turned to find the wraith again — Lady Elwin, whole and still, her hollow eyes fixed on the crown.

It came away. The body fell to dust, the chains with it, and for a breath the wraith was a woman in a noblewoman's robes, looking at her with a gratitude too old for words — and then she too was gone, and the mist that had held her went with her.[^r-crown] The gem's light guttered out. Celenneth could not have named all she had undone, but she felt it go: a knot in the air worked loose at last.

She made camp at the foot of the empty throne, barred a door, set a trap on the stair. Her sleep was thin and haunted; she woke once certain she was watched, but the room was empty and the trap unsprung. By morning the mist was gone, and the sun came up hard and clean over the broken city.

⁂

In daylight she found what the night had hidden: a trapdoor under rubble, and beneath it a small hoard. Among the coin her eye caught on a pair of boots — fine leather, Númenórean by the make, near-untouched, a rune of travelling worked into the side. Herald's boots, blessed for the road and its hurts. She traded her worn ones for them where she stood.[^t-boots]

She was leaving when the dwarf found her again and loosed a quick, agitated stream of words she had no share in. She answered the only way that needed no language: she held out a portion of the hoard, courtesy in an open hand. He looked at her, and at the gold, and eased. He beckoned, and she followed — past the flooded hall, through cramped corridors, to a concealed crevice and a stair, and the stair to a ledge above the ravine. She made her rope fast and went down, and from the ledge she saw them: the brigands, gathered again where the bridge had stood, waiting for her to come out the way she had gone in. But she had another road now.

At the bottom the dwarf loosed her rope, let it fall, and was gone without a word.

She coiled it and read the land, and it offered a way around them — broken ground where she might pass unseen. She took it carefully, every footfall chosen. And still it turned on her: her boot caught a scatter of loose stone and sent it down the slope, and the noise went up sharp, and the brigands' shouts rose to meet it.[^r-stealth]

She ran. The new boots gave her more than she had any right to, and she used all of it, until the last of the pursuit thinned into silence. She knew the shortcuts home from here, and took them with fortune at her shoulder.

She came back to the Prancing Pony worn to the bone and unbroken, and gave the other Dúnedain a full account of what waited on the Baranduin — the numbers, the fords they favoured, the routes they worked. Whatever came of it, her people would not walk into it blind.

Then she let herself stop. She rested while the wound closed, and as her strength came back she thought of Orlen, and went looking — but Butterbur told her he had gone west to a fair and would not return for weeks. She thanked him, and kept her disappointment where she kept everything, behind a small, easy smile. The road would call her out again soon enough.
