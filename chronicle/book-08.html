1Leaving for Rivendell

Súlimë 3, 2969

The fire crackled low as Linnea sat at the table with Brynja in her lap, the child tugging idly at the edge of her green cloak, and Freowyn peeled an apple by the window, her gaze flicking now and then to the tension in the room. Celenneth lingered near the door, arms crossed, braced as if against a wind.

"I need to speak with you both," she said.

Linnea's head came up. "What is it?"

"Elrond's summons isn't something I can set aside. I've decided to go to Rivendell." She looked between them. "And I want you with me. All of you. Brynja too."

Linnea's arm tightened around the child. "The road to Rivendell is long, Celenneth. And dangerous. Why take her into that?"

"Because I can't leave her. I can't leave any of you. If Elrond's message carries the weight I think it does, I'll want you at my side."

Freowyn set the apple down, her face thoughtful and a little sad. She glanced at Brynja, then back. "I understand why you'd take your family with you. But my place isn't on that road. I'll not follow you to Rivendell."

"Freowyn—"

"Brynja has strong parents in the two of you," the older woman said gently, "and I've treasured every day I've helped raise her. But this is your journey, not mine. And she's weaned now; she doesn't need me the way she did." She smiled faintly. "You gave me something I never looked to have — the chance to be part of a child's life, if only for a while. That's a gift I'll carry always. Take care of her. Take care of each other. That's all I ask."

Celenneth stepped close and took her hand. "I can't thank you enough. For all of it."

Brynja chattered to herself, oblivious to the weight of the moment, and Linnea held her a little tighter, and Freowyn's gaze lingered on the child, wistful. "I'll see you all again someday, I hope. But for now, the road is yours."

They rode out into frost-laden hills, Celenneth at the front reading the horizon, Linnea behind with Brynja bundled and secure before her on Eorlin, the chestnut mare stepping careful and sure. The air was crisp with pine and the faint promise of snow. Brynja squealed and gripped the saddle-bow, and Linnea adjusted her scarf, murmuring, "Hush, love."

They made the journey a slow schooling for the child. One evening under a gold sunset Celenneth knelt and set a small bow in Brynja's hands, and Brynja's laughter rang out as her first arrow sailed wide and buried itself in a patch of moss. "Almost," Celenneth encouraged, adjusting her grip. "Steady, this time." Brynja squinted, tongue between her teeth, and loosed again — closer, and both women cheered. Another day, in a quiet glade, Linnea crouched with her over wild garlic and fiddlehead ferns. "These are good to eat," she said. "But always look close — some plants have dangerous cousins." Brynja nodded solemnly, brushing the leaves, while Celenneth kept her watch on the treeline, her vigilance never quite at rest.

The tension of the road boiled over one evening beside a running stream. "We cross here," Celenneth said, arms folded. "The ford's shallow enough. It saves us near a day's backtracking."

Linnea frowned as she unpacked. "That stream looks gentle, but the rains upriver make it treacherous. What if the current's stronger than it seems? Would you risk Brynja being swept away?"

"We'll manage. I'll cross first and make sure. We've lost too much time already."

"And if it isn't safe? We're not two rangers on a mission, Celenneth. We have a child to think of."

"I know that," Celenneth snapped, her voice rising. "But we can't always take the safest path. Every day we delay leaves us more exposed."

Linnea stood, steady and firm. "This isn't about what's fastest. It's about what's best for all of us. We can backtrack to the proper crossing. One day, Celenneth. One day for her safety."

The words struck home. Celenneth stiffened. "Don't question my care for Brynja."

"Then show it," Linnea said quietly. "Make the choice that protects her — not just the one that pushes us on faster."

For a long moment the space between them was taut. Then Celenneth let out a sharp breath and her shoulders eased. "Fine. We backtrack. But if trouble finds us for the delay, we'd best be ready."

Linnea's expression softened. "We will be. Together."

As they neared Rivendell the very air changed — cooler, and stilled to something almost otherworldly. Golden-leaved trees lined the path, and moss softened their horses' hooves, and the wind brushed past like a whisper. Celenneth drew rein at the crest of a hill, and her breath caught as the valley opened below: the spires and arches of the Elven haven rising serene out of the mist. Linnea pulled up beside her, wide-eyed, and even Brynja leaned forward, pointing at the shimmering leaves. "It's beautiful," Linnea murmured.

As they descended, a welcoming party came out to meet them — Elves in silver and green, calm and ageless — and at their head walked Glorfindel, his golden hair bright as sunlight. "Celenneth of the Dúnedain," he said, warm and resonant. "Lord Elrond has been expecting you."

She dismounted, reverence and resolve mingled in her. "We've come far."

His gaze moved to Linnea and Brynja. "Your companions are welcome. Rivendell is a sanctuary for all."

Linnea slid down, gathering Brynja close, looking round her. "It feels safe," she said softly.

"Come," Glorfindel said. "You must be weary." And they were led in, and the weight of the road seemed to lift from them — though for Celenneth the shadow of the summons stayed, and whatever waited here, she would meet it with the two of them beside her.1

2The Glory of Rivendell

Súlimë 20, 2969

Under one of Rivendell's great arches Celenneth stood with Linnea and looked out over the valley, and even her composure gave way to awe. Twilight bathed it in gold and silver, the cascading falls shimmering like threads of starlight, the lanterns hung in the trees blending their warm glow with the haven's ethereal light. Brynja wriggled on Linnea's hip, reaching for the dancing lamps.

"I love its strangeness," Celenneth murmured, tipping her head back to the towering spires and their intricate carvings.

Linnea's voice was hushed. "It's like stepping into a song. So different from the Woodland Realm — so open, so full of light." She thought of Thalion's halls, dark and majestic beneath their canopy, cloaked in mystery; Rivendell seemed instead to embrace the stars, its beauty not hidden but sung aloud. An Elf passed, his robes flowing like water, his steps leaving no trace, and offered a serene smile; Linnea returned it, suddenly aware of her own dusty boots and frayed cloak. Another knelt to play a small silver harp, drawing a delighted squeal from Brynja, who reached to brush the strings and coax a faint sweet note. "They seem to belong to another age," Celenneth mused.

"They do," Linnea said. "Like they live outside of time." She sighed, shifting the child. "It's beautiful here. Peaceful. But I can't help feeling out of place."

Celenneth set a hand on her shoulder. "We're travelers here. But they welcome us."

The Hall of Fire lay in Rivendell's eternal twilight, and Elrond sat at the head of a low table, calm and commanding, the crackle of the hearth the only sound beneath the far whisper of the falls. Celenneth stood before him, composed despite the weight of the moment.

"Celenneth of the Dúnedain," Elrond began, steady and measured, "you have come far, and I thank you for it. But I bear tidings that will not be easy to hear."

She inclined her head, her hands tightening. "Speak plainly, Lord Elrond."

His gaze softened even as his tone carried its gravity. "Balin, son of Fundin, has fallen. He perished in Moria, seeking to reclaim its halls. Darkness rose against him and his kin, and though they fought valiantly, they were overwhelmed."

The words struck her with a quiet force. She drew a sharp breath, her jaw setting against the loss. "Balin was no fool," she said, low but steady. "He knew the risk. But he was determined. He believed Moria could be restored."

"He spoke of you with trust and respect," Elrond said. "Gandalf sent you to him for good reason. He valued your wisdom, and your strength."

Her throat tightened on the memories — Balin's laughter, his unbending spirit, the way he had spoken of his dreams for Khazad-dûm. "He was a good friend," she said quietly. "And a better leader than most credited."

An Elf stepped forward bearing a long cloth-wrapped bundle, and Elrond gestured to it. "There is more. The mithril Balin once entrusted to us has been wrought into armor — a gift in his memory, and a token of gratitude for your service." The cloth fell away to reveal a breastplate of gleaming mithril, its surface etched with swirling designs that caught the light like water under moonlight. Celenneth's breath caught as she stepped near.

"Light, and yet strong," Elrond said. "A protection worthy of one who walks in shadow to guard the light."

She reached out and brushed the cool metal, and for a moment the grief in her eased, replaced by a fierce resolve. "I will wear it," she said. "And honor Balin with every step."

Elrond inclined his head. "Let it serve you well, Celenneth of the Dúnedain. The road ahead will test you — but you are not alone."1

Later, under an ancient elm by the murmuring streams, Linnea sat with Brynja asleep against her chest, and a little way off Celenneth stood speaking with one of Elrond's counselors, her bearing easy and sure. Watching her, Linnea felt a realization settle heavy in her: Celenneth was more than a ranger. Here, among Elves who treated her with quiet reverence, it showed plainly — she was a protector whose deeds rippled far beyond their small family.

When Celenneth returned, Linnea hesitated before she spoke. "I didn't understand until now how much others look to you. How much you carry."

Celenneth's brows rose, a faint smile touching her lips. "I do what needs doing. Like anyone."

"No," Linnea said, quiet but firm. "It's more than that. And I'm starting to see — there'll be times we can't hold you back. Times we'll have to let you go."

Celenneth crouched beside her. "You've never held me back, Linnea. You gave me a reason to come home." She glanced toward the valley where the stars were kindling over Rivendell's spires. "And you've made me see something. We're nearer Bree now than we've been in years. If we're ever to visit, it may as well be now."

Linnea blinked. "Bree? After everything Elrond called you here for?"

Celenneth smiled gently. "The road will always call. But Brynja won't always be this small. And you've given me more than a duty — you've given me a home. Besides, what Elrond needed of me was mostly what I already knew; whatever comes next, he can summon others for it."

Linnea's gaze fell to Brynja's small hand curled against her, then back to Celenneth. "If we go, it's not only for Brynja. It's for us. For finding something solid."

Celenneth's hand covered hers. "Then let's make it solid. Together."

3Arriving in Bree

Súlimë 30, 2969

The gates of Bree creaked open, and the familiar smell of damp earth and woodsmoke rushed out to meet Linnea like a welcome. She sat straight-backed on Eorlin, her green cloak stirring in the breeze, and beside her rode Celenneth, her auburn hair taking the sunlight in fiery strands. Brynja, three years old now, sat before Linnea and giggled each time the mare shifted beneath her, drinking in every sight and sound with an eagerness that drew smiles from even the busiest townsfolk.

"Here we are," Linnea said, bright. "Bree. Home sweet home."

Celenneth chuckled — the mithril rings of her new armor glinting at her collar beneath the ranger cloak. Less taken than Linnea by the old town's rustic charms, she was warmed all the same by her partner's plain delight. "You speak of it like a kingdom," she teased.

Linnea beamed and guided Eorlin onto the main road. "To me it might as well be. There's the Prancing Pony — you'll find no better ale north of the White Mountains. And that bakery, just past the inn, where I used to sneak out to smell the bread before dawn. Smell it, Brynja?" She leaned to press her nose to the child's dark hair, and Brynja giggled and twisted to peer down the street after the yeasty scent.

They rode deeper into town, and Linnea felt the stares and murmurs follow them — some who knew her, others simply marking the two cloaked women and the lively child between them. Without Freowyn's familiar presence, the three of them seemed both more exposed and more striking in their difference.

She dismounted in the market square and swung Brynja down and set her firmly on her feet, and the child promptly wriggled free and toddled toward a stall bright with ribbons and painted toys. "Careful now," Linnea said, laughter in it, and Celenneth stepped close, ever watchful.

"Linnea Riverwain!" A middle-aged woman bustled from behind a stall, flour on her sleeves, and Linnea turned with a sheepish grin as she recognized Miss Calder. The older woman's eyes went over the Rohan cloak, over Celenneth's fine armor, and landed on Brynja, who had just discovered a spill of colored ribbons. "Bless me — is it really you, girl? Gone for years, and back now with a baby—" she caught herself, taking in Brynja's size "—a child, I mean, and a ranger out of a story at your side. What's come over you?"

Linnea flushed. "Hello, Miss Calder. It's a long tale. This is Celenneth." She gestured with open warmth. "My partner."

"Scandals and wonders," Miss Calder muttered, not unkindly, peering at Brynja. "And this is your daughter?"

"Yes. This is Brynja." Linnea smoothed a dark curl from the girl's brow. "Curious about everything. I had to keep her close on the ride."

Miss Calder's astonishment softened to something fond. "Well. You've explaining to do, but there's time enough for that." With a last glance at Celenneth — who returned a polite nod — she went back to her stall, calling over her shoulder, "Welcome home, Linnea."

Linnea let out a breath, her cheeks still hot. Celenneth only gave her a faint, reassuring smile — and then Brynja tugged at the ranger's cloak, and Celenneth stooped and scooped her up so she could see the stalls from a better vantage, and the child squealed and patted her shoulder as though claiming a prize mount.

They wandered the market, traders crying their wares, children weaving between the carts. A shopkeeper watched Brynja reach for a strand of Celenneth's hair. "Lively one," he remarked.

"Curious as a fox," Celenneth agreed, gently freeing her fingers. "And stronger than she looks." Her voice was warm and proud, and Linnea felt something glad and full at how naturally Celenneth wore this gentler side, so unlike her composure on the road.

A knot of children caught sight of Brynja, and one boy, freckle-nosed, pointed shyly. "Is that your baby?"

Celenneth straightened, her voice kind and thoughtful. "She's my daughter, yes."

Another gaped. "But you're a ranger. How can you be a mother too?"

A rare, gentle smile curved Celenneth's mouth. "Being a warrior doesn't mean I can't love or care for my child. It means I fight hard to keep her safe." She drew a soft green ribbon from a nearby basket and handed it to Brynja, who cooed and waved it like a banner. And Linnea stood beside them, her eyes bright, grateful for so perfect an answer.

A bold girl waved. "She's cute. What's her name?"

"Brynja," Celenneth said. "And she's curious about everything."

Brynja twisted to look at the girl, giggling, the ribbon fluttering, and the watching children smiled, and the last of the strangeness melted into an easy fascination. For Linnea there was comfort in the old familiar streets and in Celenneth steady at her side; for Celenneth, a belonging in Brynja's laughter and Linnea's warm grin; and for Brynja, Bree was a whole new world to be discovered — one leaf, one ribbon, one kind face at a time.

4Homecoming

Víressë 2, 2969

The wind swept low over the weathered hills around Bree, carrying damp leaves and distant woodsmoke, and spring lay full on the rolling fields as Linnea guided Eorlin along the rutted path toward the village she hadn't seen in years. Bree looked much the same — stout homes with moss-covered roofs under the shadow of the Chetwood — but the sight of it twisted something deep in her chest. Memories rose in the golden evening haze: her father's booming laugh coming up the South Road, her cousin Miri chasing chickens through the square, the warm smell of Aunt Merla's herb-laced bread. But they were brittle memories now, cracking under the weight of what came after — her father murdered, herself taken by bandits, the brutal days that followed. Bree had stayed untouched by all of it, peaceful and unchanged, as though the world had not shattered the day she was dragged from that road.

Celenneth rode silent at her side, her sharp gaze sweeping the calm streets from habit, the silver-edged embroidery of her green-and-gold cloak catching the low sun. At the crest of the last hill Linnea drew rein, staring down toward her uncle's house, her heart slamming, her knuckles white on the reins.

"You're quiet," Celenneth said gently.

Linnea forced her grip to loosen. "I wasn't sure how I'd feel, coming back."

"And now?"

"Like I'm chasing ghosts."

Celenneth nodded slowly, as if she understood more than could be said. "We don't have to stay long."

"No." Linnea squared her shoulders. "I need to do this."

The small stone house stood as she remembered, its moss-covered roof sagging but sound, herbs spilling wild from the crooked garden beds — rosemary, lavender, creeping thyme, all half-feral now. Bread-smell drifted from the chimney, and for a moment Linnea was twelve again, barefoot and laughing as her father lifted her over the garden fence. She swung down, and hesitated at the old gate with her hand on the worn latch. Celenneth dismounted, set Brynja on her feet, and rested a hand lightly on Linnea's arm — a silent offering of strength.

"Thank you," Linnea breathed. "Let's hope they still think of it as home."

She strode up the path and knocked, and the sound echoed like a long-forgotten bell. For a taut heartbeat there was silence — then the creak of hinges, and the door opened, and a wiry, graying man stood framed in it, his weathered face etched with hard years. His eyes went wide, his breath catching as though he might stagger.

"Linnea?" His voice cracked. "Is it — is it really you?"

"It's me, Uncle Harvin."

He braced against the doorframe, then turned and bellowed into the house, "Merla! The girl's come back!" Footsteps thudded, and her aunt emerged, sharp-featured and lined with age and hard practicality — and then her breath hitched and her flour-dusted hand flew to her mouth, and for a moment that brisk exterior cracked into something raw. "Linnea?" she whispered.

"It's me. I'm home."

Merla surged forward with a strangled cry and wrapped her in a fierce, crushing embrace that smelled of rosemary and smoke and something achingly familiar. "Stars above. We thought you were gone." Then she pushed back to grip Linnea's face and search it, and whatever she found only deepened her frown. "You're too thin," she snapped, retreating behind briskness like a shield.

More footsteps, and cousins tumbled into the room — Loran, broad-shouldered and lean now, staring as at a ghost; and Emily, sharper still and bold as ever, shoving past him with something close to anger in her eyes. "You're alive? After all this time?"

"I am. I came back."

A younger child half-hidden behind Merla's skirts pointed. "Is that a child?"

"I'm Brynja!" the girl announced, loud and clear, beaming up at them, and Merla's sharp eyes narrowed on her. "And who's this?"

Before Linnea could answer, Emily folded her arms, her voice cutting. "Where have you been, Linnea? Years. Not a word. Did you even think of us?"

Linnea held her ground. "I'm sorry. It wasn't safe to."

"That's enough for now," Harvin said, rough but steady, with the quiet authority that had kept his household in order for decades. He gestured to the long table by the hearth, where a pot of stew simmered. "Let her breathe. We'll eat first. Then we'll talk."

But the eating did not soften it. Merla paced by the hearth, her gaze cutting to Linnea with every turn, and the questions came like blows. You could have sent word. Travelers come through Bree all the time. Where the hells were you.

"It wasn't safe," Linnea said, and then, because there was no going back, gave them the truth of it. "They ambushed us on the South Road. Bandits — we never saw them coming. They killed my father first. He tried to fight, but there were too many." Harvin's breath hitched; Merla staggered back a step. "They took me after he fell. Dragged me into the forest. I escaped —" her gaze went to Celenneth "— because of her. She stole me out of their camp, right under their noses."

"Stealth was the safer choice," Celenneth said quietly.

The room fell still. "And after?" Harvin rasped.

"We ran. Through the wilds, always moving. We settled a while in Edoras, in Rohan. But Celenneth's road took her away often, and Brynja was too small to travel it."

Merla's eyes snapped to the child. "And who is she to you?"

Linnea drew a breath and exchanged a glance with Celenneth. "She's our daughter. We found her in the wild north — abandoned, barely a week old, wrapped in nothing but an old cloth. We heard her crying. There was no one else. We fed her, carried her, kept her warm through the dead of winter. We raised her. She's ours."

Harvin's voice was rough with grief. "Her mother must have died."

"We never knew," Celenneth said, her gaze gone distant. "There was no one."

Merla clutched at her apron. "And you kept her. In the wilds." Then, softer, her eyes shining with a sudden desperate yearning: "Let me hold her. Please. She's family."

Linnea hesitated, her heart pounding — then stepped forward and set Brynja on her aunt's lap. Merla's breath shuddered as she gathered the girl close, brushing the dark curls, tears standing in her sharp eyes — but her face was torn, and something harder moved under the grief. "She belongs here," she whispered fiercely. "Not out there."

"She belongs with me," Linnea said.

Merla did not loosen her hold. "She could have a real home. Here."

"She has one." Linnea's voice went cold. "With us." And when Harvin, low and stunned, asked how they had come to swear themselves to one another, Celenneth answered plainly. "We swore a life-oath. In every way that matters, we are bound." Emily recoiled; Merla stared, grappling with a thing she could not accept; and Brynja, feeling the tightening arms, began to fuss and reach for Linnea.

"And now?" Harvin asked at last. "What happens?"

"We're going north," Linnea said. "To find her people. Her rangers."

"You're leaving," Merla breathed, and then her face twisted cold. "She should stay here. She's family — she belongs—"

"With us," Linnea said, taking the child back into her arms. "You don't get to choose that."

It was Celenneth who ended it, when Emily said, bitterly, that if they meant to run again they should at least leave Brynja behind.

"No." The word rang out clear as a drawn blade, and the cottage fell silent. She stepped forward, calm and commanding and terribly certain. "Brynja belongs with Linnea. With us. You'd have her grow up here, tolerated, whispered about behind her back? Raised without her mothers, as though she hadn't already chosen us?" Her voice gentled without losing its edge. "We are her family. We made her family — with love, and with choice." Brynja stirred at the sound of her, reaching without waking, and Celenneth brushed the dark curls, the fierce protectiveness plain on her face at last. "She comes with us."

Merla begged them to stay at least until autumn; Harvin gruffly allowed they needn't rush north. For a heartbeat the thought of resting burned in Linnea like a cruel temptation — and then Celenneth's steady hand brushed hers, and she found the truth again. "We can't," she rasped. "We're already late." And when Emily demanded late for what — another adventure? — Linnea's fury broke loose. "We're not risking her life. We're saving it. From a world that doesn't want us." — "We want her," Merla cried, pale and stricken. "No," Linnea said, and the old pain cracked her voice. "You want what you think she should be."

The fire hissed low into the silence. Merla staggered a step. "So you are leaving."

Linnea's heart broke all over again, but she nodded. "We have to."

"Then go," Emily sneered. Harvin let out a ragged breath and said nothing — could not. And Linnea looked once into her family's stricken faces, aching with every word she could not take back, and turned to the door, and Celenneth's steady hand came to rest on her back and guided her out, and the door closed behind them with a heavy, final thud.

The cold wind bit as they reached the edge of Bree, the dark road stretching north like a scar. Under an old oak Linnea's legs gave out, and the sobs came up out of her at last, harsh and shaking, and she crumpled onto the frost-cold grass clutching Brynja to her chest. Celenneth dropped down and gathered her in.

"I tried," Linnea gasped. "I thought—"

"I know." Celenneth's own voice trembled. "I know."

Linnea buried her face in her cloak and wept out all the grief and rage and loss she had held back for years. "They hate us."

"They don't understand," Celenneth said gently, brushing her tangled hair. "They can't." And when Linnea whispered that she was so tired, Celenneth pressed a fierce kiss to her temple. "Then rest. I've got you."

Footsteps came crunching over the frost then, and Celenneth's hand went to her belt-knife — but a breathless voice called out. "Wait — please." Loran stumbled out of the dark path, coat half-buttoned, hands raised open in peace. "Don't go yet." He stopped just short of them, his face crumpling. "I came to apologize. For everything. I should have said something. Should have done something."

"You tried," Celenneth said quietly, and surprise, then something like respect, flickered across his face.

"I should have tried harder," he murmured. "They were wrong. About you. About all of it." He asked where they would go, and Linnea told him north, to find Celenneth's people, to try. He nodded, holding back his own tears, and his gaze lingered on the sleeping child. "She's lucky."

"We are," Linnea said, her voice breaking.

He took a hesitant step back, unwilling to break the fragile moment, and knowing he had to. "Safe travels. Stars guide you." And with one last glance he turned and vanished back down the dark path, toward the cold world he could not change. Linnea sank against Celenneth again, weeping — but differently now. "He cared."

"Yes," Celenneth said, holding her close, her fingers steady in Linnea's hair. "You're not alone."

"I know," Linnea whispered. And for the first time in years, she truly believed it.

Much later, the tears spent, they sat on a low rise above Bree, the firelight of distant homes flickering warm and strangely far away.

"They're not who I remembered," Linnea said at last, her gaze on the stars. "Or maybe I'm not. I always thought of Bree as home — the place I'd come back to. But now it just feels small. Like I've outgrown it. Outgrown them." She swallowed. "They'll always be family. But to them I'm still the girl who disappeared. Not this." She gestured at herself — the travel-worn cloak, Eorlin tethered nearby, Brynja asleep in her lap.

Celenneth rested a hand on her shoulder. "It's no bad thing, to grow. To change. To find a new home, even if it's not the one you expected."

"Do you ever feel that way?" Linnea asked, trembling. "That the road is the only place that makes sense?"

A faint, knowing smile. "Sometimes. But I've learned something else, too." Celenneth brushed her fingers against Brynja's small back. "Home isn't a place. It's the people you choose. Wherever that is — it's enough."

Linnea's breath hitched. "I'm scared," she admitted, barely above a whisper. "That one day I'll stop feeling like I belong anywhere."

"You'll always belong," Celenneth said, with quiet certainty. "With me. With Brynja. Wherever we go, we'll make our place."

Linnea let out a shaking breath and leaned into her warmth, careful of the sleeping child. "Thank you. For standing up for me tonight. For everything."

"I meant every word," Celenneth said, steady as stone. "You've become someone remarkable, Linnea. Don't let them make you forget it."

The silence that settled between them was a strong and comfortable one, and the stars stretched steadfast overhead. She had outgrown Bree — but she had grown into something far greater, and with Celenneth at her side she knew she could face whatever came next. Together, they were home.

5Celenneth's Childhood

Víressë 14, 2969

The three of them sat close by the fire in the house they had taken for the season in the Angle, the hidden Dúnedain country north of Bree. Brynja lay curled in Celenneth's lap, small fingers tangled in her cloak, and Linnea rested against the arm of her chair, her eyes soft with thought.

"Celenneth," she said after a while, "you've never told me much of how you were raised. What was it like, with the rangers?"

Celenneth's gaze went distant, though a faint smile touched her. "It was different," she said, resting her chin on Brynja's dark curls. "The rangers aren't like families in towns. They don't speak of love. They don't say I'm proud of you, or I care. But I knew they did."

Brynja tilted her head up, frowning. "They didn't say I love you, Mama?"
