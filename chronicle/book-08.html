1Leaving for Rivendell

The fire crackled softly in the hearth, casting flickering light across the modest room. Linnea sat at the table, Brynja nestled in her lap, the child tugging absently at the edge of her mother’s green cloak. Freowyn stood by the window, peeling an apple with steady hands, her gaze flicking occasionally toward the quiet tension in the room. Celenneth lingered near the door, her arms crossed, her posture braced as though against an unseen wind.

“I need to speak with you both,” Celenneth said, her voice measured but heavy with intent.

Linnea’s head lifted, her expression sharpening. “What’s on your mind?”

Celenneth took a step forward, her boots sounding softly against the wooden floor. “Elrond’s summons isn’t something I can ignore. I’ve decided to go to Rivendell.”

Freowyn paused, the curl of apple skin dangling from her knife. “Rivendell? That’s no simple journey. Are you asking us to come with you?”

Celenneth nodded, her gaze moving between them. “Yes. All of you. Brynja too.”

Linnea’s hand tightened around Brynja’s small frame, protective yet steady. “The road to Rivendell is long, Celenneth. And dangerous. Why take Brynja into that?”

“Because I can’t leave her,” Celenneth said firmly. Her eyes softened as they settled on Linnea. “I can’t leave any of you. If Elrond’s message holds the weight I suspect, I’ll need you by my side.”

Freowyn set the apple down, her expression thoughtful but guarded. She glanced at Brynja, then back at Celenneth. “I understand why you’d want to take your family with you,” she said carefully. “But I'm afraid I must stay here.”

Celenneth’s brows furrowed. “Freowyn?”

The woman’s tone was sorrowful but resolute. “I won’t follow you to Rivendell. Brynja has strong parents in you and Linnea, and while I’ve cherished every moment I’ve spent helping to raise her, this is your journey, not mine.”

Linnea’s expression softened, though it was tinged with sadness. “Are you certain?”

Freowyn smiled faintly. “I am. You’ve both given me something I never expected: the chance to be part of a child’s life, even for a little while. That’s a gift I’ll always treasure. But Rivendell is not where my path lies. And besides, she's weaned.”

Celenneth stepped closer, her voice quiet but warm. “Freowyn, I can’t thank you enough for everything you’ve done for Brynja. For all of us.”

Freowyn inclined her head, her demeanor quiet. “Take care of her. Take care of each other. That’s all I ask.”

Brynja chattered to herself, oblivious to the gravity of the moment, and Linnea held her a little tighter. Freowyn’s gaze lingered on the child, her smile wistful. “I’ll see you all again someday, I hope. But for now, the road belongs to you.”

The road stretched ahead, winding through frost-laden hills and rugged plains. Celenneth rode at the front, her sharp gaze scanning the horizon for any sign of danger. Behind her, Linnea followed with Brynja seated securely in front of her, snugly wrapped in warm layers, her mare’s gait careful and steady.

The air was crisp, tinged with the scent of pine and the faint promise of snow. Brynja shifted in her saddle, her small hands gripping the edge as she let out an excited squeal. Linnea glanced down at her daughter, adjusting Brynja’s scarf and murmuring, “Hush, love.”

Celenneth glanced back, her eyes lingering on the two. “How is she?”

Linnea’s voice carried a note of weariness. “Enthusiastic as always.”

They made camp as the sun dipped below the horizon, casting the sky in hues of orange and violet. Linnea worked alongside Celenneth to set up the tent, her movements efficient but subdued.

As the fire crackled, Linnea sat beside it, watching Celenneth with quiet curiosity. “You’ve been distant today.”

“I’ve been thinking,” Celenneth admitted, staring into the flames. “About what we’ll find in Rivendell. About what Elrond might reveal.”

Linnea shifted, her voice quieter. “And what comes after?”

Celenneth met her gaze, her expression serious. “Yes.”

Linnea nodded slowly, her hand resting lightly on Brynja’s soft hair. “Then let’s hope this journey is worth it.”

As the traveled, they took opportunities along the way to introduce Brynja to the skills of journeying. One evening, under a golden sunset, Celenneth knelt beside Brynja, showing her how to hold a small bow. Brynja’s laughter rang out as her first arrow veered far off course, embedding itself in a patch of moss. Linnea sat nearby, stitching a tear in Celenneth’s cloak, her smile soft as she watched her daughter’s delight.

“Almost had it that time,” Celenneth encouraged, adjusting Brynja’s grip. “Try again. Steady this time.”

Brynja squinted with determination, tongue peeking out as she nocked another arrow and let it fly. Though it missed the target, it landed closer, drawing a cheer from both women.

On another day, they ventured into a quiet glade. Linnea crouched beside Brynja, pointing out the vibrant green of wild garlic and the delicate fronds of fiddlehead ferns. “These are good to eat,” Linnea explained. “But always look closely—some plants have dangerous cousins.”

Brynja nodded solemnly, her small fingers brushing the leaves. Celenneth stood nearby, her watchful gaze scanning the treeline. The peacefulness of the moment was a rare gift, but Celenneth’s vigilance never wavered.

As evening fell, the trio would gather around the fire. Celenneth recounted tales of her travels, her voice low and rhythmic, as Brynja listened with wide-eyed wonder. Linnea hummed softly, her melody weaving through the crackle of the flames. For a time, the worries of the road faded, replaced by a sense of unity and calm beneath the starlit sky.

The tension had been building for days as they navigated the winding paths through early spring meadows. The air was thick with the scent of new blossoms and damp earth, but the mood among the travelers had grown taut. Exhaustion and frayed nerves finally boiled over one evening as they made camp beside a gently running stream.

“We need to cross the river here,” Celenneth said firmly, her arms crossed. “The ford is shallow enough, and it will save us nearly a day of backtracking to the proper crossing.”

Linnea’s brow furrowed as she unpacked supplies. “That stream may look gentle, but the rains upriver make it unpredictable. What if the current’s stronger than it seems? You want to risk Brynja being swept away?”

“We’ll manage,” Celenneth countered, her tone sharpening. “I’ll cross first and make sure it’s safe. We’ve lost too much time already.”

Linnea’s jaw tightened. “And if it’s not safe? If something goes wrong, what then? We’re not just two rangers on a mission, Celenneth. We have a child to think about.”

“I know that,” Celenneth shot back, her voice rising. “But we can’t always take the safest path. The longer we delay, the more we’re exposed to other dangers.”

Linnea stood, her voice steady but firm. “This isn’t just about what’s fastest. It’s about what’s best for all of us. We can backtrack to the proper crossing. It’s one extra day, Celenneth. One day for Brynja’s safety.”

The words hit their mark, and Celenneth’s posture stiffened. “Don’t you dare question my commitment to Brynja.”

“Then show it,” Linnea said quietly, her gaze unwavering. “Make decisions that protect her, not just push us forward faster.”

The space was tense between them. At last, Celenneth exhaled sharply, her shoulders relaxing slightly. “Fine,” she said. “We’ll backtrack. But if trouble finds us because of the delay, we’ll need to be ready.”

Linnea nodded, her expression softening. “We will be. Together.”

As they neared Rivendell, the air seemed to change. It grew cooler but carried a stillness that felt almost otherworldly. Towering trees lined the path, their golden leaves glimmering in the dappled light, and moss softened the sound of their horses’ hooves. Even the wind seemed gentler, brushing past them like a whisper.

Celenneth paused at the crest of a hill, her breath catching as Rivendell’s valley unfolded before her. The spires and arches of the elven haven rose like a dream from the mist, serene and eternal.

Linnea pulled up beside her, her eyes wide with wonder. Even Brynja seemed captivated, leaning forward slightly in her saddle and pointing toward the shimmering leaves with wide-eyed wonder. “It’s... beautiful,” Linnea murmured.

As they descended, a welcoming party emerged—elves clad in silver and green, their expressions calm and ageless. At their head stood Glorfindel, his golden hair gleaming like sunlight as he approached with a warm smile.

“Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” he said, his voice resonant and kind. “Lord Elrond has been expecting you.”

Celenneth dismounted, her demeanor a mix of reverence and resolve. “We’ve come far.”

Glorfindel’s gaze shifted to Linnea and Brynja. “Your companions are welcome here. Rivendell is a sanctuary for all.”

Linnea slid off her horse, cradling Brynja protectively as she looked around. “It feels... safe,” she said softly.

“Come,” Glorfindel assured her, his tone warm. “You must be weary.”

As they were led into Rivendell, the weight of the journey seemed to lift, replaced by a soothing calm. Yet for Celenneth, the shadow of Elrond’s summons lingered. Whatever awaited her here, she would face it—with Linnea and Brynja by her side.

2The Glory of Rivenell

Linnea stood beneath one of Rivendell’s great arches, her breath catching as she took in the breathtaking view. The soft glow of twilight bathed the valley in hues of gold and silver, the cascading waterfalls shimmering like threads of starlight. Brynja, perched on her hip, wriggled with excitement, her small hands reaching out toward the dancing lights of the lanterns hanging from the trees, their warm glow blending seamlessly with the ethereal beauty of the elven haven.

Celenneth, standing a few paces away, tilted her head back to gaze at the towering spires and intricate carvings of Rivendell’s architecture. “I love it’s otherworldliness,” she murmured, her normally composed voice tinged with awe.

Linnea nodded, her voice hushed. “It feels like stepping into a song. So different from the Woodland Elves—so open, so full of light.”

She thought of the Woodland Realm, with its dark and majestic halls hidden beneath a canopy of trees, cloaked in mystery. Rivendell seemed to embrace the stars, its beauty not hidden but celebrated, as if inviting the heavens to share in its splendor.

An elf passed by, his robes flowing like water, his steps so light they seemed to leave no trace. He offered a gentle smile, his ageless face serene. Linnea returned the nod, suddenly aware of her own travel-worn appearance—her boots dusty, her cloak frayed at the edges.

Brynja giggled as another elf approached, carrying a small silver harp. The elf knelt gracefully, playing a soft, lilting melody that drew a delighted squeal from the child. Celenneth chuckled, her shoulders relaxing slightly. “They seem like they belong to a different age entirely,” she mused.

Linnea watched as Brynja reached for the harp, her small fingers brushing the strings and producing a faint, sweet note. “They do,” Linnea replied softly. “Like they exist outside time.”

The elf rose smoothly, bowing slightly before departing, his presence as fleeting as a breeze. Linnea sighed, shifting Brynja in her arms. “It’s beautiful here. Peaceful. But I can’t help feeling... out of place.”

Celenneth placed a steadying hand on Linnea’s shoulder. “We’re travelers here, but they welcome us.”

Linnea looked around again, the awe mingling with a quiet gratitude. “You’re right. It’s more than I expected.”

The Hall of Fire was bathed in the soft glow of Rivendell’s eternal twilight. Elrond sat at the head of a low table, his presence calm yet commanding. Celenneth stood before him, her expression composed despite the weight of the moment. The room was hushed, the crackle of the hearth the only sound save for the faint whispers of the cascading waterfalls outside.

“Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” Elrond began, his voice steady and measured, “you have come far, and for that, I thank you. But I bear tidings that will not be easy to hear.”

Celenneth inclined her head, her auburn hair falling forward slightly as her hands tightened into fists. “Speak plainly, Lord Elrond.”

Elrond’s gaze softened, though his tone carried the gravity of his words. “Balin, son of Fundin, has fallen. He perished in Moria, attempting to reclaim its halls. Darkness rose against him and his kin, and though they fought valiantly, they were overwhelmed.”

The words struck Celenneth with quiet force. She inhaled sharply, her jaw tightening as she processed the loss. “Balin was no fool,” she said, her voice low but steady. “He knew the risks. But he was also determined. He believed Moria could be restored.”

Elrond inclined his head. “He spoke of you with trust and respect. Gandalf sent you to him for good reason. He valued your wisdom and your strength.”

Celenneth’s throat tightened as memories surfaced—Balin’s laughter, his unyielding spirit, the way he spoke of his dreams for Khazad-dûm. “He was a good friend,” she said quietly. “And a better leader than most gave him credit for.”

An elf stepped forward, bearing a long, cloth-wrapped bundle. Elrond gestured toward it. “There is more. The mithral Balin entrusted to us has been crafted into armor—a gift in his memory and a token of gratitude for your service.”

The cloth was unwrapped, revealing a gleaming breastplate of mithral, its surface etched with swirling designs that caught the light like water under moonlight. Celenneth’s breath caught as she stepped closer.

“It is light yet strong,” Elrond said reverently. “A protection worthy of one who walks in shadow to guard the light.”

Celenneth reached out, her fingers brushing the cool metal. For a moment, the grief in her heart eased, replaced by a fierce determination. “I will wear it,” she said firmly. “And I will honor Balin with every step I take.”

Elrond inclined his head solemnly. “Let it serve you well, Celenneth of the Dúnedain. The road ahead will test you, but you are not alone.”

Celenneth lifted the armor, feeling its strength and the weight of her resolve. “Thank you, Lord Elrond. For this, and for remembering him.”

As she turned to leave, the mithral caught the glow of the firelight, casting faint reflections on the walls. Though her loss lingered, Celenneth felt a renewed strength, the memory of Balin now a shield for the challenges ahead.

The soft murmur of Rivendell’s streams filled the air as Linnea sat beneath an ancient elm, Brynja asleep against her chest. The child’s even breathing matched the rhythmic cadence of the waterfalls, offering a rare moment of stillness. A few steps away, Celenneth stood speaking with one of Elrond’s advisors, her posture confident, her presence magnetic.

Linnea watched her, a realization settling heavily in her chest. Celenneth was more than just a ranger. Here, among the elves who treated her with quiet reverence, Linnea could see it plainly: Celenneth was a protector, someone whose actions rippled far beyond their small family.

When Celenneth returned, Linnea hesitated before speaking. “I didn’t realize until now how much others look to you. How important you are.”

Celenneth’s brows rose slightly, though a faint smile touched her lips. “I do what needs to be done, like anyone else.”

“No,” Linnea said, her voice firm but quiet. “It’s more than that. You carry so much, Celenneth. I see that now, and I’m starting to understand... there will be times we can’t hold you back. Times we’ll have to let you go.”

Celenneth crouched beside her, her expression thoughtful. “You’ve never held me back, Linnea. You’ve given me a reason to come home. And you’ve made me realize something.” She glanced toward the valley, where the stars began to emerge above Rivendell’s spires. “We’re closer to Bree now than we’ve been in years. If we were to visit, now might be the time.”

Linnea blinked, surprised. “Bree? After everything Elrond called you here for?”

Celenneth smiled gently. “The road will always call, but Brynja won’t always be this small. And you...” Her voice softened. “You’ve given me more than a duty. You’ve given me a home. Besides, what I provided Elrond was primarily information; what he needs next, he can summon others for.”

Linnea’s gaze fell to Brynja’s small hand curled against her chest, then back to Celenneth. “If we go back, it’s not just about Brynja. It’s about us. About finding something solid.”

Celenneth nodded, her hand covering Linnea’s. “Then let’s make it solid. Together.”

3Arriving in Bree

The gates of Bree creaked open, and the familiar scent of damp earth and wood smoke rushed out to greet Linnea as if welcoming a long-lost child. She sat straight-backed on her Rohan mare, a green cloak draped over her shoulders, its edges rustling in the gentle breeze. At her side rode Celenneth, her auburn hair capturing the sunlight in fiery strands.

Brynja, now three years old, sat astride the mare before Linnea, giggling whenever the horse shifted beneath her small legs. The child looked about with wide, curious eyes, drinking in every sound and sight with an enthusiasm that drew smiles from even the busiest of townsfolk.

“Here we are,” Linnea said brightly, her gaze swinging back to Celenneth. “Bree. Home sweet home.”

Celenneth let out a quiet chuckle. Her armor—mithral rings glinting at the collar—peeked from beneath her own ranger cloak. Though not as impressed by the old town’s rustic charms as Linnea, Celenneth found herself warmed by her partner’s obvious delight. “You speak of it as if it’s a kingdom,” she teased, voice light.

Linnea beamed, guiding her mare onto the main thoroughfare. “To me, it might as well be. Over there’s The Prancing Pony,” she announced, pointing toward the venerable old inn. “You won’t find better ale north of the White Mountains. And that bakery, just beyond the inn—where I used to sneak out to smell the bread before sunrise. Smell that, Brynja?” At this, she leaned forward, pressing her nose to Brynja’s soft hair. The child giggled, twisting in Linnea’s lap to peer down the street as though searching for the source of that yeasty aroma.

They continued deeper into town, Celenneth’s sharp eyes noting the stares and murmurs that followed their small entourage. Some recognized Linnea, others simply noted the two cloaked women—one clearly a seasoned warrior—and the lively toddler between them. Without Freowyn’s familiar presence, the pair seemed both more vulnerable and somehow more striking in their difference.

Linnea dismounted smoothly in the bustling market square, then swung Brynja down and set her firmly on her feet. The child promptly wiggled free of Linnea’s grasp and toddled a few steps forward, her attention drawn by bright ribbons and painted wooden toys. “Careful now,” Linnea said, though there was laughter in her tone. Celenneth stepped closer, ever watchful, ready to guide the curious child away from anything too breakable.

Just then, a familiar voice called out: “Linnea Riverwain!” A middle-aged woman in an apron bustled out from behind a stall, flour dusting her sleeves. Linnea turned, her grin turning sheepish as she recognized Miss Calder. The older woman’s eyes flicked over Linnea’s Rohan cloak, her traveling companion’s fine armor, and finally landed on Brynja, who had just discovered a particularly colorful array of ribbons.

“Bless me,” Miss Calder exclaimed, “is it really you, Linnea? Gone for years and now returning with a baby—” She paused, catching herself as she took in Brynja’s size. “A child, I mean—and accompanied by a ranger like out of stories! Goodness, what’s come over you, girl?”

Linnea flushed slightly. “Hello, Miss Calder. It’s a long story. This is Celenneth.” She gestured with open warmth toward her companion. “My partner,” she added gently.

“Scandals and wonders,” Miss Calder muttered, half to herself, though not unkindly. She peered curiously at Brynja, who by now had fixed her gaze on a cart brimming with bright apples. “And this must be your daughter?”

Linnea nodded. “Yes, this is Brynja.” She glanced down at the child and smoothed a curl of dark hair from the girl’s forehead. “She’s curious about everything. Had to keep her close on the ride, but now…” She laughed softly as Brynja hopped from one foot to the other, eager to explore.

Miss Calder’s astonishment softened to something affectionate, if still a bit astonished. “Well, you’ve some explaining to do, but I suppose there’s time enough for that.” With a final glance at Celenneth—who returned it with a polite nod—she moved off to tend her stall, calling over her shoulder, “Welcome home, Linnea.”

Linnea let out a breath, turning to Celenneth. The situation, half-expected, still left her cheeks hot. But Celenneth merely offered a faint, reassuring smile. Brynja tugged at Celenneth’s cloak, and the warrior stooped gracefully, scooping the child up so she could see the stalls from a better vantage. Brynja let out a delighted squeal, patting Celenneth’s shoulder as if to claim her as some prized mount.

They wandered through the market. Traders shouted their wares, while children weaved between carts, laughing and shrieking with the careless joy that Brynja’s bright eyes seemed eager to join. Though older women whispered behind raised hands, and a few men scratched their heads at the improbable trio, Celenneth remained serene. She held Brynja easily, the child’s legs dangling, tiny fists reaching out toward shiny trinkets and colorful scarves.

“She’s a lively one,” commented a shopkeeper, observing how the tot reached for a strand of Celenneth’s auburn hair.

“Curious as a fox,” Celenneth agreed, gently detangling Brynja’s fingers. “And stronger than she looks.” Her tone was proud and fond, and Linnea felt her heart swell at how naturally Celenneth embraced motherhood’s gentler side—so different from the warrior’s composure on the road.

As they moved on, a knot of children at the edge of the square caught sight of Brynja. One boy, freckles scattered across his nose, pointed. “Is that your baby?” he asked timidly, still struggling to reconcile the warrior-woman with the cheerful toddler she carried.

Celenneth straightened, her voice thoughtful and kind. “She is my daughter, yes.”

Another child gaped. “But you’re a ranger or a soldier! How can you be a mother too?”

Celenneth’s lips curved into a rare, gentle smile. “Being a warrior doesn’t mean I cannot love or care for my child. It means I fight hard to keep her safe.” She reached into a basket of ribbons at a nearby stall and pulled out a soft green one, handing it to Brynja, who cooed and waved it like a victory flag. Linnea stood beside them, her eyes bright with emotion, grateful that Celenneth had offered such a perfect answer.

A bold girl stepped forward, waving at Brynja. “She’s cute. What’s her name?”

“Brynja,” Celenneth said, her voice warm. “And she’s curious about everything.”

At that, Brynja twisted to look at the girl, giggling as the ribbon fluttered. The watching children smiled, and the tension melted into innocent fascination.

or Linnea, there was comfort in old, familiar streets and in Celenneth’s steady presence at her side. For Celenneth, there was belonging in Brynja’s laughter and Linnea’s warm grin. And for Brynja, Bree was a whole new world to discover—one leaf, one ribbon, one kind face at a time.

4Homecoming

The wind swept low over the weathered hills surrounding Bree, carrying the sharp scent of damp leaves and distant woodsmoke. Spring had come in full force, painting the rolling fields in vibrant hues. Linnea guided her horse along the familiar, rutted path leading toward the village she hadn’t seen in years. Bree looked much the same—a cluster of stout homes with moss-covered roofs nestled beneath the protective shadow of the Chetwood. But the sight of it twisted something deep in her chest.

Memories lingered like stubborn shadows in the golden haze of early evening: her father’s booming laugh as he returned from the South Road, her cousin Miri chasing chickens through the village square, and the warm smell of her aunt Merla’s herb-laced bread wafting from the open kitchen window.

But those memories were brittle now, cracking under the weight of what had come after—her father’s murder, her capture by bandits, and the endless, brutal days that followed. Bree had remained untouched by those horrors, still peaceful and unchanged, as if the world hadn’t shattered the day she was taken.

Celenneth rode silently at her side, her sharp gaze sweeping the path ahead, ever-watchful despite the village’s apparent calm. The silver-edged embroidery on her green-and-gold Rohan cloak caught the low sun, shimmering faintly in contrast to her dark leather armor. She carried herself with the quiet authority of a ranger who had survived far worse than uneasy glances or whispered suspicions.

As they crested the final hill, Linnea drew her horse to a halt, staring down at Bree’s familiar streets winding toward her uncle’s house. Her heart pounded against her ribs, her fingers tightening around the leather reins until her knuckles ached.

Celenneth reined in her horse beside her, her sharp features softening just a fraction as she studied Linnea’s tense profile. “You’re quiet,” she said gently.

Linnea exhaled slowly, forcing her grip to loosen. “I wasn’t sure how I’d feel coming back.”

“And now?”

Linnea’s gaze lingered on the crooked stone walls marking the village’s edge. She remembered walking along those same paths as a child, her father’s hand resting on her shoulder, steady and strong. “Like I’m chasing ghosts.”

Celenneth nodded slowly, as if she understood far more than Linnea could explain. “We don’t have to stay long,” she offered, though there was no judgment in her tone—only quiet reassurance.

“No.” Linnea squared her shoulders, forcing herself to sit straighter in the saddle. “I need to do this.”

The small stone house stood just as she remembered, its moss-covered roof sagging slightly but still sturdy. Overgrown herbs spilled from the crooked garden beds—rosemary, lavender, creeping thyme—all tangled and half-feral.

The smell of baking bread drifted faintly from the chimney, mingling with the scent of autumn leaves. For a moment, Linnea felt twelve years old again, barefoot and laughing as her father lifted her high above the garden fence, teasing her about her muddy boots.

Linnea swung down from her horse in one fluid motion, landing lightly despite the weight of memory pressing on her shoulders. She hesitated at the old wooden gate, her hand resting against the weathered latch worn smooth by time and use.

Celenneth dismounted with practiced grace, hoisting Brynja down before stepping to Linnea’s side. She said nothing, simply resting her hand lightly on Linnea’s arm—a silent offer of strength.

Linnea let out a slow breath, her fingers tightening briefly over Celenneth’s. “Thank you.”

“This is your home,” Celenneth reminded her quietly.

Linnea nodded, though her throat burned with emotion. “Let’s hope they still think so.”

Before she could second-guess herself, she straightened her shoulders and strode up the worn path, boots crunching over brittle leaves. She knocked firmly on the heavy oak door, the sound echoing like the toll of a long-forgotten bell.

For a heartbeat, there was only silence, stretching taut and breathless. Then came the slow creak of hinges, followed by the scrape of worn leather boots against the wooden floor.

The door opened.

A wiry, graying man stood framed in the doorway, his weathered face etched with lines of hard labor and loss. His sharp eyes widened in disbelief, his breath catching audibly. For a moment, he looked like he might stagger back under the force of memory.

“Linnea?” His voice cracked on the name, hoarse and trembling. “Is it… is it really you?”

“It’s me, Uncle Harvin.” Her voice was steady despite the knot tightening in her chest.

Harvin’s hand braced against the doorframe, as though he feared he might fall. His mouth opened soundlessly for a moment before he turned and bellowed, “Merla! The girl’s come back!”

Footsteps thudded across the old wooden floor, followed by a sharp, disbelieving voice.

“What nonsense are you shouting now, Harvin?”

The air thickened with tension as Linnea’s aunt, Merla, emerged from the narrow hallway, her sharp-featured face lined with both age and unyielding practicality.

Her breath hitched, one hand flying to her mouth as her flour-covered fingers trembled. For a moment, her sharp, no-nonsense exterior cracked, replaced by something raw and fragile—something Linnea hadn’t expected to see.

“Linnea?” Merla whispered, her voice wavering with disbelief.

“It’s me.” Linnea’s throat tightened. “I’m… home.”

For a heartbeat, no one moved. Then Merla surged forward with a strangled cry, wrapping Linnea in a fierce, crushing embrace that smelled of rosemary and smoke and something achingly familiar.

“Stars above,” Merla whispered into her hair. “We thought… we thought you were gone.”

Linnea froze for a moment, stunned by the intensity of the embrace. Slowly, hesitantly, she lifted her arms and returned the hug, feeling the trembling in her aunt’s shoulders.

Merla pushed her back just enough to grip her face between her hands, searching every line and freckle with desperate intensity. “Let me look at you.”

Linnea stood still under the sharp scrutiny, letting her aunt take in every change—every hardened edge carved by time, survival, and war. Whatever Merla saw only deepened her frown.

“You’re too thin,” she snapped, reverting to brisk practicality like a shield. “You’ve not been eating properly.”

Before Linnea could answer, more footsteps pounded down the hall as several younger cousins tumbled into the room, drawn by the commotion. Loran, now broad-shouldered and lean, skidded to a halt, staring as though he’d seen a ghost.

“Linnea?” he whispered, his voice uncertain, disbelieving.

“It’s me.” Linnea offered a small, tentative smile.

Emily, sharper-featured and still bold as ever, shoved past Loran, her dark eyes gleaming with suspicion and something dangerously close to anger. “You’re alive? After all this time?”

“I am,” Linnea said quietly, her voice steady despite the tightness in her chest. “I came back.”

The room fell into a stunned silence. Loran and Emily exchanged a glance, equal parts relief and confusion flashing between them.

“Is that a child” one of the younger children asked, half-hidden behind Merla’s skirts, wide-eyed with curiosity.

Celenneth shifted slightly, protective but calm, her hand resting on Brynja’s back with practiced ease.

Merla’s sharp eyes darted to Brynja, narrowing in confusion. “And who’s this?”

“I’m Brynja!” Her voice was loud and clear, and she beamed up at Merla.

Before Linnea could explain, Emily folded her arms, her voice cutting like steel. “And where have you been, Linnea? Years. No word—nothing. Did you even think us?”

Linnea flinched inwardly but held her ground, her jaw tightening. “I’m sorry. It… wasn’t very feasible.”

Merla’s gaze snapped back to her, fierce and searching. “Is that the truth?”

Brynja’s excitement bubbled over. “Can we follow it?”

Celenneth nodded, rising and extending her hand. “We’ll cross here. The water’s shallow, but the rocks are slippery. Step carefully.”

Brynja gripped Celenneth’s hand eagerly, her face set with determination. Celenneth crossed first, her boots finding steady purchase on the slick stones. She turned, steadying herself as she held out her hands.

Brynja took the first rock confidently, her small foot landing with careful precision. She wobbled a bit but recovered quickly. Linnea watched closely, her hands hovering protectively.

“Good!” Celenneth encouraged. “Next one—slowly.”

Brynja grinned, taking another step—then another. But on the third rock, her boot slipped, her arms flailing as she lost balance.

“Mama!” she cried out, her voice high-pitched with panic.

Time fractured.

Brynja’s legs buckled, her small body pitching sideways into the stream with a sharp, bone-jarring thud against the rocky bank. The sound of water splashing seemed to echo, stretching unbearably as the world tilted around them.

“Brynja!” Linnea’s voice pierced the stillness, sharp with terror. She was already moving, her boots splashing through the water as she rushed forward, heart pounding wildly.

Celenneth dropped to her knees, her hands already searching Brynja’s body with practiced precision. Brynja let out a whimper, tears streaming down her face, but she didn’t scream.

“It’s her leg,” Celenneth said evenly, though her voice was tight, controlled. Her fingers gently traced the girl’s shin, feeling for swelling and breaks. “It’s broken.”

Linnea dropped down beside them, her hands trembling as she cupped Brynja’s face. “Oh, my love... are you all right? Does it hurt?”

Brynja nodded, her breath shuddering, her voice small and wavering. “It hurts, Mama.”

Celenneth’s steady calm remained unshaken, though Linnea could see the tightness around her eyes. “She’ll be fine. It’s a clean break. We need to splint it and get her back to the house.”

Linnea swallowed hard, forcing herself to focus. “What do you need me to do?”

“Hold her still while I find sticks,” Celenneth commanded, already scanning the ground with swift efficiency.

Brynja bit her lip hard to keep from crying out as Linnea cradled her gently, whispering words of comfort through her own rising panic.

Celenneth returned moments later with two sturdy branches, already tearing strips from her cloak with deft, practiced hands. “Brynja,” she murmured, her voice calm but resolute, “this will hurt, but I’ll be quick.”

Brynja nodded bravely, clutching Linnea’s hand tightly as Celenneth set the splint with precise, efficient movements. Brynja let out a sharp cry but didn’t wail, her trust in Celenneth and Linnea holding her steady.

When the splint was secure, Celenneth gathered Brynja into her arms, lifting her gently. The girl shivered but rested her head against Celenneth’s shoulder, her breath still coming in unsteady gasps.

“She’ll need to rest and stay off her leg,” Celenneth said quietly, her voice softer now. “But she’ll heal.”

Linnea exhaled shakily, pressing a tender kiss to Brynja’s damp forehead. “You were so brave, my little bird. We’ll take care of you.”

Brynja sniffled, her tears slowly drying. “I... I didn’t mean to fall.”

“It wasn’t your fault,” Celenneth said gently, brushing a strand of hair from Brynja’s face. “Accidents happen. The important thing is that you’re safe now.”

As they made their way back through the quiet woods, Linnea stayed close, her hand resting on Brynja’s back, anchoring herself in the girl’s steady breath. Her heart still pounded with the memory of the fall, but Celenneth’s calm strength held her together.

They were a team, and together, they could face anything. Even broken bones... and the fears that came with them.
