1Gandalf’s Visit

Loëndë, 2969 (Mid-Year’s Day)

The wind swept briskly down from the hills, carrying the scent of autumn leaves and cold river water. Where the Hoarwell met the Loudwater, their confluence sang in a ceaseless murmur, cradling the small, solitary cabin nestled in the shelter of a pine grove. Smoke curled lazily from the chimney, promising warmth against the creeping dusk.

Inside, the hearth crackled, casting flickering light over the simple, sturdy interior. Linnea stirred a pot of venison stew hanging over the fire, the rich scent filling the room. Brynja sat cross-legged near the worn rug, her small hands carefully arranging wooden animals in an elaborate story only she understood. Across the room, Celenneth sat on a low stool near the window, sharpening the blade of her hunting knife with slow, practiced precision, her sharp eyes occasionally flicking toward the darkening forest beyond.

The sudden knock on the door shattered the quiet, its sharp, deliberate rhythm cutting through the peaceful stillness.

Celenneth was on her feet in an instant, her hand resting instinctively on the worn hilt of her knife. Linnea exchanged a glance with her, alert but composed. Visitors were rare this far north—and uninvited ones even more so.

With a nod from Linnea, Celenneth moved silently to the door and eased it open just enough to see the figure standing outside. Tall and weathered, he leaned lightly on a twisted wooden staff topped with curious carvings, his long grey cloak damp from the river mist and his wide-brimmed hat pulled low.

“Good evening,” he said, his deep voice rich and measured, warm with familiarity despite the strangers’ distance. His piercing eyes gleamed beneath the hat’s shadow, sharp and knowing.

Celenneth blinked, momentarily surprised. Then her expression softened into something almost resembling respect. She stepped back, pulling the door wider. “You’d better come in,” she said, her voice quiet but steady. “The wind’s turning.”

The old man ducked slightly to fit through the doorway, his heavy boots thudding softly against the worn wooden floor. His sharp eyes swept across the cabin’s modest interior, taking in the hearth, the simple wooden table, the child watching him curiously from behind Celenneth’s legs.

“Linnea,” Celenneth said, closing the door behind him. “This is Gandalf.”

Linnea frowned faintly, setting the wooden spoon aside. The name tugged at something in her memory—half-whispered stories told over low campfires, of a wandering wizard known across the wildlands for deeds both wondrous and dangerous. She hadn’t expected the figure standing in her home, rain-dappled and steady as an ancient tree.

“Gandalf,” she repeated cautiously, wiping her hands on a linen cloth. “You’ve come far.”

“I often do,” Gandalf said with a faint smile, inclining his head. “And rarely without reason.”

His gaze drifted back to Celenneth, who hadn’t moved from her spot near the door. She stood at ease, though her hand still rested lightly on her belt, ready out of long habit. His sharp eyes softened briefly. “You’ve built something strong here,” he observed quietly. “A good home.”

Celenneth nodded once, though her expression remained unreadable. “What brings you this far?”

“Ah,” Gandalf said, his eyes gleaming with something unreadable, “we’ll come to that in time. But if it’s no trouble... a cup of tea would be most welcome after such a long road.”

Linnea hesitated for only a moment before moving to retrieve a sturdy clay mug from the wooden shelf. “Sit by the fire,” she offered, keeping her voice even. “You must be cold.”

With a quiet murmur of thanks, Gandalf lowered himself into one of the simple wooden chairs near the hearth, stretching his long legs toward the fire’s warmth. His cloak hung heavily across his shoulders, still damp at the edges from the rivers’ mists.

Brynja peeked around Celenneth’s side, her wide eyes fixed on the strange visitor. Gandalf’s expression softened again as he inclined his head toward her. “And who might this young lady be?”

“Brynja,” Linnea answered, placing the steaming mug of tea into his weathered hands. “Our daughter.”

Brynja gave a small, cautious nod, staying close to Celenneth but watching him with unabashed curiosity.

Gandalf took a slow sip of the tea, letting the warmth settle through him. “This is fine work,” he remarked appreciatively. “Good tea is harder to come by than you might think in these parts.”

Linnea sat at the opposite end of the hearth, her eyes never leaving him. “You said you came on business,” she prompted gently. “What kind?”

Gandalf rested his staff against the arm of his chair, his fingers idly tracing its carved length. “I need someone to undertake a journey,” he said, his tone turning serious, though not unkind. His sharp gaze settled on Linnea, weighing her silently.

Linnea’s breath slowed, her pulse steady despite the sudden heaviness in the room. “What sort of journey?”

Gandalf reached into the folds of his cloak and drew out a small leather pouch, worn but carefully preserved. “The ring you carry,” he said quietly, his voice low and grave. “The one given to you by the spirit of the Northmere.”

Celenneth tensed, and Linnea met Gandalf’s gaze evenly, refusing to let her unease show.

“I still have it,” she said carefully.

“Good,” Gandalf murmured. “Its nature remains... uncertain. But I fear its history may be older—and darker—than even you realize.” His expression darkened. “There is someone in the West... in the Shire. He knows much about such things. He can examine it.”

Linnea narrowed her eyes slightly, though her voice stayed steady. “You want me to take it there.”

“Yes,” Gandalf confirmed. “To him. Quietly.”

Celenneth stepped forward, her jaw tightening. “Linnea is capable, but I’m the faster traveler.” Her voice was calm but edged with iron. “Let me take it.”

Gandalf inclined his head in acknowledgment. “You are all those things, Celenneth. But this task... belongs to Linnea.”

His voice softened, but his expression remained firm. “The burden was placed into her keeping. It is hers alone to carry.”

Linnea exhaled slowly, already calculating routes, dangers, and what she would need. “When do I leave?”

“At first light.”

Brynja shifted closer to Celenneth, her small fingers slipping into her mother’s hand. Celenneth squeezed them gently, her face unreadable but her eyes filled with something fierce and protective.

Linnea looked to Celenneth. “You know Gandalf far better than I; what do you think?”

Celenneth sighed, her shoulders slumping slightly. “If he says you need to go, you need to go. Brynja and I will take care of things here.”

Linnea stepped closer to Celenneth, her fingers intertwining with hers. “I’ll be back soon,” she promised quietly. “Always.”

Celenneth nodded once, her grip tightening. “I’ll be waiting.”

And as the wind rose again outside, the rivers whispered their endless song, while shadows deeper than autumn’s dusk gathered unseen in the farthest reaches of the West.

2Traveling Westward

Cermië 1, 2969

The wind over the Angles had a lingering chill despite the early spring sun that wrestled quietly with the remnants of winter. The land here lay south and a little east of Bree, a quiet reach of Eriador seldom spoken of in grand tales. Yet from this unassuming place, Linnea began her journey westward, guided by Gandalf’s calm presence. She had known him only briefly before, but his reputation and bearing, and the unshakable wisdom in his keen eyes, convinced her that wherever he led, it would be worth following.

Her horse, Eorlin—a gift from the Rohirrim, its lineage proud and strong—stamped the ground as if sensing their departure. Gandalf sat astride his own mount, an old but steady creature, lean with years of travel. The wizard’s robes caught the sunlight, and his beard blew like pale smoke in the breeze. He inclined his head toward Linnea, acknowledging the day’s start.

“Well,” he said, voice low yet carrying, “the road ahead is not short, but neither is it so long that we cannot endure it. Nine days of steady pace, if Eorlin is sure-footed and we meet no ill luck, should bring us within the gentle borders of the Shire. From there, Hobbiton is but a quiet ride away.”

Linnea nodded. She had heard of the Shire and its folk—Hobbits, who seldom ventured beyond their hedgerows. She imagined gentle hills and soft lantern lights under starlit skies, nothing like the rugged paths she had known traveling in the wider world. The thought brought a certain warmth to her chest. Nine days. She had endured far worse.

They set off. The Angle’s low hills gave way to mild slopes and quiet vales. The East Road, running westward through Bree-land and well beyond, would be their guide. They followed its winding course as the morning lengthened, and the only sound was the rhythm of hooves and Gandalf’s occasional humming—old traveling songs of distant lands. The wizard’s voice, low and comforting, seemed to coax the countryside into wakefulness.

Sometime after noon, as the sky deepened to a pale and cloudless blue, Linnea found herself relaxing into the saddle. The worries that had knotted her mind—lingering doubts about the road, about what lay ahead—eased under the steady rise and fall of Eorlin’s gait. Yet it was always best to remain watchful, Gandalf had said, and so she kept her eyes moving over the landscape.

They rode on until the afternoon shadows lengthened and the chill returned in subtle currents. A rustling sound brought Linnea’s gaze to a cluster of thorny shrubs not far from the roadside. Gandalf, too, slowed, raising his staff. At first, she saw nothing—only the stark shapes of thorns and stunted bushes. Then, with sudden ferocity, two ragged figures leapt out, spears raised. Men or something akin, though lean and desperate-looking, clad in stained cloaks and patchworks of leather. Brigands.

“Stay your hands!” Gandalf’s voice rang clear, but desperation seldom heeds wisdom. The brigands took a step forward, though hesitation was plain in their eyes. They had expected easy prey, perhaps a lone traveler of no consequence. But here was a wizard with keen eyes and a calm-seeming woman mounted on a horse that looked as if it could outrun any chase. And more curious still—Linnea did not cower or shriek. She studied them carefully.

Linnea knew well the value of a kind word over a drawn blade in certain circumstances. These men, ragged as they were, might respond better to courtesy than to steel. She raised a hand, showing it empty, and eased Eorlin forward. “Ho there,” she called, voice quiet but clear. “Strong men such as yourselves have no need to waylay weary riders. There’s little profit in spilled blood. Far wiser to gain coin honestly, or at least without risk of death. Let us pass—perhaps we can come to some small arrangement that spares everyone’s skin.”

Her voice was level and calm, as if she were greeting guests at a distant homestead rather than confronting highway robbers. She praised them, in her way—acknowledging their strength, their cunning—letting them believe themselves reasonable negotiators rather than cornered rats. The brigands exchanged uncertain glances, their knuckles whitening on their spear-shafts. Then the taller one, his hair a greasy tangle, spat to the side. “We ain’t fools,” he muttered, as if convincing himself. “Coin’s better than steel.”

They accepted a few silver pennies to guide her and Gandalf safely past the next rough stretch of road. The tension ebbed slowly, and soon the brigands melted back into the scraggly hills. Gandalf released a soft breath. “Well played,” he said, voice low. “Your courtesy saved us trouble—and them as well.”

Linnea smiled thinly. “Desperation makes men foolish. But I’m glad we parted without bloodshed.” She turned her gaze to the west. “Let’s continue.”

They rode on, and soon the brigand-haunted lands lay behind. The next days unfolded in a pattern both familiar and strangely comforting. Day by day, they followed the East Road westward, passing once more through the outskirts of Bree-land. The weather remained clear, but the air was still sharp. Linnea kept her cloak wrapped tight, and Gandalf lit his pipe in the evenings, sending grey curls of smoke dancing into the twilight.

Beyond Bree, the world grew quieter, the road less traveled. On their fourth day of travel, they approached the Brandywine Bridge, the winding river glittering in the early light. The Shire lay beyond—though still several days’ ride west through farmland and gentle hill-country. Even as they crossed into that gentle land, Linnea sensed a subtle change. The grass grew greener, the breeze smelled faintly of turned earth and blossoms not yet fully opened. Small flocks of sheep grazed distant hillsides, and here and there she glimpsed a round door set into a hill, smoke curling from chimneys half-buried in the earth. Hobbit-holes. Hobbit-folk themselves she did not yet see—perhaps they were still abed or indoors, attending to their quiet comforts.

Onward they rode. The road was gentle now, and the sun felt kinder. As the evening of their sixth day approached, near the rolling rises known as the Green Hill Country, an odd stillness blanketed the land. Then, drifting on the wind, came a faint singing. Clear, lovely, and yet shadowed by sadness. Linnea and Gandalf halted. “This is no Hobbit tune,” Gandalf whispered, narrowing his eyes. She called out in greeting, “Fair voice among the trees, we mean no harm! If you are friend, come forth and speak plainly!”

The singing ended. Out of the wooded gloom stepped a slender figure—an elf. Linnea had met Elves before in passing, had seen their distant company moving beneath starlight, but here was one in solitary wander. His name, he gave as Fëarnor of Lindon, and he wore sorrow like a faded mantle. Linnea dismounted and bowed gently. Her courtesy, born of travels and teachings, pleased him, and he softened his guarded stance.

He warned them of changes stirring in the quiet lands. “The barrows to the south have grown restless,” he murmured, voice as gentle as distant bells. “The Old Forest stirs, and not kindly. Dark lights flicker near Needlehole, west in the Shire’s Westfarthing. Few know what it means. Be cautious. Not all lights are meant to be followed.”

Linnea listened intently, her heart heavy with a concern she could not name. Gandalf observed in silence, as if weighing Fëarnor’s words like runes. When Linnea offered the elf company, he declined. “My path leads east,” he said sadly, as if revisiting old wounds. “Yours leads west. May the stars guide you, traveler.”

And with that, he slipped away, leaving only a faint whisper of elvish song that faded into the sigh of the wind.

They rode on, thoughtful and subdued, as sunset gilded the Shire’s gentle slopes in bronze and rose. Linnea broke the silence first. “He spoke with careful hints, but I sense something deeper. Strange lights on the moors—this is no idle fancy. I’ve heard tales of will-o’-the-wisps, lights that lead travelers astray. Usually just stories told in Bree’s common-room to frighten children.”

Gandalf nodded. “Keep your wits about you. Shadows remain in Middle-earth, even after great evils have been diminished. They linger in hollows and forgotten places, waiting for unwary souls.”

In the following days, as they rode deeper into the Shire, the land’s quiet beauty asserted itself. Gentle hills rolled by, crowned with hedgerows and dotted with small homesteads. Smoke rose from kitchen fires, and sometimes Linnea glimpsed a hobbit’s bright waistcoat or heard a distant snatch of laughter.

They pressed on. At midday of their seventh day, as they paused beside a slender stream to water the horses, Linnea decided it was time to ask the question that had weighed on her mind. “Gandalf, you speak with ease of distant lands and old alliances. I’ve heard you mention Celenneth’s name before. I know her well—better than well; she is my beloved, my partner through all these uncertain roads, and the mother of my child. She is a Ranger of the North. I know who she is to me. But I wonder… what is she to you?”

Gandalf’s face grew thoughtful, as though he were dusting off a memory. He struck a match and lit his pipe, letting the aromatic smoke drift into the spring air. “Celenneth,” he said at last, “yes, we have crossed paths a few times. Not so very long ago, I asked her to carry a message to Balin in Moria. Before that, we met near Fornost, though it was not so far in the past as I might pretend—it simply feels a lifetime ago. The Dúnedain had come to me with troubling reports of strange doings in the North, and I was seeking keen-eyed help. Celenneth joined me in studying the old stones and weathered runes.”

He took another quiet puff, gazing at the horizon. “She impressed me with her courage and her swift understanding, though she had only just begun walking the Ranger’s path. Her heart proved steadfast, her mind keen. We did not spend long together, but those hours were enough for me to trust her judgment. In uncertain times, that trust is precious.”

Linnea listened, absorbing every syllable. These were parts of Celenneth’s story that she had never heard—not because they were kept from her, but simply never spoken of. She pictured her beloved, a young Ranger striding beside this grey pilgrim, poring over inscriptions older than kingdoms.

She met Gandalf’s eye and nodded. “It seems,” she said slowly, “that you have found friends in every corner of this land. Elves, Dwarves, Rangers… Many have shared your firelight.”

Gandalf answered with a gentle smile. “All who walk under these skies have stories to tell, Linnea. I do not claim them as friends lightly, but I am grateful for each honest heart that crosses my path—including Celenneth’s. And yours, if I may say so.”

She and Gandalf spoke at length about many things as they rode—what lay beyond the distant western seas, how Men and Hobbits lived side by side in Bree-land, and whether the faint new leaves budding in the hedgerows might herald a kinder spring after all. At one point, Linnea confessed some of her lingering apprehensions:

“I cannot quite shake the feeling that the land itself watches us,” she said. “As if eyes lie hidden just beyond the edge of the road. Not hostile, necessarily, but watchful. Perhaps it’s a fancy inspired by the elf’s words.”

Gandalf nodded gravely. “Your instincts do you credit. To listen closely to the world is a rare gift. Indeed, the earth remembers much, and old powers stir quietly. Not all are ill. Some are merely curious—or protective. Still, vigilance serves us well.”

They passed a small hobbit orchard late that afternoon, branches just beginning to bud with pinkish blossoms. Linnea smiled at the gentle scene. Two hobbit children peeked from behind a hedge, watching the tall figures ride by, eyes wide. When Gandalf tipped his hat, they giggled and scampered off, probably to tell their parents of the tall traveler and his companion.

As dusk settled, they made camp in a sheltered hollow. The nights were still cold, and Linnea pulled her cloak tight, her bedroll placed near a small fire. Gandalf prepared tea from herbs he carried in a pouch. Nightingales sang somewhere in the dark, their voices silver threads woven into the tapestry of twilight. Linnea sipped the warm tea, grateful for its comfort.

The next morning brought a gentle mist, which draped itself over the hills and turned the distant fields into shapes half-seen. They broke camp and rode on, careful and steady, Eorlin’s hooves muffled by the damp earth. Linnea’s mood was calm and resolved. They had warnings, yes, but also hope. The Shire was not a land of horrors, after all—its peaceful people might not even know of any disturbance. Perhaps these flickers of unease would remain distant flickers only.

3Hospitality and Threat

Cermië 8, 2969

The sun was sinking low, painting the sky in bands of rose and amber when Linnea drew her cloak tighter, breathing in the scent of turned earth and distant chimney smoke. The wind had settled, and the evening chill sharpened as they rode. She watched Gandalf adjust his hat and thought again about all the stories swirling around him. The old wizard carried a quiet weight in his eyes—he had walked these lands longer than any mortal would ever know. Yet it was the little things he remembered: an oak that once sang with nightingales, a meadow rumored for sweet clover, a hollow where Dwarves once camped decades past. He sprinkled these recollections into their ride as if they were breadcrumbs of comfort, reminders that even in uncertain times, old paths held kind memories.

They had been talking about Hobbits, and as the evening deepened, Linnea spoke up with a wry smile, “You know, I’ve heard rumors of your fireworks. The Hobbits seem to think you’re some sort of traveling wonder-maker.”

Gandalf chuckled, a deep, genuine laugh that rose from his chest. “They do love a good story in the Shire. I can’t say they’re entirely wrong. I’ve set off a few fireworks in my time—bright ones, colorful ones. The Hobbits remember them fondly, as they remember any hint of magic that doesn’t trouble their garden walls.” He sighed, voice drifting lower. “The Shire is small, Linnea. That’s what makes it worth protecting. The larger world may thrash and burn, but here things remain gentle—at least for now.”

Linnea watched his face, seeing the hint of sadness in the lines around his eyes. He spoke often of big things—the troubles beyond far horizons. But now, he turned thoughtful and said, “In the end, it’s not always grand armies that save us. Sometimes it’s a soft word, a helping hand, or a small kindness that turns the tide. Don’t forget that. You never know when a simple act might matter more than a hero’s sword.”

The day’s last light slipped behind the distant hills. Linnea nodded, absorbing the wisdom. The road ahead remained mysterious, but somehow, with Gandalf nearby and the memory of Celenneth’s presence in her heart, she felt less alone.

They rode on until the sky brimmed with stars and the wind carried the faint smell of baking bread. Soon, a gentle glow on the western slope revealed a cluster of lights—Whitfurrows, a small Hobbit village. Gandalf pointed with his staff. “We’ll try there for the night. Hobbits are welcoming folk, especially if we arrive before all the doors are locked and the hearth fires banked.”

Linnea nodded. A warm bed beat sleeping under the open sky tonight. She guided Eorlin beside Gandalf’s horse, reassuring her mount with quiet strokes along the neck. Eorlin whickered softly, as though agreeing that a roof over their heads sounded just fine.

The road led into a tidy green where a handful of Hobbits were closing down small market stalls. At their arrival, a stout fellow with rosy cheeks leaned on his walking stick and called, “Travelers at twilight! Looking for supper and a bed, I reckon?”

Gandalf tipped his hat. “Exactly so, my good Hobbit. If there’s such a thing as a spare room and maybe a ladle of stew, we’d be grateful.”

The Hobbit eyed them a moment, then nodded. “You’ll want to talk to Marigold Bramblefoot, just over yonder. She’s got a home that’s had more than a few guests from away. Follow me.” He hobbled on, and they followed at an easy walk. The house they approached had a round green door and windows glowing softly. The smell of rosemary drifted through the evening air.

Inside, Marigold Bramblefoot—a plump, warm-eyed Hobbit matron—beamed at their request. “I’ve got stew on and bread fresh from the oven. I’ll not turn you away. Come in, come in!” She paused, lowering her voice just a notch. “Strange lights have folks uneasy these days. No trouble, I hope?”

“Just travelers in need of comfort,” Gandalf said smoothly. “A warm meal and a roof, that’s all.”

The Hobbits ushered them into a snug sitting room. Linnea let Gandalf handle the introductions and the easy chatter. She noticed the slight tension in some of the Hobbits’ eyes. Rumors must have reached even here—tales of odd lights in the Westfarthing, strange whispers drifting across the moors. But they said nothing openly.

When it seemed all was settled—Marigold was clearing space in a small guest room at the back—Linnea slipped outside to tend the horses. She needed a breath of quiet to steady her thoughts. In the stable, she brushed down Eorlin and Gandalf’s mount, running her fingers through their manes, feeling their warm breath on her hands. The silence of the Shire at night was a comfort, yet she couldn’t help a prickle of unease at the memory of the Elf’s warning days ago.

As she stepped back from the stable door, something subtle shifted. The air no longer felt entirely empty. She stood still, straining to listen. A faint rustle whispered through the hedgerow beyond the stable—a presence too quiet to be an animal. The horses sensed it too; Eorlin tossed her head, ears flicking in nervous arcs.

Linnea’s heart tightened. She had been on the road long enough to trust her instincts. Without making a sound, she circled to the stable’s side, keeping low, letting the shadows wrap around her. A figure moved past the back gate—slow, careful. She caught a glimpse of a hooded shape, blending with the night. Whoever it was, they were too cautious to be a simple traveler. And the faint scrape of metal on wood suggested a drawn blade.

She needed Gandalf’s counsel, but scaring the Hobbits would do no good. Linnea crept toward the house and tapped lightly on a windowsill. Inside, she saw Gandalf turn, his eyes alert. He politely excused himself and came out, staff in hand, his old eyes serious now.

“There’s someone out there,” Linnea whispered. “Casing the stable, at least, and armed.”

Gandalf frowned. “Not good. Show me.”

Linnea led him silently to the back gate. He studied the trampled grass, touched the bent stems. “Fresh tracks,” he muttered. “Two-legged, and not a Hobbit.” He rose, gripping his staff. “We need to be careful. We’re not alone tonight.”

Linnea nodded. Gandalf offered to ward the house while she kept watch. She agreed, heart pounding but steady. After all, this was what she knew—silent vigilance, the weight of a drawn bow, and the patience to wait for an enemy to show themselves.

With bow in hand, she crouched near a low hedgerow. The village’s distant lanterns glimmered behind her, and the stable loomed quietly. Minutes ticked by. She focused on the hush of night: the soft rustle of leaves, the distant murmur of Hobbit voices indoors, the gentle creak of stable beams.

Then she saw him again—a faint shape slipping along the stable’s edge. He moved like a wraith, trying not to be seen, blade gleaming dully in the moonlight. Linnea’s heart clenched. This was no curious wanderer. This was someone who meant harm—to the horses, the Hobbits, or her and Gandalf. She took careful aim, tension in her arms steady and controlled. If she let him inside, he might kill the horses or slip into the house.

She whispered a silent apology to the night air and released the arrow.

Her aim was true; the arrow struck his shoulder. The man staggered, hissing in pain. His hood slipped back, revealing a gaunt face etched with fear and anger. Instead of rushing in, he tried to flee. Linnea didn’t hesitate. With two quick strides, she leapt over the fence and took off after him.

The wounded intruder dashed into the field beyond the village, weaving among low hedgerows. Linnea pursued, guided by moonlight and the faint blood trail. She moved swiftly, tracking him as he stumbled over a narrow deer path. He was clever, but desperation made him clumsy. She caught up at a small clearing where he stopped, pressing a hand to his wound, breathing hard.

Linnea drew her bowstring again. “Stop!” she hissed, voice low and sharp. “Throw down your blade, or I’ll put the next arrow through your heart.”

The man’s eyes darted like a trapped animal’s. For a moment she thought he’d charge, but he faltered, knife slipping from his fingers. He raised trembling hands. The moonlight revealed more than fear—there was something strange on his forearm: a blackened brand, a twisted mark carved and burned into his flesh.

Linnea kept her bow trained on him, but called softly, “Gandalf! Over here!” Her voice carried just enough. Soon she saw a silver glow in the darkness—Gandalf’s staff-light, cutting through the gloom.

The wizard approached, face grim. He studied the trembling intruder, noting the brand that marred the man’s flesh. With each passing second, Gandalf’s frown deepened. “Where did you get that mark?” he asked quietly.

The intruder grimaced, shivering. “No master,” he spat, but his eyes darted away. The brand told a different story.

Gandalf took a measured step closer, staff light pulsing faintly. “You bear a sign of enslavement,” he said sternly. “Some darkness made you its servant. Who commands you?”

The man shook his head, sweat beading on his brow. “You... you don’t understand. If I talk, they’ll come. They’ll find you. It’s too late.” His voice was raw, hollow with fear.

Linnea’s heart clenched. This wasn’t just a desperate bandit; this was someone trapped in something cruel, something old and unkind. She softened her tone, though her bow remained aimed. “Look, I don’t want to kill you. But you must leave these Hobbits alone. Swear it, and we’ll let you go. If there’s still something human left in you, don’t harm them.”

He looked at her like a man offered a scrap of hope in a wasteland. “If I stay, worse will come,” he muttered. “Fine. I’ll go. I swear. I’ll leave this place and do no harm here.”

Gandalf gave a small nod, staff glowing brighter for a moment, as if sealing the bargain. “Then go,” he said. “But know this: if we meet again and you bring darkness with you, mercy ends here.”

The intruder wasted no time. He fled into the night, stumbling and panting, vanishing among the distant trees.

Linnea exhaled, lowering her bow. She spotted something on the ground where he’d dropped his blade: a small wooden charm, carved into the shape of a twisted hand with vines clawing around it. Its surface was darkened by ash, and when she picked it up, a faint, unsettling pulse thrummed beneath her fingertips. She shuddered, slipping it into her pouch as Gandalf stepped closer.

“This is worse than I feared,” he said quietly, peering at the pouch as if he could see through it. “A charm of enslavement and summoning. It binds the bearer to old powers, long dormant.”

Linnea suppressed a shiver. “So he really was forced here?”

“Likely,” Gandalf answered, drawing a warding sign in the air. “Or at least driven by something beyond ordinary greed.”

They headed back toward the Bramblefoot home, picking their way through the silent fields. At the door, Gandalf adopted a genial smile and a twinkle in his eye. “Marigold, forgive our brief absence. We thought we heard foxes prowling near the stable. False alarm!”

Marigold, busy ladling stew into wooden bowls, chuckled. “Goodness, you’re quite thorough guests! Come sit. The stew’s still hot, and I’ve fresh tea as well.”

Linnea managed a polite smile and joined the meal. She forced herself to relax her shoulders, listening to gentle Hobbit chatter about harvests and weather, acutely aware of the charm hidden in her pouch. This quiet home felt a world away from curses and brands. She dipped bread into her stew, savoring the rich warmth on her tongue, and tried to push aside the chill creeping into her thoughts.

Gandalf talked easily with the Hobbits, guiding the conversation to local gossip and pipe-weed harvests, never hinting at the danger just beyond their door. Linnea ate quietly, answering occasional questions about her travels. She made her speech simpler now—no need for formality. “Rode from Bree not long ago. Roads haven’t been too rough,” she said when asked about her journey, though her voice was softer than her words, betraying the tension inside.

She hadn’t lived in Bree for a while now, having moved on to build a life in the Angles with Celenneth. Still, Bree held its share of friendly faces and safe havens.

By late afternoon, she reached Bree’s outskirts. The bustling town rose from the gentle landscape, a cluster of timber and stone buildings encircled by hedges and low walls. People came and went on the roads—merchants, farmers, travelers. Linnea passed under the watchful eye of the gatekeeper, who barely glanced at her as she rode in. She didn’t look threatening—just another tired traveler returning from distant places.

There was someone in Bree she meant to see. Orlen and Maren—an old connection. Linnea remembered Maren from childhood: a kind woman who had known her parents at least in passing. Orlen was someone Celenneth had once admired, maybe even loved at a distance. They’d never acted on those feelings, as far as Linnea knew, but that old bond lingered in stories and letters. She’d heard bits and pieces over the years, enough to know that Orlen had married Maren. Celenneth had once written him, and Orlen had replied, but as with many things in Celenneth’s past, nothing more had come of it.

Linnea approached their house, a modest home on a quiet street near Bree’s edge. She tied Eorlin at a post outside and knocked softly. After a pause, Orlen opened the door. He looked older than she imagined, lines at the corners of his eyes and a seriousness that made her think of old regrets. He frowned, clearly not recognizing her.

“Can I help you?” he asked, polite but uncertain.

“I’m Linnea,” she said quietly, meeting his eyes. “Celenneth’s partner.”

For a moment, Orlen stood still, as if struggling to place the name. Then confusion and surprise flickered across his face. Hearing Celenneth’s name must have stirred old memories. He stepped back, wordlessly inviting her inside. The house was warm, the scent of stew and baking bread lingering in the air. Maren emerged from a back room, her expression puzzled until Linnea introduced herself again.

“Linnea... I remember you as a child,” Maren said softly, her tone friendlier than Orlen’s cautious silence. “It’s been years.”

They settled in a small sitting room. Linnea saw the way Orlen’s eyes drifted now and then, as if searching her face for signs of Celenneth. She knew he’d once cared for her partner deeply, though nothing had come of it. Linnea didn’t intend to linger on that past, but she also knew honesty was best.

“She’s well, as far as I know,” Linnea said gently. “We’ve built a life together, though it’s been full of traveling. I know she wrote to you once, Orlen.”

Orlen nodded, clearing his throat. “I wrote back after her letter. I... never heard anything. I wondered if she got it, or if...” He trailed off, eyes dropping to his hands. “I’m glad she found someone who sees her worth.”

Linnea offered a quiet smile. “She did. We have a daughter, Brynja, and... we’re a family. It took Celenneth time to settle, to trust in what we had. But she’s strong, and I think she’s happier now. She never forgot the people who mattered. She might not say it often, but the past is still with her.”

Orlen looked a bit sad, but not bitter. Maybe just wistful. “Well,” he said, voice low, “I’m glad she’s happy. That’s all I ever wanted.”

Maren reached for his hand. Linnea gave them both a respectful nod, understanding this was a moment of quiet acceptance for Orlen. After a bit more talk—mostly simple news of Bree and mutual acquaintances—Orlen retired early. Maren led Linnea to a small guest room, invited her to stay the night, and said they could talk more after he rested.

Later, by the hearth, Maren seemed eager to understand more about Linnea and Celenneth’s life together. Her questions started polite but curious: where had they traveled, how had they met Brynja? Then Maren’s tone grew a bit uncertain, and she lowered her voice.

“I hope you don’t think me rude,” she said, fiddling with a corner of her apron, “but I never expected Celenneth to find a woman to share her life with.” She looked genuinely perplexed. “I mean, how does that work? I... don’t mean to offend, I’ve just never known anyone like that.”

Linnea smiled gently, understanding Maren’s ignorance came from lack of experience, not malice. “It’s all right,” she said. “It’s not so different from any other love. We care for each other, look out for each other. We share our lives, our burdens, and our joys.”

Maren leaned forward, voice low. “But physically... how do you... well, satisfy each other?” She blushed at the question, clearly feeling awkward. “I’m sorry, I just don’t understand.”

Linnea kept her tone calm and friendly. “There are many ways to be close,” she said simply. “Love and intimacy aren’t limited to one idea. It’s about understanding each other’s bodies, listening, being patient and gentle. We find ways to make each other happy, just as any couple would, man and woman or otherwise. The details may differ, but the point is that we share love, trust, and pleasure. We’ve learned what works for us, and that’s what matters.”

Maren absorbed this, nodding slowly. “I see. I’d never thought of it like that. Thank you for explaining. I suppose love is love, no matter who it’s between.”

Linnea’s eyes softened. “Exactly.”

They spoke of lighter things then—Maren’s garden, the changing seasons, bits of Bree gossip. As the hour grew late, Linnea felt her eyelids droop and finally excused herself. The guest bed felt heavenly after so many nights on the road. She slept deeply, dreaming of Celenneth and Brynja, of holding them close and seeing their smiles.

Morning came bright and soft. Orlen had left early—Maren said he had business in town—but he’d told her Linnea was welcome anytime. Linnea sensed he needed space to process all he’d learned. She left a quiet note of thanks with Maren and stepped out into the sunlight, feeling rested and more at peace.

She mounted Eorlin and set out again. The Angles weren’t far now, a day’s ride at most. The rolling landscape turned more familiar with each mile. Memories of her and Celenneth building their home together warmed her thoughts. Brynja would be playing by the stream, maybe chasing dragonflies. Celenneth might be busy with some chore, or sitting quietly and waiting for Linnea’s return. The image brought a small smile to her lips.

The roads were quiet, and the weather was kind. She passed a few travelers, nodded greetings, but didn’t stop to chat. She had a final stretch of journey, and nothing would deter her now. With each hoofbeat, the tension of the past days melted away. The men in the Marish, the stolen artifact, the worries about what it all meant—those things could wait. She would deal with them in time. For now, reaching home was the only goal that mattered.

By late afternoon, she spotted familiar signs: a tall birch tree with a bent trunk, a rocky outcrop that formed a natural landmark. And then, over a gentle rise, she saw the low building they called home. Smoke curled lazily from the chimney. She urged Eorlin into a canter, heart beating faster. The sound of the horse’s approach likely carried, and as she drew closer, she thought she glimpsed a small figure racing out to see who was coming.

Brynja. The little girl’s laughter reached Linnea’s ears before she saw her face clearly. A moment later, Celenneth appeared in the doorway, hand lifted to shield her eyes from the sun. Linnea swung off the saddle and Brynja barreled into her legs, giggling. Linnea lifted her daughter up, holding her close, inhaling the sweet scent of sunshine and grass in the child’s hair.

Celenneth approached, a slow smile forming. She looked relieved, maybe even a little concerned. Linnea grinned back, heart full.

“I’m home,” Linnea said softly. “Miss me?”

Celenneth nodded, eyes bright. “Always,” she murmured, touching Linnea’s arm. “Long trip?”

Linnea sighed, still holding Brynja snug. “Long and strange. There’s a lot to tell you. But I’m here now.”

Celenneth didn’t press. She knew Linnea would talk when ready. Instead, she guided her inside, Brynja chattering about butterflies and sparrows and all sorts of small adventures. The warmth of their home embraced Linnea as she stepped through the door. It smelled like cooking herbs and fresh linen. Everything was as it should be.

She would tell Celenneth about the men in the Marish, about the Wyrmscale Sigil hidden in her pack, and about meeting Orlen and Maren in Bree. For now, she let herself enjoy the simple, priceless comfort of home, grateful for every step that led her back to where she truly belonged.
