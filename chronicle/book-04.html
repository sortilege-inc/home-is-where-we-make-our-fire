1Allies & Lovers

Narquelië 11, 2965

The map lay open between them on the council table, its marked trails and staging-points a grim account of everything gathering in the south. Bain studied it with his brow furrowed, and Celenneth waited until he was ready to hear her before she spoke.

"We need allies," she said. "And the nearest with strength enough to matter are the Woodland Elves. Thalion's people sit closest to Dol Guldur of anyone. If we can bring them in, it changes the whole reckoning."

Bain leaned back, skeptical. "Thalion is not an easy man to move. He's proud, and his people have small love for the affairs of Men. Even with the Shadow at the door, I'd not wager on him seeing reason."

"He might," Linnea said, quiet but sure, "if it's put to him the right way. The Elves have fought the Shadow longer than any of us have been alive. Show them it won't stop at their borders — that Mirkwood is the road it means to travel — and standing aside stops looking like safety."

Bain turned that over. "Thalion guards his people above all else. He may decide closed borders serve them better than a war that isn't yet theirs." He tapped the table. "And if Dale falls, the Woodland Realm is next — I'll grant you that. But even should he agree, what would he give? His archers are deadly, yes. Will he spend them on someone else's fight?"

"Perhaps he needn't spend them all at once," Linnea said. "Scouts, to start. Word of the orcs' movements. His people know these forests as no one else does — that alone could be the edge we lack. And we should carry something to offer in return. Thalion respects strength, and he respects a fair exchange. Aid for his borders, supplies — enough to show we come as partners, not beggars."

Celenneth nodded. "We don't demand. We appeal to what he already knows: that the dark doesn't keep to the lines drawn on a map."

Bain rubbed his temples, and at last let out a long breath. "You make the case well. Thalion is proud, but he's not a fool, and Dol Guldur is a name that frightens wiser men than me." He looked up, resolved. "Go to the Woodland Realm. Speak with him. But tread carefully — if you sour this, you'll leave us weaker than you found us."

Celenneth inclined her head. "I understand."

There was a bittersweet weight to packing up the room they had been given, folding away the small comforts of a roof and a real bed. Celenneth was double-checking that nothing had been left behind when Linnea caught her by the waist and spun her, and walked her back until her shoulders met the door.

"We don't know when we'll next have a bed like this," Linnea murmured, her mouth close enough that Celenneth felt the words more than heard them. She caught Celenneth's earlobe lightly between her teeth and tugged, then set her lips to the side of her throat.

The answer moved through Celenneth before thought did. She took Linnea by the hips, lifted her clear off the floor, and carried her the few steps to the bed to drop her on her back. "You asked for it."

She shed her clothes without ceremony and climbed over her, and let her hair fall around Linnea's face, and kissed her slow and deep until Linnea's mouth opened under hers and her arms came up to pull her down flush. Linnea arched to meet her, and Celenneth pulled back just enough to speak.

"You know," she said, and there was mischief in it, "the Woodland Elves are famous for more than their arrows. There's a thing they're said to do — with the mouth." She sat up and worked the fastenings of Linnea's dress, spreading it open to the cool air. Then she bent to kiss her once, firm, before setting a slow line down — the hollow of her throat, the rise of each breast, the soft of her belly — until she had settled between Linnea's parted thighs. "May I?"

Linnea nodded, nervous and wanting at once. Celenneth touched her first with a single finger, gentle, parting her, and then followed it with the flat of her tongue, unhurried, learning the whole length of her before she narrowed to the place that made Linnea's breath catch. Linnea shuddered. Celenneth glanced up, and smiled to see what she'd done, and gave her more — a soft, closing pressure of her lips, the tip of her tongue, patient at the swollen peak of her while Linnea grew wet and open under her mouth. The last of the tension went out of Linnea's body and left only this; her hips began to rock, and her hands found Celenneth's hair and held on.

When it broke over her she cried out and shook with it, and Celenneth stayed with her, easing, until the waves let her go. Then she stretched back up along her, and Linnea caught her face and kissed her hard, tasting herself on Celenneth's mouth, and laughed, breathless.

"That was — " she started, and gave up on the word. "You have to let me do that to you. Just — not this instant. I need to remember how to move."

Celenneth laughed, low, and pressed a kiss to her damp temple. "We've time. I'll get Nimroch ready, and then I'll come back for you, lover."

2Guidance

Narquelië 12, 2965

They rode north under a clean sky, the Forest River glinting away on their right, and for a morning the going was gentle — Nimroch easy beneath them, the wind moving in the grass, and away ahead the dark wall of Mirkwood growing steadily nearer against it. Celenneth let herself feel the small mercy of open country while it lasted. It would not last long.

At a quiet bend where the water slowed, they came on a figure standing at the river's edge — cloaked, his face lost in the hood, a pack set down at his feet. He raised a hand in greeting and made no move toward them.

Celenneth signed Linnea to hold back and swung down, one hand resting easy near her sword. "What brings you here?" she called, level.

The stranger pushed his hood back a little — an older man, weathered, with sharp and careful eyes. "A wanderer, no more," he said. "The road is long, and the river's decent company. And you? Bound into the wood, by the look of you."

Linnea stayed mounted, her fingers near her bowstring. "What's it to you?"

He chuckled, his gaze moving between them. "Nothing at all. Only a word of advice: the wood is unkind to those who go in unready."

"And what would you know of Mirkwood?" Celenneth asked.

"A fair bit." He leaned on his stick. "Enough to know its paths, and which shadows to keep clear of. I'd share it — for a price."

"I carry little coin," Celenneth said plainly. "But if the offer's honest, we might trade in something else."

He tilted his head, interested. "Something useful for the road would suit me. A well-forged blade. A vial of Elvish physic. Or a tale well told."

She glanced back at Linnea, who lifted one shoulder. We've supplies, the look said, and you have your songs. Celenneth turned back. "I'll give you a thing more lasting than coin," she said. "A song. A tale to keep you company on your own long road."

Something shifted in his face — real interest now. "A song, is it? I know the old ones, all of them. You'll have to give me something I've not heard."

She did not often sing where strangers could hear. But she felt the weight of the moment, and after a pause she nodded. "Then I'll give you one worth the road."

She began low, the melody carrying grandeur and grief braided together. It was a song of Lif — the dwarf who had stood against Malech, the one-eyed orc that had made a slaughterhouse of the deep halls — of his blade bright in the dark and his spirit that would not bend even when the end was on him. Then the tune darkened, and she sang the truth of it: that courage had not been enough, that the spear had found his side all the same, and of the stillness that came after. Her voice caught once, and she carried on through it, and let the song hold the whole cost of the thing.1

When the last note faded, Linnea was staring at her. "I've never heard that one," she said softly. "Malech, and Lif — did the dwarves teach it to you?"

Celenneth met her eyes, quieter now. "I made it. In Lif's memory — a stone to mark him, and a warning."

"It's beautiful," Linnea whispered. "And so sad."

The wanderer let out a long breath, his sharp features gone soft. "You were there," he said — not quite a question. "You saw it, with your own eyes."

"I fought beside him," Celenneth said, her gaze gone far off. "His courage earned remembering."

The man shook his head slowly, admiration plain on him. "A tale as deep as the halls it happened in," he murmured. "You've honored me, Ranger. I'll answer in kind, with good counsel." He gestured toward the dark line of the wood. "Take the left fork, and you'll keep clear of the worst of the bogs. If ever the trees creak louder than the wind can account for, put the river between you and them. And beware anything that moves in the shadows without a reason to." With a last nod he turned and went off down the trail, and was gone.

They remounted, and Linnea rode a while in thought before she spoke. "You've seen so much," she said. "Not just places — the stories in them. Do you ever wonder how much of what we know will just be lost? The way Moria was lost."

Celenneth considered it. "That's why we keep the stories," she said. "Even the sad ones. Forget them, and you lose the lesson, and the place, and the people who were there. Someone has to carry them out."

Linnea nodded slowly, and they turned toward the eaves of the wood, the lament of the dwarf-halls still moving quietly in her, and in the darkening trees ahead.

3Cold Nights

Narquelië 13, 2965

The wood took them in and closed behind them. They had left the main path long since, trading the known road for an eastward line along the Forest River, and the river ran fast and fretful at their side, hurrying past as though it were fleeing something upstream and thought them fools for walking toward it.

Celenneth led at her steady pace, but her eyes never rested — every shift of the branches read like the flick of an ear, some watcher turning to mark them. There was no birdsong here, no daytime sound at all, only the water and the vast indifferent patience of old trees. The air itself sat heavy, as if steeped in the fear of everyone who had come this way before and not come back.

Linnea kept close behind, her fingers worrying the hilt of her dagger. "It's like the wood's angry," she muttered. "Like it knows we've no business here."

"Maybe we don't." Celenneth glanced to where the river bent out of sight into deeper shadow. "But here we are. If there's something ahead, I'd sooner meet it looking at its face."

Linnea gave a thin laugh. "You always did run toward what everyone sane runs from."

"Someone has to." A flicker of a smile, gone as fast.

They had both walked Mirkwood's edges before, and both knew this was not that. This was older and more awake. The dark here was not the absence of light but a thing in its own right, a weight that leaned on the chest and thickened with every step deeper. Where the trees drew in ahead and the river narrowed, the way forward looked less like a path than a throat.

"I feel it too," Celenneth said, before Linnea had to ask. "Stay near. If we have to cut our way through something, we do it together."

"Together," Linnea echoed, and the word steadied her.

Night came down hard and cold, until every breath hung white between them. They made their camp beside the river, and the simple comfort of a fire proved beyond them — for when Celenneth first knelt to lay one, the trees answered. Their branches groaned and shifted, tightening their ring overhead with a slow, deliberate menace, and the meaning of it needed no words. No flame. So there was no fire, and there was cold instead.

Linnea pressed close under the shared cloak, and Celenneth wrapped an arm around her and pulled her in, and Linnea tucked her face into the warm crook of her neck and took what heat there was to take.

"This is miserable," she said, muffled against Celenneth's skin. "We could freeze out here."

"If we freeze, we'll do it in each other's arms." The humor came out flatter than Celenneth meant, but she drew the cloak tighter over them both. Around them the wood held its cold silence, and the river ran on whispering its warnings to the dark, and neither of them slept much.

Narquelië 14, 2965

Dawn came grudging and grey and gave no warmth. They untangled stiff and slow, breath fogging, dark circles under both their eyes from a night that had granted little rest.

"We need a fire," Linnea said, her voice cracking. "We can't do that again."

Celenneth sat up, scanning the close-standing trunks. Their bark caught the weak light like wrinkled, scornful faces. "The trees don't like it," she murmured. "Last night, when I tried — I'd swear they moved."

"Maybe they did." Linnea remembered the groan of the branches, the windless stirring of the canopy, the pressure of it. That had frightened her worse than the cold.

But another night unwarmed would leave them weak, and weak was worse than watched. Celenneth weighed it with her jaw set, then reached for the flint. "A small one. Kept low. Just enough to get us through."

The spark caught. As the little flame took, they both held their breath — and the wood seemed to hold its own. The branches creaked and the shadows swayed inward, ringing the fire with their disapproval, but no limb came down to smother it. The flames steadied, and warmth reached their frozen fingers, and for a while it was enough.

Linnea sat close to the heat with her back to the river, eyes half-shut, letting the numbness leave her hands. Then her head came up. She'd heard something under the crackle of the fire — faint, almost the wind, and not the wind. A thread of melody, impossibly far off, a tune hummed by someone who had no place being out here in the dark.

"Do you hear that?" she whispered.

Celenneth frowned. "Hear what?"

"Music. I swear it — music." Linnea turned her head, straining after it. The tune wavered, faded, and then, close behind her, came a soft breathy giggle.

She spun with the dagger already in her hand — and there was nothing there. Only the leaves and the gurgle of the water, and the air gone thick and expectant, as though it were waiting for her to lose her footing.

"There was someone," she said, pale. "Laughing. Right at my shoulder. I swear it."

Celenneth's face went flinty, her hand to her sword-hilt, her eyes on the black between the trunks. This she could not parry or outrun. She'd take orcs over it gladly — orcs she could kill. This was a thing that meant to work its way into the cracks of them and prise them open from inside. "Keep the dagger close," she said, harder than she intended. "If anything comes, we don't wait to see what it wants."

Linnea nodded and leaned into her side, and their shared warmth carried an edge of fear in it now. The fire burned small against the dark, and somewhere far off the melody went on, patient, and Celenneth did not tell her that she too had begun to catch it — the faintest curl of a song at the very edge of hearing, winding, coiling closer.1

The fire had sunk to embers by dawn, and with the grey light the pressure of the wood drew back a little, the music and the laughter gone as though they had been a bad dream.

Celenneth woke first. Linnea lay curled against her, her face half-buried in the crook of her arm, her breathing deep and slow with a rest that had been rare in these woods. For a moment Celenneth only watched her — the tension she carried by day smoothed away, dark hair loose across her cheek, her lips parted on a soft breath. Beyond their small camp the wood was still hostile and cold; here under the cloak, warmed by each other, the world could be held off a little longer.

She leaned in without deciding to and pressed her lips to Linnea's brow. Linnea stirred, murmured, her eyes drifting open — and before she could speak, Celenneth kissed her mouth, slow and deep, her hand slipping behind her neck to draw her in.

Linnea sighed into it and woke fully under the kiss, her hand finding Celenneth's waist and pulling. There was a heat between them that owed nothing to the dead fire — the pull that had been building unspoken through every shared danger, every glance held a beat too long — and Linnea kissed her back fiercely, as if to pour all her fear into it and be rid of it.

When they parted, their breath mingling in the cold, Celenneth rested her forehead to Linnea's. "We need to move," she said, and her voice came reluctant and thick.

"I know." Linnea's fingers lingered at her waist, unwilling. "I know."

Celenneth drew back slowly, her thumb brushing Linnea's cheek as though to keep the moment before the wood pressed in again. Then she rose and went to see to Nimroch, who stamped and flicked his ears, wary of the trees, and steadied under her sure hands. Linnea watched her work — the calm efficiency of it a fixed point in a place with no others — and pulled the cloak around her shoulders, the cold biting at her again, and a warmth still lit beneath her ribs where the kiss had been. The forest watched on. But for now there was warmth, and there was each other, and whatever waited they would meet it together.

4The Clearing

Narquelië 15, 2965

The wood grew meaner as the day wore on. By afternoon the path had strangled to nothing — a wall of thorn and root and ancient wood that would not let them through. Celenneth cursed under her breath, and behind her Linnea groaned as her foot fouled yet again on a buried root.

With nowhere left to walk, they took to the river. The shallows were bitter cold, numbing their feet as they waded upstream against a current that shoved at their legs as though it, too, wanted them gone. It was a long, grinding fight — soaked to the thigh, clothes heavy with river-water, the chill working into the bone — and by nightfall they hauled out wet, shivering, and sore.

Celenneth set to gathering what dry wood she could find while Linnea led Nimroch to a patch of grass and loosened his saddle with trembling fingers, murmuring to him low. When the fire finally caught, its warmth was almost painful against skin gone numb.

"We should dry off," Celenneth said, and glanced toward the water with a tired smile. "And we could stand a proper wash, after that."

Linnea rolled her eyes but her cheeks colored. They peeled off the sodden layers and hung them over a line strung near the flames, and stepped back down the bank into the shallows — cold enough to make them gasp, but it carried the grime and the ache of the day off with it as they waded to the waist. Celenneth ducked under and came up sleeking her wet hair back, and caught Linnea watching her, and smiled.

"Come here."

Linnea came, resting her hands on Celenneth's shoulders, and Celenneth turned her gently and worked her fingers through the tangles of Linnea's wet hair, patient, loosening the knots. Linnea's eyes fell shut, her body easing back into the touch.

"Did you ever hear the stories?" Linnea asked, half a dream in her voice. "About the Woodland Elves — that they'd charm travelers who wandered too deep, make them dance until they forgot their own names?"

Celenneth's fingers slowed at her scalp. "I heard them. My mother used to say they'd make you dance until your feet bled — until you couldn't tell your name from the sound of the wind."

Linnea shivered, and it wasn't the cold. "Do you think it's true?"

Celenneth traced the line of her collarbone, her breath warm at the back of Linnea's neck. "There's truth in every story," she said. "These woods are older than the kingdoms of Men. Who knows what still walks in them, or what it wants."

Linnea turned in her arms, searching her face, and reached up to touch her cheek. "Then let's give it no reason to keep us," she said, a small smile curving her mouth.

Celenneth leaned in until their brows touched, and gathered her close, and the cold of the river went out of the world. "We keep each other," she murmured against Linnea's ear — a promise made low, against the dark that waited past the firelight.

It was the fire that gave them true comfort in the end. They climbed the bank with their fingers laced and spread the dry cloak on the ground beside the flames, and knelt there facing each other, the light gilding the lean lines of them, the cold biting at their damp skin and the warmth of each other more than answer to it.

Linnea reached out and traced the planes of Celenneth's face, and leaned in, and the kiss began soft — a whisper of warmth that deepened as their mouths learned each other again. Celenneth's hand rose to cradle the back of Linnea's neck, fingers threading her damp hair. There was no urgency in it tonight, only the slow thrum of it, the crackle of the fire, the river running past. Linnea's fingers drifted down the curve of a shoulder, nails light on skin, and drew back to look at her, half-smiling.

"Your hair's a mess," she murmured against Celenneth's jaw.

"Your fault." Celenneth's voice had gone to a rasp. She found the shell of Linnea's ear, grazed the lobe with her teeth, and Linnea laughed, soft, and tightened her fingers in Celenneth's hair.

They moved slow. Their hands went to familiar places — old scars read by touch, the steady pulse beneath the skin, the salt the river had left behind. Linnea dragged her nails light down Celenneth's spine and pressed her mouth to her throat; Celenneth sighed and drew her in, and their bodies pressed close and warm along the whole length of them, and they let the night take them into a slow, unhurried tangle by the fire, in no hurry to arrive anywhere but here.

When the cold began to creep back, they drew apart at last, the heat of it still hanging between them. Celenneth pulled the cloak around them both and gathered Linnea into her, chin resting atop her head.

"Sleep," she said, barely a breath. Linnea curled into her, fitting where she always fit, and the fire burned steady beside them and the wood held its peace, and they slept.

Morning came reluctant, grey light straining through the tangled canopy. They woke wound together, the fire gone to embers, and dressed slow in their dried clothes and forded the cold river to the far bank, Nimroch picking his way beside them over the slick stones. Then upstream again, the path a shifting maze of shadow and thin light.

It wasn't long before Linnea heard it — the melody, drifting on the air. She stopped, head tilting. The tune was soft and coaxing, the kind that curled into the thoughts and made a home there, and under it ran voices, whispering in no tongue they knew, their cadence pulling at the edge of the senses.

"You hear it," Linnea said. Not a question.

Celenneth nodded once, her hand going to her sword, her eyes cutting through the trees. The music came and went, never staying long enough to fix a direction on, and there was something mocking in the way it slipped away each time.

Then the air began to warm. It rose from the ground itself, unnatural, thick and close, and clung to them until sweat stood on their brows — a humid, breathing heat that made every step feel like wading. "It's wrong," Linnea muttered, tightening her grip on Nimroch's rein.

"It is," Celenneth said, and felt the wrongness press against her chest. The music and the whispers went on, always just out of reach, fading and returning like a half-remembered dream that would not let go.1

They walked into the strange heat all the long afternoon, and toward dusk the trees gave way at last. The forest opened onto a wide grassy clearing where the river ran lazy through it and the sky stood clear overhead — deep indigo, the first stars already pricking through. Celenneth stopped, and looked up, and her breath caught at the sweep of them. She turned to Linnea and gestured to the open ground.

"Here," she said softly. "We rest here tonight."

5The Stars Their Witness

Narquelië 16, 2965

Linnea tipped her head back and the breath went out of her at the sight. The sky over the clearing was endless, the stars so thick and near they seemed a thing she might reach up and gather. "It's perfect," she said.

They made camp with an ease neither had felt in days. The clearing was open and it felt safe, the weight of the trees left behind them, and though the air was still warmer than a night this deep in autumn had any right to be, under the naked sky it troubled her less. Celenneth coaxed a fire to life; Linnea led Nimroch to the river's edge to drink, her ears turned toward the treeline out of habit — but here the music thinned, lost under the run of the water and the wind in the grass.

The fire took, and Celenneth looked up and found Linnea's eyes across it, and they traded a smile that needed nothing said. For tonight they had the stars, and each other, and the forest could wait.

Sleep took them under the shared cloak, and the warmth grew as the night went deeper — no longer the fire's comfort but something heavier, close, cloying. Celenneth stirred once and shoved the cloak off, her shirt damp and clinging. Beside her Linnea turned and turned, restless, and half-asleep her fingers went to the ties of her tunic and pushed the layers away, her skin sheened with sweat. And under it all, threading the dark, the music came again.

Linnea's eyes drifted open, a haze over them. The heat had left her limbs loose and heavy and strange, and the tune tugged at something under her ribs, a summons she felt more than heard — move, sway, come. She rose, slow, dreamlike, bare feet finding the cool grass, and she began to dance. Her arms lifted, her head fell back, her eyes half-closed, and she gave herself to the melody, turning in the firelight with her shadow thrown long behind her.

Celenneth woke to the sight of her — Linnea silhouetted against the stars, swaying, her dark hair catching the light, her body moving as though it weighed nothing at all. For a heartbeat it stopped Celenneth's breath, the beauty of it. Then the wrongness of it landed.

"Linnea?" Her voice came rough with sleep.

Linnea didn't answer. Her eyes were glassed, her lips parted, a soft breathless laugh spilling out of her as she spun, further from the fire, drawn toward the dark treeline a step at a time.

Celenneth was up and across the grass before she'd finished thinking. She caught Linnea by the shoulders, turned her, took her face in both hands. "Linnea. Look at me." She held her gaze and would not let it slide away. "You're dreaming. Come back."

For a moment longer the melody had her — and then it broke, and Linnea blinked, and came to herself with a stumble and a small, embarrassed sound. She looked around at the clearing, at Celenneth's frightened face, her breath coming quick, her skin damp. "I don't — " she started. "I was — "

"I have you." Celenneth drew her in against her, felt the racing of her heart. "It's the wood. It went looking for the loosest thread in you and pulled. You're back now."1

But the heat did not let go of them, and the music did not stop, and where fear had been a moment before there was now something else rising to meet it. The air lay thick and feverish on the skin, and the drums began — low in the earth, felt through the soles before they were heard, slow and building, a pulse that found the pulse in Celenneth's own chest and matched it.

Linnea's hand closed on her wrist. "Celenneth — I — " The words wouldn't come whole. The need in her was plain, and it was answered; Celenneth felt the same fever climbing in herself, and knew it for what it was — the wood's glamour, yes, but laid over a want that had been theirs and true long before the wood ever touched it. She had spent the day wading through the heat of this place. She would not fight this. She would rather burn toward the one thing here that was real.

"I need you," she breathed, and pulled Linnea's mouth to hers, and the kiss was nothing like the slow one by the fire — it was teeth and open breath and hunger, both of them gasping into it. They came down together onto the crushed grass, and there was no patience left in either of them. Celenneth's fingers found Linnea slick and ready and Linnea's found her the same, the two of them working each other in the dark with the drums driving under it, hips rocking to a rhythm that was half theirs and half the wood's. Linnea's breath sobbed against Celenneth's throat; her nails dragged down Celenneth's back and Celenneth pushed into the sting of it, wanting more of everything at once.

"Don't stop," Linnea gasped.

"I'm not going anywhere." Celenneth turned her onto her back and followed her mouth down — throat, the peak of one breast drawn between her lips, the sweat-salt hollow of her navel — while Linnea's hands fisted in her hair and her hips lifted after her. She settled between Linnea's thighs and this time she did not tease; the fever wanted more than teasing. She took her with her whole mouth, tongue flat and firm where Linnea most needed it, two fingers curling up into her in time, and Linnea came apart almost at once, arching off the ground with a cry that the drums swallowed, and Celenneth stayed and drew it out of her in wave after wave until she was wrung empty and shaking.

The drums faded then, as though they had gotten what they came for. The oppressive heat lifted all at once, a cool breeze moving across their damp skin, and overhead the stars seemed to shimmer and drift, alive, though that might only have been Linnea's spent and swimming sight.

She turned her head and found Celenneth's face gilded in the last of the firelight. Celenneth leaned in and kissed her — slow now, tender, all the fever gone out of it and only the deep thing left. "I love you," she whispered, searching Linnea's eyes as though she needed to see it answered.

Linnea's breath caught. She reached up and traced Celenneth's cheek. "And I love you," she said, unsteady with it. "More than anything."

They kissed again, gentle, and lay tangled under the drifting stars while the music of the wood thinned away to a lullaby, and slept at last with the whole clearing for their witness.

6Discovery

Narquelië 17, 2965

By daylight the clearing was another country. The sun stood well up, pouring gold through the gaps in the canopy onto grass and wildflowers that stirred in a cool morning breeze; the oppressive heat of the night had burned off entirely, and the river ran bright and easy beside them. Birds sang overhead — a sound they had not heard since they entered the deep wood. Everything felt scrubbed clean, as though the land had granted them a reprieve after the fever of the dark.
