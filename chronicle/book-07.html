1Tending to One Another

Cermië 11, 2966

The house was quiet at last, the fire throwing its warm light across the walls. Freowyn had carried a worn-out Brynja to bed after an evening of tears and laughter, and left Celenneth and Linnea alone, the months apart sitting heavy in the silence between them.

Celenneth set aside her cloak and gear first, slow and deliberate, and when she unfastened the bindings of her tunic the raw marks on her wrists and the fading bruises on her arms came to light. Linnea's breath caught.

"Celenneth. What did they do to you?"

"Later," Celenneth murmured, not meeting her eyes, bending to her boots — and Linnea saw how stiffly the leg bent, favoring the crude splint.

"Your leg—"

"Broken. Fangorn wasn't kind."

Linnea's mouth thinned. She fetched a basin and a cloth. "Sit down." It brooked no argument, and Celenneth obeyed, sinking to the edge of the bed with a wince. Linnea knelt and unwound the splint, her hands steady though her heart was not, and began to wash the dirt and dried blood from her skin. Each bruise, each scar told its own hard story, and somewhere in the telling her composure cracked, and tears spilled over.

"It was so hard without you," she got out. "Every day I wondered if you were alive. Every day I had to decide whether to give Brynja hope her mother was coming home — and risk breaking her heart — or not."

"Linnea—"

"I tried to keep going. To be bright for her, to hold it all together. But inside I was coming apart. I thought I'd lost you for good."

Celenneth reached up and brushed her cheek. "I'm so sorry. I never meant to be gone so long."

"Then tell me what happened."

She let out a long breath. "I thought I could take a small detour on the way home — learn what I could along the road. But the Entwash flooded and cut me off. I looked for another way and walked into an orc patrol. They took me. Marched me toward Dol Guldur." She hesitated. "I escaped into Fangorn, and broke the leg there, in a fall."

Linnea's hands trembled at the marks on her wrists. "But the forest sheltered you?"

"It wasn't shelter. It was a test. Fangorn doesn't trust easily. I spent weeks only surviving — snares, foraging, mending. It let me go in the end. But not before I'd proved I wasn't what the Shadow had tried to make me."

"And the patrol?"

"They found me at the forest's edge. I didn't feel it was my place to demand they drop everything and carry me to Edoras. They had their own duties. I was trying not to be a burden."

Linnea nodded slowly, the tears softening to something gentler, and rested her brow against Celenneth's. "I thought you'd forgotten us. Forgotten me."

Celenneth cupped her face in both hands. "Never. You and Brynja were the only thing that kept me alive."

Linnea's voice dropped to a whisper. "When I saw you with Garin, I thought—" She couldn't finish it.

Celenneth's expression softened to understanding. "I was oblivious. I didn't see what she felt. And I'll be honest with you, because I always will be — had she asked me to share her bed, I don't know that I'd have thought to say no. And none of it would have felt like a betrayal to me. You hold my heart. You always will. That doesn't move."

Linnea searched her eyes, tears standing again. "I know you have a more... plain relationship with your body than I do. I even love that in you, though I don't know if I'll ever feel the same way about mine." Her voice trembled. "I just need to know I'll always matter to you."

Celenneth's grip gentled on her face. "You do. You always will. I swore it to you once. I'll swear it again as many times as you need."

Linnea's lips quirked, trembling. "You don't have to. Just stay. With me. With us."

Celenneth leaned in and pressed a kiss to her brow. "Of course, my love."

Linnea turned so her back settled against Celenneth's chest, and Celenneth wrapped her in her arms, and their breathing slowed into a single rhythm, and they slept still folded together.

2Anniversary

Narvinyë 12, 2967

The midwinter cold had begun to soften as the Yule feasting wound down in Edoras, and in the household of Celenneth and Linnea a quieter celebration took its place. It was a year to the day since they had found Brynja abandoned in the winter borderlands — the day that had made them a family — and they meant to mark it.

Freowyn bustled at the hearth, sleeves rolled, a stew of rosemary and roasted roots filling the house with its smell. "Enough for a feast," she declared. "Even if it's only us, it should feel like one."

Linnea stood stringing small dried flowers onto a length of twine. "In Bree we'd hang garlands like this for a child's first birthday," she said, a faint smile on her. "For blessings in the year ahead."

"And in the North," Celenneth added, "we'd leave a ring of bread and fruit on the doorstep — a gift to the spirits, for watching over the child." She set about arranging a small offering by the door, careful and reverent.

Brynja toddled between them, wide-eyed, her small hands grasping at the garland and the bread. "For — me?" she managed, on two of her still-few words, and the wonder in it made them both laugh.

"Every bit," Linnea said, scooping her up and kissing her cheek. "Because today's the day we found you, little star."

As the sun sank and gilded the room, a few friends of the household arrived. Eadwyn, the horsewoman Linnea trained under, brought a small carved horse for Brynja, its surface polished bright. Hild, a neighbor of Freowyn's, came with a basket of honey cakes.

"A child of Rohan must have a horse of her own," Eadwyn said with a grin, pressing the carving into Brynja's hands. "Even a little one, for now."

Brynja hugged it hard against her chest. "Horsey!"

Freowyn laughed and set the stew on the table. "A fine name. Now come — eat, before the cakes vanish."

The meal was a weave of their three homes: the hearty stew beside a flatbread from Celenneth's ranger days, and a sweet berry pudding Linnea had made in the way of Bree. The table was strewn with small tokens of their travels — a polished northern stone, a Rohan weave, a wooden trinket from Bree. After, they gathered by the fire for stories: Freowyn told of a brave mare who saved her rider in battle; Celenneth followed with a ranger's fable of stars guiding the lost toward home; and Linnea, her eyes lingering on Brynja, spoke a Bree tale of the strength of unlikely families.

When the telling was done, Linnea drew a small silver pendant from her pocket, a simple star worked into its face, and fastened it gently at Brynja's neck. "This is yours, Brynja. To remember that you're our light, even on the darkest nights."

Brynja's fingers found the pendant, her small face gone serious for a moment — then split into a wide grin. "Star," she said, pleased with herself.

"Our star," Celenneth agreed, pulling her into a warm embrace.

As the night deepened the fire settled low, and Brynja dozed in Linnea's lap with the pendant clutched in one fist. Freowyn hummed as she tidied, and Eadwyn leaned back with a rare softness in her eyes as she watched the family. Celenneth reached for Linnea's hand, her thumb brushing her knuckles. "We're lucky to have her," she murmured.

Linnea nodded, her gaze on the sleeping child. "And lucky to have each other."

For a while the wide dangerous world seemed very far off, and there was only the warmth of the house and the family they had made against every odd.

3Winter 2967

Nénimë 8, 2967

The days after Brynja's celebration passed in quiet contentment. Winter's grip began to ease, and Edoras turned its mind to the mending and readying that the turning seasons asked. For Celenneth and Linnea it was a time of taking root — of learning not only the ways of Rohan but the songs handed down through its generations.

It was Freowyn who first pressed it, humming over the hearth one evening as she kneaded dough. "If you're to live among us, you should learn our songs. They're more than music — they're our history, our stories, the heart of who we are."

Brynja took to it first, her small voice mimicking snatches of a Rohirrim lullaby. Linnea hung back, and later, helping Celenneth string bows, confided her hesitation. "I feel like I'm intruding. The songs of Rohan belong to its people. Who am I to take them for my own?"

Celenneth tilted her head. "And yet you sing the songs of Bree, far from Bree. You carry them because they're part of you. Why not let the songs of Rohan be part of what we're becoming?"

Linnea's hands stilled. "I hadn't thought of it that way."

"Then try. No one asks you to claim them. But singing them doesn't take from their meaning. It adds to it."

The next day Freowyn gathered a small circle in the hall — Celenneth and Linnea among them, and a few of the neighboring families. She began with the tale of Eorl the Young and the founding of Rohan, her voice ringing clear, weaving the words with such feeling that even Celenneth leaned forward, tapping the rhythm on her knee. "Now," Freowyn said as the last note faded, "your turn."

Linnea hesitated, then took a breath and gave a Bree ballad of a cunning fox that outwitted its hunters. Tentative at first, she found her feet as the room quieted in appreciation, and the Rohirrim laughed aloud at the clever ending, and Brynja clapped to hear her mother's voice rise above the hall. When Celenneth's turn came she hesitated for a different reason — the song in her chest was an old Northern one, of the Númenóreans risen to greatness and fallen to ruin, and it felt almost too sacred to spend. But Linnea's encouraging smile decided her. Her voice went low and steady, and the melody was a haunting thing, and for a moment the whole room fell silent under the weight of it, and when it ended even Freowyn seemed at a loss, her eyes bright.

"You've brought us something rare," Freowyn said after a moment. "But now you'll learn one of ours." They began with a simple children's tune of the swift horses of the Mark and the bond of rider and steed, and Brynja caught it faster than anyone, her voice high and clear, and Linnea's hesitance melted as the others drew her in, until the hall swelled with many voices woven together. As the days went on the lessons continued; Celenneth took to weaving her own Northern airs into the mix, her wariness giving way to a quiet pride, and one evening by the fire she caught herself humming a Rohirrim song under her breath.

Linnea glanced over, smiling. "It's growing on you."

Celenneth's eyes warmed. "Perhaps. But only because it's ours now, too."

Linnea stood at the crest of the hill, the thin winter sun throwing long shadows over the snow, while a few steps off Celenneth crouched lashing a sled together for Brynja. The child stomped about in boots a size too big, a small bundle of energy against the white.

"You can't just pull her down a hill and call it parenting," Linnea said, arms crossed against the cold.

Celenneth looked up, brow furrowing. "She asked for a sled ride. I'm giving her one. What's wrong with that?"

"What's wrong," Linnea said, stepping closer, low and heated, "is that there's more to raising her than playing the hero. A few games in the snow don't make up for how long you were gone."

The words struck deep. Celenneth straightened, the rope still in her hands, her jaw setting. "I'm doing what I can, Linnea. I came back. I'm here now."

"And still not fully here." Linnea gestured at Brynja, blithely unaware of them. "You think showing up means all's well? That she doesn't feel how little she knows you anymore?"

Celenneth set the rope down and stepped toward her, steady but frustrated. "I'm trying. Maybe not perfectly, maybe not the way you'd have it — but I'm trying. Would you rather I didn't?"

Linnea's face softened, though her shoulders stayed stiff. "I just need you to be careful. Not only with her — with us. You come back into our lives like a storm, and when you go, we're the ones left to gather up the pieces."

Celenneth's mouth opened to answer — but Brynja's voice cut across them. "Mama! Go now?"

Celenneth turned, and gave the child a smile that did not quite reach her eyes. "Almost, little one. A moment."

Linnea sighed, her breath clouding. "She loves you, Celenneth. So do I. But love doesn't excuse carelessness."

Celenneth bowed her head. "You're right. I'm sorry." She drew Linnea into her arms, and Linnea held her back — and then she turned to the child, and the shadow lifted from her voice. "Ready to go fast?"

4Spring 2967

Víressë 9, 2967

The request came on a crisp morning as Celenneth restrung her bow at the edge of the yard, the sun laying long shadows over the frosted grass, the sound of horses drifting from the stables. A rider approached, cloak snapping in the wind — Ealdric, one of the patrol captains, his face serious but not unkind.

"Celenneth. Your skill as a tracker is known to us. We're mustering a patrol for the northern borders, and your experience would be worth much. Will you ride with us?"

She glanced toward the house, where Linnea and Brynja were at the morning chores, and hesitated only a moment before she nodded. "If my skill can serve Rohan, I'll go. When?"

"First light tomorrow. The main gate. Garin leads the patrol." At the name, Celenneth's mouth pressed thoughtful, but she nodded again, and Ealdric rode off. Linnea came up wiping her hands on her apron.

"A patrol?"

"The northern borders. They need a tracker. I said yes."

Linnea studied her, then gave a small, resigned smile. "Of course you did. It's who you are." Her fingers brushed Celenneth's. "Just come back to us."

Celenneth's hand tightened over hers. "Always."

That evening Linnea slipped away to the stables, where she knew Garin would be readying for the road, and found her brushing down her bay mare.

"Linnea," Garin said, straightening, mildly surprised. "What brings you here?"

Linnea stepped closer. "I wanted a word. About the patrol. And about Celenneth."

Garin's brow furrowed. "What about her?"

Linnea met her eyes and said it plainly. "I know how you look at her. I'm not here to ask you to stop. I only want you to know — if Celenneth ends up sharing your bedroll on this ride, it's not a hurt to me. As long as she comes home to me, I'm at peace with it."

Garin's composure faltered for the first time. "You'd truly be all right with that?"

"What matters to me is that Celenneth and I are honest with each other, and that she knows where home is. She loves you as a friend, I don't doubt. If there's more to it, I don't see the harm — so long as our bond holds."

Garin was quiet a long moment, then nodded, a faint smile tugging at her. "You're remarkable, Linnea. Most wouldn't be so clear about it."

Linnea shrugged. "I know what we have. I've no cause to fear anyone, least of all you. Only promise you'll never try to come between us."

"I promise. I'll always stand behind what you two have. My word on it."

Satisfied, Linnea turned to go. "Safe travels, Garin."

The patrol left at dawn, hooves ringing over the frost. For two weeks Celenneth rode north with Garin and the others, her eyes on every horizon. Past the Snowmarch, near the shadow of the Greyfells, they cut orc-sign — a scouting party testing the border's strength, small but dangerous — and tracked it four days through the foothills before setting an ambush at Frostcrag Hollow and taking it unawares. It was over quickly, and no rider was lost. The work was hard and the land colder and harsher than Celenneth had remembered, and in the quiet hours of camp she and Garin found their way, at last, into each other's arms.1

When the patrol rode home the sun was setting the sky to gold and crimson. Celenneth swung down and met Garin's eyes, and they shared a brief, tender kiss, and parted with a warmth that held no possession in it. Then she quickened her steps toward the gate, where Linnea waited with Brynja on her hip, her face lighting with relief.

That night, with Brynja long asleep, Linnea drew Celenneth to their bed. "Tell me everything," she said, resting her head on Celenneth's shoulder. "Start with what the patrol found. And then how it was with Garin — I want all of it. And hold me while you tell it."

Celenneth exhaled, and slid into the cadence of a ranger's report. "We rode north past the Snowmarch, near the Greyfells. Found tracks — orc raiders, testing the border. We ran them four days through the foothills. They were quick, and clever. Not clever enough. We set the ambush at Frostcrag Hollow, and it was quick, and we lost no one." Her gaze drifted to the fire. "But it wore at me. The land's harder than I remembered up there. Colder."

"And Garin?" Linnea prompted, quiet, curious, and unafraid.

Celenneth's lips curved at the memory. "We grew close. I half-expected it — after what you told me before I left." A pause. "I didn't expect her to know you'd spoken to her. One night after a hard ride, she pulled me aside and told me."

Linnea smiled. "She needed to hear it, too."

Celenneth shook her head, still half in wonder at it. "I didn't know what to say. Only thank you." And she told it as it had been.

The campfire had burned low, its glow carving long shadows across the snow, her breath clouding as she worked a whetstone down her blade. Garin crouched beside her. "Cold night. Mind some company?" They sat in an easy quiet for a time, no words needed. Then Garin spoke, low and unsteady. "Linnea spoke to me. Before we left." Celenneth looked up. "What did she say?" — "That if something happened between us, she'd not mind. As long as I respected what you two have." Garin's voice trembled, her gaze searching. "I never thought she'd offer that." Neither had Celenneth — and yet it fit; Linnea's love had always been a steady, unshakable thing. She set the blade down and studied Garin's open face. "She meant it." Garin reached out, her fingers grazing Celenneth's hand. "And what do you want?" The cold seemed to fall away between them. "You," Celenneth said. "Tonight."

Garin's mouth found hers, tentative and then not, and the wind howling against the thin walls of the tent became nothing at all. Garin bore her back gently into the furs and braced above her, and their eyes held before she kissed her deep. Celenneth arched as Garin's calloused hand traced a burning line down her side, their thighs tangling, and gasped when Garin's mouth left hers to trail down her jaw to her throat. She fisted a hand in Garin's wind-tossed hair and guided her lower, her thighs parting willingly, and Garin's mouth found her and worked her with slow, deliberate circles until her hips rose helplessly to meet it.

But Celenneth would not simply take. Her fingers tightened in Garin's hair. "Your turn." She shifted with unexpected strength and rolled Garin onto her back, held her gaze a beat, and said, low, "Turn around." Garin obeyed, her frame rolling until they lay aligned and opposite, each open to the other's mouth. Celenneth set her hands to Garin's hips and lowered herself, and gave her what she was taking, the two of them moving in an unspoken rhythm — giving and taking in equal measure, tongue and lips and breath, every shudder answered. Celenneth brushed a gentle kiss across her mouth. "Neither did I. But I'm glad."

As the telling faltered, Linnea pressed her lips to the side of Celenneth's neck. "Thank you for telling me," she whispered. "I'm glad she was there, when you needed her."

Celenneth let out a shaken breath. "You're incredible."

Linnea's fingers traced her jaw and tilted her face round. "I know you. You need to be held sometimes — even when you've forgotten how. And you're home now." And she kissed her, fierce and unhurried at once. "Enough talking. My turn."

She shifted astride Celenneth's waist in one smooth motion, thighs pressing to her hips, her breath unsteady but her gaze steady and unyielding. 5Summer 2967

Cermië 12, 2967

The morning air was crisp with the smell of fresh hay and damp earth as Linnea cinched the saddle on a chestnut mare, her hands moving deftly now after months of it. Eadwyn leaned against the fence, her gold braid swinging, watching with a critical eye.

"You've improved," she said, and meant it. "Your hands are steady. The horse trusts you more."

Linnea glanced over her shoulder, half a smile on her. "You say that like it's a miracle."

"For someone who once held the reins as though they might bite? It is." Eadwyn pushed off the fence. "Now let's see you ride. Speed and balance today."

Linnea swung up and took a breath, her hands light. She had been training with Eadwyn since not long after Celenneth first left for Gondor, and the sessions had only grown more frequent — for with Celenneth mostly settled in Edoras now, Linnea was more set than ever on honing herself, on being someone who could stand at her partner's side in strength as well as spirit.

Eadwyn vaulted onto her own black gelding and gave a sharp whistle. "Ride!" They tore off across the open field, the wind snatching at them, and Linnea's heart raced as the mare's hooves pounded the earth and her body found the rhythm of it. "Lean into the turn! Heels down — good! Now faster!" She obeyed, and grew more fluid with every pass, feeling the change in herself — strength in her legs, steadiness in her hands, a confidence where the old fear had been. When they slowed to a trot at the far fence, Eadwyn nodded. "You ride like a proper Rohir now. Keep this up and you'll outpace me by next spring."

Linnea laughed. "Let's not get carried away. I've a long way yet."

The morning did not end at the saddle. They cooled the horses and moved to the training ground, where swords rang as they worked footwork and strikes. "Your footwork's better," Eadwyn said as Linnea turned a blow aside. "But you hesitate. Don't second-guess. Commit to the strike." Linnea set her stance and struck true, and Eadwyn stepped back, grinning. "That's it. No hesitation." And after, at the archery butts, Eadwyn watched her nock and draw. "Breathe. In through the nose, out through the mouth. Let the bow be part of you." Linnea loosed, and the arrow struck the target — off-center, but nearer than she'd have managed a month gone, and she let out a satisfied breath.1

As the sun climbed they sat at the edge of the ground, catching their breath, Linnea's legs aching pleasantly with the strangeness of it all. "You've come a long way," Eadwyn said, more serious now. "I don't say that lightly. There's a strength in you, Linnea — not only of swords and bows and horses. The kind that makes folk want to follow."

Linnea looked at her, surprised. "I'm not trying to lead anyone. I only want to be strong enough to protect the people I love."

Eadwyn's mouth curved. "That's exactly why they'd follow you. But for now — keep training. You're becoming something rare."

Linnea's smile grew, and she looked out to where the fields ran on without end, and thought of Celenneth and Brynja, and the family they were building, and the strength she would need to help keep it whole. "Thank you, Eadwyn. For everything."

Eadwyn shrugged, the grin returning. "Thank me when you're sparring circles around me."

6Autumn 2967

Narquelië 15, 2967

The kitchen was fragrant with dried herbs as Linnea crushed chamomile flowers in a small carved mortar, Freowyn across the table with her arms crossed and an eyebrow raised, the warm afternoon light streaming over the bundles of plants hung from the ceiling.

"This one," Linnea said, calm and sure, "is for easing pain and helping sleep. Chamomile works well in a tea or a poultice — but you can't mix it with anything too strong. Gentle, but it does its work."

Freowyn leaned in over the crushed petals. "And this grows common in Bree?"

"Very. My grandmother kept it just outside our door. She always said there wasn't a trouble that couldn't be eased with a good cup of chamomile."

Freowyn twirled a sprig of thyme between her fingers. "And this? I've only cooked with it."

"Good for cleaning a wound," Linnea replied. "A wash or a salve. Best for small cuts and scrapes — and it settles a sour stomach, brewed as a tea."

Freowyn's eyes lit. "I've seen enough scrapes in the stables to put that to use."

Their lessons became a part of the days' rhythm — Linnea guiding Freowyn through tinctures and salves and teas, sharing what she'd gleaned in Bree and on the road with Celenneth; and in turn Freowyn began to teach her the skills of a lifetime in Rohan. It began with the horses.

"A foal's no different from a soldier," Freowyn said one day in the stables, where a mare stood heavily in foal, her breathing slow and labored. "Both need care and steadiness from their first moment in the world. It isn't only what your hands do — it's being calm. They feel everything you feel." Linnea listened close as Freowyn showed her how to ready the birthing-place with clean hay and water, and when the foal came, Freowyn's steady hands and low voice guided her through it — checking the mare, clearing the foal's nose and mouth, seeing both safe. After, watching the foal find its first wobbling steps, Linnea let out a breath she hadn't known she was holding. "That was — incredible."

Freowyn grinned, wiping her hands. "You did well. A few more, and it'll come as easy as mixing one of your tinctures."

In time the lessons grew past horses. Freowyn began to teach her the rudiments of a midwife's craft for women too — the herbs to ease a hard labor, to hold off fever. "This isn't only about birth," she said one evening as they worked over a bundle of comfrey. "It's about life. About standing with someone in the hour they're most helpless, and helping them find their own strength." Linnea took in every word, her hands steady as she made up a poultice, and saw more and more how the two crafts were kin — the quiet care of the herb-garden and the hands-on mercy of the birthing-room, both asking patience, and intuition, and a steadfast will to stand by those in need.1

7Winter 2968

Nénimë 10, 2968

Through the long frigid nights, Celenneth and Linnea and Brynja would settle by the fire, wrapped in thick furs against the cold, the flames throwing their shifting light on the walls of the small home. These evenings became a kind of sanctuary, a time for closeness.

Celenneth often took the lead, her voice low and steady as she spun tales of the rangers and their endless watch — of her younger sister, and of far lands she had crossed in her youth. Her words carried the weight of years, and yet came easy in the warmth of the hearth, her gaze drifting to the fire as she spoke, somewhere between memory and the present. Linnea sat close, her head on Celenneth's shoulder, sensing the love and loss woven under the stories, the truths that never quite surfaced; now and then she asked a soft question or laughed low, and in her steady presence Celenneth found herself sharing pieces she rarely showed. Brynja nestled between them clutching a small wooden carving, wide-eyed at each tale's beginning — though more often than not the rhythm of Celenneth's voice and the warmth of them lulled her to sleep.

"Do you think the stars truly guide us?" Linnea asked one evening, as a tale of the Northern skies ended.

Celenneth's gaze lingered on the fire. "I think they remind us where we come from, and where we're going. Whether they guide us or not, they're always there. Watching over us."

Linnea smiled, her fingers brushing Brynja's hair. "Perhaps that's enough."

The nights fell into a ritual after that. Linnea told simple tales of Bree — cunning animals and busy markets that set Brynja giggling before she dozed — and Celenneth wove in her own of bravery and sorrow, steady even when the memories cut. One night Linnea told of her own losing: of leaving Bree behind, and the lively, mischievous cousins who had taught her the market songs and the secrets of the woods outside the village. Her voice softened as she owned how she missed them, the ache of it sharper each passing year. "Someday," she murmured, "I'd like to go back. It's been so long, and it hurts to think how much I've missed."

Celenneth listened with the same care Linnea had always shown her. "It is hard to be far from those you love," she said when Linnea finished, her hand finding hers. "And I've no doubt they'd delight to see you again."

Linnea's smile was small and warm. "Not something we need do at once. But before Brynja gets too big."

The fire settled, and Brynja shifted closer in her sleep, and Celenneth's arm wrapped around them both. Outside the world was cold and uncertain; within these walls there was something that would not be shaken — a family, and the strength to face what came.

A hush lay over the plains the morning the season's first snow softened every edge with white. Brynja all but danced at the town's edge, her mittened hand tugging Celenneth on, her laughter bright against the quiet.

"Mama — go see!" she cried, pointing at a little stand of trees ahead.

Celenneth smiled and snugged the cloak's collar around her daughter's rosy cheeks. "All right, little one. Mind your step — the ice is tricky underfoot." They went into the grove where the branches stood bare and frost-etched, and Brynja scampered ahead, boots crunching, and dropped to her knees over a frozen puddle, breath clouding its surface. "Look! Like glass!"

Celenneth crouched beside her and rested a gloved hand on the ice. "It is. But careful — ice is as slippery as it's pretty."

Brynja brushed the snow aside to peer deeper. "Fish inside? Like the story?"

"They're sleeping now," Celenneth said, tilting her head, "waiting for spring to free them. They hide under the ice — patient, and wise."

Brynja looked up, her whole trust in her face. "You know lots, Mama."

Celenneth drew her close and kissed her brow. "Not everything, my love. Just enough to keep you safe."

As the light faded they turned back for Edoras, Brynja bearing a crooked stick she had named her wand, and Celenneth felt her heart grow lighter with every snowy step, warmed by the small adventure more than any hearth could have managed.

8Spring 2968

Víressë 12, 2968

The training field hummed as Linnea pressed her thighs to her horse's flanks and urged it to a canter, the Rohirrim spurring forward all around her, hooves thunderous, a stout wooden target waiting ahead. She leaned low, the wind tugging her hair, her heart pounding in time with the drum of hooves, and loosed her arm — and the spear-tip met wood with a sharp splintering crack. She shouted in triumph and wheeled back toward the line, where Garin galloped up grinning.

"Not bad," Garin teased, "for someone who's spent more of her life on her feet than in a saddle."

Linnea laughed, flushed. "I'm getting the hang of it. Though I'll always love a sword's hilt better."

"Give it time," Garin said, real pride in her face. "You've a fighter's spirit. That's half the battle won." They rode together again, side by side, Linnea steadier with each pass, buoyed by the company of these people who met every challenge head-on. By day's end her arms ached and her legs shook and her lungs drew the crisp air deep — tired, sore, and wholly alive. She swung down and stroked her horse's neck, murmuring thanks, and looked out past the fields to where her thoughts always went: to Celenneth and Brynja, and the future of safety and strength she was building toward, one honed skill at a time.

That evening the hall was warm with good spirits, and Celenneth held the room. She stood by the long table, aglow, her voice carrying easily over the rumble of talk. "So there I was," she began, auburn hair catching the firelight as she spread her arms, "clinging to a sheer cliff, the wind shrieking in my ears — and one miserable little root between me and a very long fall." She pinched thumb and forefinger to show how scrawny it was, and the riders leaned in.

"You didn't fall?" a young one breathed.

"Not that time. But just as I lunged for the ledge, the root tore free. For a heartbeat I dangled there, thinking that was the end of me." She let the silence stretch until the fire's crackle was the loudest thing in the hall.

"Then what?" Garin demanded, and pulled everyone tighter into the lamplight.

Celenneth clapped her hands together. "Out of the storm a hawk stooped down, shrieking — and startled a poor goat grazing above me. The creature bolted, kicking loose a scatter of stones that shifted my saddlebags and gave my rope just enough of a pull — and I hauled myself up." She ended in a flourish, and laughter and cheers filled the hall, tankards thumping the boards.

"Only you, Celenneth," a rider called through his guffaw, "would live thanks to a panicked goat."

She raised her mug. "Never underestimate the resourcefulness of desperate livestock."

From a quieter corner Linnea watched, admiring — and, if she was honest, envying a little — how easily Celenneth lit a room, how every eye danced with the reflection of her smile. When the riders begged another tale, Linnea let the corner of her mouth quirk. "I'll grant her this," she murmured to the older rider beside her, "she spins a story well enough."

The woman nudged her, knowing. "You might as well just tell her you're proud. It's already written on your face."

Linnea looked away, feigning indifference — and then leaned in despite herself as Celenneth launched into the next tale, of a flooded river and a half-drowned packhorse. She'd never hear the end of it if she said so aloud; but there was no denying it was a splendid tale.

Sunlight lay soft across the floors the next day as Celenneth stood at the small table carving careful slices from a bright red apple. Linnea watched from the doorway, half-amused, half-fond, as her partner mangled the fruit; from the next room Brynja's laughter rose and fell with Freowyn's. For once it was just the two of them in the kitchen, the world shrunk to a warm bubble.

"You really do butcher that fruit," Linnea teased, coming to take a crooked slice. "How did you ever survive alone? I'd have thought you'd starve."

Celenneth popped a piece in her mouth, grinning. "Surviving was never about clean cuts. It's about stubbornness."

Linnea rolled her eyes and took the knife. "Give it here." Under her sure hands even the simple task turned graceful, and Celenneth watched her, softening. "You're good at this."

"Someone has to be." Linnea offered her a perfect slice. "Try not to choke."

But what began light turned, as such talks did lately, toward the one thing they could not settle. Celenneth paced, her voice rising. "You can't shield her from everything. She needs to understand the dangers out there — to learn to stand on her own feet."

"She's a child!" Linnea's voice cracked, fear and anger braided in it. "She doesn't need a sword in her hand before she can write her name."

Celenneth halted, frustration in her face. "I'm not asking her to fight. I only want her ready. What if—"

"What if she turns out like you?" The words were out before Linnea could catch them, and she regretted them the instant she saw the hurt spark in Celenneth's eyes.

Celenneth stepped back, all the heat gone out of her. "I see. I push too hard."

Linnea closed the distance, her stance softening. "I'm sorry," she said, thick with remorse. "You're not wrong. I just can't bear the thought of her losing her childhood to our fears."

"I don't want that either," Celenneth said, quieter. "I only want her safe."

For a moment the tension hung — then Linnea reached for her hand. "We'll find the way. Together."

That night the firelight lay low and golden, and Linnea sat by the hearth mending a piece of Brynja's clothing, her hair loose around her shoulders. Celenneth lingered in the doorway, her eyes tracing every line of her.

"You're beautiful," she said, barely a breath.

Linnea looked up, coloring. "For a ranger, you're no good at sneaking around quietly." She set the sewing aside.

Celenneth crossed and knelt beside her, taking her hand. "I mean it. I've been away too much of late. And every time I come back, seeing you is like coming home."

Linnea leaned her forehead to Celenneth's. "You're my home, too," she whispered, the words carrying all the weight of a vow. They met in a tender kiss, each press of their lips shutting out the world, the fire soft beside them and the scents of pine and wool mingling as Linnea's fingers tangled in Celenneth's hair — two people finding their way back to each other's warmth after too many nights apart.

9Summer 2968

Cermië 20, 2968

The sun hung low over the wide golden fields of the Riddermark, gilding the gathered Rohirrim in honeyed light. They stood in a broad circle — riders and their proud mounts, all turned inward — and at its center stood Linnea, straight-backed, her dark hair stirring in the breeze, while a little apart Celenneth watched with her arms folded and a faint smile on her lips.

At the circle's head stood Eadwyn, her helm cradled in the crook of her arm, and she raised her voice for all to hear. "We Eorlingas do not give our honors lightly. Blood ties us together — but so do shared purpose and earned trust. Though Linnea was not born to the Mark, she has ridden with us, learned our ways, and shown her heart to be true. She has tended our people, and proven herself in the saddle and at the bow." A murmur of assent moved through the riders, and Linnea's cheeks warmed, and she lowered her eyes a moment under the weight and the blessing of their regard. "Let all bear witness," Eadwyn went on, "that we name Linnea one of the Mark in spirit, if not in blood. May she ride under our banner, and be counted kin."

Garin stepped forward bearing a folded green cloak trimmed in gold, a white horse galloping across an emerald field stitched at its breast, and Eadwyn took it and settled it over Linnea's shoulders, where it caught the last of the day's light. A cheer rose and echoed off the far hills. Linnea lifted her head, her eyes shining. "I am honored," she said, steady despite the lump in her throat. "I'll strive to be worthy of it."

Then Eadwyn signaled, and a rider led forward a tall, sleek mare — deep chestnut, her mane and tail dark and glossy, her step proud and fluid. Eadwyn took the reins and brought the horse before Linnea. "Her name is Eorlin," she said, a quiet smile creasing her face. "Swift, strong, and loyal — and yours. You may remember her; she was a new foal when last you saw her."

Linnea drew a sharp breath. This was more than a token — it was a bond of trust laid in her hands. She reached out and stroked the mare's neck, feeling the ripple of muscle beneath the silken coat. "I'll ride her with honor," she said softly, meeting Eadwyn's eyes. "Thank you."1

Across the circle Celenneth's smile brightened, and she came through the crowd to stand at Linnea's side. "You wear their colors well," she murmured, low, for Linnea alone.

Before Linnea could answer, Eadwyn turned to Celenneth and held out a second cloak, and a profound hush fell. "Celenneth of the Dúnedain. You have fought beside us, shared our dangers, shown your mastery in the wilds. We offer you the same honor, that you might stand fully among us."

Celenneth's eyes softened, and for a heartbeat something wistful crossed her face. Then, with gentle finality, she shook her head. "I am deeply honored," she said, and the hush held each word as sacred. "But my loyalty lies with my own people. To take your cloak would be to claim a bond I could not fully keep. I cannot accept it — though I treasure the offering."

A ripple of understanding moved through the Rohirrim, and Eadwyn inclined her head, accepting it with respect. "You remain a friend of the Mark. May the road rise to meet you, wherever you wander."

As the riders raised a cheer — some for Linnea's welcome, some for Celenneth's conviction — the two women stood side by side, and Linnea reached out and brushed her fingertips to Celenneth's arm. When the celebration swelled again into song and toast, Linnea felt something new settle in her: a belonging no birthplace or bloodline could have granted. With Eorlin at her side and the green cloak of Rohan on her shoulders, she was proud of the life she had built here.

10Autumn 2968

Narquelië 15, 2968

Mornings in the wood had taken on a gentle rhythm since Celenneth began Brynja's training — though at not quite three years old, "training" was more a string of playful lessons than anything else. With the child's small hand wrapped safe in hers, she led her through the undergrowth, pointing out bright berries and soft leaves, teaching her which were good and which to leave be.

"See that bird?" she whispered, crouching by a fern, and Brynja followed her gaze, wide-eyed, to a sparrow flitting between branches. "Move very quietly, and you can watch it without frightening it away." Brynja crept on tiptoe, giggling into her mother's cloak whenever a twig cracked underfoot. In the afternoons, while Celenneth loosed arrows at a distance, Brynja stood close with a little wooden bow her mother had carved her — no string, only a curved stick for pretending — and "aimed" at pinecones, delighted whenever Celenneth praised her good eye. Linnea helped in quieter ways: taking Brynja home after the lessons, tending the small scrapes she earned tripping over roots, and after their bread and stew, curling beside her by the fire with simple stories of heroes who were kind, and lullabies about looking out for one another.

The night air over the Riddermark lay cool and still when Celenneth jolted awake, her heart slamming. Outside, the shapes of horses shifted in their corrals and the breath of sleeping riders drifted through the camp; inside, her own breath came ragged, her eyes still glittering with a terror she could not shake. The dreams had returned.

Linnea was at her side in an instant, her hand hovering over Celenneth's shoulder, wanting nothing more than to still the tremors she could feel through the linen. "It's all right," she whispered. "It was only a dream." But Celenneth shook her head, caught still in whatever had seized her, her eyes unable to focus, her lips pressed thin. She swallowed hard and turned away, drawing her knees to her chest, and Linnea tried again — soft words, the warmth of her hand, a tune hummed low — and none of it reached past the wall of Celenneth's silence. Linnea's heart squeezed. She would have offered comfort until dawn, but she understood now that the old terrors would not yield to simple kindness; there were places in Celenneth's mind she could not follow. After a long moment Celenneth laid a hand over hers and gave it a single, understanding squeeze. Then she wrapped her cloak about herself and stepped out into the night.

The moon was a thin shard over the plains. She moved quietly, drawn toward the circle of elders who kept watch over the Rohirrim — and by the far fires, beyond the sleeping horses, three still stirred: Eorlfryd, his beard braided, his hands weathered; Birgith, whose sightless gaze seemed to pierce the dark; and Hildis, who had steadied many a frightened heart with her even voice. They greeted her with kind nods and shifted to make room on a bench carved with running horses.

"I am troubled," Celenneth began, her voice tight. "Shadows have followed me from far lands, and in sleep they crowd my mind. I can't outrun them, nor cut them down with sword or bow. I don't know how to be rid of what stalks me in the dark."

Eorlfryd spoke first, low and rumbling. "We have seen warriors carry wounds no blade can heal. Some hurts cling like briars to the spirit."

Birgith inclined her head. "Your dreams carry truth, Celenneth. They will not fade by force of will. They must be understood."

Hildis laid a warm hand on Celenneth's wrist. "In Rohan we speak of dreams as visitors. Some bring warnings, some bring lessons. Few can be turned from the door without cost."

Celenneth lowered her eyes, the firelight dancing over cheeks that she wondered held the shine of tears. "I don't know how to understand this torment. I only know it grows heavier each night."

"We will help you," Hildis said, quiet and certain. "Tell us what you can of these visions, as much as you are able — and then listen to what your heart, and the old wisdom of the Mark, tell you in return."

At her words the weight on Celenneth's shoulders seemed to shift, just slightly. She glanced back to where Linnea stood at a distance — shadowed, concerned, waiting. Linnea's love was steadfast; but this burden asked a different strength. "I will try," she said, subdued but resolved. "If the way forward lies in facing these shadows, I'll face them. I have too much to protect, and too many at my side, to yield to fear." The elders nodded, their acceptance gentle and patient, and in time they would begin to guide her through the tangled country of her dreams.1

11Winter 2968/9

Nénimë 6, 2969

The winter wind rattled the shutters of their small home in Edoras, carrying the sharp scent of snow where spring should have been. Brynja, just turned three winters old, dozed in the corner by the little hearth, wrapped in blankets. Outside, the low sun threw long shadows over the frost-laden thatch, and the murmur of stable hands drifted through the chilled air. Inside, all warmth seemed to have fled.

Linnea sat at the rough table, her palms pressed flat to it, her knuckles taut. Celenneth leaned against the far wall, arms crossed, her face shadowed. Between them stretched a silence thick with things unsaid.

"I've been thinking," Linnea began at last, steady but subdued, her eyes on Celenneth. "About Brynja. About what she needs."

Celenneth's posture stiffened. "And what do you think that is?"

Linnea's gaze dropped to the worn grain of the table, and her words came slow and deliberate. "Stability. A home where the ground doesn't shift every time you ride away. She needs a community, Celenneth — neighbors, friends, familiar faces over the years, not a string of passing strangers." She paused, softening. "I've been thinking of Bree. It isn't perfect. But it might be the kind of place where she could grow up rooted. What do you think?"

Celenneth straightened, uncrossing her arms. "Bree?" She sounded almost baffled. "You always hated how cramped it felt. The noise, the crowds. Why now?"

Linnea closed her eyes a moment, pressing her fingers into the wood. "It's not that I've forgotten those things. But Brynja's growing, learning her words, making sense of the world. She deserves a place where she can run the same courtyard day after day, where people know her name." She looked up. "And maybe we need that too. Somewhere to learn who we are when we're not always on the move."

Celenneth stepped nearer, resting her hands on the back of a chair as though it might anchor her. "Are you asking me to settle in Bree?" The edge in her had softened to something closer to confusion.

Linnea shook her head, a faint, bittersweet smile touching her lips. "I'm not saying Bree is the only answer. I'm only wondering if it's time we thought about something steadier. A place where Brynja can feel safe — where we can. But more than anything, Celenneth, I want to know what you want. For yourself. For us." Her voice gentled further. "I need to know you're here because you choose to be. Not because you feel bound to."

At that, Celenneth's grip tightened, her knuckles paling against the old chair. She opened her mouth, and closed it. Outside a stable door creaked, and a distant rider's call drifted in. Within, there was only the small hearth and Brynja's soft breathing.

"Linnea, I—" Celenneth's voice was barely above a whisper. "I don't know what to say."

Linnea rose and pushed her chair back without a sound, and crossed to the shuttered window, pressing her palm to the cold wood. In the quiet she could almost see the roads winding away from Edoras — east toward Bree, or south along Celenneth's old paths. "Then don't say anything," she murmured, not looking back. "Just think on it."

The silence returned, heavy and long, and Celenneth watched her, feeling the whole weight of the choice. The wind sighed through the thatch, and Brynja stirred but did not wake.

12Elrond's Missive

Súlimë 3, 2969

The pale sun hung low over a road still dusted with late frost when the messenger appeared — a grey shape against the muted land, his cloak rippling, near seamless with the winter-worn country. Celenneth marked him first, her eyes narrowing at the lean, swift horse and the elegant tack that spoke of Rivendell. The Elf dismounted with a fluid, almost too-smooth grace, his gaze sweeping the small homestead, and the folk near the stable paused their work to watch the unlooked-for arrival.

"Celenneth of the Dúnedain?" he asked, his voice resonant even in its formality.

She stepped forward, straight-backed, her auburn hair tied but tousled from the day's work. "I am she."

The Elf inclined his head and drew a cylindrical case from his saddlebag — dark wood inlaid with fine silver filigree, unmistakably Rivendell's work. "This missive comes from Lord Elrond. For your eyes alone."

Surprise flickered across Celenneth's face before her reserve closed over it. She took the case and inclined her head. "You've come far. Will you not rest before your return?"

His lips curved faintly, his gaze flicking once to Linnea, who lingered a few steps behind, her hands tucked in her cloak, quietly watchful. "Your offer is generous, but my errand allows no delay. I bid you well." And without another word he mounted and turned toward the horizon and rode off as swiftly as he had come.

Celenneth watched him go, the case heavier in her hands than its size warranted.

"What is it?" Linnea asked, stepping closer, wariness beneath the curiosity.

Celenneth traced the smooth surface. "Something of weight, if it comes from Rivendell." She was already working it open. Within lay a single folded parchment, its edges finely scripted, sealed with Elrond's crest. She broke the wax and unfolded it, her eyes moving over the page, her focus deepening to a slow furrow.

"Celenneth?"

She let out a measured breath. "A summons," she said at last. "Elrond asks me to come to Rivendell. He writes of troubling movements in the east."

Linnea's frown deepened. "Movements? What does he mean? Are you leaving?"

Celenneth met her gaze, a flicker of uncertainty in her own. "I don't know yet. But Elrond doesn't call without cause. I'll need time to think on it."

Linnea's jaw tightened. Her voice stayed steady, but it carried the weight of long familiarity. "You'll go."

"I haven't decided."

"You will," Linnea said quietly, a note of resignation in it as she turned back toward the house. "You always do."

Left alone in the cold, Celenneth stood a while with the letter in her hands, the wind tugging her cloak, the elegant script carrying its freight of duty. She had answered many summonses in her life, each one drawing her away from home toward the unknown. But this time, the thought of leaving sat heavier than it ever had.1
