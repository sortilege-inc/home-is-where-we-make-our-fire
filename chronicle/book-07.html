Eorlfryd spoke first, his voice rumbling low. “We have seen warriors who carry wounds no blade can heal. Some pains cling like briars to the spirit.”

Birgith inclined her head. “Your dreams carry truth, Celenneth. They will not fade simply by force of will. They must be understood.”

Hildis placed a gentle hand on Celenneth’s wrist, her grip warm and steady. “In Rohan, we speak of dreams as visitors. Some bring warnings, some bring lessons. Few can be denied without cost.”

Celenneth lowered her eyes. The fire’s glow danced over her cheeks, and she wondered if it reflected the tears gathering at the corners of her eyes. “I do not know how to understand this torment. I only know it grows heavier with each passing night.”

Hildis’s voice was quiet, certain. “We will help you. You must share what you can of these visions, as much as you are able, and then listen to what your heart and the Mark’s old wisdom tell you in return.”

At her words, Celenneth’s posture eased, as if the weight pressing her down had shifted slightly. She glanced back over her shoulder, where Linnea stood at a distance—shadowed, concerned, and waiting. Celenneth knew Linnea’s love was steadfast, but this burden required a different kind of strength.

“I will try,” she said softly, voice subdued but determined. “If my path forward lies in facing these shadows, then I will face them. I have too much to protect, and too many who stand beside me, to yield to this fear.”

The elders nodded. Their acceptance was gentle and patient, a warmth as subtle as the fire’s glow on a cold night. In time, they would guide her through the tangled forests of her dreams.

11Winter 2968/9

Winter 2969

The winter wind rattled the wooden shutters of their small home in Edoras, carrying with it the sharp scent of snow instead of spring. Brynja, just turned three winters old, dozed lightly in the corner beside the small hearth, wrapped in blankets. Outside, the low sun cast long shadows on the frost-laden thatch roofs, and the distant murmur of stable hands and watchful guards drifted through the chilled air. Inside, however, all warmth seemed to have fled.

Linnea sat at the rough-hewn table, her palms pressed flat against its surface, knuckles taut. Celenneth leaned against the far wall, arms crossed, face shadowed by uncertainty. Between them stretched a silence thick with unspoken fears. Beyond the door, Edoras bustled with quiet industry—Rohirrim riders tending horses, preparing for the long months ahead—but in this small room, the world had narrowed to two hearts and a choice not yet made.

“I’ve been thinking,” Linnea began at last, her voice steady but subdued. She kept her eyes on Celenneth. “About Brynja. About what she needs.”

Celenneth’s brow furrowed, and her posture stiffened. “And what do you think that is?” she asked, voice low.

Linnea hesitated, gaze dropping to the table’s worn grain. Her words came slowly, deliberately. “Stability. A home where the ground doesn’t shift each time you ride away. She needs a community, Celenneth. Neighbors, friends—familiar faces over the years, not just passing strangers.” Linnea paused, her tone softening as she continued. “I’ve been thinking about Bree. It’s not perfect, but it might be the kind of place where Brynja could grow up feeling rooted. What do you think?”

Celenneth stood straighter, uncrossing her arms. “Bree?” She sounded almost baffled. “You’ve always hated how cramped it felt. The noise, the bustle. Why consider it now?”

Linnea closed her eyes for a moment, pressing her fingertips into the wood. She could almost feel the weight of Brynja’s future balanced there. “It’s not that I’ve forgotten those things,” she said softly. “But Brynja’s growing, learning her words, making sense of the world. She deserves a place where she can run in the same courtyard day after day, where people know her name.” Linnea looked up, her gaze meeting Celenneth’s. “And maybe… maybe we need that too. Somewhere to figure out who we are when we’re not always on the move.”

Celenneth stepped nearer, resting her hands on the back of a chair as though it might anchor her. “Are you asking me to settle in Bree?” Her tone had grown quiet, the edge of hurt softened to confusion.

Linnea shook her head, a faint, bittersweet smile touching her lips. “I’m not saying Bree is the only answer,” she replied. “I’m just… wondering if it’s time for us to think about finding something steadier. A place where Brynja can feel safe, where we can feel safe. But more than anything, Celenneth, I want to know what you want. For yourself, for us.” Her voice softened further. “I need to know you’re here because you choose to be, not because you feel obligated.”

At that, Celenneth’s grip tightened, knuckles paling against the old chair. She opened her mouth, then closed it again. Outside, a stable door creaked, and the muffled sound of a distant rider calling out drifted in. Inside, only the crackle of the small hearth and Brynja’s soft breathing offered any comfort.

“Linnea, I… I don’t know what to say,” Celenneth admitted, voice barely above a whisper.

Linnea stood, pushing her chair back without sound. She walked to the shuttered window, pressing her palm against the cold wood. In the quiet, she could almost see the winding roads leading away from Edoras—east toward Bree, or south following Celenneth’s old paths. “Then don’t say anything,” she murmured, not looking back. “Just think about it.”

For a long moment, the silence returned, heavy and profound. Celenneth watched Linnea, feeling the burden of choice. The wind sighed through the thatch, and Brynja stirred but did not wake.

12Elrond’s Missive

Súlimë 3, 2969

The pale winter sun hung low on the horizon when the messenger appeared, a shadow against the frost-dusted road. His gray cloak rippled in the crisp breeze, blending almost seamlessly with the muted landscape. Celenneth spotted him first, her sharp eyes narrowing as she took in the rider’s swift, lean horse and the elegant tack that marked it as coming from Rivendell.

The elf dismounted with fluid grace, his movements almost too smooth for mortal eyes, and his gaze swept over the small homestead with an air of quiet precision. The gathering of people near the stable paused their tasks, curiosity tugging their attention to the unexpected arrival.

“Celenneth of the Dúnedain?” the elf asked, his voice resonant and melodic even in its formality.

Celenneth stepped forward, her posture straight, her auburn hair tied back but tousled from the day’s work. “I am she,” she answered, her tone calm but edged with curiosity.

The elf inclined his head, drawing a cylindrical case from his saddlebag. It was a masterwork of Rivendell—dark wood inlaid with intricate silver filigree, the craftsmanship unmistakable. “This missive comes from Lord Elrond. It is for your eyes alone.”

Celenneth’s expression flickered briefly with surprise before she masked it behind her practiced reserve. Taking the case, she inclined her head in acknowledgment. “You’ve traveled far. Would you not rest before your return?”

The elf’s lips curved faintly, a smile touched with the subtle grace of his kind. His gaze flicked momentarily to Linnea, who lingered a few steps behind Celenneth, her hands tucked into her cloak and her expression quietly watchful. “Your offer is generous, but my errand allows no delay. I bid you well.”

Without another word, the elf mounted his steed and turned it toward the horizon, riding away with the same swiftness with which he had arrived. Celenneth watched him go, the case in her hands feeling heavier than its size warranted.

“What is it?” Linnea asked, stepping closer. Her voice held an edge of wariness beneath her curiosity.

Celenneth’s fingers traced the smooth surface of the case. “Something significant, if it comes from Rivendell,” she said, already working to unseal it.

Within was a single folded parchment, its edges adorned with elegant script and sealed with Elrond’s crest. She hesitated briefly, then broke the wax seal and unfolded the letter. Her eyes scanned the page, her expression shifting from sharp focus to a slow furrow of her brow.

“Celenneth?” Linnea’s voice softened, sensing the shift in her companion’s demeanor.

Celenneth let out a measured breath, her fingers tightening on the parchment. “It’s a summons,” she said at last. “Elrond requests my presence in Rivendell. He writes of troubling movements in the east.”

Linnea’s frown deepened. “Movements? What does he mean? Are you leaving?”

Celenneth turned to meet her gaze, a flicker of uncertainty in her eyes. “I don’t know yet. But if Elrond calls, it’s never without cause. I’ll need time to think on this.”

Linnea’s jaw tightened. Her voice was steady, but her words carried the weight of familiarity. “You’ll go.”

Celenneth tilted her head slightly, her gaze softening. “I haven’t decided.”

“You will,” Linnea said quietly, a note of resignation in her tone as she turned and walked back toward the house. “You always do.”

Left alone, Celenneth stood for a moment in the cold, the letter still in her hands. The wind picked up, tugging at her cloak, and she stared down at the elegant writing that carried with it the weight of duty. She had answered many summonses in her life, each one drawing her away from home and toward the unknown. But this time, the thought of leaving felt heavier than it ever had before.
