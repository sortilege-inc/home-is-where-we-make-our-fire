1Summons

Súlimë 4, 2966

Eofred's hall was quiet but for the murmur of his captains and the crackle of the central hearth. Celenneth crossed it at a purposeful stride, the fire's warmth brushing her travel-worn cloak, and found the marshal at the head of the long table, bent over a map with the weight of the Riddermark's defense in the set of his shoulders.

He looked up as she reached him. "Celenneth. I've been weighing the road ahead. Dol Guldur's shadow reaches far, and though winter bought us time, the spring will ask for action." He gestured her closer, and laid three fingers on three points of the map.

"There are three roads I could send you down. First — we need clearer eyes on the enemy. A watch kept on the Greenwood, to number them and read their intent. You've worked with Thalion's folk before; you could go to them again." His hand slid west. "Second, Rohan's ties to Gondor want tending. Their vigilance on the Anduin matters as much as our patrols. A messenger to Minas Tirith, to lay what we know before Steward Turgon and ask his aid in kind — you've earned the trust to carry that with weight." His finger moved north-east, and his face darkened. "Third, Dale and the dwarves of Erebor sit far enough from Dol Guldur to think themselves safe. Someone must remind them the Shadow's reach doesn't stop at their borders — and that will not be an easy telling."

Celenneth studied the three points. "Each has its risk. And each is worth the taking."

"Indeed. But the Mark cannot spare you for all three, and spring comes quickly. You must choose where your skill will serve best." He paused. "I called you here not to command you, ranger, but to counsel with you. Where do you believe you'll do the most good?"

She let her gaze travel the map — the dark eaves of the Greenwood where orcs and worse things stirred; the white towers of Minas Tirith, where a word in the right ear could move armies; the mountain-halls whose lords must be won one wary conversation at a time. "If it's to be one," she said at last, meeting his eyes, "let it be the one that binds our strength together against Dol Guldur. Give me a day to think on it."

Eofred nodded, his respect plain. "Take it. But spring waits for no one."

The choice sat heavy on her that evening, by the hearth of the quarters she shared. Linnea sat across from her with Brynja asleep in her arms, the baby's small fingers curled into the corner of her cloak — and for all the warmth of the scene, a chill had opened in the quiet between them.

"You've already decided," Linnea said. Not a question.

Celenneth's eyes stayed on the flames. "Eofred's right. Gondor has to know what's coming. If they falter, the Shadow spreads unchecked. And I'm a ranger, a messenger — this is the thing I was made for."

Linnea's jaw tightened. She looked down at Brynja and smoothed her dark hair. "And us? And her? You'll just ride off and leave us. Why does it have to be you?"

Celenneth flinched, and made herself meet Linnea's eyes. "It isn't about leaving you. It's about protecting you. Both of you. Rohan needs its riders here, and we've been the thread between these peoples — I can carry this where others can't. I don't believe this is the great trial we're meant to face together. And if Brynja can't have us both on this road, she should at least have you."

"And if something happens to you out there?" Linnea's voice cracked. "What then? What happens to her when she loses a mother before she ever knew her?"

Celenneth drew a shaking breath. "I can't promise nothing will happen. But I can promise every step I take will be for her, and for you. I wouldn't ride into danger if I thought it was for nothing."

Linnea shook her head, tears standing in her eyes. "You always have to be the one to carry it. You can't ever let someone else take the weight."

The fire filled the silence, and Brynja's soft breathing. At last Linnea let out a slow breath. "I'll stay," she said, deliberate. "For a season. Until Brynja's strong enough to travel with us. Freowyn can help, and I can keep training with Eadwyn. But Celenneth — if you don't come back—"

Celenneth crossed the room and knelt beside her and set a hand on her arm, her voice low and raw. "I will come back."

Linnea turned to her, a tear slipping loose. "You'd better," she whispered. "Because I don't know that I can do this without you."1

2The Road to Minas Tirith

Súlimë 5, 2966

Before dawn Linnea woke to find Celenneth leaning over her, already dressed for the road. "I'm packed. I'm leaving Nimroch with you — I'll travel light and fast. Two weeks, if the road's kind." She bent and kissed her, and Linnea threw her arms around her neck and pulled her down. "I love you. May the road be short and swift."

Celenneth extricated herself at last, and crossed to Brynja's crib, and kissed the sleeping infant's brow without waking her. Then she was gone into the grey.

The first day ran with fortune. The weather held, the road lay open, and the Númenórean boots wove their old spell of ease into her steps, until the miles fell away almost without her feeling them. A game trail through a copse of budding oaks shaved hours from her path, and by nightfall she had camped by a brook that murmured over mossy stones. The stars burned bright, and for once she slept without dreaming.

Súlimë 6, 2966

The next day the wind turned sharp and restless, and the road climbed into rockier ground that slowed her. By midday it narrowed into a shadowed gulch, and there she caught sight of the orcs — a patrol of four, ambling with a casual arrogance that turned her stomach, trading low talk and sharp laughter. She went still behind a jagged boulder and weighed it, the memory of her fight against Uftak fresh enough to counsel caution.

Discretion won, and fortune was with her. The orcs kept their attention on one another, and when their voices had faded down the trail she slipped past their line without a sound. She marked, as she went, how bold they had grown to walk this country so openly.1

By dusk the spires of Calenhad rose against a setting sun, and she reached the beacon-fort's gates worn to the bone, the sentries nodding her through with friendly faces. That night at its inn she turned the near-miss over and over, and slept little.

Súlimë 7, 2966

She moved on at a radiant dawn, the boots carrying her light over even ground, and by afternoon stood at the foot of Nardol, another of Gondor's ancient beacon-hills, its summit crowned with the ruin of an old watchfire platform. Voices reached her as she climbed — low, weary, human — and cresting the ridge she found a small band in the travel-stained colors of Gondor gathered near the platform. An older man with silver in his dark hair turned, his hand going to his sword.

"Peace," Celenneth said, raising her open hands. "A ranger, bound for Minas Tirith."

He studied her, then eased his grip. "You've the look of the long road. Come, share our fire, if you bring no ill will."

She joined them, and they gave their names — Turion, the older man; Arvin, young and wiry and a soldier to the bone; Liseth, quiet, with the look of a woman not to be crossed. The talk turned to Gondor, and Turion's voice grew heavy. "Our strength wanes. The borders stretch thin, our forces scatter, and the beacons stand cold. The lands beyond the White Mountains run wild while our lords bicker over pride, and the enemy gathers in the east."

"Even the common folk feel it," Liseth said. "The villages watch their roads. There are whispers of darker things moving."

Celenneth listened with a heavy heart; she had read the same signs herself — the bold orc patrols, the abandoned watchposts. But Arvin's eyes held a fierce spark. "There are still those who hold the line," he said. "We've not forgotten who we are. Gondor stands, even bloodied. The enemy will find no easy victory here."

She admired the fire in him, and the weariness under it, and gave what she could in return — tales of her own hard roads, and the strength she'd found in her allies. It was a small thing, but the fire seemed to burn a little brighter for it.

Súlimë 8, 2966

She broke camp before the others woke, and thanked them as they rose. Turion clasped her hand. "You go to Minas Tirith. Carry what you've heard: the people still fight for Gondor. It's the lords who must be roused to an active defense, not a slow surrender."

"I will," she promised. "Gondor won't be forgotten."

She came down off Nardol into gentle country and made good miles. In a glade of towering oaks a pheasant caught her eye, its plumage bright in the dappled light, skittish and unaware of her. She crouched, and studied its nervous grace, and when it turned just right she loosed, and the arrow took it clean. She knelt and thanked it, and made of it a good supper of roasted bird and forest herbs — the feathers set by for fletching.2

Súlimë 9, 2966

The land rose at last into the craggy foothills that marked the approach to the White City, and Minas Tirith climbed the horizon before her — white walls and soaring spires, the banners of Gondor bright on the wind. She quickened her pace up the winding road to the great gates, where silver-armored guards watched her come.

"Halt. What is your business in Minas Tirith?"

"I am Celenneth of the Dúnedain. I carry messages of import, and seek audience with your lords."

The guard appraised her a moment, then signaled the gates open, and she stepped through into the humming life of the city — voices, footfalls, the far ring of a smith's hammer. The streets wound upward through seven levels of pale, marvelous stonework, and at every level she glimpsed Gondor's people at their trades — and, beneath the bustle, an undercurrent of unease, the shadow of the city's troubles lingering even here. She climbed to the Citadel, past towering statues of dead kings, and was brought to a chamber outside the great hall, where a steward's man received her.

"The lords of Gondor grant you audience," he said. "You'll be called shortly."

She stepped back, her hand brushing her sword-hilt out of habit, and took in the grandeur of the place, and readied herself.

3Steward Turgon

Súlimë 9, 2966

The great hall stretched before her, its vaulted ceiling arching like the ribs of some vast beast, sunlight pouring through high windows onto pale stone. None of the grandeur eased the knot in Celenneth's shoulders as her boots echoed toward the dais, where the Steward of Gondor sat beneath the banner of the White Tree, his face grave, his eyes sharp as they took her measure.

She stopped a respectful distance off and inclined her head. "My lord Steward. I come as a ranger of the Dúnedain, bearing tidings of unrest in the north and east. I ask your time and your counsel, for the shadows lengthen, and Gondor's allies cannot stand alone."

He leaned forward, resting his hands on the arms of his chair, his lined face wary and curious both. "You have come far to stand in this hall, Celenneth of the Dúnedain. Speak plainly. Tell us why Gondor should turn its eyes north when its borders are pressed on every side."

"Because Dol Guldur stirs again, my lord. The orcs of the mountains grow bold, raiding farther and more often than in living memory. The Woodland Realm is beset; Dale and Esgaroth whisper of dark powers moving beyond the peaks. If Gondor does not strengthen its bonds with these realms, the Shadow will grow unchecked, and spread like rot into every corner of Middle-earth."

A murmur ran through the small council behind him. He raised a hand to still it. "The plight of our northern kin is not unknown to us. But Gondor's strength is not what once it was. Our forces are stretched thin against the east — the Easterlings are relentless, the corsairs of Umbar harry our coasts. I do not deny the wisdom in your words. Neither can I ignore what we face."

Celenneth stepped closer, her voice softening without losing its edge. "I understand Gondor's burdens, my lord. But strength does not come from standing apart — it comes from unity. The north remembers Gondor's aid in years past. Now the north asks the same in return. If the Shadow takes us one by one, no fortress, no city, no kingdom will be left standing."

For a moment the weight of his office seemed to press down on him. "Your words carry the fire of conviction," he said at last. "And yet the choice is not a simple one. Gondor cannot march north while its own borders crumble. We are not blind to the rising dark. But our resources are finite, and our enemies many."

A heavy silence fell. Celenneth bowed her head to hide her disappointment. "I have done my duty in bringing this to Gondor," she said quietly. "I can ask no more than that it be weighed."

"Weighed it shall be." His expression gave nothing away. "You have my leave to remain in Minas Tirith. I trust the city's hospitality will ease your road."

She left the hall with her footsteps ringing in the stillness, her mind churning. She had known the odds were slim — but to hear Gondor's hesitation laid bare stung worse than she cared to admit, and the weight of her errand sat heavier on her as she stepped out into a sunlight the gleaming towers did nothing to warm.1

4Rumours

Súlimë 9–14, 2966

The days after her audience passed in a slow, restless rhythm. Celenneth moved through the levels of the White City like a shadow, taking in its sights while keeping her ears open. The streets teemed — merchants crying wares, smiths at their forges, children darting between armored legs — and beneath the bustle ran that same undercurrent of unease, taut and waiting.

She spent her mornings on the stone parapets above the Pelennor Fields, her bow and sword laid beside her, their familiar weight a small comfort. Her afternoons she gave to the winding streets, lingering near the markets and taverns where news moved freely.

It was in the shadowed corner of an inn, over the clink of mugs, that she first caught it — two soldiers, their voices low.

"Strangest thing," one said. "We reckoned the orcs were thick along the Anduin or up in Ithilien. But there they were, all the way out by the Entwash. What draws them there?"

"Desperation, maybe," said the other. "Or something worse. Captain said they weren't passing through — they'd a camp. A proper one."

Celenneth kept her eyes on her cup and her ears open.

"Find anything in it?"

"Nothing of use. Captain had us torch the lot, leave no sign. Still doesn't sit right. Orcs that far north-west — it's too bold by half."

The talk moved on, but the seed was planted. She finished her drink and slipped out, her mind turning. The Entwash lay far from any orc's usual haunt; a deliberate move there spoke of a purpose she could not yet see.

The next day, walking the outer wall, she sought out more — careful, casual — and found a weathered soldier at his whetstone. "I hear your patrols have been busy," she said. "Orcs near the Entwash?"

He looked up, frowning. "Aye. Strange business. We drove them off, but they'd made a foothold. Like they were waiting on something. Or someone."

"And now?"

"Quiet. Too quiet, if you ask me. But we've no orders to pursue. Not the men to spare."

That night in her rented room, the city's noise fading, she weighed it. She had come to warn Gondor, and the Steward had heard her and done nothing. She could turn north and regroup — or follow this thread and learn what the orcs wanted by the Entwash.

The decision came quickly. She had seen too much of the Shadow's cunning to call this chance. If Gondor could not spare the men, she would go herself; it was little enough out of her way back to Edoras, and if she could not win the Steward's word, perhaps this service would prove worth as much to the Free Peoples.1

5Tracking

Súlimë 15–20, 2966

The plains west of the Entwash ran green and gold to the horizon, rippling in the spring wind. Celenneth kept to the river's edge, reading the faint ground, the soldiers' words in her mind: a deliberate camp, torched and abandoned. Even the most thorough burning left traces for a patient eye.

She foraged well as she went — roots and wild greens from the thawing soil, a grouse taken clean off its nest — and camped the first night beneath the stars with the Entwash's swollen roar for company, its current fat with mountain snowmelt. She kept it in sight but not close.

The searching grew deliberate as the ground near the water softened, footprints held in the mud. Before midday she found what she sought: the charred bones of the camp, crude structures and fire-pits stark against the fertile earth. The soldiers had done their work, but not perfectly — and among the ashes were fresh signs, prints leading north, recent. Someone had come back to this place, and meant to again.

Her pulse quickened. Whoever it was had not been content to abandon the position. She followed the tracks toward the river, reading a small group moving quick and precise — worth the risk of return, whatever they came for. But the afternoon darkened with the promise of a storm, and the Entwash grew louder, churning against its banks.

Then a deeper rumble than thunder reached her, and the ground trembled underfoot. The spring flood broke its bounds. A wall of water surged down the channel, carrying broken trees and torn shrubs in its fury, devouring the banks. Celenneth cursed and scrambled for higher ground, her feet pounding the uneven earth, and gained a rocky outcrop just as the flood tore past where she had stood. The trail she had followed was gone in an instant, erased — and with it any hope of the orcs' purpose.1

The waters raged two days more, carving the land, and she pressed upstream in search of a crossing, finding none — the Entwash had become a leviathan, and any attempt at it would have been suicide. The country grew wilder as she climbed, rocky and thicketed, the nights cold and damp, and exhaustion crept in like a thief and dulled her edge. She made her camps in hidden places and kept her watch — but no ranger, however skilled, can guard every hour.

They came in the last dark before dawn, moving like wraiths over the wet earth. She woke to a hand clamped over her mouth and her arms wrenched behind her, and though she kicked and twisted, the grip was iron.

"Lively one," an orc sneered, breath foul against her ear. "Bind her, quick."

They lashed her wrists and ankles, tore her weapons from her, rifled her pack. One yanked her head back by the hair, blackened teeth bared. "A ranger. Thought you were clever, poking where you don't belong." Her only answer was a glare, and he struck her across the face hard enough to leave her reeling. "We'll see how clever, when the masters are done with you." They hauled her up, blindfolded her with rough cloth, and drove her forward with jabs of their weapons into a muffled, disorienting dark.2

She walked blind for days. She fixed on her breathing, on the ground shifting beneath her boots — soft loam, then rock, then whispering grass — on the fragments of speech that reached her. Most was the harsh guttural tongue, but Westron words broke through: Dol Guldur, spoken often, with grim laughter; ranger, and spy, with venom and a flicker of fear. One voice stood apart, deeper and raspier, giving orders the rest obeyed without question — Grusk, the leader. Always their march bent northward, toward the dark fortress she had warned Gondor of, and she understood: she was their prize, and they were carrying her to it.

They pushed her hard, and her endurance seemed to gall as much as impress them. "Strong, this one," one muttered, grudging. "Stronger than you," another jeered, and earned a snarl. Celenneth held to her pride like a stubborn flame, and to something colder and more useful: every sound, every scent, every shift of the wind became a clue, and she filed each away — their voices, their patterns, the cadence of their watches — and waited.

One night, feigning sleep, she caught two of them talking low of something stirring in the shadows, a power that would rise again — the sorcery, one said, and a master's will — and the words settled a sick dread in her that only confirmed her fears. She clenched her jaw against the ache in her legs and marched on, and turned her whole mind to the one thing that mattered: finding the crack she would slip through.

6Infighting

Súlimë 21–26, 2966

The blindfold pressed at her eyes as the march dragged on, each step a fight against hunger and fatigue and the orcs' relentless prodding. She had learned to count the hours by the rhythm of their travel and the turning of the air — cold mornings, warm afternoons, the biting chill of dusk. And she had learned her captors: their voices, their tempers, the grudges seething under their crude hierarchy. They were far from united. If she played it carefully, that could be a blade she need not hold in her hands.

It was at an evening halt that Morga made his move. She heard the whip-orc's lash trailing the ground as he sauntered up, and straightened where she sat bound against a tree.

"Still so quiet, ranger," he said, his Westron unpleasantly smooth. He crouched close, his breath washing over her. "Halfway to Dol Guldur, and not a word. Must be that stubborn Dúnedain blood. Tell me why a lone ranger wanders so far south. Spying for Gondor? Plotting with the horse-lovers?"

"I have nothing to say to you," she said, level.

He flicked the whip, the barbs hissing across the dirt near her face. "Grusk wants you alive. He didn't say pretty." When her silence only bored him he stood. "Fine. You'll have time to regret it when the master has you." He turned to go.

"You're afraid," Celenneth said.

He froze. "What did you say?"

"You're afraid. You keep the whip close, but you don't strike — not unless you're sure the others won't laugh when you fail."

His eyes narrowed, and for a moment she thought the lash would come. Instead he leaned in, inches away. "We'll see who's afraid, when you're begging in Dol Guldur." And he stalked off, his whip cracking the empty air, while the others watched and said nothing. It was a small thing. It was a beginning.

Over the nights that followed she worked at the cracks. To Snaga, the wiry scavenger sulking at the edge of the fire: They laugh at you, don't they? They call you the runt, the rat, when they think you can't hear. To Uglat, the huge one with the stone-grafted arm, when he stomped past: You're the strongest here, and everyone knows it — even Grusk. That's why he keeps you on such a short leash. He's afraid of what you'd do if you saw you didn't need him. And to Morga again, low: You follow him like a hound, but you're the cleverer. Grusk's pride will get you all killed.

Each seed she planted with just enough detachment to leave doubt and deny nothing. And they grew. Snaga muttered louder and glanced at Uglat; Uglat sharpened his weapon with unnecessary force; Morga's whip cracked at every startle, his eyes flicking to Grusk more and more. Even silent Zograt withdrew, and Rukha traded jeers with the others where before there had been only sullen obedience. Only Grusk held his composure — though she could feel his frustration thickening with each day, his orders sharper, his glares more suspicious.

It came to a head on the last night. She heard Grusk stand apart, working a whetstone along Bite-Blood with slow, deliberate care, and then his heavy tread crossing to her. The others fell silent as he stopped over her.

"You've a clever tongue, ranger," he said, low and dangerous. "Stirring my lads against each other. You think it saves you?"

"Perhaps," she said, calm as a still pool. "Or perhaps I only see the cracks you're trying to hide."

He growled, the sound rumbling in his chest. "I've seen your kind before. Full of pride, till it's beaten out of you."

"Then why haven't you?" She leaned forward as far as the ropes allowed. "You've had days to break me, to kill me. Here I am. You're afraid, Grusk. Afraid that if you spill my blood, your precious plan comes apart in your hands."

The clearing seemed to freeze. His iron jaw clenched, and she braced for the blow — but he only leaned close, his rancid breath on her face. "Watch yourself. You think you've won something. All you've done is sign your death warrant." He straightened and rounded on the others. "Get your heads straight! I don't care what grudges you're nursing — we take her to Dol Guldur, and if any of you thinks of crossing me, you'll wish you hadn't." None dared answer him openly. But the tension held, thicker than ever, and beneath her blindfold Celenneth allowed herself the smallest smile.

It was Uglat's watch that night. She could feel him pacing near her, restless, unable to settle — the stone-arm rasping against the ground as he moved. An opportunity she would not waste.

"You're restless, Uglat," she said, low but clear. "Thinking what you'd do if Grusk weren't around?"

His steps stopped. "Keep talking, ranger. It'll be your last."

"It must gnaw at you. Everyone knows you're the strongest here — Grusk most of all. Why else keep you on watch while he sleeps sound? You're the only one who could take him, and he knows it."

His boots came closer, deliberate. "Shut your mouth before I do it for you."

"Go on, then." Her voice went soft, taunting. "Do what you've wanted since the first day. Kill me. Show him you're not his puppet."

For a moment, only silence. Then he lunged.

She had been ready — every muscle coiled for it. In the days of the march she had worked her wrists raw against the ropes, and now she twisted her hands free of the loosened bonds and rolled aside as his stone-arm crashed into the tree, splintering the bark where her head had been. She swept her legs under him and he stumbled; she seized the jagged shard of stone she had palmed and hidden, and drove it up beneath his jaw. His roar choked to a gurgle. He swung wild; she ducked under it, twisted the shard, and used his own weight to bring him down. He fell heavy, and lay still.1

She stood over him, breath ragged, and wiped the shard on the grass and gripped it like a dagger. The camp was quiet — the others deep in exhausted sleep, or careless. She knelt and stripped Uglat of his belt-knife, a crude but welcome blade, then took up her bearings in the dark. Her bonds were gone, a weapon in her hand, one captor dead and the rest turned against each other. The odds were still against her. But the balance had shifted — and she slipped from the ring of firelight and vanished into the night.

7Lost in Fangorn

Súlimë 26–30, 2966

The sun sank behind mist-cloaked hills as Celenneth stumbled into what she knew must be Fangorn Forest, each breath rasping, each step driven by stubborn will alone. Behind her the cries of the orc hunters thinned and lost themselves in the broken country she had barely escaped.

The forest loomed dark and ancient, its twisted branches clawing the sky. Fangorn was a place of shadowed legend — where paths shifted, trees kept secrets, and the unwary were never seen again. A cursed refuge. It was the only one she had.

The underbrush snagged at her cloak, but even worn to the bone her hunter's eye held. She read the ground for game-trails and shelter, and fashioned crude snares from root and vine along the narrow paths she'd marked. Hours later a snap brought her limping to a hare caught struggling in the loop; she dispatched it quick and clean, and the simple success of it steadied her — something ordinary, something she could master. Back at a makeshift camp beneath a vast old oak she skinned and roasted it, and for the first time since her capture, let herself hope.1

The next morning she went deeper after water, following the murmur of a stream, the ground treacherous with roots half-buried in moss. One caught her boot, and she pitched forward with a cry, her body twisting as she fell down a short rocky embankment into a cluster of boulders. Pain erupted white in her right leg, a sickening jolt through her whole body, and when she made herself look, her heart sank — the lower leg lay at an unnatural angle, broken.

For a long moment she only lay there staring up at the tangled canopy, unable to think past the pulsing agony. But despair was a luxury she could not afford. She dragged herself back against a weathered trunk, hauled herself upright, and searched with trembling hands for a splint — a fallen branch, long and sound; her belt, frayed but strong. Biting back a scream, she forced the bone into place and bound it fast. Fresh agony surged and left her gasping — but the leg was braced. Functional, barely. She collapsed against the tree, soaked in sweat.2

Darkness swallowed the forest again, cold and indifferent, and she huddled by the embankment with her makeshift spear across her lap, every muscle tensed for a threat she could not fight. Pain and exhaustion dragged her under at last, into a dream.

She stands before the black gates of Dol Guldur, jagged spires piercing a sky of ash and fire. Cold whispers coil around her like serpents, promising power, demanding surrender. Through the choking mist come shapes twisted and inhuman, their soulless eyes gleaming, and they surge toward her — reaching, clawing—

She woke with a strangled gasp, the still cold air of Fangorn sharp in her lungs, the pain in her leg a searing proof that she lived — and that escape had come at its own price.3

8Secrets of the Forest

Víressë 1, 2966

The cold morning gnawed at her as she woke from restless sleep, the broken leg throbbing like fire. She flexed her fingers against the chill and made herself focus. The pale light through the dense canopy brought little comfort; Fangorn's shadows lay as deep by day as by night.

She could not stay by the embankment — exposed, weak, hunted. She needed better shelter, water, and more snares before hunger finished what the fall had begun. Leaning on a crude staff, she limped deeper into the ancient wood, her eye out for defensible ground, every sound amplified in the stillness: the far rustle of leaves, the creak of wind-weary branches, the calls of unseen birds. Once, resting against a mossy rock, a prickle ran up the back of her neck — the sense of watching eyes. She turned, weapon up, and found only silent forest. But the quiet had a wrongness to it, as though the wood were holding its breath.

She pressed on until she found a rocky alcove where twisted roots formed a partial shelter beneath an old ash, and sank down there, panting, and made a thin infusion of her last dried herbs to dull the pain. It was then a sound reached her — a soft, rhythmic tapping, like hollow drumbeats on bark, slow and deliberate, too heavy for a man's tread and far too purposeful for wind. The shadows shifted at the clearing's edge; for a moment she thought she saw a massive shape slip between the trees, near indistinguishable from the bark and foliage. Then it was gone, and the tapping ceased.

Whatever it was, it was watching, and it was in no hurry. She reinforced her hollow with shaking hands and kept her spear close, and the eerie silence lingered long after the sound had died.

When she could bear the stillness no longer she forced herself up and went toward the murmur of the stream she'd heard, weaving through moss-covered roots and towering trunks, and found it at last running clear over pale stones. She knelt awkwardly and drank deep, and in the rippling water saw her own reflection — worn, hollow-eyed, grim — and, just beyond her shoulder, a hulking mass barely told apart from the bark-covered trees. She spun, staff raised. Nothing. Only stillness, and wind in the canopy.

She did not relax. Whatever moved through this wood was clever — cleverer than any beast. She set makeshift alarms about her streamside camp, sticks wedged in stones, thin trip-lines of vine strung to brittle branches. And as she worked, her fingers brushed something that did not belong: cold metal, half-buried in the earth. She unearthed a rusted dagger, its blade scarred and pitted, faint runes etched along the dark steel — symbols she did not know, and yet that stirred something dark at the back of her memory. Holding it sent an uncomfortable thrumming up her hand, as though the blade remembered a purpose she did not. She should have let it lie. She kept it.1

A low creak echoed from deeper in the woods then — a sound of straining wood under great pressure, unnatural and deliberate, and it came again, closer. The forest itself seemed to breathe. She backed toward her shelter, her broken leg screaming, and her foot struck something soft: tracks, pressed deep, their edges jagged like shattered wood, made by nothing she knew — something that walked, and was not flesh. The silence closed thick around her again. Whatever watched her was drawing near, and it was no orc, and no animal.

9The Ent's Test

Víressë 2, 2966

Celenneth crouched low against the trunk of an ancient oak, her breath shallow as she scanned the twisted expanse of Fangorn. Her staff lay across her knees, the rune-etched dagger secured at her side. The deep groaning came again — that resonant creak of aged wood under strain — and this time it seemed to encircle her, moving with slow and deliberate purpose. It knows I'm here.

Then a sharp crack: her trip-line had sprung. Something had triggered it. She rose, favoring her good leg, staff in hand. The undergrowth rustled, and a shadow shifted between the trees — not hurried, not panicked, but measured.

"Show yourself," she commanded, steadier than she felt.

The silence held. Then, with agonizing slowness, a colossal form emerged from the foliage. Its skin was gnarled bark, ancient and scarred; thick roots twisted down its arms like veins; its legs cracked the earth with each ponderous step, and its head was crowned with jagged, leafless branches spread like antlers. Two deep-set golden eyes glimmered beneath its brow — watchful, old, and knowing. No orcish horror; no servant of Dol Guldur. An Ent.

Its great head tilted as it studied her, weighing her presence in its forest. When at last it spoke, its voice was the groan of old trees in a fierce wind. "Hoom… a wanderer. Far from where she belongs."

Celenneth lowered her staff. "I seek no harm. Only shelter."

"Shelter is not freely given, in Fangorn." Its gaze lingered on her broken leg, the raw marks of rope on her wrists, the dark blade at her belt. "But perhaps… you are not as lost as you think. Come. The forest will decide whether you remain."

With no other choice, she steadied herself on her crutch and followed, leaving the ruined traps and the strange tracks behind, the trees creaking closed around her like a will made of living wood.

She struggled to keep the Ent's slow, earth-cracking pace, her leg a torment on the uneven ground, but she pressed on. Pain means I'm alive. The canopy thickened until Fangorn lay in perpetual twilight, the air heavy with damp earth and moss and something older. Without warning the Ent stopped, and turned its head with glacial slowness, its golden eyes glowing faint.

"You tread on sacred earth. Few of your kind leave."

"You led me here," Celenneth said, holding its gaze. "I don't believe it was to destroy me."

The Ent regarded her in long silence. Then the trees behind her shifted, their branches curling inward like claws, moss writhing over the roots as though the wood itself had drawn breath to watch. A deep groan rolled through the soil; roots snapped like whips, carving shallow trenches around her feet.

"You bear marks," the Ent rumbled, its gaze on the scars at her wrists — the chains of Dol Guldur.

"I am no servant of Dol Guldur," she spat, fire in it despite her exhaustion. "I escaped them."

"Escape does not cleanse what clings."

The dagger at her hip pulsed cold, and she gritted her teeth, knowing she could not explain it. The branches groaned louder, judging. And then the ground heaved, and roots snapped upward like striking serpents and coiled about her boots and dragged her to her knees, her staff clattering away, the forest's ancient will bearing down on her very soul until her vision darkened.

"You carry darkness," the Ent intoned, slow and terrible. "You will face it, or be consumed."

The world twists, and she stands in the haunted halls of Dol Guldur — stone walls slick with decay, cruel laughter ringing off cold iron gates. Chains snake around her wrists again, unbreakable. Out of the void come towering figures robed in black, their hollow eyes burning, waiting, and an insidious voice hisses through the dark: "Come… you belong here."

But Celenneth fights. She rips the spectral chains from her wrists with a scream, fury igniting in her — her pain, her fear, none of it will command her. "I escaped!" she roars, and the illusion shatters.

She came back to the living world on her knees in the damp earth, her body aching, her head swimming, her leg afire. The Ent watched in heavy silence. Slowly the creeping roots loosened and withdrew into the soil, and the trees' groaning ceased, and Fangorn seemed to exhale.

The Ent inclined its massive head, just slightly. "You fight," it said, and its voice had softened, just enough to hint at approval. "Perhaps there is hope, yet." Then the grove fell still once more, and she was alone — tested by a forest that had judged her, and allowed her to remain. For now.1

10Gaining Strength

Víressë 4, 2966

Days stretched into weeks beneath Fangorn's dark canopy, marked by quiet determination. Celenneth's broken leg mended slowly, the ache a constant reminder of her fragility, but in time she could limp farther, extending her range for foraging and traps. The forest gave little and allowed enough — small game in cunning loops, roots dug from mossy ground, bitter herbs brewed thin to dull the throb. She rose before every dawn and reinforced her shelter beneath the ancient ash against the creeping chill. Survival was no longer a desperate scramble; it had become her life.1

Sometimes she saw the Ent — through a mist-wreathed glade, or past a gnarled trunk that seemed to twist at the edge of her sight — always watching, never approaching. It never spoke to her again. But once she came back from her traps to find a pile of dry kindling stacked by her fire-ring, gathered by no hand of hers; another time the stream near her camp ran clearer and stronger, its stones subtly shifted. She never spoke to the forest, but she always whispered her thanks.

The dreams returned on a moonless night, the cursed dagger burning cold at her side like a poisoned heartbeat.

She stands again in Dol Guldur, its cruel towers scraping a blackened sky, and a hooded figure extends the dagger toward her, its dark blade glistening with promise. "Take it," the voice hisses, seductive and sharp. "The power is yours to command." Her fingers brush the hilt — and recoil, haunted by the memory of iron on her wrists. "You have no power over me," she snarls, and the dream shatters like glass.

She woke trembling in the cold, her hand closed white-knuckled around the dagger's hilt. She hurled it into the shadows. But it would return. It always did.2

One night a sound that was not the Ent's slow tread brought her alert — a snap, the shuffle of heavy feet on dry leaves. She crept to the edge of her shelter and saw torches flickering between the trees. Orcs — four at least, hunters, fanning out with brutal purpose, their torchlight raking the hollows. Running was impossible with her leg; they were too close.

Then the groaning began. The ground beneath the orcs shuddered, small at first, then deepened to a low resonant creak full of malice. One snarled and raised its blade — and thick roots erupted from the earth and coiled about its legs and dragged it screaming into the dark. The others scattered, hacking at branches that clawed for them with deadly precision, at bark that seemed to twist into glaring faces. One stumbled back into a rotting log, and the log snapped open into jagged bark-lined jaws, and his torch guttered out in the wet earth. Then silence. When it lifted, the clearing was still, and the orcs were simply gone — no bodies, no dropped blades, only disturbed earth and faint trails of crushed leaves.

Celenneth understood the truth of it then, watching. The forest had not defended her. It had defended itself, because the orcs did not belong. There would be no alliance with this ancient place — only a shared survival, hers a season's, Fangorn's an age's. It would never fight for men or elves or any mortal cause; but it would fight for itself, with an implacable and patient strength.

More weeks passed, and her strength returned in slow, painful increments, until her leg — scarred and stiff, but whole — could bear her weight along the paths she had come to know. She mapped Fangorn as best she could, learned its best foraging and avoided the places that felt wrong, and felt always beneath it the wood's old, patient, unyielding purpose. One night she whispered into the dark, "You may never trust me. But I know now you will never fall." And for the first time, the woods fell utterly still, as though listening.

The cursed dagger stayed at her side, a burden she could not be rid of; each time she flung it into a shadowed glade she woke with its cold weight against her hip again, as though the forest meant it to leave with her.

By midsummer she stood at the forest's edge, a sturdier ash staff in her hand, her camp dismantled and its traces erased from a hunter's habit. Behind her Fangorn stood dark and silent and watchful. "You were never my friend," she said. "But you let me live." A breeze stirred faintly through the leaves — acknowledgment, perhaps. Perhaps not. She wrapped her cloak close and turned toward the open plains of Rohan. Home was far, but no longer unreachable.

11Riding with the Rohirrim

Cermië 1, 2966

The plains of Rohan opened wide and golden before her, a mercy after the shadowed press of Fangorn. Every step through the open grass felt like a blessing, the cool wind carrying earth and wildflower. Celenneth moved slow, favoring the leg, her supplies meager and the ache in it a constant reminder — but the thought of Edoras, of friendly faces and rest, drew her on.

Riders came into view on the far horizon, dark shapes against a low sun. Her hand went to her sword out of habit — until she made out the green and gold of Rohan on their banners. She stood tall as the patrol slowed and approached, and a broad-shouldered rider at their head raised a hand to halt them.

"You there," a woman's voice called, firm but not unkind. "Who are you, so far from any road?"

"Celenneth of the Dúnedain. I've traveled far, and have need of rest. I seek Edoras."

The riders traded glances. Then the one who had called swung down and came to her — tall and lean, her dark hair braided tight, keen blue eyes that took in Celenneth's every worn detail. "Garin, rider of the Mark," she said. "You've the look of someone dragged through the fires of Mordor. What happened to you?"

"A long tale," Celenneth said, a faint smile touching her lips. "One I'd gladly share over a fire. For now, I'd be grateful for your aid."

Garin studied her a moment more, then nodded and gestured to her horse, a sturdy bay mare. "You'll ride with me. Come — I'll help you up." Celenneth hesitated, unused to leaning on anyone, but the pain in her leg humbled her, and she let Garin hand her up into the saddle, wincing. Garin swung up behind her and took the reins, and the patrol resumed, closing loosely around them.

"Lucky we found you," Garin said after a while. "This country looks peaceful, but it's been restless of late — orcs, wolves, more than we've seen in years."

"I've seen it myself," Celenneth said, her eyes on the horizon. "The Shadow stirs, and the world is ill at ease."

Garin was quiet a moment. "We'll get you to Edoras."1

Riding with the Rohirrim was a strange comfort after so long alone. The patrol moved with practiced ease, but their days were never simple — scouting a ridge for orc-sign, carrying word to scattered farmsteads, reading the aftermath of a wolf-raid. Celenneth kept mostly to herself, riding before Garin on her bay, but Garin seemed set on drawing her out. She had a quick wit and a ready laugh, and she told stories — of her family, her years in the saddle, the old lore of the Mark.

"You know," she said one evening at their fire, "the horses of Rohan are the finest in Middle-earth. Better even than Gondor's."

Celenneth glanced up from restringing her bow. "I've seen them outrun storms and ford rivers as if it were nothing. I'll not argue."

Garin grinned and leaned in. "And their riders? Do they not impress you as well?"

"Your riders are skilled," Celenneth said, thoughtful. "Strong, loyal, brave. It's plain why Rohan holds them so dear."

Garin's smile turned sly. "And this rider in particular? Does she meet your high standards?"

Celenneth tilted her head, puzzled. "I'm not sure what you mean."

Garin laughed and shook her head. "Sharp as an arrow when it comes to danger, Celenneth — but blunt as a practice-sword when someone pays you a compliment."

"A compliment?"

"Never mind," she said, still laughing, teasing. "I'll have to try harder, it seems."

Over the days that followed her easy warmth went on — a hand offered as Celenneth dismounted, an extra portion of food pressed on her, a stray strand of hair brushed from her face with a casual familiarity. Celenneth remained politely oblivious, her mind on the road and her mending leg. The other riders noticed, trading knowing looks and half-hidden smiles, but none said a word.

The patrol's errands slowed their progress, and Celenneth did not mind; the rhythm of it — the scouting, the shared meals, the camaraderie — steadied her after Fangorn and the captivity before it, and the constant motion strengthened her leg for all the ache. One evening, as the sun went gold across the plains, Garin rode beside her in a rare quiet, her usual play gone from her voice. "You've come a long way," she said. "Where will you go, once we reach Edoras?"

The question caught Celenneth off guard. "I don't know," she admitted. "Rest there a while. I never stay anywhere more than a season."

Garin nodded slowly. "Well — wherever you go, I hope you'll remember Rohan fondly. And perhaps its people."

Celenneth gave her a small, genuine smile. "I already do."

The lights of Edoras came into view after a week and more of travel, the Golden Hall glimmering atop its hill. Relief washed through her at the sight. Garin reined in and looked at her with a wry smile. "Welcome to Edoras. Try not to get lost in all the excitement."

Celenneth laughed softly, the warmth in her chest unfamiliar but welcome. "Thank you, Garin. For everything."

And the riders began their descent toward hearth and home — and for Celenneth, a step closer to healing, and to whatever waited beyond it.

12Home Is Who You Have

Cermië 11, 2966

The patrol rode in through the gates of Edoras to a city alive with welcome, townsfolk waving from windows and doorways. Garin swung down and turned to help Celenneth from the saddle, her hands steady at Celenneth's arms until her feet found the ground.

"Come," Garin said, giving her shoulder a friendly squeeze. "You've earned a place at our feast tonight. Let us show you how Rohan honors its own."

Celenneth hesitated, her thoughts already pulling toward Linnea and Brynja — but the smell of roasting meat and spiced mead was hard to refuse, and Garin's eyes held an easy encouragement. "Briefly," she allowed. "Then I need to find someone, to let them know I've come back." And she limped after Garin into the Golden Hall.

Inside, the feast was in full swing — laughter and song, long tables groaning under bread and cheese and roasted meat. Garin brought her to a place near the central fire and pressed a cup of mead into her hand. "Try to enjoy yourself," she said, teasing. "Whatever the wild threw at you is behind you now."

Celenneth managed a laugh, and her shoulders eased as she drank — and then, across the hall, her gaze caught on a figure she knew. Linnea stood near the far wall, Brynja on her hip, the baby's small fist curled in the collar of her dress. Linnea's face was pale, her eyes wide and fixed on Celenneth. The cup nearly slipped from Celenneth's fingers.

"I have to go," she said, setting it down.

Garin blinked. "What—"

"My family," Celenneth murmured, thick with feeling. "I need to see them." She was already moving, and did not see the way Garin's warm expression faltered as her gaze followed Celenneth's to Linnea and the child, nor the quiet understanding that settled over her. "Go, then," Garin said softly, to her back.

Linnea stood frozen, watching Celenneth cross the hall. She had not let herself believe she would see her again. For months she had half-convinced herself that Celenneth was dead, her heart breaking a little more with every silent week. And here she was — alive, whole, walking toward her with that same determined stride, favoring one leg.

And yet Linnea's eyes cut to the rider Celenneth had just left, the way the woman had touched her shoulder, the closeness of them. Relief and joy and a hot thread of jealousy tangled in her chest, and her hand tightened on Brynja.

"Linnea," Celenneth said as she reached them, quiet with wonder. Her gaze dropped to the baby, and softened, and she sank carefully to a knee. "And you. Look at you."

Brynja regarded her with a baby's solemn, uncertain stare — four months was a long time in a life so short, and there was no knowing in the small face, only wariness at a stranger. Something in Celenneth's chest cracked at that. She held out a hand, slow, and let the child decide; and after a long moment Brynja's fingers closed around one of hers, and a bubbling, wet sound that was almost a laugh broke out of her, and she reached to fist a hand in Celenneth's hair. Celenneth shut her eyes and pressed her brow gently to the baby's, her own tears slipping loose. "I'm here," she whispered. "I'm here."

When she rose, Linnea's eyes were wet too — but the anger she had held so long broke its banks all the same.

"Where have you been?" Her voice shook.

"It's a long story—"

"No. Not this time. No stories, no 'I'll explain later.' You were gone for months, Celenneth. We thought you were dead."

"I never meant to be gone so long," Celenneth said. "There was no way to send word. I tried—"

"Tried? Did you ever once think we needed to know you were alive? I waited. I thought—" Her voice cracked. "You left us."

"I never meant to. I was captured. I escaped. I—" Celenneth reached for her.

But something past Celenneth's shoulder drew Linnea's eye: the rider, lingering at the edge of the firelight, tall and watchful. Her stomach twisted. "Who is that?"

Celenneth glanced back, caught off guard. "Garin. She helped me, after I crossed the plains."

Linnea's eyes narrowed. "And what was she to you?"

"A friend. A rider of the Mark."

"A friend." Linnea's voice dropped. "She's watched you this whole time. She looks at you like a woman whose heart is breaking."

Celenneth's brow furrowed, and she opened her mouth to answer — and faltered. Her mind ran back over things she had waved away: the lingering glances, the ready hand, the teasing, the way Garin had never quite let her out of her sight. Had she?

"No," she said, quiet but firm. "It wasn't like that."

"Maybe you didn't see it," Linnea said, arms crossed, trembling with anger and something deeper. "But she did."

Memories surfaced unbidden — Garin's hand at her waist as she dismounted, her fingers brushing her hair back, her laughter chasing off the shadows of Fangorn. Celenneth's breath caught. Garin had cared, and she had never noticed.

"I didn't see it," she admitted, stunned. "Because I was too set on getting back to you."

Linnea stared at her, raw and open. "You didn't see it?"

"Because my heart was here." Celenneth held her gaze. "It was always here. With you."

The fight drained from Linnea's shoulders, and left something more fragile behind. For a moment neither moved — and then Linnea surged forward and wrapped her free arm around Celenneth, fierce and desperate, Brynja squashed happily between them. "I thought I'd lost you."

"You never did," Celenneth said into her hair, holding them both, her tears falling silent. "I'm home."

When the storm had passed and only the trembling relief was left, Linnea looked across the hall to where Garin still stood by the hearth, and squeezed Celenneth's hand. "Introduce us."

Surprise crossed Celenneth's face, but she nodded and guided them through the crowd. Garin straightened as they neared, her shoulders squared as if for a reckoning, and her sharp gaze gentled as it took in Linnea and the baby clinging to Celenneth's side.

"Garin," Celenneth said, steady and warm. "There's someone I'd have you meet. This is Linnea — my oathsworn." The words carried a quiet pride, a plain statement of where she belonged. "And our daughter, Brynja."

Linnea inclined her head, cautious but civil. "It's good to meet you."

Garin bowed her head. "Linnea. Brynja." Her tone was measured, and genuine. "Seeing you makes it far plainer why she fought so hard to come home." A brief quiet stretched between them — neither hostile nor guarded, only weighted with an understanding that needed no words.

"I'm grateful," Linnea said, softer now. "For helping her back to us."

"It was an honor," Garin said, and hesitated only a moment. "And a privilege." The meaning was clear enough — but there was no possessiveness in it, only respect, and Linnea met her eyes steadily and found the reassurance she had needed. Whatever had lived in Garin's heart, it belonged to the past now, and she laid it down with grace.

Celenneth offered Linnea a small, knowing smile. "We should get home."

Garin put out her hand, a last gesture of closure. "Safe roads, wherever they take you. And good fortune."

Celenneth clasped it firmly. "Thank you. For everything."1

They stepped out of the Golden Hall into the cool night, and Linnea leaned into Celenneth's side — no longer out of wariness, but trust. "I believe you," she said quietly.

Celenneth let out a slow breath. "Thank you."

Brynja had gone heavy and drowsy against Linnea's shoulder, one small hand still tangled loosely in Celenneth's sleeve, unwilling even in sleep to let the returned stranger go. Celenneth gathered her up despite the ache in her leg, and the child settled against her chest with a soft, contented sound. "Let's go home, little star," she murmured, and the three of them walked out together into the quiet, the warm light of the hall fading behind them.
