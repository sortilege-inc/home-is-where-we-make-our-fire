1Morning Sickness

Lótessë 12, 2970

The cool of the spring morning lingered in the air, reluctant to give way, though the days had lengthened and the last of the season's chill was burning off with the sun. Through the small windows of their home the pale light fell in soft bars across the wooden walls, warming the room by slow degrees. Outside, dew still silvered the grass, and the green of new growth had crept up to the very threshold.

Inside, Celenneth sat hunched over a wooden bucket, her auburn hair sticking to her damp forehead as a fresh wave of nausea gripped her. She breathed deeply, trying to steady herself, though her stomach twisted in protest. The moments dragged, each one longer than the last, and she clutched the bucket's rim with trembling hands and waited for it to pass.

Linnea entered quietly, her step familiar and grounding. She knelt beside Celenneth, her dark eyes soft with concern, and reached out to brush a strand of hair from her face. "How bad is it this morning?"

Celenneth grimaced, swallowing hard as she leaned back. "Not my worst," she managed, her voice hoarse but steady. "But it's not how I'd have chosen to start the day."

Linnea's mouth curved, and her touch lingered on Celenneth's cheek before she rose and crossed to the hearth. A small pot of water already steamed there, and she shaved the root into it with the ease of long practice—the same root she had taught Celenneth to use.

"This will settle it. You know the way of it by now—chew it slow, let it work."

While the root softened in the hot water, Celenneth drew the bucket a little closer, only in case. Her other hand settled without thought over the low curve of her belly, over the new life she was still learning to carry. She closed her eyes and breathed until Linnea came back with the cup.

"Drink. Slowly, or you'll only be back at the bucket."

Celenneth huffed a weak laugh and took it. "I'd sooner not." She sipped, and the warmth eased her raw throat; the sharp taste of the root lingered on her tongue, but she had grown used to it—a small price for the relief that followed.

Linnea sat beside her, watching. "You're bearing this better than you think, you know."

Celenneth arched a brow at her. "It doesn't feel like bearing anything well, with my head in a bucket."

Linnea laughed, low and warm. "Even so. You're finding your way through it. I've never known you to back down from a thing—not even this."

The words drew a tired smile from her. She set the empty cup aside and leaned back against the wall, her hand returning to her belly. "It's a strange thing," she said. "I've faced wolves, and orcs, and worse than either. This feels heavier than any of them. Bigger. As though I'm carrying something that matters more than I do."

Linnea's face gentled. She reached over and covered Celenneth's hand with her own. "You are, love. But you're not carrying it alone. I'm here—for all of it."

They sat a while in the quiet, the fire ticking in the hearth, the house waking slowly around them. The scent of herbs and woodsmoke, the steady warmth of Linnea at her side—it wrapped around Celenneth like a poultice against the weight she carried.

After a time she let out a long breath, and the tension went out of her shoulders. "Thank you. For thinking of the root, for putting up with my grumbling. For being you."

Linnea squeezed her hand. "Always. You're my heart. And soon enough you'll have someone else who calls you theirs."

Celenneth's gaze dropped to her belly, and her lips twitched. She could feel it now, if she was still enough—the faint stirrings of a life not yet her own to hold. A daunting thought. But sitting here in the quiet strength of Linnea's presence, something else rose to meet the fear and steady it: hope.

"We'll be all right, won't we?" she asked, barely above a whisper.

Linnea leaned her head against Celenneth's shoulder. "We will. We always find a way. And this little one? They're fortunate already—to have you."

They stayed like that as the morning unfolded around them, the fire warming the room and the spring light climbing the wall. Whatever waited ahead, they would meet it together. And in that moment, in the quiet hum of their own home, Celenneth let herself believe it.

2The Fractured Blade

Nárië 28, 2970

The forge was alive with heat and hammer-fall when Celenneth came to it, the low swell of her belly shifting her balance so that she walked with a new care. Eadric looked up from his anvil, hammer stilled mid-swing, his broad shoulders framed by the red glow of the coals behind him.

"Now there's a sight—Celenneth at the smithy," he said, a lopsided grin creasing his soot-streaked face as he set the hammer down. "And I'd wager it's not a horseshoe you're after."

She smiled faintly and unwrapped the broken blade from the cloth she carried. "No horseshoes today, Eadric. This was given to me—by a man who was as a father to me, with the last strength he had, before he died." The words still cost her. "Brandor. One of the two rangers who raised me."

Eadric's grin faded into something more careful. He took the blade from her and tilted it into the light, turning it slowly, and his brow furrowed as he read the steel. "This isn't only old. It's ancient. Númenórean work, no mistaking it—look at these etchings, the way they catch the light. This is an heirloom of a lost age." He glanced up. "Fractured, though. Clean through. What is it you want of me?"

"I've done what reading I could," Celenneth said, her voice low with something near reverence. "I believe it was forged to guard the bloodlines of Arnor—the wardens of the old kingdom carried its like." She looked at the broken edge. "Can you make it whole again?"

Eadric ran a thumb along the fracture, and his expression darkened. "I can shape steel as well as the next smith. But this—it isn't only the metal, Celenneth. There's something in the making of it I'd not trust myself to keep. If it's to be done, and done right, I'll want help."

"Help?"

He nodded and set the blade down with care. "There's a woman I apprenticed under, years past. Niril—one of your folk, of the Dúnedain. If anyone living knows how to mend a thing like this without driving the spirit out of it, it's her. I'll send word. But understand—it won't be quick, and it won't be light."

Celenneth's hand had drifted to the hilt of the sword at her own hip—the keen blade that had carried her faithfully through more years and roads than she cared to count. Her green eyes settled, and she drew it and turned it once in her hands before she held it out to him.

"This isn't payment," she said. "It's a gift given for a gift. You've done fine work for me before, and this blade has served me well and long. Let it serve you now, while the other one waits for its second life."

Eadric took it with plain surprise, running his hand down the honed edge. "You don't need to do this. This blade's as sharp as the day it left the forge."

"I know." She said it simply. "But it belongs to my past. The other belongs to what comes after—to my family, and theirs. It's yours now."

He held the sword the way a man holds something he means to be worthy of. "You've a way of making a smith feel he's been handed a trust," he said at last. "I'll do right by you, Celenneth. And I'll see this broken thing isn't broken much longer."1

3Jealousy

Cermië 18, 2970

A low fire burned in the hearth against the cool of the summer night, filling the small cabin with its steady glow. Rain whispered against the windowpane, soft and unhurried, tracing crooked paths down the glass. Inside, the warmth wrapped around them—the furs, the quiet, the nearness—in something that felt almost outside of time.

Celenneth lay on her side, the round of her belly a gentle rise beneath the blankets, deep in sleep, her breaths slow and even. Her auburn hair spilled across the pillow like banked coals. Behind her lay Linnea, her hand resting light on Celenneth's stomach, feeling now and then the faint flutter of the life growing within.

The scene should have been perfect. For a moment it very nearly was. But something turned over uneasily in Linnea's chest, a quiet ache she had not expected and could not name. Her fingers curled inward against Celenneth's belly, uncertain of their place, and the firelight caught the tension gathering in her brow.

It was not Celenneth who had put it there, nor the child—she loved them both past any words she owned. It was the thought of the thing she could not share. The thing she would never feel.

She was happy. She told herself so, fiercely, trying to still the whirl in her chest. She was. So why did it still hurt?

The quiet of the night pressed close, but it did nothing to calm her. Her gaze kept returning to the swell beneath her hand, and the pang came again, sharp and unwelcome. She had always wanted this—to carry a child of her own, to feel it move under her heart. But her life had been too uncertain for it, too full of the road and its dangers, and now, as Celenneth carried their child, Linnea could not help the feeling that she had been left standing on the far side of a door.

The baby shifted faintly beneath her palm, and her breath caught. She swallowed and smoothed a hand through Celenneth's hair, but even that touch felt hesitant, as though she had forgotten how to give comfort she could not feel herself.

Celenneth stirred, her brow creasing, and turned to face her. Her green eyes opened, hazy with sleep, and cleared into concern the moment they found Linnea's face. "Linnea? What's wrong?"

Linnea hesitated. The words would not come loose. Her eyes strayed to the fire. "I don't know how to say it," she murmured, and her voice shook.

Celenneth's fingers brushed her arm. "Then try, love. I'm here."

Linnea closed her eyes a moment, gathering herself, and made herself meet Celenneth's gaze. "I'm happy for you. For us. Truly, I am." Her voice broke on the turn. "But I want this too. I want to carry a child. I want to feel what you're feeling. And watching you like this—as much as I love it—it hurts. I feel jealous. And I hate that I do."

The words hung heavy between them. Celenneth did not flinch from them. Her face softened instead, and she lifted a hand to cup Linnea's cheek, her thumb catching a tear before it fell.

"You don't have to hate it," she said quietly. "It's honest. And it changes nothing—not how much I love you, not what you are to me."

Linnea leaned into the touch, her eyes brimming. "But I'm not the one carrying this child. You're becoming a mother, and I feel like I'm standing outside a thing I've wanted my whole life."

Celenneth's brow drew down. Her hand slid to cover Linnea's where it lay on her belly. "You're not outside anything. Linnea—you're already a mother. To Brynja, in a thousand ways every day. This child grows in me, but they'll be yours just as surely as they're mine."

"It isn't the same," Linnea whispered. "I want to feel them move inside me. To know what it's like. I feel like I'm missing something I can never have."

Celenneth leaned forward until her brow rested against Linnea's. "I won't pretend I understand all of it, and I'm sorry that it aches. But hear me: it's because of you that this child will be born into a house full of love, and strength enough to weather anything. And I need you. Not beside this—in it."

Linnea let her eyes fall closed and let the words move through her. Slowly the ache eased—not gone, but gentled. "I only want to be enough," she said.

"You're more than enough," Celenneth answered, and there was no softness in it now, only certainty. "You're everything. You've been with me through battles and storms, through every joy and every grief I've had. We're in this together. We always have been."

Linnea's mouth trembled toward a smile, and her hand settled once more on Celenneth's belly. The faintest movement met her palm, and her heart rose to it, joy and longing braided together past untangling. "I love you," she said, and meant it to the bone.

"And I love you." Celenneth pressed a soft kiss to her lips.

They stayed like that, folded into each other while the fire ticked low and the rain whispered against the glass. The ache of Linnea's longing had not wholly gone; but it lay quiet now, warmed through by the love that held her, and by the promise of the life they were building, together.

4The Reforged Blade

Narquelië 10, 2970

The weeks turned, and Celenneth's belly grew heavy with the child. Then came the day Eadric's word reached her—urgent, and eager beneath it—and she went to the forge with Linnea at her side, her gait slow now but no less certain. Beside Eadric stood a Dúnedain woman: tall, weathered, iron-grey hair bound back in a plain braid. Niril, the master-smith whose name was passed in half-whispers through the wilds. Her hands were old, but the strength of the craft still lived in them, and her eyes held the sharpness of one who knew the weight of what she made.

"You carry a blade out of an elder age," Niril said, her voice like steel wrapped in silk, "and one worth the honoring. It was forged to shield the line of Arnor's kings, and there is a magic in it wrought by the old hands of Númenor. Making it whole again was no light task."

Eadric stepped forward and held it out. Where the steel had been broken it now ran unbroken, a clean line of Númenórean work, the runes along its length glowing faintly in the forge-light. The hilt had been remade—rich leather, silver at the guard—and its balance sat true in a way only a master could reach.

"Much of the shaping was Eadric's," Niril said, meeting Celenneth's eyes. "The heart of it—the making beneath the making—I saw kept whole. It will serve you as it once served kings. Wield it with purpose, Ranger. Its long years in shadow are ended."

Eadric laid the blade in Celenneth's hands, and it settled there with a weight both familiar and somehow more awake than steel had any right to be. The runes along the edge seemed to stir, whispering a promise she felt rather than heard.

"Its edge bites hardest at whatever dwells in shadow," Niril went on. "It will part the malice of corrupted men and the cold stillness of the dead alike. And it is balanced to your hand—one, or both, as the moment asks of you."

Celenneth raised it, the forge's glow sliding along the steel and catching in her green eyes. "It's a gift I won't squander. You have my thanks—both of you."

Niril inclined her head, and something in her face eased a fraction. "It was no gift, Ranger. It was a duty. Such work is not ours to hoard—it is ours to pass on, that it might be wielded by hands that will honor it. And honor it, you will."

Celenneth sheathed the blade with care, its weight a quiet comfort against her hip, and turned to Eadric, offering her hand. He clasped it firmly.

"You've given me back a piece of my people," she said. "My family."

He smiled, faint and true. "And you'll pass it down, one day. May it keep you—and all you love—when the need comes."

With that she stepped out, Linnea falling in beside her, her hand brushing Celenneth's arm as they went into the cool autumn air. "A kingly blade," Linnea murmured, "remade for a queen." She meant it lightly, but pride ran under it.

Celenneth's mouth curved, her gaze steady on the road ahead. "And one day, perhaps, for our princess. Or for this one."1

5Kicks

Narquelië 28, 2970

The cabin was warm against the autumn dusk, the fire burned low and steady in the hearth, and its flames threw soft, shifting shadows across the wooden beams of their home in the Angle. Woodsmoke and dried herbs hung in the air, and the wind whispered at the windows, and the whole room felt drawn in close around its own quiet.

Celenneth sat on the edge of the bed, a thick blanket over her legs, her hands resting on the curve of her belly. Her auburn hair fell loose about her shoulders as she looked down, a small smile at her lips. The carrying had changed her in ways she had not foreseen—her movements slower, her strength gone tender at the edges. And now, as the life within her grew stronger, every kick and turn drew her nearer to the child she was aching to meet.

Small feet padded on the floorboards, and she looked up to find Brynja hesitating in the doorway, dark curls framing her round, watchful face, her brown eyes wide with the plain wonder of a child of four.

"Mama," Brynja said softly, tilting her head, "what are you doing?"

Celenneth smiled and patted the bed beside her. "Only resting, love. Come and sit with me."

Brynja's face lit, and she scrambled across the room and climbed up, her gaze going at once to the round of Celenneth's belly, her small hands twitching with the want of touching it.

"Is the baby kicking?" she asked, awe and eagerness tangled together.

Celenneth nodded, laying a hand on her stomach. "They are. Would you like to feel?"

Brynja gasped, her mouth falling open. "Yes—yes, please!"

Gently Celenneth took the small hand and guided it to the place where the movements came strongest. "Hold it here, little star. And wait. Be patient."

For a moment they sat in stillness, the fire's crackle the only sound. Then a small, sudden kick pressed up against Celenneth's belly, and Brynja gasped loud, her whole face alight.

"Mama! I felt it! I felt the baby kick!" She kept her hand pressed to the spot, as though she might catch it again. "They're really in there. They're moving."

Celenneth laughed low, watching the joy spill out of her daughter unchecked. "Yes. Stronger every day."

Brynja leaned in close, fingers splayed wide. "It's like a little tap. Like when I throw my ball at the wall, but softer. How do they do that?"

"They're stretching," Celenneth said, brushing a curl back from her forehead. "Testing their little arms and legs. Getting ready to meet you."

Brynja's eyes went round, and her voice dropped to a whisper. "I'm going to be the best big sister. I'll teach them all my games, and show them where the best berries grow. And I'll make sure they're never scared at night."

Celenneth's heart pulled tight, and she bent to press a kiss to Brynja's head. "They're going to love you, little one. And so do I."

Brynja nestled closer and laid her cheek against the curve, as if to listen for the baby herself. "I can't wait to meet them, Mama. I'll tell them stories when they come out—just like you tell me."

Celenneth stroked her hair, her smile soft and full. "I think they'll love your stories. They'll be lucky to have you."

The room seemed to still around them, the firelight lying gentle over mother and daughter, Brynja's small hand resting where it lay, her cheek pressed to the swell as though she could reach the brother or sister she had not yet met. And in that quiet, with the fire low and Brynja warm against her, Celenneth felt something she had not quite let herself feel before: ready.

6The Lore-Keeper

Hísimë 14, 2970

The road to the loremaster's door was no road at all by its end. It had begun plainly enough, a track through the thinning autumn woods, but with every mile it grew fainter, swallowed by leaf-fall and reaching bramble, until Celenneth was reading the way by old signs rather than by any path—a cairn gone green, a blaze grown over on a beech. A day's ride and more it took them, Celenneth riding careful with the weight she carried and Linnea keeping easy watch at her flank, and the deeper they went the quieter the forest grew, until the cottage rose out of a glade so hidden it might have kept itself secret on purpose. Smoke curled from a crooked chimney. The place felt untouched—a small pocket of the world the years had passed over.

They knocked, and the door creaked open on an old woman with sharp grey eyes that missed nothing and silver hair braided long and loose. A heavy woolen shawl hung from her narrow shoulders. Her look held a faint suspicion until it fell on Celenneth's cloak and the way she stood.

"Well, there's no mistaking you for aught but one of our own, Ranger. Come in—you look burdened. Let's see if we can't lighten it."

From behind her a man's voice rasped out, low and gravelly, muffled by the walls. "Another of your scroll-collectors, Almedra? Tell them I want more tea."

"Hush, Berthor. You've had tea enough for three men." Almedra shook her head, a faint smile tugging at her, and waved them in.

Inside, the cottage was warm and close and crowded. Shelves lined every wall, bowed under scrolls and manuscripts and aged tomes, their spines faded, their edges soft with handling. A small table by the hearth held an inkpot and stacked parchment, half-covered in a tight, precise hand. At the far end sat Berthor—a tall man gone frail, his large hands idle in his lap, reclined in a chair heaped with blankets. His blue eyes, weary as they were, tracked the strangers with care.

"We've found something," Celenneth began, setting down her pack. "Relics of the old world. Númenórean, we believe—out of the ice, from a ruin that has forgotten its own name. But we don't understand all of what we carry."

She drew them out and laid them across the table: a silver-bound book, a rolled scroll, and a strange dark shard. Almedra's breath went sharp, and she leaned close, her fingers trembling over the silver bindings.

"Stars above. Where did you come by these?"

"In a place best left buried," Linnea said quietly, and the reverence in her voice was shot through with unease.

"And you'd know what they are." Almedra's tone dropped low, her eyes fixed on the table. "I've not laid eyes on the like since I was a girl—not in the flesh. Give me the night. My library will have answers, if I ask it rightly."

Celenneth settled into a chair while Linnea set water for tea from the herbs they carried, and Berthor offered up dry, grumbling commentary from his blankets, though Linnea's wit soon had him half-charmed despite himself. The night wore on to the rustle of parchment and the scrape of a quill. Now and then Almedra muttered to herself, pulling books from precarious stacks, squinting at runes by candlelight. Linnea dozed by the fire. Celenneth watched the old woman's shadow move steady across the wall, and found something steadying in it—a quiet, patient strength, bent to its work.

As grey dawn began to seep through the shutters, Almedra emerged from her books weary and triumphant, an open volume in her arms with loose pages tucked between the leaves.

"You've brought me relics of a forgotten age, Celenneth. Listen well."

She laid a hand on the silver-bound book first, her voice gone reverent. "This is the lorebook of Arnenir—one of Númenor's great loremasters. He set down not only their journeys into Eriador but their secrets: hidden paths, buried ruins, knowledge meant for his own kin. His legacy lives in these pages. Read it well, and it will guide you when every other map has failed."

She set it aside with care and turned to the scroll, tracing its runes with a fingertip, whispering as she read. "This is a ward. Old magic—practical, not lofty. These marks were carved to turn back corruption and intrusion. Used wisely, they'll shield a place, or a person, for a while at least."

Last she took up the shard, narrowing her eyes as she tilted it to the light. "And this—this is part of something greater. A key, yes. But a seal, too—made to lock a thing away, or to guide one to places of power. Its work is unfinished. But the magic in it holds."

Celenneth listened, the weight of the things on the table growing heavier as she came to understand them. At last she rose and reached for the silver-bound book, lingering over its bindings, and turned back to Almedra.

"This should stay here. With you, and with Berthor. You've earned it—and you'll keep it as it was meant to be kept."

Almedra blinked, caught off her guard. "Celenneth—you can't mean that. It's a priceless thing—"

"And it belongs with one who can preserve it, and pass its wisdom to those who'll need it. I'll carry away what I've learned. But its home is here."

For the first time the old woman looked unsteady. "You honor us," she said softly. "Truly. It will be cared for. I swear it."

From his chair, Berthor cleared his throat loudly. "Well. If nothing else, it'll keep her busy. I've run clean out of ways to entertain her."

Linnea laughed and shouldered her satchel. "Then let it keep the pair of you out of trouble a while."

They stepped out into the morning, and the forest was loud with birdsong, and the weight they carried felt somehow lighter for the leaving of the book. Linnea's fingers strayed to the shard-key where it hung at her belt. "Are you certain? Leaving it behind?"

Celenneth nodded, her gaze on the trees ahead. "Its knowledge is safe, and that's what matters. What we carry now will serve us—and those who come after us."1

7Appreciation

Hísimë 20, 2970

Outside, the wind came off the autumn hills and worried at the canvas of the tent like some low, hungry thing. Inside there was only warmth—a small, defiant warmth built of shared breath and slow hands.

Celenneth lay stretched beneath the thick furs, her back cushioned on the pelts they had layered against the cold ground. The brazier's dim glow moved over her bare skin, catching the gentle swell of her belly, the curves she had not yet learned to see as anything but strange. Her hands rested at her sides, fingers half-curled, caught between wanting to relax and wanting to cover herself.

Linnea knelt beside her, dark hair spilling in soft waves over her shoulders, her sharp eyes tracing every line of her—not weighing, not measuring, only looking, as at something worth the looking.

Celenneth's breath caught. Her gaze slid down to where the pregnancy had stretched her, unfamiliar and unyielding, and her arms crossed loosely over her middle before she had decided to move them at all. "I'm different now," she said, low, the words rough with a vulnerability she rarely let show.

Linnea caught her wrists, gently, and held them. "You are." She bent and brushed her lips across Celenneth's knuckles, holding her gaze. "You're more."

Whatever protest Celenneth might have made was swallowed as Linnea drew her hands away and pressed a slow kiss to the inside of her wrist, her warm breath chasing off the chill of self-consciousness.

"You've always been strong," Linnea murmured, trailing her mouth up the length of Celenneth's forearm, over the faint pale scars written there—the leavings of old battles fought and outlived. "Every mark. Every line. This is the story of your surviving."

Celenneth's chest rose sharply. Her eyes stung. "But I don't look the same," she whispered. "Not like before."

Linnea went still, her dark eyes lifting, fierce and quiet. "Good." Her voice was low and certain. "I don't want before. I want you. All of you."

Before Celenneth could answer she dipped lower and set her lips to the curve of her shoulder, her fingers drawing slow reverent paths down her sides, pausing where the fuller weight of her breasts began—heavier now, softer than she remembered them.

"You're beautiful here," Linnea said, framing her ribs with careful hands. "You hold life here. How could that be anything but sacred?"

Celenneth's breath shuddered, and her hands slowly uncurled from where they had guarded her. She reached for Linnea instead, threading her fingers into the dark hair, anchoring herself in its familiar silk-weight.

Linnea pressed her kisses lower, down the line of her, her mouth lingering a moment over the beat of her heart. "This strength isn't only yours anymore," she whispered. "It's ours."

There was no hurry in her, no urgency—only patient, deliberate tenderness. She went on down, her lips grazing the soft rise of Celenneth's belly, and there she paused, resting her cheek against the warm swell as if listening for something only she could hear.

"You carry the future here," she said, her voice breaking on it. "A future we made together—even if it came to us through them." She pressed a kiss to the curve, infinitely careful. "I love you."

Celenneth choked on a soft, disbelieving laugh, her hands trembling on Linnea's shoulders. "Even now?"

Linnea rose just enough to meet her eyes. "Especially now."

With unhurried patience she drew Celenneth's hands from their guarding, laced their fingers, and pressed them gently to the pelts beside her head. Her mouth traced the dip of Celenneth's hip, her breath warm against the sensitive skin.

"This body has carried you through war and wild country," she said softly, her kisses moving lower. "It's fought, and endured, and outlasted all of it. And now it's making something new."

Celenneth's breath caught again—but not from fear this time, nor doubt. From the sheer force of being seen. No part of her hidden, no imperfection passed over or waved away; every touch, every whispered word, said the same thing: you are worth this. Not the fighter. Not the shield. Only herself.

"Let me show you how much I love all of you," Linnea murmured against her skin, and Celenneth reached for her with no hesitation left in her at all—only trust, and want, and love.

Linnea kissed lower, along the curve of her thigh, her fingers following in slow strokes that left no part of her untouched. Celenneth's breath fractured as those hands cradled her hips with a steady, knowing strength; there was no haste in it, only certainty.
