1Morning Sickness

The chill of winter lingered in the air, clinging stubbornly despite the lengthening days. Through the small windows of their home, the pale morning light cast soft patterns on the wooden walls, illuminating the warmth and care within. Outside, frost still dusted the earth, though it would melt with the promise of spring’s approach.

Inside, Celenneth sat hunched over a wooden bucket, her auburn hair sticking to her damp forehead as a fresh wave of nausea gripped her. She breathed deeply, trying to steady herself, though her stomach twisted in protest. The moments passed slowly, each one dragging as she clutched the edge of the bucket with trembling hands.

Linnea entered quietly, her soft footfalls familiar and grounding. She knelt beside Celenneth, her dark eyes filled with gentle concern as she reached out to brush a strand of hair from her partner’s face. “How bad is it this morning?” she asked softly.

Celenneth grimaced, swallowing hard as she leaned back from the bucket. “Not my worst,” she replied, her voice hoarse but steady. “But it’s not exactly how I planned to start the day.”

Linnea smiled faintly, her touch lingering on Celenneth’s cheek before she stood and moved to the hearth. A small pot of water was already steaming there, and she carefully added the root she had taught Celenneth to use.

“This will help. You know the drill—chew it slow, let it settle.” Linnea spoke with calm reassurance, the words of someone who had already thought of every step.

As the root softened in the hot water, Celenneth pulled the bucket closer again, just in case. Her other hand rested instinctively on the curve of her belly, the weight of the new life within her a constant reminder of the changes she was navigating. She closed her eyes, breathing deeply, as Linnea returned with the root-infused water.

“Drink this. Not too fast, though, or you’ll just end up back at the bucket.”

Celenneth chuckled weakly, taking the offered cup. “I’d rather avoid that if I can.” She sipped carefully, the warm liquid soothing her raw throat. The sharp taste of the softened root lingered, but she had grown accustomed to it—it was a small price to pay for the relief it brought.

Linnea sat beside her, watching closely. “You’re handling this better than you think, you know.”

Celenneth looked up at her, arching a brow. “It doesn’t feel like it when I’m stuck with my head in a bucket.”

Linnea laughed softly, the sound light and warm. “Even so. You’re adapting. I’ve never known you to back down from anything—not even this.”

The words brought a small, tired smile to Celenneth’s face. She placed the empty cup aside and leaned back against the wall, her hand returning to her belly. “It’s strange… I’ve faced wolves, orcs, even worse, but this feels… heavier. Bigger. Like I’m carrying the weight of something more important than myself.”

Linnea’s expression softened as she reached out, covering Celenneth’s hand with her own. “You are, love. But you’re not carrying it alone. I’ll be here—for all of it.”

They sat together in silence for a moment, the fire crackling softly in the hearth. Outside, the world stirred with the quiet hum of the morning. The warmth of the house, the scent of herbs and firewood, and the steady presence of Linnea all wrapped around Celenneth like a balm against the weight she carried.

After a time, Celenneth sighed deeply, the tension in her shoulders easing. “Thank you, Linnea. For… everything. For thinking of the root, for dealing with my grumbling, for just… being you.”

Linnea squeezed her hand gently, her dark eyes shining with affection. “Always, Celenneth. You’re my heart. And soon, you’ll have someone else calling you theirs.”

Celenneth’s lips twitched into a soft smile, her gaze dropping to her belly. She could feel the stirrings of life within, faint but undeniable. It was a daunting thought, but as she sat there, held in the quiet strength of Linnea’s presence, she felt something else bloom—hope.

“We’ll be all right, won’t we?” Celenneth asked quietly, her voice barely above a whisper.

Linnea leaned her head against Celenneth’s shoulder, her voice calm and steady. “We will. We always find a way. And this little one? They’re lucky already—to have you.”

The two women stayed like that, the fire warming the room as the day slowly unfolded. Whatever challenges lay ahead, they would face them together. And in that moment, surrounded by the quiet hum of their home, Celenneth allowed herself to believe it.

2The Fractured Blade

The forge was alive with heat and movement when Celenneth arrived, her rounded belly shifting her balance slightly as she walked. Eadric looked up from his anvil, the hammer in his hand still mid-swing, his broad shoulders framed by the soft glow of the coals behind him.

“It’s a surprise to see you at the blacksmith’s, Celenneth,” Eadric said with a lopsided grin, setting his hammer down. “And I’d wager it’s not for a new horseshoe.”

Celenneth smiled faintly, unwrapping the broken Númenórean blade from the cloth bundle she carried. “No horseshoes today, Eadric. I found this on the road—well, ‘found’ is the wrong word. It was given to me.”

Eadric’s curiosity deepened as he took the blade from her, tilting it into the light. His brow furrowed as he examined the steel, turning it over carefully. “This isn’t just old—it’s ancient. The craftsmanship is Númenórean for sure. Look at these etchings... they practically hum. It looks like an heirloom piece. Where did you say you got this?”

“A long story,” Celenneth replied, her voice tinged with reverence. “I received it someone who was like a father to me, shortly before he died. I did some research, and I believe it’s Nurhael’s Warden Blade—meant to guard the bloodlines of Arnor. But it’s fractured. Can you make it whole again?”

Eadric leaned closer, running a thumb along the fracture. His expression darkened slightly. “I can shape steel as well as the next smith, but reforging this… it’s not just about the metal, Celenneth. It’s about the spirit of the blade. If I’m going to do it right, I’ll need help.”

Celenneth tilted her head. “Help?”

Eadric nodded and set the blade down gently. “There’s a woman I apprenticed under. Niril. She’s one of your folk—the Dúnedain. If anyone knows how to mend this while keeping its magic intact, it’s her. I’ll send for her. But know this: it won’t be quick work, and it won’t be light work.”

Celenneth nodded, her hand instinctively resting on the hilt of her own sword—the keen blade that had served her faithfully. Her green eyes flickered with decision, and she pulled the blade free from its scabbard, turning it in her hands before extending it to Eadric.

“This isn’t payment. It’s a gift-in-exchange. You’ve done fine work for me before, and this blade has carried me far. It will serve you well while this one—” she nodded toward the broken Númenórean blade “—waits for its new life.”

Eadric took the blade with a look of surprise, running his hand down its finely honed edge. “You don’t need to do this, Celenneth. This blade’s as sharp as the day it was forged.”

“I know,” she replied simply. “But this one belongs to my past. The Warden Blade is for my future—for my family’s future. It’s yours now.”

Eadric nodded slowly, his roughened hands holding the sword as if it were a treasure. “You’ve got a way of making a smith feel honored, you know that? I’ll do right by you, Celenneth. And I’ll see that this broken blade isn’t broken for long.”

3Jealousy

The fire crackled softly in the hearth, filling the small cabin with its comforting glow. Snow whispered against the windowpane, its delicate patterns etched on the glass. Inside, warmth enveloped the space, the cozy furs and quiet murmurs of home wrapping Celenneth and Linnea in an embrace that felt almost timeless.

Linnea lay close behind her, her hand resting lightly on Celenneth’s stomach, feeling the faint movements of the life growing within.

The scene should have been perfect, and for a moment, it almost was. But something stirred uneasily within Linnea, a quiet ache that she hadn’t expected. She shifted slightly, her hand pausing on Celenneth’s belly, her fingers curling inwards as if uncertain of their place. The firelight flickered across her face, highlighting the tension in her furrowed brow.

It wasn’t Celenneth or the child that caused this ache—she loved them both with a depth she could scarcely put into words. It was the thought of what she couldn’t share, what she couldn’t experience.

I’m happy, she thought fiercely, trying to steady the whirlwind in her chest. I am. But why does this still hurt?

The quiet of the night pressed around her, but it did nothing to calm the storm. Her gaze fell to Celenneth’s belly, and the pang of jealousy flared again, sharp and unwelcome. She had always wanted to be a mother, to carry a child of her own, but life had been too uncertain, too full of danger and distraction. Now, as Celenneth carried their child, Linnea couldn’t help but feel left behind.

The baby moved faintly beneath her hand, and Linnea’s breath hitched. She swallowed hard, her fingers brushing through Celenneth’s hair, but the touch felt distant—hesitant, as though she didn’t know how to soothe the ache rising in her heart.

Celenneth stirred, her brow creasing as she shifted to face Linnea. Her green eyes fluttered open, still hazy with sleep, but as they focused on Linnea, they filled with concern. “Linnea? What’s wrong?”

Linnea hesitated, unsure how to give voice to her feelings. Her gaze flickered to the fire, the words catching in her throat. “I… I don’t know how to explain it,” she murmured, her voice trembling with emotion.

Celenneth reached out, her fingers brushing lightly over Linnea’s arm. “Then try, love. I’m here.”

The words hung heavy in the air, but Celenneth didn’t flinch. Instead, her expression softened, and she cupped Linnea’s face gently, her thumb brushing away a tear that had escaped.

“You don’t have to hate it, Linnea,” she said softly. “It’s honest. And it doesn’t change how much I love you, or how much you mean to me.”

Linnea leaned into Celenneth’s touch, her eyes brimming with unspoken emotion. “But I’m not the one carrying this child. You’re becoming a mother, and I’m… I don’t know. I feel like I’m on the outside of something I’ve always wanted.”

Celenneth frowned slightly, her hand slipping down to cover Linnea’s. “You’re not on the outside. Linnea, you’re already a mother—to Brynja, in a thousand ays. This child may be growing inside me, but they will be yours just as much as they are mine.”

Celenneth’s brow creased, and she leaned forward, pressing her forehead gently against Linnea’s. “I won’t pretend to understand completely, and I’m sorry it feels like you are missing out. But know this; it is because of you that this child will be born into a home filled with love and strength. And I need you.”

Linnea closed her eyes, her breath trembling as she let Celenneth’s words wash over her. Slowly, the ache in her chest began to ease, though it didn’t fully vanish. “I just want to be enough,” she whispered.

“You’re more than enough,” Celenneth replied, her voice firm. “You’re everything. You’ve been with me through battles and storms, through every joy and sorrow. We’re in this together, Linnea.”

Linnea’s lips trembled into a small smile, her hand returning to rest on Celenneth’s belly. She felt the faintest movement beneath her palm, and her heart swelled with a mixture of joy and quiet longing. “I love you,” she said, her voice filled with certainty.

“And I love you,” Celenneth whispered, pressing a soft kiss to Linnea’s lips.

They stayed like that, wrapped in each other’s arms as the fire crackled and the snow whispered against the window. The ache of Linnea’s longing remained, but it was tempered by the warmth of Celenneth’s love and the promise of the life they were building together.

4The Reforged Blade

Weeks passed, and Celenneth’s belly grew heavy with her child. The day came when Eadric sent for her, his message urgent but eager. She arrived at the forge with Linnea at her side, her gait slow but determined. Waiting beside Eadric was a Dúnedain woman—tall and weathered, with iron-gray hair tied back in a simple braid. Her name was Niril, a master smith with a reputation whispered in the wilds. Her hands, though aged, held the strength of her craft, and her eyes glinted with the sharpness of someone who knew the weight of legacy.

“You bear a blade with an old name, and one worth honoring,” Niril said, her voice like steel wrapped in silk. “Nurhael’s Warden Blade. Its purpose was to protect the line of Arnor’s kings, and it carries within it a magic wrought by the old hands of Númenor. Reforging it was no easy task.”

Eadric stepped forward, holding out the blade. Where it had once been fractured, it now shone with new life—a perfect line of Númenórean craftsmanship, its runes glowing faintly in the forge light. The hilt was reforged, wrapped with rich leather and silver inlays, its balance true in a way only a master could accomplish.

Niril continued, meeting Celenneth’s eyes. “Eadric did much of the shaping, but the magic, the heart of it, I ensured would remain. It will serve you well, as it once served the line of kings. Wield it with purpose, Ranger, for its time in shadow has ended.”

Eadric handed Celenneth the blade, its weight familiar but somehow more alive in her hands. The runes along the edge seemed to shimmer, whispering their silent promise of strength.

“Its edge will strike hardest against those who dwell in shadow,” Niril explained. “It will pierce through the evil of men and the unnatural stillness of the dead. And should you need it, its balance will allow you to wield it as you see fit—one hand or two.”

Celenneth raised the blade, the soft glow of the forge reflecting in her green eyes. “It is a gift I will not squander. You have my thanks. Both of you.”

Niril inclined her head, her expression softening ever so slightly. “It was not done as a gift, Ranger. It was done as a duty. Such works are not ours to hoard—they are ours to pass on, to see wielded by those who will honor them. And honor it, you will.”

Celenneth sheathed the blade with care, its weight a comfort against her hip. She turned to Eadric, offering her hand, which he clasped firmly.

“You’ve given me back a piece of my people. My family.”

Eadric smiled faintly. “And you’ll pass it down, one day. May it protect you—and all those you love—when the time comes.”

With that, Celenneth stepped away, Linnea falling into step beside her, her hand brushing Celenneth’s arm. “A kingly blade, remade for a queen,” Linnea teased softly, though her voice was filled with pride.

Celenneth smiled, her gaze steady as they stepped into the cool afternoon air. “And someday, perhaps for our princess, or this one.”

5Kicks

The cabin was warm, filled with the steady crackle of the fire that burned low in the hearth. Flames danced in a soothing rhythm, casting soft shadows across the wooden beams of their home in the Angles. The scents of woodsmoke and herbs lingered in the air, wrapping the room in a cocoon of peace as the wind whispered faintly against the windows.

Celenneth sat on the edge of the bed, a thick blanket draped over her legs and her hands resting lightly on the curve of her belly. Her auburn hair fell loosely over her shoulders as she gazed down, a soft smile playing at her lips. The pregnancy had changed her in ways she hadn’t anticipated—her movements slower, her strength tempered with a new tenderness. And now, as the life within her grew stronger, each kick and flutter brought her closer to the little one she longed to meet.

A small sound of footsteps on the wooden floor drew her attention, and she looked up to see Brynja, standing hesitantly in the doorway. Her dark curls framed her round, curious face, and her brown eyes shone with the innocent wonder of childhood.

“Mama,” Brynja said softly, tilting her head, “what are you doing?”

Celenneth smiled and held out her hand, patting the space beside her. “Just resting, love. Why don’t you come sit with me?”

Brynja’s face brightened, her excitement spilling into her wide grin as she padded across the room and climbed onto the bed. Her gaze was immediately drawn to Celenneth’s rounded belly, her small hands twitching in anticipation.

“Is the baby kicking?” Brynja asked, her voice a mix of awe and excitement.

Celenneth nodded, resting a hand on her stomach. “They are. Would you like to feel?”

Brynja gasped, her mouth falling open in delight. “Yes, yes! Please!”

Gently, Celenneth took Brynja’s small hand and guided it to her belly, pressing it just above where she felt the faint movements. “Hold it here, little star, and wait. Be patient.”

For a moment, they sat in stillness, the soft crackle of the fire the only sound in the room. Then, a sudden, tiny kick pressed against Celenneth’s belly. Brynja gasped loudly, her face lighting up with pure wonder.

“Mama! I felt it! I felt the baby kick!” Her voice rang with awe, her hand pressing against the spot as though she might feel it again. “They’re really in there! They’re moving!”

Celenneth chuckled softly, watching her daughter’s uncontainable joy. “Yes, they’re getting stronger every day.”

Brynja leaned in closer, her fingers splaying over Celenneth’s belly. “It’s like a little tap! Like when I throw my ball against the wall, but… softer. How do they do that?”

“They’re stretching, testing their little arms and legs,” Celenneth explained, brushing a lock of curls from Brynja’s forehead. “They’re already getting ready to meet you.”

Brynja’s eyes went wide, and her voice dropped to an awed whisper. “I’m going to be the best big sister. I’ll teach them all my games, and I’ll show them where to find the best berries. And… I’ll make sure they’re never scared at night.”

Celenneth’s heart swelled, and she leaned down to press a kiss to Brynja’s head. “They’re going to love you, little one. And so do I.”

Brynja nestled closer, resting her cheek gently against Celenneth’s belly as if trying to hear the baby herself. “I can’t wait to meet them, Mama. I’ll tell them stories when they come out—just like you tell me.”

Celenneth stroked Brynja’s hair, her smile soft but full. “And I think they’ll love your stories. They’ll be so lucky to have you.”

The room seemed to grow still, the firelight casting its gentle glow over mother and daughter. Brynja’s small hand stayed on Celenneth’s belly, her cheek resting there as if she could already connect with the sibling she hadn’t yet met. In that peaceful room, with the fire crackling softly and Brynja’s warmth pressed close, Celenneth felt ready.

6The Lore-Keeper

The secluded cottage stood nestled deep in the forest glade, smoke curling lazily from its crooked chimney. The road had turned faint and overgrown long before Celenneth and Linnea reached it, but the place felt safe—a timeless haven untouched by the chaos of the world.

When they knocked, the door creaked open to reveal Almedra—an old woman with sharp gray eyes that missed little and silver hair woven into a long, loose braid. She wore a heavy woolen shawl draped over her narrow shoulders, her expression one of faint suspicion until she recognized Celenneth’s cloak and bearing.

“Well, there’s no mistaking you for anything but one of our own, ranger. Come in. You look burdened—let’s see if we can lighten it.”

Behind her, the voice of a man called out—a low, gravelly sound muffled by the walls. “Is it another of your scroll collectors, Almedra? Tell them I need more tea.”

“Hush, Berthor, you’ve had enough tea for three men.” Almedra smiled faintly, shaking her head as she gestured for Celenneth and Linnea to enter.

Inside, the cottage was warm, though crowded. Shelves lined every wall, buckling under the weight of scrolls, manuscripts, and aged tomes, their spines faded and edges worn. A small table beside the hearth held an inkpot and stacks of parchment, half-scribed with tight, precise handwriting.

At the far end of the room sat Berthor, a tall man now frail with age, his large hands resting idly in his lap as he reclined in a chair piled with blankets. His sharp blue eyes, though weary, tracked them carefully.

“We’ve found something,” Celenneth began as they set their packs down. “Relics from the old world—Númenórean, we believe. But their nature isn’t fully clear to us.”

She withdrew the silver-bound lorebook, the scroll, and the strange shard, carefully laying them across the table. Almedra’s sharp intake of breath was immediate as she leaned closer, fingers trembling over the silver bindings.

“Stars above, where did you find these?”

“In places that have forgotten their own names,” Linnea said softly, her voice carrying both reverence and unease.

“And you wish to know what they are.” Almedra’s tone was hushed, her sharp eyes fixed on the items. “I’ve not seen anything like this since I was a girl—not in the flesh. I need time. My library will have answers. If you’ll give me the night, I’ll learn what I can.”

Celenneth nodded, settling into a nearby chair while Linnea began brewing tea from the bundle of herbs they carried. Berthor offered dry, grumbling commentary from his chair, though Linnea’s wit kept him entertained as the fire burned low.

The night passed with the faint sounds of rustling parchment and the scrape of a quill as Almedra worked. At times, the old woman would mutter to herself, pulling books from precarious stacks or squinting at runes by the light of a candle. Linnea dozed near the fire while Celenneth watched Almedra’s silhouette move steadily in the dim light, her dedication a quiet, steady strength.

As the dawn began to seep through the cracks in the shutters, Almedra emerged, weary but triumphant, carrying an open book with loose papers tucked between its pages.

“You’ve brought me relics from a forgotten age, Celenneth. Listen well.”

She gestured to the silver-bound lorebook first, her voice reverent. “This is the Lorebook of Arnenir—one of Númenor’s great loremasters. He chronicled not only their expeditions into Eriador but their secrets: paths, ruins, and lost knowledge meant for his kin alone. This book carries his legacy. Use it well, and it will guide you when all other maps fail.”

Almedra carefully set the book aside, her gaze falling on the scroll. She traced the runes with her fingertips, whispering softly to herself. “This scroll is a ward. Old magic—practical, not lofty. These runes were carved to protect against corruption and intrusion. Use them wisely, and they’ll shield a location or a person, at least for a time.”

Finally, Almedra held up the shard, her eyes narrowing as she tilted it in the light. “This… this is part of something greater. A key, yes, but it’s also a seal—used to lock away or guide someone to places of power. Its work is unfinished, but it still holds its magic.”

Celenneth listened intently, the weight of the relics growing heavier with understanding. Finally, she stood and reached for the Lorebook of Arnenir, lingering over its silver bindings before turning back to Almedra.

“This book should stay here, with you and Berthor. You’ve earned it, and you’ll care for it as it was meant to be cared for.”

Almedra blinked, clearly caught off guard. “Celenneth, you can’t mean that. It’s a priceless treasure—”

“And it belongs with someone who can preserve it, who can share its wisdom with those who will need it. I’ll take what I’ve learned, but its home is here.”

For the first time, Almedra looked unsteady, her voice soft as she murmured, “You honor us, Celenneth. Truly. It will be cared for, I swear it.”

Berthor, from his chair, cleared his throat loudly. “Well, if nothing else, it’ll keep her busy. I’ve run out of ways to entertain her.”

Linnea laughed, shaking her head as she shouldered her satchel. “Then let this keep you both out of trouble for a while.”

As they stepped out into the morning light, the forest was alive with birdsong, and the weight of the journey felt somehow lighter. Linnea glanced at Celenneth, her fingers brushing the Frostward Key where it hung from her belt.

“Are you certain about leaving the book?”

Celenneth nodded, her gaze on the road ahead. “Its knowledge is safe, and that’s what matters. What we carry now will serve us—and those who come after.”
