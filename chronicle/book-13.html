1Eryndil, The Elf

The months drifted by in gentle rhythm, the world beyond their cabin shifting from winter’s hush to spring’s quiet awakening. Within the Angles, the small home Celenneth and Linnea had built was filled with the laughter and cries of their children—Brynja, vibrant and curious, and the infant Hallas, who was beginning to discover his voice. Afternoons brought the scent of warming earth and the comfort of routine: Linnea knelt in the garden, coaxing seedlings from the thawed soil; Celenneth sharpened tools and tended to repairs; Brynja helped with the baby in a manner only a child with a heart as large as hers could—singing silly songs to him or holding his tiny hand with solemn pride. Life moved calmly, and in the gentle hush of this green place, it seemed they were shielded from the world’s greater tumults.

But peace rarely endures unchanged, and fate often arrives unannounced.

It was early morning when Celenneth first saw the figure on the horizon. She had been finishing her tea by the window while Linnea stepped outside to hang fresh linens. Beyond the budding branches, a single traveler approached with measured steps. Tall and lithe, the figure wore a cloak of deep blue, the fabric catching the sunlight in subtle ripples—like waves upon a distant shore.

Celenneth’s heart gave a curious flutter as she recognized the stranger’s bearing. This was no wanderer of mortal lands. The figure’s movements were fluid and assured, steeped in an elegance that spoke of centuries rather than decades. The elf—Celenneth knew it at once—was no woodland spirit but one of Lindon, a people shaped by western seas and long memories. Beneath the hood, as the visitor drew nearer, Celenneth glimpsed sea-grey eyes filled with calm intensity and silver-streaked hair adorned with tiny shell-like beads that shimmered softly.

She could hardly breathe as Linnea stepped back inside, wiping her hands on a towel, her brow lifting in silent query.

“Linnea,” Celenneth said, voice hushed, “someone comes—a Lindon Elf, I believe. Not from these parts. She carries herself as one who has journeyed far. I think she seeks me.”

Linnea drew close to the window, her expression thoughtful but unafraid. They both knew Celenneth’s past held more than the simple story of a ranger settling down. Linnea’s hand brushed Celenneth’s gently, a quiet reassurance. “Shall we meet her?”

Celenneth nodded, slipping her sword belt around her waist, more out of habit than hostility. “Yes. Let’s see what she brings.”

Together, they stepped out into the warming day, Brynja following behind, Linnea balancing baby Hallas on her hip. As they approached, the elf halted gracefully. Removing her hood, she revealed a face sharp yet compassionate, timeless and strong. Her eyes were the grey of a distant storm at sea, reflecting depths and horizons Celenneth could not yet fathom. Her long hair, silver-streaked, hinted at memories of western coasts. She wore fitted leather armor etched with patterned sweeps, and at her collar shone a silver clasp shaped like a star, a subtle herald of her origins.

“I am Eryndil of Lindon,” the elf said, her voice steady and resonant, reminiscent of far-off tides. “I come bearing counsel from Elrond of Rivendell. I traveled east to seek his wisdom, and he, in turn, sent me here.” She paused, her gaze resting upon Celenneth. “He spoke of you, Celenneth—of your bloodline and the part you might play in a matter of great import. There is a relic I carry, a legacy of Númenor. It must be wielded—or understood—by one who bears the heritage of that lost land. Elrond believes you are the one who can help me unlock its truth.”

From beneath her cloak of blue, Eryndil drew forth an object: a piece of adamant carved with intricate symbols, each line faintly glowing with a soft, oceanic light. Its aura filled the space between them, solemn and undeniable.

Celenneth’s breath caught, and Linnea’s hand found hers. Brynja stood quietly now, eyes wide with wonder. Hallas cooed softly, oblivious to the gravity that settled upon them.

Eryndil’s tone was measured, yet tinged with awe. “I seek a place long lost, a site known only in half-remembered lore. It is said to lie west of these lands, hidden by time and tide. The relic may reveal its secrets—but only if one of Númenórean blood stands beside me. Elrond suggested you, Celenneth, as the best person to join me.”

Celenneth felt as though the ground beneath her had shifted. She had settled here to find peace—yet destiny, it seemed, had found her again. “I… I understand your request,” she managed, voice steady but heart heavy. “But I cannot make such a choice in haste. My family, my life, is here.” She looked at Linnea, at their children, at the home they had built with careful hands. “I must speak with my wife. We will talk—together—and then with you. I cannot decide this alone.”

Eryndil inclined her head gracefully. “I will wait by my camp, beneath the birch trees. Speak with me when you are ready.” With a final, respectful nod, she withdrew, the blue cloak whispering softly against the new grass.

Inside, the cabin seemed smaller and warmer with the weight of this decision pressing in. Night fell, and once the children were asleep—Brynja curled protectively around Hallas’s cradle—Celenneth and Linnea sat at their modest wooden table, illuminated by the glow of the hearth.

“She needs me,” Celenneth began softly, fingers entwined with Linnea’s. “But what she asks… it feels enormous. I left behind the life of a wandering ranger, the duties of blood and old histories. Here we found peace.”

Linnea’s eyes did not waver. “But the past lives in you, love. If this relic, this secret, could guide fate itself, how can we ignore it?”

Celenneth’s voice trembled. “I fear leaving, fear what lies beyond our safe walls. We have the children to think of.”

“And I fear not answering,” Linnea said gently. “I fear that by staying, we might miss something greater than ourselves. If you must walk this path, then I will walk it at your side. I will not let us be parted. The children, too. We have chosen this life together, and we will face its challenges together.”

They sat in silence, letting the crackle of the fire and the gentle breathing of their children soothe their hearts. Slowly, resolution began to settle. They would not be driven apart by fate’s call. If Celenneth was needed, then Linnea and the children were needed as well. The warmth of their bond would carry them forward.

“We’ll go,” Celenneth said finally, voice quiet but firm. “But not tomorrow, or even the next day. We must prepare, pack what we can carry, ensure the children’s comfort and safety. We must close this chapter of our life here with care.”

Linnea nodded, relief and determination mingling in her eyes. “Then let’s tell Eryndil. We will need her patience, and she shall have it.”

When dawn touched the windowsill, they stepped outside into the cool, dew-kissed morning. Eryndil stood amidst the birch trees as promised, her bearing patient and unyielding as the tides. She turned as they approached, the soft light catching on the shell-beads in her hair.

Celenneth met that sea-grey gaze and spoke with quiet conviction. “We will go, Eryndil. But we go as a family. Brynja, Hallas, Linnea, and I—we will not be separated. We’ll need time, however. To pack, to ready ourselves, to settle what must be settled.”

Eryndil’s calm demeanor did not falter. She nodded, and in her eyes Celenneth glimpsed respect and understanding. “Then I shall wait, and help where I may. The journey is long, but shared burdens grow lighter. When you are prepared, we will follow the path that leads westward, toward the mystery only you can unlock.”

And so, the quiet life they had known began to shift. Dawn stretched its fingers over the Angles, and in its gentle light, Celenneth and Linnea set forth on the first steps of a new journey—one guided not merely by fate or relics, but by their bond as a family and the certainty that whatever came next, they would face it together.

Inside the cabin, daylight slanted across the wooden floorboards as preparations for the journey began. Brynja amused Hallas near the hearth, playing soft clapping games and singing nonsense rhymes that made the infant grin and gurgle. Linnea moved through their home with quiet purpose—sorting what must be packed and what would stay behind, folding linens, and bundling herbs. Meanwhile, Celenneth organized their gear—her ranger’s cloak rolled with care, their provisions portioned for travel.

Eryndil had been invited to stay inside their home, an offer she accepted with the gracious inclination of her head. She stood near a small window, hands folded before her, observing the family’s gentle bustle. The warmth of this house, the harmony between Celenneth and Linnea, and the soft hum of children’s voices seemed to settle something in Eryndil’s gaze.

As the afternoon lengthened, and while Hallas dozed in his cradle, the elf spoke quietly of news beyond their peaceful refuge. Her voice was calm and resonant, as though shaped by distant waves, yet now it carried the weight of tidings from distant lands.

“I bring word from Rivendell,” she began, her gaze touching Celenneth, then Linnea. “Before I left, Elrond’s halls were filled with whispers of trouble in the south. Dol Guldur—dark and ancient—had stirred again. For a time, the shadow deepened. Orcs and foul things crept through Mirkwood, and the Elves of Thranduil’s realm struggled to contain them. For a season it seemed the tide would not turn.”

Linnea paused, cradling a pot of dried herbs. Celenneth came closer, arms folded, listening with solemn attention.

“But a coalition was formed,” Eryndil continued, her words steady as a ship’s prow cutting through uncertain seas. “Men from the West and the Elves of the Woodland Realm joined forces. Allies came forth from unexpected quarters, each lending sword and skill. Slowly, by courage and cunning, they pushed the enemy back. The front was forced southwards, deeper into Mirkwood’s shadows.”

A tiny line etched between Celenneth’s brows. “So the threat… is it lessened?”

Eryndil inclined her head. “Not ended. Dol Guldur remains a wound upon the forest’s heart, and the darkness there does not yield easily. Yet the allies have bought precious time and space. The worst of the assault has been stilled, if not fully driven off. In Rivendell, some even spoke of a cautious hope.”

Linnea let out a breath. “Good. It is something, at least.”

In the quiet that followed, Celenneth stepped back to the table, the relic from Eryndil tucked in a leather pouch nearby. The homey clink of utensils and shifting of bundles seemed at odds with the tidings of war and darkness, but this was the nature of their world—beauty and love existing alongside hardship and strife.

Eryndil’s eyes drifted to Brynja, who was gently rocking Hallas. A flicker of a smile graced the elf’s features. “Your family’s unity and care—it is a gift. Such things give strength beyond what blades can bring. I believe what we embark on together may hold some key, some truth to be uncovered, that can help guide these troubled times.”

Celenneth nodded slowly, her heart heavier with each piece of news. Yet here, in their home, surrounded by warmth and comfort, there was resolve as well. They would follow this path—and though they ventured into uncertainties and distant lands, they did so with eyes open, hearts steady, and bonds unbroken.

Outside, the light began to fade. Still, inside the cabin, careful preparations continued. Each item they packed was chosen with care, each whispered plan tempered by caution and hope. And in the hush that followed Eryndil’s solemn news, the quiet strength of this family’s choice settled more firmly into place.

As the family’s packing neared completion, Celenneth realized there were still questions lingering around the relic Eryndil bore. Its faintly glowing symbols—so unlike any craft Celenneth or Linnea had encountered—needed clearer interpretation, a grounding in human memory and local knowledge. Only one person nearby held such knowledge: Almedra, the lore-keeper of their region. Though elderly and reclusive, Almedra’s understanding ran deep, preserving fragments of history and wisdom long thought lost.

It was late afternoon when Celenneth, Linnea, and Eryndil set out, weaving through the birch and hawthorn until they found the modest home. The children stayed behind with a trusted neighbor, allowing the trio to travel without hurry. Almedra’s cottage clung to a gentle hillock near the river, sheltered by willow trees that draped their branches like green curtains. Smoke curled from the chimney, a thin line of grey against the pale spring sky.

The air hummed softly when Eryndil stepped forward, her presence quiet yet unmistakably otherworldly. Celenneth had spoken of Almedra to Eryndil: a kind but reserved woman, recently widowed, who carried the weight of old tales and unfading sorrows. Celenneth rapped gently on the door, then pushed it open with care.

Inside, Almedra’s home was warm but subdued. Dust motes danced in the slanting light. Shelves of old tomes and scrolls lined the walls, along with dried herbs and bundles of pressed flowers. A quilt of intricate patchwork covered a bench, and against one wall stood a spinning wheel still draped with a half-spun thread.

Almedra rose from her chair, leaning on a carved walking stick. Thin lines traced her face, and her eyes looked older than her years. Since her husband’s passing in the heart of winter, she had been quiet and withdrawn. Yet she greeted Celenneth and Linnea with a soft nod, her gaze immediately flicking to the tall, silver-braided stranger standing at their side.

Celenneth made introductions: “Almedra, this is Eryndil of Lindon, an elf who bears a relic of Númenor’s making. We seek your lore, if you can spare it.” She spoke gently, as one approaching a shy animal.

Almedra’s eyes widened. Though she had read of elves and their deeds in old manuscripts, never had she met one face-to-face. The presence of Eryndil—her timeless bearing, her subtle grace, the faint glimmer of shells braided into her hair—seemed to fill the small room with quiet resonance. For a moment, Almedra’s grief was eclipsed by wonder.

“You are… an elf,” Almedra managed, voice hushed. It was not a question but a statement laden with awe.

Eryndil inclined her head. “I am indeed, lore-keeper. I know this must be strange to you, as it is a solemn honor to meet one whose memory guards the tapestry of mortal lives. You have my condolences for your loss,” she added softly, sensing the recent wound in Almedra’s heart.

Almedra’s lips pressed together, and she nodded. “He was a good man,” she said of her husband, voice trembling slightly. “He passed as the snows were deepest, and I have carried on alone. But we all carry something, don’t we?”

Celenneth stepped forward, easing the conversation. She gently reached into her pouch and drew forth the relic of adamant, careful not to let its subtle glow startle the lore-keeper. “We hoped you might have insight,” she said. “Elrond of Rivendell believes my heritage might help unlock its secrets. Eryndil found her way here because of that counsel.”

Almedra’s brows rose as she leaned in, fascination overcoming her reserve. Under the lamplight, the relic revealed lines and swirls etched into its surface—patterns reminiscent of waves, stars, and ships. Carefully, Almedra traced the contours with her eyes, her fingers hovering just short of touching it. She could feel an almost gentle warmth radiating from it.

“Adamant,” she murmured. “Harder than diamonds, rarer in these parts. The craft reminds me of stories—the old stories of Númenor. Ships crossing wide seas… gifts of friendship and alliances. My husband and I collected accounts of such things, though they were fragmentary.” Her voice steadied as she delved into familiar territory: lore and words she knew well.

Linnea and Celenneth exchanged a hopeful glance. Eryndil stood still as a drawn bowstring, intent on Almedra’s every word.

“It’s been said,” Almedra continued slowly, “that in certain relics, a memory is held—like a seed that needs particular soil to sprout. If this requires Númenórean blood, then perhaps it can be awakened by the presence and will of someone like Celenneth. You might need to recite certain passages, or stand in a place resonant with old powers. Without my husband, I lack some pieces of the puzzle, but I recall references…” Her face fell slightly, sadness returning as she remembered her late partner’s careful handwriting, now forever stilled.

Eryndil’s voice was gentle: “Anything you can share will guide us. We travel west, toward lands that echo with old legacies. Even fragments can lead to greater understanding.”

Almedra nodded and, after a moment’s hesitation, reached for a slender, leather-bound notebook. Its spine was worn from years of careful perusal. She flipped through pages filled with tidy script and delicate sketches. “Here,” she said, stopping at a set of verses written in a delicate hand. “My husband copied these lines from an old source—an account hinting at a hidden site in the West. It might help guide you. The words suggest a threshold—perhaps a place where land and sea meet, where something long overlooked can be revealed under starlight.”

She looked up, eyes shining with the memory of shared discoveries. “This, I give you freely, for old friendship’s sake—and for the hope that your quest might bring light to dark places.”

Celenneth offered a solemn bow of gratitude. Linnea took Almedra’s hand briefly, conveying comfort and thanks without words. Eryndil inclined her head, the shells in her hair catching the lamplight, shimmering like distant shores.

“Your kindness will not be forgotten, lore-keeper,” the elf said softly. “Your words may shape the path ahead.”

As they took their leave, Almedra stood by her open door, staff in hand. The quiet dusk settled around them. Before Celenneth, Linnea, and Eryndil departed into the twilight, Almedra called out softly: “May your journey bring meaning, and your return bring peace.”

Eryndil looked back, offering a gentle smile. Their footsteps then carried them away, guided now by new fragments of lore and the memory of a kind woman who had never expected to meet an elf—but in doing so had helped shape a fate that might reverberate well beyond their quiet corner of the world.

2A Stop in Bree

Bree’s market hummed with energy—shouts of merchants hawking fresh vegetables, the laughter of kids weaving through the stalls, the smell of warm bread and damp earth mingling in the spring air. Linnea tried to move through it all without drawing attention, but that was nearly impossible. Beside her, Celenneth held baby Hallas, and Eryndil, an elf of serene composure, trailed close behind. Little Brynja clung to Linnea’s hand, her eyes darting left and right, taking in every curious stare.

It didn’t take long before some familiar faces took note. Evanor—one of Linnea’s cousins—spotted them first, nearly spilling her basket of apples. Her teen siblings, Pelle and Mareth, fell silent beside her, gawking openly at the unexpected reunion.

“Linnea?” Evanor called, voice louder than she probably meant. Heads turned their way. “You’ve come back.” Her eyes drifted over Celenneth, the elven ranger Eryndil, and of course the baby. It was a lot to take in, and the look on Evanor’s face said as much.

Pelle leaned over and whispered, not exactly discreetly, “She’s got an elf with her.” Mareth, bolder, jabbed a finger toward Hallas. “And a baby. What’s going on?”

Evanor cleared her throat and tried to sound authoritative. “You sure know how to make an entrance, Linnea. Where’ve you been? And who’s the little one?”

Before Linnea could so much as answer, the crowd around them thickened. Folks recognized her, muttering and craning their necks to catch a glimpse of the odd little troop. Soon, Tharen and Jocelyn—more cousins—showed up, drawn by all the buzz. Tharen looked intrigued, maybe even a bit amused, while Jocelyn’s gaze was sharp and skeptical.

“Well, isn’t this a surprise,” Tharen said with a grin that almost looked friendly. “Linnea, back in Bree with a baby and an entourage. Got some explaining to do, don’t you think?”

The questions began to fly, fast and pointed:

“Where’ve you been all this time?” demanded Evanor. “Who’s the father?” snarked Tharen, eyeing Eryndil. “Don’t tell me it’s the elf.” Jocelyn sneered, “You really thought you’d just roll in here, say nothing, and that we’d be fine with it?”

Brynja clutched at Linnea’s hand, looking up with worried eyes. “Mama,” she whispered, “why are they so mad?” Her little voice cut right through Linnea’s chest. This was supposed to be home, but it sure didn’t feel like it.

Celenneth stepped forward, her voice calm and steady, rising above the chatter. “The child bears his father’s name,” she said simply. “Hallas. That’s all you need to know.” The quiet firmness in her tone actually shut Tharen up for a moment, though Jocelyn’s eyes narrowed.

“Hallas?” Jocelyn repeated, tone dripping with insinuation. “As in that Ranger? So you’ve been playing house with more rangers?”

It might have gone on like that for a while, if not for Eryndil’s mere presence. The elf’s silence, the gentle tilt of her chin, made some folks shuffle back nervously. Even the bolder ones felt the weight of that calm gaze.

Then Merla and Harvin—older relatives who’d never hidden their disapproval of Linnea’s life choices—pushed through the onlookers. Merla fixed Linnea with a stern look. “What’s the meaning of this? Strolling into Bree with strangers and a baby, causing a scene in the middle of the market?”

Harvin crossed his arms, scowling. “You’ve got a lot to explain, girl.”

Linnea took a deep breath. She’d planned to be civil, maybe even kind. She wanted to show them she’d grown, that she was happy and safe. So she tried, voice measured and even, “Merla, Harvin, everyone—please. I know this looks strange. But I came back to let you know that I’m alright. Yes, I have a child, and yes, Celenneth and Eryndil travel with me. But that doesn’t change who I am…”

She trailed off as Jocelyn scoffed loudly, and Merla’s mouth pulled into a tight line. “You think waltzing in here after months of silence, with an elf and a ranger’s baby, is supposed to make us feel better? We’re the ones left behind with questions and rumors, Linnea. You’ve embarrassed us before—now this? What will folks think?”

Any hope of smoothing this over popped like a soap bubble. Linnea’s hand clenched at her side. She could feel Brynja trembling slightly. The sound of her family’s snide remarks, their horrified whispers, it all boiled in her chest. The courtesy she’d tried to muster failed spectacularly. Something inside her snapped.

“What they think?” Linnea’s voice rose, fury and hurt warring inside her. “Since when is that more important than your own blood? Is your pride worth more than my happiness?” She jabbed a finger toward Hallas, safe in Celenneth’s arms. “This is my son. He’s named after a good man—better than you give him credit for! And Celenneth here, she’s more family to me than you lot have ever been!”

Merla’s mouth hung open, eyes wide with shock and anger. Jocelyn curled her lip, as if about to spit some insult, but Linnea didn’t let her get a word in.

“You,” Linnea hissed, turning her glare on Jocelyn and Tharen, “always so quick to judge. I left because I couldn’t breathe in this place. I came back hoping for some shred of understanding, maybe even kindness. But that was foolish, wasn’t it?”

The crowd watched, some thrilled by the drama, others uncomfortable. Brynja tugged at Linnea’s sleeve, voice small: “Mama, please don’t be mad.” But it was too late. The dam had burst.

Jocelyn, eyes burning, lunged forward as if to slap Linnea across the face. The crowd gasped. But Jocelyn’s hand never landed. Celenneth was faster, her ranger reflexes swift and sure. She caught Jocelyn’s wrist mid-swing, her grip firm but controlled.

“No one touches my wife,” Celenneth said quietly, but every word rang clear. Jocelyn froze, shocked into silence. The hush in the market was absolute.

Linnea’s heart hammered. She reached up, resting a hand on Celenneth’s arm. “Let her go,” she said softly. “She’s not worth it.” Celenneth released Jocelyn’s wrist, and Jocelyn stumbled back, glaring but not daring another move.

Merla and Harvin tried to salvage what was left of their dignity. “Just go,” Merla said, her voice wavering. “You’ve made your point.”

Linnea gave them all one last look. “Bree isn’t home,” she said, voice firm. “Not anymore. Home is where my family is—Hallas, Brynja, Celenneth. We’re leaving, heading west to the Grey Havens. If I never set foot here again, that’s too soon.”

With that, they turned their backs on the crowd and left. The tension dropped behind them like a heavy stone. Brynja, still clinging to Linnea’s hand, looked up with worried eyes. “Mama, are we really never coming back?”

Linnea’s voice was gentler now, quieter. “Not if I can help it, love. We’ll find somewhere better.”

But before leaving Bree entirely, they sought a bit of respite at the tavern portion of the Silver Lily. Inside, lanterns cast a cozy light, and the gentle hum of talk soothed frayed nerves. They found a corner table, and Eira, approached with a curious grin.

“Look who decided to drop by, talk of the town and all,” she teased. “Bree hasn’t had this much excitement in years.”

Linnea groaned, resting her forehead in one hand. “I’m sure everyone’s talking about us.”

Eira shrugged, half-amused. “People love gossip. And an elf, a ranger’s child, and a runaway daughter returning in style? That’s a feast of chatter.”

Plates of roast and bread arrived, and they tried to eat, though the interruptions were constant. A merchant raised a mug in salute. A shy young woman asked Eryndil about the Grey Havens, and the elf’s soft-spoken answer made her nearly swoon. Someone else offered to buy them drinks, grinning ear to ear and calling Linnea “brave” and “bold.”

Brynja tugged at Linnea’s sleeve. “Why are they nice now, Mama? Weren’t they angry before?”

Linnea sighed. “Not everyone was angry, sweetheart. Some people are just curious. Some are actually supportive.” She smiled slightly, catching the eye of a man hovering near their table. He had a timid look, fiddling with his cloak’s hem. When he finally spoke, his voice was barely above a whisper.

“I just—wanted to say, I admire what you’re doing. Being open about who you are. It’s… not something I’ve managed yet.” He glanced around, as if afraid of being overheard. “What you said out there—it means a lot. Maybe someday… well, maybe I can find that courage too.”

Linnea’s heart gave a tug. She nodded, voice gentle. “I hope you do. I believe you have that capacity for courage inside you.”

The man nodded quickly, eyes shining with gratitude before he slipped back into the crowd.

As the evening wore on, the inn grew quieter. Brynja dozed off, head against Linnea’s arm, and Hallas snoozed peacefully in Celenneth’s gentle hold. Eventually, Eira said her goodbye, expressing support and a hint of regret that Linnea wouldn’t be sticking around. Linnea thanked them, genuinely touched that not everyone in Bree stood against her.

Come morning, they finally approached the gates leading out of town. The air was crisp, and the sun was just peeking over the rooftops. They had almost cleared Bree’s borders when they heard footsteps behind them. Turning, Linnea saw Pelle and Mareth, the younger cousins, hurrying up, their faces earnest and a bit red from running.

“Hold on!” Pelle called.

Linnea tensed, worried about another confrontation. But Pelle and Mareth looked more contrite than angry.

“We’re sorry about yesterday,” Mareth said, voice soft. “We didn’t handle it right. We should’ve spoken up. We should’ve supported you.”

Pelle nodded, offering a small cloth-wrapped bundle. “This is for Hallas. It’s a traveling blanket our mum made. Good luck charm, she said.”

Mareth produced a carved wooden horse for Brynja. “Thought you might like something from home. We didn’t want you leaving without something good to remember.”

Brynja’s face lit up as she took the horse, and Linnea felt tears prick at her eyes. She cleared her throat. “Thank you. It means a lot that you came all the way out here. Maybe… maybe there’s hope for this place after all.”

They exchanged a few more quiet words, then Pelle and Mareth turned back, and Linnea’s family pressed forward, heading west. As they made their way down the road, the weight lifted from Linnea’s chest. Bree would remain a memory—some bitter, some surprisingly sweet. But she was done letting it define her. Her home was right here, with Celenneth, Brynja, Hallas, and the promise of a brighter horizon ahead.

3A Shelter in a Storm

The morning after leaving Bree was still crisp with lingering dew, but a thread of warmth hinted at the coming day. Celenneth guided Nimroch at a steady pace westward, Linnea and Eryndil close behind. Brynja sat in the saddle before Linnea, bright-eyed in the early light, while baby Hallas dozed in a wrap pressed to Celenneth’s chest. The road stretched ahead in long, gentle curves, a mixture of trampled earth and loose gravel that had seen its fair share of travelers over the years.

Yet for all the easy warmth and the soft breeze, there was a tension in the air that none of them could ignore. Bree sat behind them, its gate and watchful eyes now a memory—some of those eyes had been curious, others openly hostile. Linnea could still feel the sting of harsh words from certain family members, and she exhaled softly, reminding herself not to let old resentments fester. She had her wife, her children, and the open road ahead. That was enough.

Brynja glanced up. “Mama, are the people in Bree still angry with us?”

Linnea tucked a stray strand of the girl’s hair behind her ear. “Some, maybe,” she said gently. “But not all. We also met people who were kind—or who wished us well. That’s how it is in towns, love. Some folk will judge you for who you are, and others will open their arms.”

Celenneth adjusted Hallas’s position, giving him a tiny sip from the water skin whenever he stirred. “Besides,” she added, “our path is our own to walk. Bree was… just a stop along the way.”

Eryndil said nothing, but her sea-grey eyes flicked across the horizon. The dappled mare Silme matched Nimroch’s stride easily. There was an unspoken question on the elf’s face, but she kept her thoughts to herself. She seemed to sense that Linnea and Celenneth needed a small measure of quiet after the emotional upheaval in Bree.

They passed rolling fields dotted with early-spring wildflowers—yellows, whites, and occasional bursts of pale violet. One or two passing merchants on rickety carts greeted them with polite nods. Brynja waved enthusiastically, but the travelers moved on without much conversation. Midday came and went in relative peace, the sky overhead a benign tapestry of thin clouds. The entire party felt the relief of an uneventful march after so many tensions in Bree.

As the sun dipped toward late afternoon, though, a shift came over the weather. It began subtly: a slow thickening of the clouds until they formed a heavy grey mass over the landscape. The breeze picked up, brushing against them with a faintly cooler edge. Celenneth cast a quick glance skyward, noticing how quickly the day’s light was being swallowed by darkening clouds.

Linnea, who had just passed a slice of dried apple to Brynja, frowned. “Storm, do you think?”

“It looks that way,” Celenneth replied, her tone calm but purposeful. “We should look for a suitable place to camp—or better yet, some kind of shelter.”

The wind’s whistle became more pronounced, a low moan across the open fields. Hallas, perhaps sensing his mother’s slight tension, began to fuss softly. Celenneth soothed him with gentle pats, all the while scanning the horizon for any sign of a farmhouse, a copse of trees, or a barn. Eryndil’s elven sight was keener than any of theirs; she rode forward a short distance, half-standing in her stirrups, her gaze combing the rolling terrain.

Brynja scrunched her nose, tucking her chin against Linnea’s chest. “It’s getting colder,” she said, her voice a near-whisper. Indeed, the temperature had dropped noticeably. Heavy raindrops began to plop here and there, large enough to splash mud upon their boots before rolling off.

It wasn’t a gentle spring shower that opened upon them—it was a sudden, driving downpour, the kind that roars across the sky and churns the earth below into a mass of soupy mud. One moment they could see a half-mile ahead; the next, the world shrank to a murky veil of rain pounding the earth. The breeze turned into gusts that rattled the sparse trees and sent the horses sidestepping nervously.

“Stay close!” Celenneth called, voice raised above the roar of rainfall. Linnea had Eorlin in hand, Brynja clinging tightly. Hallas’s fussing rose to an anxious wail as the noise and damp assaulted his senses. Eryndil, though less troubled by the weather, seemed equally concerned about the children’s well-being, urging Silme alongside Eorlin to help keep the group together.

Within minutes, the once-firm path turned slick with mud. Nimroch’s hooves sank into it at awkward angles, requiring Celenneth’s careful guidance. Eorlin and Silme fared little better, the animals snorting and tossing their heads. The rain drummed against cloaks, soaked hair, and trickled into boots, carrying with it a chill that sank into their bones.

Linnea’s arms shook slightly from the effort of holding Brynja secure. The child’s face was pressed to her mother’s shoulder, tears mixing with rain as fear overcame her. “Mama, I’m cold…” she whispered.

Celenneth exchanged a worried look with Linnea. They needed shelter—and soon. If Hallas stayed exposed for too long, he would catch his death of chill. Brynja, too, was shivering, her small frame not suited for prolonged soaking.

Yet the rain was so thick that seeing more than a few dozen yards ahead seemed impossible. They pressed on, focusing on each step. The horses labored, sides flecked with foam as they tried to maintain footing. On top of that, the sky kept growing darker, not from sunset alone but from the storm clouds swirling overhead.

Then, through the murk, Celenneth glimpsed what might be a farmhouse. She caught a faint, flickering orange glow—lamplight or firelight behind a window. She reined Nimroch around, calling out in a voice that fought the wind, “There’s a house ahead!”

The relief in Linnea’s eyes was immediate. Eryndil guided Silme closer, verifying that Celenneth wasn’t just seeing a trick of the rain. No, there was definitely a small building perched a short way off the main path, with the welcoming glow of a lantern in the window.

Step by careful step, they guided the horses up a gentle incline that had turned treacherous under the rain’s onslaught. Mud caked the hooves and spattered everyone’s cloaks. Brynja coughed, burying her face in Linnea’s collar to keep the rain away. Hallas’s crying was broken by little hiccups as Celenneth tried to shelter him with her own body.

When they finally reached the house, Celenneth dismounted with swift caution, her boots squelching into the mire. She hurried to the door, rapping on it with more force than she intended. A tense heartbeat passed, then another, while the storm hammered the roof. At last, the door opened a crack. A middle-aged woman, worry etched onto her features, peered out. She saw Celenneth’s drenched state, then noticed Linnea and the children, followed by Eryndil’s slender, elven silhouette behind them.

A flash of hesitation crossed her face, but compassion outweighed caution. “Oh, my goodness,” she breathed. “Come in, come in! You’ll catch your deaths out there!”

The travelers needed no second invitation. Celenneth ushered Linnea inside first. Eryndil slipped in last, leading Silme under a small overhang to keep the horse as dry as possible. The interior warmth hit them like a welcome embrace. The woman hustled them in, urging them to set cloaks, packs, and boots near the door. She glanced over her shoulder. “Luthan! Luthan, get some blankets—and stoke the fire, quickly!”

A tall man with broad shoulders appeared from another room, expression cautious but not hostile. His eyes briefly widened at the sight of Eryndil (who moved with that uncanny elven grace even when soaked to the bone) but softened when he saw Brynja trembling in Linnea’s arms and heard Hallas’s ragged cries.

Linnea lowered Brynja to the floor. “Thank you—truly,” she managed, forcing a shivering smile. “We wouldn’t have made it much farther.”

“You’re safe here,” the woman said kindly. “I’m Martha. This is my husband, Luthan. We don’t see many travelers on a night like this, but I’m not about to turn away a family in need.”

Celenneth peeled back the soggy wrap around Hallas, checking the baby’s face. His cries were quieting, though he still fussed at the unfamiliar surroundings. “I’m Celenneth,” she said, voice tight with relief. “My wife is Linnea. These are our children, Brynja and Hallas.” She motioned toward Eryndil. “And this is Eryndil of Lindon.”

An odd hush followed her words—the presence of an elf in one’s home was no small matter. But Martha simply cleared her throat, fighting off any awkwardness. “Well, an elf is no more welcome or unwelcome than any other soul soaked to the skin,” she declared, her tone determinedly practical. “Let’s get you dried off.”

Luthan hurried to the fireplace, stoking the flames until they roared. Warmth flooded the modest front room: a simple but comfortable space with a rough-hewn table, two chairs, and a couch covered in a faded quilt. A kettle hissed on the hearth, sending curling steam into the air.

Martha bustled to fetch blankets. She draped a thick woolen one around Brynja, who gazed up with shy gratitude. Another she offered to Celenneth for Hallas. The baby’s cries softened as the heat seeped into his cold limbs, his cheeks taking on a healthier flush.

Linnea let out a shaky breath, tears of exhaustion and relief welling in her eyes. She sank down onto the couch, pulling Brynja close. “Thank you,” she whispered again, unsure how else to convey the depth of her gratitude.

Martha smiled, placing a hand on Linnea’s shoulder. “Children first. Always,” she said. “I’ll get you some stew. It’s plain fare, but warm.”

From behind, Eryndil politely cleared her throat. “Might I help?” she offered, hands folded. Though she was soaked through and must have been miserable, an almost regal composure lit her face. “I have some small experience cooking on the road.”

Luthan’s glance flicked to Martha. He shrugged, half-amused, half-intrigued. “If you want, I’ve got vegetables that need chopping. Name’s Luthan, by the way.”

Martha nodded, her tone brightening. “Yes, come along. Help me keep these extra mouths fed!” She disappeared into a tiny kitchen space, and Eryndil followed, moving with quiet grace.

Meanwhile, Celenneth rummaged in her pack for a dry cloth to dab Hallas’s face. The baby was calmer now, eyes wide and curious at the flickering firelight. Brynja, nestled in Linnea’s arms, seemed content to doze after her ordeal.

By the time stew was ladled into bowls, a new storm raged outside, thunder booming in the distance. Rain hammered the roof in a wild cacophony. Yet inside, the travelers found a small island of warmth and comfort. They huddled around the table, even Brynja perched on Linnea’s lap. Hallas, having nursed under Celenneth’s cloak, was dozing lightly in the cradle of her arm.

Over dinner, they traded simple pleasantries. Martha and Luthan had lived in this farmhouse for over twenty years, rarely hosting anyone but neighbors. Their own fields—mostly vegetables and a few goats—lay just south, but the storm had forced them indoors for the day’s remainder. They had no children, they explained, though they were godparents to at least half a dozen nieces and nephews from distant cousins.

“We’re not used to visitors,” Luthan said, a hint of apology in his tone. “Certainly never entertained an elf before. I hope we haven’t been… rude.”

Eryndil shook her head, offering a small smile. “You have shown more than kindness,” she said. “This is a welcome haven.”

Linnea, leaning back with Brynja half-asleep against her, ventured a question: “Is this kind of storm common in these parts?” She was thinking of the fierce onslaught and the treacherous mud that had nearly unseated them.

Martha pursed her lips. “Spring storms can be fierce, especially in these western border-lands. Usually not this bad so early in the season, but the weather can be capricious.” She glanced toward the window. “You’re lucky you found us when you did.”

Despite the flicker of concern for the road ahead, Celenneth felt deeply grateful. The quiet warmth of this place reminded her of the Angles, of simpler days before fate came knocking in the form of Eryndil’s relic. She listened to the soft crackle of the fire, the gentle breathing of her children, and the subdued conversation around the table. For a night, at least, they were safe.

At length, Martha stood up, collecting empty bowls. “We can set out blankets near the hearth if you’d like,” she offered. “It’s not the biggest space, but the fire’s warm enough to keep you cozy through the night.”

“That would be wonderful,” Linnea murmured. She carefully rose, adjusting Brynja so the girl didn’t wake. “Brynja’s exhausted, poor thing.”

Celenneth ran her hand over Hallas’s tiny back. She too felt the weariness tug at her—muscles sore from battling the rain and mud. “Just point us where to lay ourselves down, and we’ll be no trouble,” she said softly.

Martha insisted on helping. She and Linnea laid out a thick quilt and several smaller blankets near the fire. Eryndil watched from a few paces away, presumably unused to the domestic bustle but offering her assistance whenever asked. There was a gentleness in her eyes, a distant longing that Celenneth sometimes glimpsed: perhaps a memory of homelands far older than any mortal roof.

When at last they settled, the storm raged on outside, flashes of lightning occasionally limning the windows in ghostly light. Thunder rumbled across the hills. Yet in that snug farmhouse, the travelers felt cocooned against the elements. Martha and Luthan withdrew to their own small bedroom, but not before insisting that if the baby cried, they should wake them—“No sense dealing with a colicky infant alone,” Martha had declared.

Linnea cradled Brynja under one blanket, while Celenneth propped Hallas carefully near her chest. Eryndil, refusing an offer of the couch, spread a bedroll by the far corner, as if ready to rise at a moment’s notice. The hearth’s glow fell across their tired faces, turning them gold and soft. Brynja’s breathing steadied into a gentle rhythm. Hallas stirred only once, letting out a small whimper that Celenneth soothed with a whispered lullaby.

Drifting toward sleep, Linnea felt an odd calm envelop her. The memory of Bree’s anger, the uncertain future with Eryndil’s quest, the dread of potential dangers on the road—all were kept at bay by the simple kindness of strangers and the warmth of a shared fire. In a rare moment of ease, she allowed the sound of the rain to lull her into much-needed rest.

They woke to a hush. Though a muted grey still hung in the air, the violent storm had passed sometime in the pre-dawn hours. Droplets clung to the windowpanes like jewels, and the land outside shimmered under a watery sky. Brynja was the first child to stir, her eyes fluttering open to the sight of embers glowing faintly in the hearth. She yawned, curling against Linnea’s side.

Martha emerged soon after, hair still in a simple night braid, and immediately busied herself with a kettle of water for tea. “Morning,” she greeted softly. “If you need more rest, don’t mind me. I’m just an early riser.”

Celenneth stretched, careful not to jostle Hallas, who was still asleep on her makeshift pillow. Eryndil rose with a fluid grace that belied how little rest she might have taken. “Good morning,” the elf said, inclining her head to Martha.

Linnea rubbed her eyes, glancing around. “Is it still raining?” she asked.

“Not at all,” Martha replied, a faint smile on her lips. “But be warned, the road’ll be muddy. You might want a hearty meal in you before you set off.” She paused, then added more quietly, “And if you’d rather wait a while—stay until midday—it wouldn’t be an inconvenience.”

That generosity caught Celenneth off guard. She looked at Linnea, then Eryndil. They had a long journey ahead and dangerous errands to fulfill, yet the idea of waiting for the road to dry, giving the children a less arduous start, was tempting.

Linnea’s expression showed relief. “I wouldn’t mind. Brynja and Hallas need a bit of calm.”

Eryndil nodded. “I see no harm, if our hosts are truly willing. A safe rest could spare the children from a harsher road.”

So it was decided. They lingered through a leisurely morning. Luthan joined them eventually, rummaging through his pantry for extra ingredients to feed so many mouths. He laid out onions, a few root vegetables, and some precious strips of salted pork for a substantial stew. Eryndil offered her assistance in cutting and seasoning once more, which both intrigued and delighted Luthan, who found himself marveling at the elf’s uncommon cooking methods.

Meanwhile, Linnea took Brynja outside to look at the puddles, encouraging her not to wander too far from the farmhouse. The child hopped around the sodden yard, giggling as her reflection danced in muddy pools of water. Celenneth, Hallas cradled in one arm, helped Luthan gather a bundle of tinder from a small shed behind the house. The ground was soft and slick, but manageable now that the storm had ceased.

By midday, the sun had burned through the low cloud cover, turning the fields a brighter green. Steam rose from the drenched earth. With the children fed and Hallas gently dozing, the travelers prepared to depart. Cloaks had been hung by the hearth, drying overnight. Swords were checked for rust; arrows for dampness. Eryndil ensured Silme was well-rested and well-fed with oats from the farmhouse’s small reserve.

Finally, the company assembled outside. Martha and Luthan stood at the threshold, exchanging last words of farewell. Brynja, emboldened by a safe night’s rest, ran forward to hug Martha’s legs. The woman bent down, ruffling the girl’s hair. “You be safe,” she whispered, her tone thick with unexpected emotion.

Celenneth and Linnea exchanged glances. They had found a pocket of genuine kindness in these strangers—an unexpected gift in a world so often overshadowed by suspicion and strife. “Thank you,” Celenneth said, her voice catching slightly. “We owe you much.”

“You owe us nothing,” Luthan said firmly. “We only did what any decent folk would do.” There was a shine in his eyes that spoke of quiet pride in having sheltered them.

While Martha was distracted by Brynja’s goodbyes, Celenneth removed a small keepsake from her pouch: a Númenórean brooch she had carried for sentimental reasons, etched with swirling wave patterns. With a silent, reverent motion, she set it on the mantel just inside the door. She didn’t call attention to it—she preferred they discover it later, a small thanks beyond mere words.

With that, the party rode away from the farmhouse. The road glistened with puddles, but was passable enough. The sun was high, promising a gentler day than the one before. When Celenneth looked back once, she saw Martha and Luthan waving, the house a small beacon of warmth amid wide, rain-soaked fields.

Though the storm had tested their endurance, it had also gifted them a reminder: compassion could be found in unexpected places. The memory of hot stew, laughter, and the quiet crackle of a shared hearth would buoy them for many leagues ahead. As they continued west, hearts were lighter—even if the mud still clung to their boots.

4Briars

After a few days of travel, the land changed subtly, turning from gentle farmland to rolling slopes. The group skirted the fringes of the Barrow-downs, an ancient region known in hushed whispers for its eerie barrows and lingering shadows. The sky remained mostly clear, but a certain tension crept back into the air, as though the breeze carried faint echoes of an older darkness.

On a mild afternoon, when Brynja was singing a nonsense tune to amuse Hallas, Eryndil reined in Silme without warning. Her silvery mare tossed her head, and Eryndil lifted a hand to signal a halt. Celenneth, guiding Nimroch at the head, immediately recognized that something was amiss.

“What is it?” Linnea asked, holding Brynja steady on Eorlin.

Eryndil did not speak at first. She slid from the saddle in one smooth motion, stepping onto the grass. The elf’s eyes were fixed on a patch of brambles sprawling across a bend in the path. Tangled thorns twisted over each other in an almost netlike barrier, faintly shimmering with a sickly sheen. One could almost imagine they were… moving, swaying in a nonexistent wind.

“These vines are tainted,” Eryndil said quietly. “Perhaps by the old darkness that still lingers near the Barrow-downs.”

Celenneth narrowed her eyes, recalling stories of shadowy remnants that plagued travelers near this region. She approached cautiously, keeping Hallas well away. “They block the path entirely. Is there a way around?”

“Possibly,” Eryndil allowed. “But these brambles may spread if left unchecked.”

Linnea, dismounting to stand beside Celenneth, observed the sinuous vines. “We can’t let it remain here if it’s truly a danger to others,” she said. Brynja gripped her hand, eyes wide. “But how do we remove it without risking ourselves?”

Celenneth turned to Eryndil, remembering the elf’s lore. “Fire,” Eryndil replied simply. “That which is tainted in this manner usually recoils from flame.”

Their decision was unanimous: they would burn the brambles, clearing the road for themselves and any future travelers. Linnea and Celenneth scouted for dry wood—though “dry” was scarce after the recent storms. Eryndil carefully tested the edges of the creeping thorns, recoiling at the palpable malevolence emanating from them. Brynja stayed safely mounted on Eorlin, looking on with anxious fascination while her mother and Celenneth piled what kindling they could gather.

After coaxing sparks into a small torch, Eryndil advanced on the brambles. Each step seemed to stir the vines, as if they sensed the approaching threat. Hallas, bundled close against Celenneth, let out a soft whimper—perhaps disturbed by the unnerving stillness that had settled over the area.

When the torch flame finally licked the brambles, a hiss sounded, like a thousand serpents spitting at once. The vines writhed in protest, but the fire caught swiftly, feeding on their twisted tangles. A cloud of dark smoke billowed upward, acrid and pungent, making Brynja cough.

Linnea shielded her daughter’s face. “We’ll move back,” she called to Celenneth over the crackle of flames. “No sense breathing in that smoke.”

Celenneth and Eryndil stayed long enough to ensure the fire took hold, stepping away only when the brambles were thoroughly consumed by flickering orange tongues. The hiss subsided into a steady roar of burning, and in due time, the twisted vines collapsed into cinders. The taint, for now, was destroyed.

Brynja peered from behind Linnea’s arm, eyes watering. “Mama, are there more of those?”

Linnea stooped to ruffle her hair. “There could be,” she admitted gently. “But we know how to handle them now, don’t we?”

Brynja nodded with a child’s brave solemnity, but she clung a bit tighter as they rode on. Something dark or foul had brushed across them, a reminder that their journey would not be free from the echoes of ancient evils. Even in the sunshine, a faint shadow seemed to linger in the air.

They pressed forward, leaving the blackened patch of brambles behind. The remainder of the day passed without incident, though the travelers remained vigilant. Celenneth rode near Eryndil, quietly discussing possible threats lurking in these lands. Linnea followed with Brynja, who eventually broke into soft humming to soothe Hallas, perhaps to chase away her own unease.

The next morning arrived clear and bright, as if trying to dispel the lingering heaviness from the day before. They broke camp early, hoping to make good distance before midday. The terrain continued in gentle waves, though the grass appeared more wild, the trees fewer and farther between. Occasionally, they glimpsed the old Barrow-downs to the north, silent mounds dotting the horizon.

By late morning, Hallas awoke from a doze and began fussing, his cries ringing out across the otherwise quiet land. Linnea was tending to Brynja, adjusting the girl’s saddle blanket, when she spotted something unusual up ahead: scraps of cloth snagged on low rocks, along with a faint, ruddy stain. Blood. She inhaled sharply.

Celenneth saw it too. Halting Nimroch, she handed Hallas to Linnea to calm. Eryndil dismounted, crouching beside the rocks. Her keen eyes flicked over the fragments. “Recent,” she said tersely. “Someone came through here not long ago.”

A quick survey revealed shallow drag marks in the earth, as though a wounded person had been half-carried or pulled. Brynja wrinkled her nose, glancing up at Linnea with wide, questioning eyes. “Are they hurt, Mama?”

“Possibly,” Linnea replied gently. “And if they are, we should help them.”

Hallas’s cries, which Linnea tried to muffle in her cloak, echoed in the stillness. If someone was hiding, or if a wounded stranger lay just out of sight, they would certainly hear the baby. Eryndil’s gaze shifted to Celenneth. “They’ll know we’re coming.”

Celenneth, scanning the horizon, nodded. “Then better to approach openly.” She reached for her sword belt but did not draw her blade yet. “Let’s see if we can find them before something else does.”

Eryndil slipped ahead, moving soundlessly among the rocks. Celenneth and Linnea followed at a cautious distance, the children on Eorlin. The trail led into a shallow ravine dotted with scrubby bushes and stunted trees. Finally, they found two figures: a man and a young woman, both weary and tense. The man’s leg was tightly wrapped with bloody bandages, and he leaned heavily on his companion’s shoulder. Their shoulders stiffened at the approach of the travelers.

The woman spoke first, voice hoarse. “Stay back,” she warned, though she had no weapon drawn.

Linnea raised a hand in a gesture of peace. “We saw blood on the road. We came to see if you needed help.”

The man grimaced, clearly in pain. “We—something attacked us,” he said through gritted teeth. “A small, twisted figure… goblin-like, but worse. Fast. It vanished after taking a bite out of my leg.” He hissed with the memory.

Eryndil exchanged a quick glance with Celenneth. The possibility of a tainted goblin lurked in her mind. “You’re wounded,” she said gently. “We have a healer—Linnea. Let her tend you.”

Suspicion warred with desperation on the strangers’ faces. But the man’s trembling knees were about to give out, and the woman cast an uncertain glance at the baby whimpering in Linnea’s arms. That small sign of motherhood must have softened her resolve, for she gave a short, grim nod. “All right. But if this is a trick—”

“It’s no trick,” Celenneth assured.

Linnea passed Hallas to Brynja’s arms briefly, sliding down from the saddle. She knelt beside the injured man, gently examining the bandages. “My name’s Linnea,” she said. “What’s yours?”

“Torric,” he replied through clenched teeth. His companion introduced herself as Sera.

Linnea unwrapped the bloodied cloth. The wound was deep and ragged, the flesh hot around the edges. She frowned, rummaging in her pouch for herbs and salves. “You’ll need these cleaned out properly, Torric. Sera, help me hold him still.”

As Linnea began the work, Celenneth stationed herself nearby, watchful, her sword within reach. Eryndil circled around, scanning the ravine for signs of the creature that had attacked them. Brynja stayed on Eorlin, hugging Hallas close. Despite the seriousness of the situation, the child had a calm readiness about her—a testament to the many journeys she had already taken at her mothers’ side.

Linnea’s healing skill shone through. She cleansed the wound, grinding fresh herbs into a poultice that soothed Torric’s pain. After binding his leg anew, she let out a breath. “It’s bad, but not fatal if you rest. No more dragging him around. He needs at least a few days’ recovery.”

Sera’s rigid posture finally eased. “Thank you,” she murmured, voice trembling with relief. “We… didn’t think anyone would stop.”

Celenneth offered a small nod. “We’re glad we did. The road can be cruel.” She briefly explained how they’d seen the blood and felt compelled to help. Torric managed a wan smile, half-grateful, half-embarrassed by his vulnerability.

“What about the goblin?” Sera asked, eyes flicking nervously to Eryndil. “We heard it scuttling in the brush last night. It might still be around.”

Eryndil’s expression was calm but resolute. “If it remains, we’ll deal with it. You needn’t worry.”

The travelers helped Sera and Torric to a more sheltered spot, making sure they had water and enough supplies to tide them over. Though Torric protested that they could continue onward, Linnea urged him to keep weight off his leg for at least a few days.

Before the group could discuss further, a ragged shriek sounded from somewhere beyond the ravine’s edge. Celenneth and Eryndil locked eyes. This was no chance traveler’s cry but the guttural rasp of something tainted—and hungry.

“It’s back,” Sera hissed, pulling Torric behind a rock.

Celenneth motioned for Linnea to stay put with the children. “We’ll be back,” she said, voice tense with the promise of battle. Eryndil moved like a wisp of smoke, quiet and lethal. Together, they disappeared beyond a bend in the ravine, leaving Linnea’s heart pounding in her chest.

Their confrontation with the goblin would be swift and fierce, but its details were lost to the shelter of the rocks, where Linnea, Brynja, and the wounded travelers could only wait and listen. Eventually, the snarling and the clash of blades died out, replaced by an eerie stillness. Then Celenneth and Eryndil returned, blades in hand, expressions grim yet victorious.

“It won’t trouble anyone again,” Celenneth told Torric and Sera simply.

The couple looked both shocked and relieved to see the tall elf and the Númenórean ranger alive and unhurt. Whatever new apprehension they had about Eryndil’s presence, it was replaced by gratitude. In their eyes, elf or not, she had saved them from a lurking horror.

“We owe you,” Sera said softly, looking from Celenneth to Eryndil. “If there’s anything we can do in return...”

Linnea gently adjusted Torric’s bandage. “We only ask that you rest and then make your way safely to your next destination. Try not to travel at night, especially near these lands. The shadows here can be treacherous.”

With that, the travelers prepared to move on, having ensured that Sera and Torric were as secure as circumstances allowed. And so they rode onward, the specter of the Barrow-downs receding behind them, the memory of stormy nights and twisted brambles marking one more step in a journey guided by courage, unity, and quiet acts of kindness.

5Traveling West

The early morning light played across the wide fields, gilding each dew-laden blade of grass in gentle gold. Far behind them now lay the memories of Bree—painful, sweet, and complicated in turn. Celenneth, Linnea, and Eryndil pressed westward with Brynja and Hallas, forging on in hope of days less fraught than the last.

Hallas lay dozing against Celenneth’s chest in a snug sling, his soft exhalations nearly lost in the shuffle of hooves. The road underfoot, though still somewhat damp, was solid enough for decent travel.

Eryndil, astride her dappled mare Silme, took point. She rode with an upright poise that seemed to come naturally to the Elves of Lindon. Her sea-grey eyes scanned the terrain with habitual vigilance. Today, at least, the weather had decided to bestow gentle breezes and pale sunshine instead of torrential rain.

Eryndil drew rein and raised her hand. Nimroch, Celenneth’s mount, snorted and slowed as Celenneth peered around Eryndil’s slender figure. “What is it?” Celenneth asked quietly.

Eryndil’s face remained calm but alert. “A bear,” she murmured. “Off to the left in those brambles. I saw movement—large, brown-furred.”

At once, Celenneth’s arms tightened around Hallas. She glanced back at Linnea, who gave Brynja’s shoulder a reassuring squeeze.

Brynja’s eyes went wide. “A bear?”

“Likely a young adult,” Eryndil said, swinging gracefully off Silme. She moved close to Celenneth, voice low. “It’s not hunting, but it’s restless. We must pass calmly—no sudden noise or threat. Bears rarely seek conflict unless cornered.”

A thrill of apprehension rippled through Linnea, but she gave a firm nod. “Celenneth, how do we do this?”

Celenneth adjusted the sling holding Hallas, slipping one hand instinctively near her sword. She knelt by Brynja, meeting her daughter’s gaze with utmost seriousness. “We’re going to walk our horses past the bear,” she explained. “Slowly. No loud voices, no running.”

“Stay calm,” Linnea added quietly. “And watch me, Brynja. If I dismount, you stay close.”

Brynja swallowed and nodded. “I—I’m brave,” she whispered, her voice trembling ever so slightly. Despite her words, she gripped Linnea’s cloak tighter.

Eryndil took the lead, returning to Silme’s saddle with effortless grace. She let the mare proceed at a careful pace, and Celenneth followed. Step by deliberate step, the company skirted the patch of dense undergrowth. At first, all they saw was a shifting of branches—brown fur mingling with damp leaves. Then the bear itself emerged into clearer view. It was indeed a sturdy creature with thick, dark fur. A faint huff passed its muzzle as it turned its head toward them. Even from this distance, its size was awe-inspiring: a reminder that nature’s realm, too, held dangers not to be trifled with.

Brynja went rigid in Linnea’s arms, eyes wide as she took in the bear’s raw power. But she made no sound. Hallas, perhaps sensing the tension around him, let out a soft whimper. Celenneth stroked him gently, voice a near-silent hush, hoping to soothe him.

Eryndil lifted a hand, palm outward, a calm gesture. “We see you,” she spoke clearly. “We’re merely passing through.”

The bear snorted again and rose to all fours. For a heartbeat, it seemed to consider them with dark, intelligent eyes. The travelers continued forward at a crawl, giving the animal a wide berth. Brynja pressed her face briefly to Linnea’s collar, but managed to peek out enough to watch it with half-fearful fascination. Celenneth felt her stomach clench, half expecting the bear to charge.

Instead, it simply watched as they moved along the path. Perhaps it was more curious than threatened—or perhaps it had already eaten its fill of roots, berries, and small game. After a long, tense moment, it ambled away into the underbrush, the wet branches rustling behind it.

Celenneth let out a measured exhale and caught Linnea’s eye. Brynja still clung to her mother, but her trembling had lessened. Eryndil threw one last glance over her shoulder, ensuring the bear was indeed gone. They pressed forward, hearts pounding.

When they’d put enough distance behind them, Celenneth leaned over to Brynja. “You did well, love.”

Brynja lifted her chin, cheeks still flushed from held breath. “I—I wasn’t scared,” she said, though her tight grip suggested otherwise.

Linnea smiled and ruffled the girl’s hair. “Bravery doesn’t mean never being scared,” she murmured. “It means doing the right thing even when you are.”

By noon, the tension from the bear encounter had eased, replaced by easy conversation and the thump of hoofbeats over mostly solid ground. Hallas settled into a nap, lulled by the rhythmic motion. Brynja began humming a tuneless song, proud of how “not scared” she was. Celenneth’s gaze roamed the skies, noting that the mild weather remained—bright but breezy, with no ominous stormclouds on the horizon.

They had barely finished a midday halt—eating dried fruit and bread—when Celenneth, riding at the front, spotted something unusual in the landscape. The ground ahead, though grassy, had a subtle line of weathered stones. At first glance, it looked almost natural, but a second glance revealed an undeniable pattern.

She lifted a hand to slow Nimroch. “Hold. There’s something here.”

Linnea guided Eorlin closer, Brynja perched before her. Eryndil came up quietly on Silme’s flank, scanning the area with her keen elven eyes. Indeed, what Celenneth had seen took shape: a faint outline of mossy paving stones that ran in a diagonal across the field, half-swallowed by time and wild growth.

“An old road, maybe?” Linnea said, dismounting to run her fingers over the stones.

Eryndil knelt and brushed away decades of moss, revealing faint carvings. “Look.” She pointed to a swirl pattern that ended in a stylized star. “These are Númenórean in origin. Very old. I can’t read the entire design, but it’s a mariner’s motif... perhaps from an age when travelers from the western coasts ventured further inland.”

Celenneth felt her pulse quicken. Her own ancestry tied her to those old lines, and her ranger’s training had taught her a deep respect for Númenor’s legacy. “Shall we follow it?” she asked softly. “It might be worth seeing where it leads. Or we might find a ruin or a tower nearby.”

Linnea straightened, gazing at Brynja, who was already bright-eyed with curiosity. “We have time,” Linnea decided. “The day is yet young.”

So they trailed the mossy stones, letting Nimroch, Eorlin and Silme pick their way around half-sunken blocks of old pavement. Gradually, the path angled into a small stand of trees that rustled overhead. Within the shade, Celenneth saw the glimmer of stone walls. Brynja let out a small gasp.

What lay before them was indeed the husk of a small Númenórean structure—perhaps it had been a tower, or part of a storehouse. Now, only partial walls stood among the tangled roots. Vines crawled over the stone, and crumbled debris littered the ground. The hush of the place was profound, broken only by the faint sigh of wind through leaves.

“We’ll make camp near here,” Celenneth announced after a thoughtful pause. “We can explore it in earnest tomorrow when we have full light.”

Linnea cast an appraising look at the old walls and nodded. The area around the structure was fairly level, with enough open space to tether the horses under a stand of birch. They pitched a modest campsite, building a small fire ring from scattered stones. Hallas, fully awake again, gurgled as Linnea unwrapped him for a moment to let him wiggle in the fresh air. Brynja helped gather kindling, her eyes darting often toward the half-collapsed ruins.

By the time dusk fell, the group sat around a low fire, finishing a meal of dried meats, soft cheese, and a few crisp apples. Eryndil entertained Brynja by describing the old maritime orders that had once tied the Elves of Lindon to Númenor. While the girl mostly listened in rapt wonder, Celenneth, too, found herself drawn into the quiet lore.

“They were explorers,” Eryndil explained, “who sailed west to east, bringing news and gifts. In some older records, they’re referred to as the Guild of the Star and Sea, for they used both celestial navigation and oceanic lore. They sometimes placed small outposts or watchtowers far inland, though few remain intact.”

Linnea’s eyes drifted to the looming silhouette of the ruined tower. “I wonder what they stored here, or why they came so far from the coasts.”

“There might have been trade routes.” Celenneth ran a hand absently through Hallas’s sparse hair. “Or they needed vantage points. In any case, it’s worth looking around.”

Night wore on, and the conversation lulled as each realized their fatigue from the long march. Brynja curled up against Linnea, dozing off. Eryndil offered to take first watch, pacing a slow circuit around the camp with Silme. Celenneth and Linnea settled together, letting the fire’s gentle crackle and Hallas’s soft breathing lull them to sleep. Overhead, stars—bright, distant—watched in silent testimony to all that had changed since Númenor’s heyday.

Celenneth woke just before sunrise to find Eryndil still on watch, perched upon a low fragment of wall. Thin swirls of mist curled around the stones, giving the ruin an otherworldly look. Gathering her cloak, Celenneth stood and gently checked on Linnea, who was still asleep near Brynja and Hallas.

A faint chirp of morning birds accompanied Celenneth as she ventured toward Eryndil. The elf nodded a greeting. “Quiet night,” Eryndil said softly. “A few nocturnal creatures, nothing more.”

Celenneth exhaled a grateful breath. After the tensions of Bree and storms and brambles, a still night was a blessing indeed. “Thank you for keeping watch.”

Eryndil only inclined her head. “We should explore soon, before the day grows old.”

In time, Linnea and Brynja stirred, yawning and rubbing sleep from their eyes. Hallas woke in hungry fuss, to which Celenneth responded by preparing a quick meal for them all. Once the children were settled, they turned their attention to the ruin.

The structure lay at the end of the mossy road. Its outer walls had partly collapsed, and only about half the tower stood—crumbling steps spiraled upward into a precarious second story. At the base, thick vines disguised a side entrance. Brynja tried to peer inside, but Linnea gently drew her back.

“Careful, love. Let us check for any hazards first—loose stones, animals seeking shelter.”

Celenneth, used to scouting in her days as a ranger, led the way with deliberate care. Eryndil followed, stepping lightly over fallen rubble. Inside, the air smelled of cool stone and soil. Ferns and creeping plants dominated corners where sunlight filtered through cracks.

Here and there, they found faint carvings on the walls—worn symbols reminiscent of the star-and-wave emblem Eryndil had mentioned. Linnea knelt to trace one such carving with her fingertips, revealing a stylized star wreathed by curling lines that might have once been waves. “Look, Mama!” Brynja called softly from behind, pointing to another motif hidden under moss. Indeed, it was the same crest repeated.

While Eryndil rummaged through small alcoves, apparently used long ago for storing items or scrolls, Celenneth pressed her hand against an area of wall that felt less secure. She gave it a slight push, but it held. If there was a hidden chamber or door, it refused to yield to a casual search.

Hours passed in this slow exploration. Brynja amused herself collecting scraps of old pottery—mostly shards worn too smooth to glean any detail from. Hallas, tucked in a sling, observed with bright, curious eyes as Celenneth walked the perimeter.

Linnea at one point found a carved stone near the tower’s base, depicting a ship under a star. She called Eryndil over, who studied it intently. “The Guild of the Star and Sea indeed,” the elf whispered. “They had a presence here. But whatever they left behind is likely eroded or looted by now.”

A thorough search unearthed no grand relics—no treasure troves, no ancient library. If any secrets lay deeper, the group’s efforts did not reveal them. Still, the presence of these old carvings, the maritime imagery, and the hush of lost centuries lent a mysterious air to the place.

By early afternoon, Celenneth conceded that they wouldn’t find more. “We’ve done what we can,” she said, exchanging glances with Linnea. “Brynja, how are you holding up?”

“Tired,” the girl replied honestly, though her eyes sparkled with fascination. “I still think there might be treasure hidden somewhere.”

Linnea ruffled Brynja’s hair. “Treasure can be knowledge, too,” she teased. “We’ll keep a memory of this place.”

Eryndil, looking vaguely disappointed, led them out. “A shame not to find more, but at least we know it existed.”

Celenneth nodded. “And we learned a little about the old ties between Lindon and Númenor. That knowledge alone matters.”

With that, they returned to their camp, pulled down the tents, and set off again. The children soon dozed, lulled by the afternoon warmth and the slow pace. With a last backward glance at the ruin, Celenneth felt a pang of bittersweet longing. She could almost sense the echo of the past, as though watchers from centuries gone stood behind those crumbling walls, lamenting their tower’s decay. But the present called them onward, west and away, footsteps blending with the hush of the wind.

Their next day’s march proved fruitful and fair. The horizon stretched wide beneath a brilliant sky, and a gentle wind made traveling pleasant. With Celenneth’s ranger skills guiding them, they covered more ground than usual. Brynja’s mood soared, and Hallas was content enough to nap or coo at his mother’s face.

Then, almost as though gifted by fate, they stumbled upon a hidden glade. Eryndil’s sharp eyes noticed a faint footpath branching off from the main route, marked by smooth stones that might have once formed a border or boundary. Curiosity drew them in. What they found was a small woodland clearing around a clear spring.

Tendrils of sunlight filtered through a canopy of tall oaks, illuminating a natural basin where water welled up, cold and pure. Wildflowers adorned the edges of this spring in a rainbow hush—pinks, yellows, purples swaying gently in the breeze. A delicate scent, like mint or lavender, graced the air.

Eryndil slid off Silme, stepping lightly to the water’s edge. “This was no ordinary path,” she said softly, cupping some water in her palm. “The Elves of Lindon often blessed places such as this for weary travelers.”

Linnea, dismounting with Brynja, felt an instant calm. The air seemed somehow lighter, as though sorrow itself would have trouble lingering here. Even Hallas let out a tiny, gurgling laugh as Celenneth dismounted with him. The baby reached out a little hand toward the flowers.

“This is wonderful,” Linnea murmured, pressing a hand over her heart. “We should rest here.”

“No doubt,” Celenneth agreed, kneeling to let Hallas tentatively dip his fingers in the water. The baby squealed in delight. “We’ve traveled hard these last few days. A respite would do us good.”

And so they lingered. They let the horses graze on the fresh grass, watered them at the spring. Brynja, with Linnea’s help, gathered some wildflowers to press in her small notebook. Eryndil’s expression, usually reserved, softened as she took a moment to remove her boots and rest her feet in the cool water, eyes half-closed in momentary peace.

Celenneth drank deeply of the spring, feeling a gentle warmth flow through her limbs, washing away tension. If there had been any lingering fatigue, it melted into the quiet hush of the place. She glanced at Linnea and caught the same wonder reflected in her wife’s eyes. The spring’s gentle influence felt like a blessing on their hearts.

They spent the afternoon in restful quiet. Brynja napped on a mossy patch near Linnea, Hallas content in Celenneth’s arms. Eryndil kept watch, though her vigilance was more comfortable than tense; no sign of threat intruded on this glade. When dusk approached, they decided to spend the night in that tranquil haven, building a modest cookfire near the water’s edge and sharing a meal while stars blinked into view overhead.

The next morning found them all revitalized, as though they had stolen a day and night from some timeless realm of peace. Eryndil alone looked slightly pensive, but she said nothing. Celenneth wondered if the elf felt the press of her mission—the relic from Elrond’s counsel—calling her onward. Yet for a time, they were allowed this lull, an unspoken gift in the face of a world often marred by strife.

The day after leaving the spring, as the company once again put miles behind them, something changed. At first, it was just a feeling: a subtle weight in the air, an oppressive hush that settled over the landscape. The morning sun offered warmth, yet somehow they each felt a prickle of unease that had no visible cause.

Eryndil’s posture shifted, her elven senses evidently picking up on more than the rest. She reined in Silme and motioned for silence. Linnea, Celenneth, and Brynja halted, scanning the rolling hills around them. All seemed calm, but a certain tension seeped into the horses’ manner—Eorlin twitched her ears, Nimroch stamped as if sensing something amiss.

“What is it?” Linnea whispered, keeping her voice low. Brynja leaned in close, clutching at her mother’s arm.

Eryndil’s eyes roved the horizon. “We are not alone,” she murmured, voice tight. “There is movement in the distance. Too deliberate for wanderers.”

Celenneth’s heart thumped. The sense of being watched was unmistakable now. “Could be bandits, or orcs, or… something worse,” she speculated softly. She glanced at Hallas, who began to whimper. “We should try to slip past them, if we can.”

Linnea nodded, though worry gnawed at her expression. “Brynja, hush now,” she whispered to her daughter. “We have to be quiet.”

Eryndil dismounted, leading Silme to a small thicket off the side of the road. Celenneth and Linnea followed suit, tying up Nimroch and Eorlin near some concealed brush. Brynja slid to the ground, clinging to Linnea’s cloak. Hallas, jarred from Celenneth’s chest, started crying in earnest—a plaintive wail that might as well have been a beacon in the silence.

Celenneth froze, softly patting Hallas’s back. “Hush, hush…” she begged, but the child’s cries did not relent.

“We must hide,” Eryndil urged in a fierce whisper. “They’re getting closer. Do you hear it—the rustle in the brush?”

Celenneth’s jaw clenched. “The baby’s crying will give us away. Linnea, find cover for Brynja and Hallas.” She gestured toward a hollow beneath a large tree whose roots tangled into a natural nook. Without hesitation, Linnea lifted the children into that spot, draping a cloak over them. Brynja’s face was pale, but she nodded her understanding.

Celenneth exhaled to steady her nerves. She unslung her bow. “If they get too close, we’ll have to fight. But maybe we can avoid—”

Eryndil’s eyes snapped to a patch of shadow between two boulders. “No time,” she hissed. “They’re here.”

Even from their vantage, the travelers could see dark shapes—man-sized, or slightly smaller—moving with unnatural coordination through the scrub. A low muttering in a guttural tongue floated on the wind. Celenneth’s heart sank. These were no mere travelers. Something had led them with unnerving precision.

The baby’s cries continued, though muffled by Linnea’s cloak. The shapes angled in that direction. Celenneth turned to Eryndil, grim resolution in her eyes. “It’s now or never,” she whispered. “We can’t let them get near the children.”

Without another word, they readied their bows, stepping into the open. Linnea joined them, though she kept glancing back toward the tree roots where Brynja and Hallas huddled. The approaching figures—some men, others twisted goblin-like creatures—caught sight of them and let out a chorus of hisses and rough shouts.

An arrow whistled from Celenneth’s bow, finding its mark in the chest of one of the leading assailants. He crumpled with a strangled cry. Eryndil’s arrow followed a heartbeat later, striking another squarely in the throat. That creature toppled, leaving only a handful of dark shapes pressing forward.

Linnea’s arrow missed wide, thunking into the mud. She bit her lip in frustration and quickly reached for another shaft. The enemies hissed back with a volley of their own. One or two arrows zipped by harmlessly, but it was clear that some among them carried crude bows.

Celenneth slung her bow across her shoulder. “They’re closing in,” she muttered, drawing her Warden Blade instead. “I’ll hold them off in melee. Eryndil, Linnea, keep firing.”

Sword in hand, she advanced. A pair of the foe—shadowed men in ragged leathers—darted to meet her. They hissed vile curses in a twisted dialect of Westron. Celenneth parried a scything blow, sparks leaping from the clash of steel. She pivoted smoothly, the Warden Blade’s edge biting into one man’s side with a sickening thunk. He reeled but did not fall.

Linnea, heart pounding, drew again, sighting on a small goblin-like creature that was aiming at Eryndil. Her arrow let fly—and missed, skittering off the rocks. She cursed under her breath, hands shaking from adrenaline. The goblin cackled, notched its arrow, then promptly took a shaft through the chest from Eryndil’s bow. The creature gurgled and collapsed.

Meanwhile, Celenneth ducked a heavy swing from another foe—this one taller, wearing heavier armor. A brutal, dark blade whistled by her ear. She hammered her sword against his, forcing him back. The ring of steel echoed in the clearing. Another arrow from Linnea soared overhead, narrowly missing the armored figure. Eryndil shifted position, letting a second arrow fly. That, too, struck the target, though he shrugged it off with a guttural snarl.

Their leader, or so it seemed: a towering man, darkly corrupted, eyes blazing with hatred. Celenneth recognized the malevolence in him, something fueled by old shadow. She braced for another blow. His heavy weapon came down in a vicious arc, and Celenneth twisted aside, though the force jarred her to the bone. She felt pain shoot through her ribs—he’d clipped her enough to bruise or break. Anger flared in her chest, but she pressed on, battered yet unyielding.

Linnea and Eryndil loosed more arrows at him. The figure roared as each struck home, staggers telling the story of their effect. Meanwhile, lesser foes circled, only to be taken down one by one in a storm of arrows and Celenneth’s lethal steel. When the ring of enemies grew thin, Celenneth turned her wrath on the leader, driving forward in a final flurry. Her blade, forged to fight the darkness, found purchase in a gap of his armor—and with a roar, the man toppled, wounded beyond repair.

Only one remained: a shadowed man who had hung back, scanning for an opening. Seeing all others fallen, he turned to flee—but Celenneth was on him in a heartbeat, her blade knocking aside his weapon. With a twist, she sent it clattering to the earth. Before he could recover, she pressed him up against a tree trunk, sword at his throat.

“Yield,” she ordered in a low, commanding voice.

He bared his teeth, eyes flicking from Celenneth to the scattered corpses of his companions. Fear warred with defiance. Slowly, he raised his hands in surrender.

Linnea, breathing hard, lowered her bow and ran to check on Brynja and Hallas. She found them huddled under the roots as instructed, hearts hammering. Brynja clutched Hallas, tears in her eyes. “Mama!” she cried, relief flooding her voice as Linnea helped them out.

Eryndil moved among the fallen foes, scanning for signs of life or gleaning any clues. The heavily armored leader was truly dead, an arrow through his chest. She found little more than crude armor and twisted weapons. Then she paused, glancing over at Celenneth’s prisoner.

Celenneth, blade still pressed near the man’s throat, asked in a cold, clear tone, “Do you speak Westron?”

He nodded, eyes darting. She demanded, “Then speak it—why are you here? Why track us?”

A moment’s hesitation. “We—there was something we were told to find,” he muttered, voice gravelly. “We saw your group, we—”

He fell silent as Linnea approached, arrow still in hand. She looked at him with equal parts anger and exhaustion. “You attacked us for no reason.”

The man squirmed under Celenneth’s unyielding blade. “Orders… from higher up. We track a presence. That’s all I know. We found you… or maybe it found you. I don’t ask questions.”

Celenneth exchanged a baffled look with Linnea. “A presence?” she echoed. “What does that mean?”

He gave no further clarity, only shook his head, fear flickering in his eyes. Eryndil joined them, gaze cold and calculating. “He could bring more of them if we let him go,” she said flatly.

Celenneth’s lips tightened. “We are not butchers,” she said quietly. Despite the sting in her side, she held firm. “We have the information we can get from him, though it’s little enough. I’ll release him.”

“Celenneth—” Eryndil began, but Linnea laid a hand on the elf’s arm.

“She’s right,” Linnea murmured. “We won’t stoop to killing an unarmed prisoner.”

The man’s eyes flickered with surprise. Celenneth pulled him away from the clearing, swiftly bound his wrists to a tree while they decided. She cast a rueful look at Eryndil. “We’ll let him go once we’re out of sight. No more blood.”

Eryndil’s posture betrayed her disapproval, but she said nothing. Brynja, who had been clinging to Linnea’s side, stared at the bloodied battlefield with wide, frightened eyes. Hallas whimpered softly, sensing the tension. They led the children and horses away from the carnage, forging a path around the bodies.

Only when they were safely distant did Celenneth return to the prisoner. She loosened his bonds, meeting his gaze with stern clarity. “Leave. And don’t cross our path again.”

He all but bolted, half-limping, half-running into the undergrowth. Celenneth watched him go, sorrow and anger mingling in her eyes. When she turned to rejoin Linnea and the children, she found Eryndil was already mounted, her face unreadable.

They gathered the children and pressed on, eager to leave behind that site of death. Brynja gave a shaky nod, blinking away tears, striving for bravery. As they turned a bend in the path, Eryndil lingered. Her horse, Silme, stood poised. In one fluid motion, Eryndil notched an arrow, aimed at the fleeing figure in the far distance, and loosed. With a soft hiss, the arrow vanished into the brush. A faint cry echoed in reply, then silence.

Eryndil replaced her bow without expression, guiding Silme forward to catch up with Celenneth and Linnea. The deed was done. If Celenneth suspected anything, she said nothing. The group continued west, hearts laden with the knowledge that shadows, once alerted, do not easily relinquish their prey.

They rode on in a tense hush, the children kept close. Hallas fussed, though he gradually settled under Linnea’s soothing. Brynja asked no questions, seeming to sense that grown-up matters had taken an irreversible turn. Celenneth winced from a bruised side, and Eryndil maintained her customary silence, though her eyes remained vigilant for any sign of further pursuit.

Despite the clash that had just erupted, the road itself lay peaceful ahead, winding into the distance. The late afternoon sun cast long shadows, and in those slanting rays, the travelers felt a curious mix of triumph and foreboding: they had survived a deadly ambush, yet hints of a deeper threat lingered. What presence these enemies sought remained veiled in uncertainty—and, for now, Celenneth and Linnea could only hope that no other band of shadowed pursuers would soon darken their path.

6Arrival in the Grey Havens

The wind that brushed the canopy overhead carried a hint of salt. Celenneth could taste it on her lips, faint yet unmistakable, as if a secret messenger whispering that the sea—and the Grey Havens—lay not far off. Hallas, snug in his sling, fussed now and then at the unfamiliar tang, but Celenneth soothed him with soft words, her own heart fluttering with anticipation.

They had traveled many leagues since fleeing the site of their last battle. The day’s early brightness was mellowed now by hours of riding through tangled forest paths. Brynja was perched before Linnea on Eorlin, humming a little tune, though the child’s voice sometimes caught with tiredness. Eryndil rode behind on her dappled mare Silme, ever watchful, scanning the shadows beneath the trees for any sign that the danger lurking behind them might still be near.

It was late morning when Brynja’s quiet humming faded. She turned her head and asked in her small, serious voice: “Mama Celenneth... what happened to the bad people?”

Celenneth glanced back from atop Nimroch, meeting the girl’s wide, curious eyes. For a moment, she hesitated. How could one explain the grim necessity of violence to a child so young? “They won’t harm us anymore,” she answered gently. “We had to fight them to keep you safe.”

Brynja’s small hands tightened on Eorlin’s mane. “Did you hurt them?”

“Yes,” Celenneth replied, no avoidance in her tone. “Sometimes, to protect the ones we love, we must stop those who mean us harm. But it’s never an easy choice.”

Linnea placed a comforting arm around Brynja’s middle, her voice as soft as a lullaby. “We do it because we want you and Hallas to grow up safe and free.”

Brynja nodded slowly, her brow furrowed with a child’s earnest worry. “I’m glad you’re here,” she said at last, leaning back against Linnea’s chest. “And that Hallas is safe, too.”

Celenneth and Linnea exchanged a tender look. The exhaustion of the road lifted, just a fraction, at the reminder of why they traveled—together, as a family, forging ahead despite fear and uncertainty.

The forest opened onto a gently sloping ridge, and for a while, the group found some ease in the wider path. Yet as midday stretched into afternoon, the terrain grew unexpectedly difficult again—thorny undergrowth snared the horses’ hooves, and low-hanging branches forced them to detour.

Linnea, acting as scout, tried to find the clearest route, but luck was not with her. She led them down a track that seemed passable at first but quickly turned treacherous. The trail all but vanished into a dense thicket, obliging them to circle back a good half-league. By the time they regained the main path, the sky’s light had begun to slant, an orange cast heralding evening.

Brynja, normally full of questions, was uncharacteristically silent; the day’s detours had sapped her energy. Even Eorlin, docile as she was, tossed her head in weary frustration. Linnea’s shoulders drooped with unspoken apology as she and Celenneth took stock of their slow progress.

Eryndil guided Silme up alongside them. “We could press on, but we’ll wear the horses to the bone,” she offered quietly. “Better to camp now and hope for a smoother day tomorrow.”

Celenneth ran a hand over Nimroch’s perspiring neck, feeling the horse’s trembling muscles. “Agreed,” she said. “Let’s stop before full dark.”

And so they found a small clearing near a trickling stream. Linnea dismounted heavily, lips pressed together in a mixture of fatigue and regret. “I’m sorry,” she murmured, mostly to Celenneth. “We lost so much time.”

Celenneth touched Linnea’s cheek gently. “It happens,” she said. “We’ll manage. We have to trust that tomorrow will be kinder.”

They set to making camp: Eryndil collected fallen branches for a fire, while Celenneth scouted the perimeter for signs of predators or unfriendly eyes. Brynja helped Linnea spread bedrolls on soft pine needles. The mood was subdued, but the crackle of the fire brought a little cheer.

Later in the evening, Hallas fed and dozing contentedly, Celenneth rejoined the others by the fire. Brynja had drifted off in Linnea’s lap, curls spilling over Linnea’s arm. Eryndil sat nearby, methodically checking the fletching on her arrows. The hush of the forest lay over them, punctuated by chirping crickets.

Linnea spoke softly, trying not to wake Brynja. “I keep thinking about how close we are,” she said. “If the stories are true, we’re only a few days from the Havens. It almost doesn’t feel real.”

Celenneth nodded, gazing at Hallas’s sleeping face. “We’ve come so far—further than I ever imagined I would, in so short a time.”

Eryndil lifted her gaze from the arrows. In the firelight, her sea-grey eyes reflected flickers of gold. “You have something rare,” she remarked, voice gentle. “Such devotion to each other... it is a gift to see.”

Linnea pressed a kiss to the crown of Brynja’s head. “We haven’t always been so fortunate,” she murmured. “We’ve fought and lost and struggled. But still we stand together.”

A moment of companionable silence fell. The stars blinked into view between high branches, and for all the day’s missteps, the quiet promise of family and friendship made the long road seem worthwhile.

Dawn came glimmering and mild. Determined to make up for lost time, the company packed up camp in brisk efficiency. Linnea’s mouth was set in a firm line—she was clearly bent on redeeming herself after the previous day’s misdirections. They started well, following a clear path that wound along the base of a rugged slope. Overhead, the sun lent the leaf canopy a bright glow, and for a time, it seemed the day would finally smile upon them.

Then, without warning, a distant rumble disturbed the morning peace.

“What’s that?” Brynja asked nervously, clinging to Linnea’s belt from the saddle.

Celenneth felt the hairs on her arms rise. Nimroch quivered beneath her as the rumble deepened into a low thunder that seemed to come from within the earth. “Something’s moving... up there!”

She had barely spoken when a thunderous roar overtook them. Rocks and dirt began to cascade down the steep hillside, dislodging in a chain reaction that sent boulders crashing. An entire section of the slope seemed to shift, the ground trembling underfoot.

“Rockfall!” Eryndil cried, swinging off Silme in one fluid motion. Her voice rang with urgency. “This way—move!”

The horses spooked, whinnying in terror as clods of earth rained down. A torrent of stones battered the trail, raising clouds of dust that choked the air. Linnea clutched Brynja, forcing Eorlin forward despite the mare’s attempts to shy away.

Celenneth’s heart hammered. With Hallas pressed tight against her, she urged Nimroch after Linnea. The path threatened to vanish under sliding gravel. A deadly hail of smaller rocks pummeled the ground, and it was clear that only swift action would save them from being trapped—or crushed.

“Overhang!” Linnea shouted, voice cutting through the chaos as she spotted a protruding shelf of rock ahead. The shelf jutted out from the base of the slope, forming a shallow alcove. If they could reach it, they might avoid the worst of the avalanche.

Eryndil, on foot, grabbed Silme’s reins with one hand and half-dragged, half-led the panicked mare in that direction. Celenneth spurred Nimroch, the horse’s eyes wild with fear. Stones smacked the ground mere yards behind them. Dust choked Celenneth’s lungs, forcing her to cough. She held Hallas to her chest, shielding him from the swirling debris.

Linnea reached the overhang first. She dismounted in a flurry, yanking Brynja down just as a chunk of hillside tumbled across the path behind them. Brynja gave a frightened wail, pressing her face into Linnea’s cloak. Eorlin skittered sideways, neighing in blind panic.

“Easy, girl!” Linnea gasped, pulling the reins down firmly. Beneath the overhang, the air felt marginally safer, though the pounding above them was deafening.

Eryndil appeared seconds later, practically wrestling Silme beneath the shelter. One final boulder crashed onto the trail where they had been standing mere heartbeats ago, sending up a spray of earth and cracked stone. Celenneth brought Nimroch in last, forcing the terrified stallion to squeeze into the small space.

Dust billowed everywhere, making it hard to see or breathe. Rocks continued to tumble for a while—though blessedly smaller in size—until at last the thunderous roar faded to a grinding rumble, then to echoes, then to silence.

For several long breaths, no one spoke. Brynja clung to Linnea, tears streaking through the dust on her cheeks. Hallas squirmed in Celenneth’s arms, letting out a shrill cry, no doubt picking up on the raw panic that still gripped them.

Linnea pressed a kiss to Brynja’s temple, murmuring reassurances: “You were brave, love. We’re safe. We’re safe now.” Then she turned to Celenneth, voice trembling, though relief shone in her eyes. “Are you hurt? Hallas?”

“We’re fine,” Celenneth managed between coughs. “Just shaken. You saved us with that overhang... If we’d been a moment slower—”

Eryndil ran a hand over Silme’s heaving flanks, calming the mare with elven whispers. Dust coated her usually pristine hair, but her posture was as steady as ever. “It’s passed,” she said softly, peering out from the alcove. “Though the path behind is ruined.”

Celenneth dismounted stiffly, her legs wobbling. Nimroch snorted, foam flecking his mouth. “Steady,” she soothed, rubbing the horse’s muzzle. Her gaze swept the scene beyond the overhang. The trail was littered with piles of debris, chunks of rock as large as barrels. In some places, earth had slid away, leaving the hillside naked and treacherous.

Eventually, they led the horses out from under the overhang, picking their way around smaller mounds of rubble. The slope above still looked unstable in places, so they moved cautiously, hearts lurching at every minor shift of gravel.

“Any more, and we’ll be pinned,” Celenneth muttered, scanning the hillside with wary eyes.

Eryndil pointed to a safer rise of ground. “Let’s make camp beyond that ridge. We need to recover—everyone is rattled, the horses especially.”

No one argued. The short distance to the ridge took the better part of an hour, thanks to the precarious footing. At last, they found a sheltered hollow dotted with sturdy trees, well away from the broken slope. The sun, once shining warmly, now felt oppressive in their fatigue, and though it was still afternoon, they decided to stop for the night. The day’s misfortune had sapped their strength beyond measure.

Linnea slipped off Eorlin, turning a worried gaze to Brynja, who had kept silent since the rockfall. The girl’s eyes were distant, as if replaying the moment the hillside thundered down. Linnea scooped her daughter close. “I’ve got you,” she whispered.

Brynja simply buried her face in Linnea’s tunic.

Celenneth gently unwrapped Hallas. He wailed louder than usual, perhaps hungry, perhaps unsettled by the tension in the air. She rocked him, heart twisting at the memory of those thunderous seconds. She could still feel the dust in her throat and the echo of panic in her bones.

Eryndil remained outwardly composed, but when she set about gathering firewood, her hands trembled slightly on the axe’s handle. Once the campfire was lit, they sank onto bedrolls and fallen logs, trying to muster enough will to prepare a proper meal.

Between them, they cobbled together a stew from dried vegetables and leftover broth. Brynja mostly picked at it, though Hallas seemed content once he was fed. Celenneth and Linnea exchanged worried glances, uncertain how to soothe Brynja’s shaken spirit—other than to offer presence and quiet reassurances.

As night fell, tension began to ease, replaced by a shared gratitude for survival. Brynja eventually pressed her head against Linnea’s lap, eyelids drooping at last. Eryndil paused in her tasks to look upon them all, dust-smudged and exhausted, her voice quiet: “Truly, each day with loved ones is a gift. This day’s terror reminds us how fleeting such gifts can be.”

When morning broke, the company rose stiffly. Their muscles ached, and the horses still showed signs of nervousness, but the path ahead looked mercifully open. They pressed onward, pushing through the final leg of their journey. By midday, Brynja’s spirits began to revive; she asked a few hesitant questions about how the Havens might look, or whether the sea would be bigger than the rivers she’d known. The adults did their best to answer, weaving hope into their replies.

A little luck finally found them. They discovered a faint side trail that allowed them to skirt around more hazardous slopes. It cut some corners and saved them a day’s worth of detours—an unexpected boon. Eryndil recognized subtle Elven markings along the way, as if ancient travelers had once left hints for safe passage.

Celenneth offered a small smile. “A shortcut indeed. Let’s hope it leads us all the way to the Grey Havens without more surprises.”

And, as twilight neared, the final bend in the path revealed a sight that chased away lingering fears. The land dipped into a vast, open vista where the sea glimmered under the dying sun. The Grey Havens—or Mithlond, as the Elves called it—sprawled along the shore, a shimmering cluster of spires, domes, and graceful quays. Lights twinkled like stars descending to earth, reflecting in the quiet harbor.

It was near sunset by the time they actually reached the city’s gate. The air smelled tangibly of salt and kelp, mingling with the perfume of flowers draping from stone arches. Brynja let out a hushed “Oh!” at the first true glimpse of open water. Hallas, half-asleep in Celenneth’s sling, stirred at the distant cry of gulls. Linnea’s mouth parted in wonder at the tall towers shining gold and silver in the twilight.

Eryndil guided them through the gates, where Elven sentinels nodded in serene welcome. The thoroughfares within were crafted of smooth, pale stone that gleamed under softly lit lanterns. Everything felt at once ancient and ageless, as though the city had weathered millennia without losing its youthful grace.

Brynja’s eyes flitted from one marvel to the next: an intricately carved pillar topped with a lantern that glowed like starlight; a fountain shimmering with faint bluish luminescence; distant spires that seemed to touch the sky. “Mama Linnea,” she whispered, “it’s so beautiful.”

Linnea embraced her gently. “It is. A place like no other.”

Celenneth, exhausted yet enraptured, gazed around, her mind spinning with the surreal reality that they were finally here. Eryndil pointed out a tall tower crowned with silver mosaic. “The Tower of the Stars,” she said. “From its top, one can see all the way to the wide sea beyond.”

Celenneth clutched Hallas closer, her heart full. The travails of the forest, the battle, the rockfall—everything—they had survived it all to stand in this city of wonders.

Following a winding side street, they reached a quieter district of the Havens. Smaller homes lined the lane, each built with Elven elegance but scaled for comfort rather than grandeur. Eryndil dismounted near a tidy wooden door framed by ivy.

“This is mine,” she said, a note of apology coloring her voice. “I travel often, so it’s modest compared to most. Yet it’s a safe place.”

She led them inside. The main room was cozy, with carved shelves holding a few books, scrolls, and shells. A subtle fragrance of cedar drifted in the air. Brynja blinked at the nautical maps pinned to one wall, each annotated in a flowing Elvish script.

Eryndil turned to them. “Please, make yourselves at home. The horses can stay in the small courtyard outside. I only wish I had more space, but you’ll have my bed. I’ll manage on the sofa.”

Linnea, arms aching from the long ride, gently set Brynja on her feet. “Thank you,” she said, voice earnest. “Truly, we can’t repay your kindness.”

Eryndil smiled faintly. “You needn’t repay anything. I’m glad to have your company tonight.”

With dusk settling into darkness, they stripped off travel-stained cloaks and boots. Brynja was nearly asleep on her feet, so Linnea guided her to the foot of the bed. The child murmured something incoherent before drifting off, face still etched with lingering wonder from the day’s discoveries. Hallas, likewise exhausted, nursed briefly in Celenneth’s arms, then let out a soft coo and fell asleep.

Eryndil offered a few simple refreshments—bread, fruit, and cool water from a pitcher. Despite their weariness, Celenneth and Linnea found themselves nibbling, relief and awe warring in their chests. Outside, Elven lanterns cast a soft glow across the lane, and distant singing drifted through open windows: a gentle, timeless melody that mingled with the hush of waves lapping the harbor.

“We’ll stay here tonight,” Eryndil explained, tidying a small stack of charts on a side table. “In the morning, I’ll bring you to meet Círdan, the Shipwright. He is... father to this place, in a sense. He has guided these Havens for countless years.”

Celenneth slipped onto a small stool, cradling sleeping Hallas. “We owe him no small debt for maintaining this haven of peace,” she murmured. “Though perhaps tomorrow we’ll learn how to unlock the secrets Eryndil carries.” She glanced at the elf. “And how my Númenórean blood might help.”

Eryndil inclined her head, her silver-streaked hair falling about her shoulders. “I suspect so,” she said, her tone thoughtful. “But first—rest. All of us.”

They settled for the night in Eryndil’s dwelling. Linnea and Celenneth lay side by side on the narrow bed, Brynja curled up at their feet, and Hallas snug between them. Though the bed was hardly spacious, an air of warmth and closeness wreathed the family. Eryndil, for her part, took the sofa with graceful acceptance.

Outside, the city of the Grey Havens seemed never to truly sleep. Soft Elven songs rose and fell in the distance, weaving through the hush of the waterfront. Sometimes, a gull’s cry punctuated the melody, reminding them all of the nearness of the sea. In that peaceful lullaby, the nightmares of rockfall and flight gave way to a gentle, dreamlike calm.

Celenneth found herself reflecting on how far they had come: from the quiet cabin in the Angles, to Bree’s hostility, to storms and shadows on the road, culminating in that terrifying moment when the hillside nearly buried them. And now, here they were, in a place so far removed from fear it felt almost unreal. A small tear pricked the corner of her eye—relief, or gratitude, or both.

She closed her eyes, breathing in the faint cedar scent and the salt-tinged breeze drifting through the window. Tomorrow promised new revelations. For now, she would rest with her beloved family, content to let the city’s soft magic cradle them.

7Morning by the Harbor

Dawn broke over the Grey Havens in a gentle wash of gold. Winding lanes and silvery spires took on a soft glow that seemed almost otherworldly. Inside Eryndil’s modest house, the family stirred awake one by one, rousing slowly from the warmth of blankets and the lingering comfort of their last night on solid ground.

Linnea was the first on her feet, stretching out the fatigue of the past days. She caught sight of Brynja, sitting on a low stool and peering at the window as if expecting something miraculous. The child had been brimming with excitement watching the tall sails in the harbor.

Celenneth rose soon after, carefully lifting Hallas to her breast. The baby fussed briefly, then accepted the morning’s first feed with a few soft coos. Watching her wife nurse Hallas, Linnea felt a wave of contentment mingled with anticipation. They had come so far—through storms, battles, even a rockfall—and now they would depart on a vast new undertaking.

Eryndil entered from the adjacent kitchen alcove, bearing a simple tray of bread, cheese, and tea. “Best to keep our strength,” she said, giving them a subdued smile. “We’ll be busy once we reach the docks.”

Brynja hopped to her feet, heading straight for the table. “Is today the day?” she asked breathlessly, eyes flicking between Eryndil and her mothers. “Do we go on the ship now?”

Linnea ruffled her hair, laughing softly. “Soon, love. We have to meet someone, first.”

Brynja’s face lit up, her impatience momentarily checked by the promise. Hallas gave a tiny grunt of protest, as though aware of the bustle around him. Celenneth murmured reassurance, shifting him to a more comfortable position. In that moment, the small group felt an electric buzz of anticipation—a realization that once they left this house, their lives might change forever.

Some time later, with cloaks and packs readied, they ventured back into the city’s winding lanes. Dawn had blossomed into a bright morning, casting delicate shadows on the ivy-draped walls. The Grey Havens was vibrant with Elven activity: merchants carried parcels from nearby farmland, and mariners prepared for voyages of their own. Even so, the harbor retained its innate serenity, a hush born of centuries of tending to the sea and its mysteries.

Eryndil led Celenneth, Linnea, Brynja, and baby Hallas up a winding path along rugged cliffs, where seabirds cried overhead and the wind carried a briny tang. At the path’s summit, they arrived at a stone terrace overlooking the Gulf of Lune. There, the waves shone beneath the midday sun, and Círdan the Shipwright stood gazing out as though he could read fortunes in the distant horizon.

He turned at their approach, his silver beard catching the light, expression calm but curious. Eryndil bowed her head. “My lord Círdan,” she began clearly, “allow me to present Celenneth, a ranger of the North—and her wife Linnea, with their children, Brynja and Hallas. They have come through many trials: a rockfall that nearly claimed them on the road, a confrontation with hostile forces, mistrust in Bree. And”—Eryndil’s tone grew more reverent—“they survived the Frost Maw.”

Círdan’s thoughtful gaze swept from Eryndil to the small family. Brynja peered back with open curiosity, while Linnea kept a protective hand on her shoulder. “I see you stand together,” Círdan observed, voice resonating with mild approval. “Elrond suggested there was one among the Dúnedain who might serve a particular need—yet he named Celenneth, not the more renowned rangers. I appreciate your acceptance of this undertaking.”

Círdan beckoned them closer, stepping away from the terrace’s edge. “I would speak with you, Celenneth,” he said, his voice kind yet clearly expectant. “Elrond’s counsel is rarely misplaced. But I admit—I know little of you. Why should you, above others of the Dúnedain, be chosen for this task?”

Celenneth released a soft breath, exchanging a glance with Linnea. Then she stepped forward. “My lord, I’ve been a ranger most of my life. Brandor and Orlec—renowned mentors among our people—took me in at nine years old, adopting me when I was orphaned. By fourteen, I was already roaming the wilds, aiding travelers against orcs, trolls, and worse. It’s been nearly two decades of such work.”

Círdan arched an eyebrow. “You began your patrols so young. That speaks of skill—or perhaps necessity.”

Celenneth’s lips curved in a faint, wry smile. “A little of both. I’ve patrolled the Greenway, the Weather Hills, and the outskirts of Fornost’s ruins. I’ve seen glimpses of what lurks beyond quiet homesteads. But it was in the northern mountains—the Frost Maw—where everything changed. We discovered an ancient site, rumored to hold a bound evil from an elder age. Its chains were weakening. My wife Linnea and I helped strengthen those wards, if only for a time, and in doing so, I stumbled upon fragments of Númenórean lore long buried beneath ice.”

At this, Eryndil nodded in support. “She risked her life there—both of them did. What they found went beyond rumor: ward and relics tied to the faithful of Númenor.”

Celenneth inclined her head. “I never expected word of my actions to travel far. Then Brandor entrusted me with a blade—a Nurhael’s Warden Blade, though it required reforging.”

“Fascinating,” Círdan murmured, stepping closer, his sharp eyes flicking to the hilt at Celenneth’s side. “Tell me more of your grasp on Númenor’s history. Can you name the founding pillars of Arnor, or the lineage of Elendil?”

Celenneth answered steadily, “Elendil established Arnor after the downfall of Númenor. He arrived in Middle-earth and made Annúminas his seat. For generations, Arnor’s heirs maintained a line unbroken, though the kingdom split into Arthedain, Cardolan, and Rhudaur. Many records were lost in those troubled days, but the Dúnedain survived—Orlec insisted I memorize aspects of our history.”

Círdan nodded, satisfaction deepening the faint lines at his eyes. “And Númenor itself? What can you tell me of its legacy, particularly as it relates to the faithless kings who led to its downfall?”

Her voice lowered. “Tar-Ancalimon’s era seeded the division between the Faithful and the King’s Men, culminating in Ar-Pharazôn’s pride. Númenor fell beneath the waves, save for those led by Elendil who refused to forsake the old ways. My family’s oath has ever been to remember that doom and attempt to learn from it..”

Linnea gently hushed Hallas, who stirred with a fussy cry, while Brynja watched Celenneth with shining admiration—rarely had her mother spoken so completely of her ranger’s heritage.

Círdan, for his part, let a slow exhale escape. “You are well-versed indeed. Many among your folk know these tales in fragments, but you speak them with coherence and conviction.” He paused. “Tell me, then—what is your desire, Celenneth, daughter of the Dúnedain? Why have you come to the Havens?”

Celenneth’s gaze flicked to Linnea, to Brynja, then returned to meet Círdan’s. “Elrond told Eryndil I might be of use to you. If my blood or my knowledge can preserve what remains of Númenor’s wisdom—or if I can help hold back the darkness still bound in hidden places—then I will stand ready. My family and I want a future free from creeping shadows, not just for ourselves, but for all free folk.”

A breeze whispered across the terrace, carrying the tang of salt and distant gull cries. Something warm flickered in Círdan’s ancient eyes. “Your feats speak louder than mere rumour, Celenneth. The Frost Maw alone would test any ranger, yet you emerged with deeper insight—and the skill to re-bind an evil best left dormant.”

He glanced at Eryndil. “You have done well, seeking this heir at Elrond’s counsel.”

Eryndil bowed her head. “She has surpassed my every expectation, my lord.”

Círdan folded his hands before him, regarding the whole family—Linnea’s quiet determination, Brynja’s bright innocence, Hallas’s small coos. “Truly, you have come a long way, Celenneth of Arnor’s line. And in you, I glimpse a spark of Númenor’s old light. This is more than I had dared hope when I sought Elrond’s advice. In the tasks that lie ahead, you shall have the support of these Havens.”

He offered a gracious nod, then allowed a gentle smile. “Let us speak further of your next steps. There is a journey—by sea—should you choose to undertake it.”

Círdan guided them into a spacious chamber facing the sea, where a broad map lay spread upon a polished table. He rested his fingertips along its edges, voice calm and assured. “There is a place,” he began, scanning the faces of those gathered, “far to the west and north, some three weeks by ship. At first glance, it’s a lonely rock in the ocean—barren, windswept—but my people discovered a remarkable ruin within its depths.”

He traced a path across the parchment to a small, unmarked spot in the western waters. “This ancient site bears symbols and architecture unlike anything else we’ve seen. It was clearly Númenórean in origin—yet we soon learned it was warded. The very stones recognize only certain bloodlines, turning away all who are not heirs of Arnor.”

Celenneth’s breath caught, her hand brushing against the hilt of her Warden Blade. “So… you believe I can pass into it?” she asked, her tone steady, if tinged with curiosity.

Círdan gave a small nod. “Indeed. Your heritage runs deep. Only one of Númenórean descent—strong in that lineage—might pass those wards. Within this ruin, there may be knowledge or artifacts that could illuminate much we’ve lost over time. Until now, no one has been able to open its doors.”

Eryndil tilted her head thoughtfully. “So you propose a voyage,” she said.

A hint of reverence touched Círdan’s expression. “Yes. It is no small task: the seas can be perilous, but we have the best of ships and sailors. If we prepare well and proceed with resolve, the journey can be accomplished.”

Celenneth glanced at Linnea, who met her eyes with quiet resolve. “You want me to join the expedition,” Celenneth said, though there was little doubt in her voice.

“Yes,” Círdan replied, turning his full attention on her. “Your presence is the key. What dwells behind those wards could reshape certain histories—or at least preserve them from fading away forever.”

A hush fell. Brynja tugged at Linnea’s sleeve, voice hushed. “Mama… are we going?” she asked.

Linnea crouched beside her, smoothing the girl’s hair with a gentle hand. “We’ll discuss it, my love,” she said, her tone warm but serious. “And we’ll decide as a family.”

That evening, as the sunset painted the sky in strokes of gold and violet, Celenneth followed Eryndil out to the shore. Over the rhythmic hush of waves, they spoke in hushed tones about the voyage that lay ahead.

“The sea is no tame road,” Celenneth murmured, arms folded against the gathering chill. “Storms, sudden squalls… an island no one frequents. Are we truly prepared?”

Eryndil offered a careful nod. “Such journeys are never without risk. But Círdan will see that the ship is strong and the crew well-chosen. I trust his judgment; the Sea-Elves do not take voyages lightly.”

Celenneth glanced out at the broad waters, a frown creasing her brow. “And what of the island itself? If it’s warded for my bloodline, there could be other dangers. My family—”

Eryndil’s voice softened. “I understand your worry. Yet from what we know, the ruin has stood for ages, silent and sealed. Whatever challenges wait inside won’t likely stir until it’s opened. In other words, if anyone has the best chance of success—and safety—it’s you.”

Celenneth allowed the words to settle, the hush of surf washing through her thoughts. Finally, she let out a slow breath. “All right,” she said quietly. “Let’s trust Círdan. I’ll speak with Linnea.”

They returned to Eryndil’s modest home, where the interior glowed in firelight. Linnea stood by the hearth, Hallas dozing in a cradle at her side and Brynja already curled up on a floor cushion. She looked up when Celenneth entered, tension flickering in her eyes—an unspoken fear that she might be told to stay behind.

Celenneth crossed the room with calm purpose. “I’ve weighed the risks,” she began softly, “and I believe this voyage will be in good hands. I want you with me, Linnea. All of us—together.”

Linnea’s eyes widened, and for a moment she simply stared. “I thought…” She swallowed, glancing at the sleeping children. “I assumed you’d want me to remain here.”

Celenneth shook her head, reaching to take Linnea’s hand. “We’ve always faced life’s trials side by side. I can’t imagine stepping aboard a ship without you and the children.”

Relief washed over Linnea’s features, followed by a hesitant smile. “I’ve never sailed,” she admitted, a laugh tinged with uncertainty escaping her lips. “But if you think we should, then yes—I’ll come. We’ll face the sea and whatever follows.”

Celenneth squeezed her fingers gently, the flickering fire casting dancing shadows on the walls. “I’m just as uncertain, but I want to explore the world with my family at my side.”

Early sunlight streamed through the windows, rousing the family to new purpose. Brynja perched on a stool, her legs swinging idly while Celenneth and Linnea knelt beside her. Outside, Hallas gave a brief, cheerful cry, as though sensing the stir of adventure in the air.

“We have big news,” Linnea began, drawing Brynja’s attention. “We’re going on a journey.”

Brynja leaned forward, eyes bright. “Where?” she breathed.

Celenneth gave her daughter a reassuring smile. “Across the sea—there’s an island we need to visit. Círdan will help us sail there.”

The girl’s face lit up with a delighted gasp. “A ship with sails? I’ll get to see it up close?”

Linnea chuckled, smoothing Brynja’s hair. “Yes, a real ship. It might be bigger than any boat you’ve ever imagined. But the sea can be unpredictable, so you’ll need to be our bravest explorer.”

Brynja folded her small arms, resolve gleaming in her eyes. “I’m not scared,” she declared. “I want to see everything.”

Celenneth exchanged a look with Linnea, heart buoyed by Brynja’s enthusiasm. “Then it’s settled—we sail together. And Hallas, too,” she said, nodding at the baby nestled against a cushion.

Brynja giggled softly, sliding off the stool. “I want to help! Can I pack my things now?”

Linnea laughed, giving Brynja’s shoulder a playful pat. “Go ahead, little scout. We have a busy day ahead.”

In that moment, the once-distant idea of a sea voyage suddenly felt real and immediate. As Brynja dashed off to gather her belongings, and Hallas cooed in the morning’s light, Celenneth and Linnea locked eyes—each silently acknowledging the step they were about to take, unified by hope and a touch of daring.

In the stillness of early afternoon, Celenneth and Linnea made their way through the winding lanes of the Grey Havens, Brynja skipping a few paces ahead and Hallas snug in Celenneth’s sling. Eryndil walked quietly at their side, offering a reassuring nod whenever Linnea cast a glance her way—this meeting felt momentous, and there was no telling how Círdan would respond.

Eventually, they arrived at a tall, arched entrance opening onto a broad courtyard lined with slender birch trees. A cluster of elves—likely attendants and scribes—were gathered near a pillared veranda. Upon seeing the newcomers, they bowed in greeting and guided them inside.

They found Círdan in a modest, elegant receiving room, its windows overlooking the harbor beyond. Seabirds circled overhead outside, the sound of their distant calls drifting in on the breeze. As Celenneth, Linnea, Eryndil, and the children stepped forward, Círdan turned from a low table spread with charts, his silver hair catching a sunbeam filtering through the high windows.

“Welcome,” he said in a measured tone, inclining his head with quiet respect. “I trust you have given thought to my proposal.”

Linnea placed a hand on Brynja’s shoulder to keep her from wandering around the unfamiliar room, then nodded at Celenneth. Celenneth cleared her throat and met the shipwright’s kind but discerning gaze. “We have, my lord. After much discussion, we wish to undertake the voyage. Our children and horses will join us as well.”

Círdan’s keen eyes flicked between them—Linnea’s steady resolve, Celenneth’s calm determination, Brynja’s bright anticipation, and Hallas’s tiny curious glance from his sling. A subtle curve of approval touched his lips. “Then let us speak of the vessel that shall carry you.”

He led them out onto a side terrace overlooking the harbor, then through a smaller gate that opened onto the quays. The crisp salt wind teased Brynja’s hair as they rounded a corner. Linnea kept a protective arm around her daughter’s shoulders, while Celenneth hitched Hallas higher against her chest, eyes scanning the bustling piers. Eryndil followed in thoughtful silence, her gaze flicking occasionally to Círdan, noting his assured pace.

At length, Círdan paused near a sleek, moored vessel. Its bow bore a graceful, flowing script that caught the sunlight:“Here is the ship awaiting you,” Círdan said, letting them take in the sight. “We have gathered supplies, charts, and crew. She is nearly ready.”

Celenneth and Linnea approached the gangplank, Brynja trailing behind with unabashed wonder in her eyes. The hull of silvery-white wood glinted faintly, tall masts rising toward the sky with sails folded tight. Hallas stirred, letting out a small, intrigued coo as if responding to the hushed marvel around him.

The ship was manned by Captain Calion—a tall, composed Elf with a dignified bearing—and Ithildor, his first mate and navigator, whose star-etched instruments lay carefully on a table near the helm. The rest of the dozen-strong crew were equally poised, each fulfilling roles with an efficiency that spoke of centuries of shared seafaring traditions.

Brynja pressed herself against Linnea’s side, eyes round as she glimpsed a small cluster of crates lashed in neat rows. “It’s so big,” she whispered, voice tinged with awe.

One morning, the weather shifted quickly, dark clouds marching across the sky. The waves grew choppy, rocking the Eärlindë more fiercely with each hour. Celenneth tried to brace herself against the rail, swallowing down any queasiness. She caught sight of Linnea holding Brynja close, the little girl’s face pale with fear, and Eryndil drifting from person to person, offering calm words and water.

Celenneth’s own stomach knotted each time the ship lurched upward and plunged down again. She reminded herself she had endured worse on land—battling orcs or facing bitter storms in the Frost Maw—but there was no ground here, only undulating water that obeyed no mortal will. Eventually, the seas calmed, leaving Celenneth shaken but grateful as she joined Linnea in reassuring Brynja that the worst had passed.

Not two days later, a new obstacle appeared. Ithildur noticed an ominous pattern in the water—a reef stretching under the surface, barely visible except for the way waves broke unexpectedly. The crew sprang into a tense dance of orders and quick movements. Calion slowed the ship, urging caution. Eryndil positioned herself near the bow, her sharp eyes scanning for hidden rocks, while Lindarë, the quartermaster, coordinated the deckhands to adjust sails and lines in an effort to weave around the reefs.

Celenneth, determined to help, handed Hallas to Linnea and rushed forward to manage extra ropes and lines alongside the Elves. She focused on each shouted command, sweat beading at her temple. For a time, it seemed they might pass the reef without harm—until the wind changed unpredictably, and the ship jolted against a submerged rock. A scraping noise ripped through the air, the hull shuddering beneath Celenneth’s boots. She nearly lost her footing, gripping the rail hard as voices rose in alarm around her.

After a moment of panicked scrambling, the Eärlindë steadied, but the damage was evident. Calion and Ithildur both wore grim expressions as they confirmed the hull had taken significant damage. Mallor, the carpenter, disappeared below deck, returning with the troubled assessment: the needed repairs could not be safely made out on open water.

Calion called the crew together, standing at the center of the deck with quiet resolve. He explained they would need to find a harbor to address the hull’s damage. Though the plan involved a detour that would cost them time, there was no choice if they wished to sail on without risking a dangerous leak. Celenneth exhaled, aware of Linnea’s steadying presence at her side. She could see the worry in Brynja’s eyes, the confusion in Hallas’s small whimper, and the resolve in Eryndil’s calm face.

When the ship turned course, Celenneth felt a pang of frustration. Every day spent detouring meant slower progress toward the distant island that Círdan had asked them to find. At last, after a few tense days, land appeared at the horizon—rocky cliffs forming a natural bay, safe enough for the Eärlindë to anchor without fear of deeper hull damage. Celenneth exhaled in relief. She spotted Linnea holding Brynja aloft so the girl could see the coastline, and Hallas roused from a nap, gazing around with wide eyes.
