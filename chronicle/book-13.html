1Eryndil, the Elf

Súlimë 12, 2971

The months had drifted by in a gentle rhythm, the world beyond the cabin shifting from winter's hush to the quiet green of spring. Within the Angle, the small home Celenneth and Linnea had built was full now with the sounds of two children—Brynja, five years old and forever curious, and the infant Hallas, three months into the world and beginning to discover his own voice. The afternoons carried the scent of warming earth: Linnea coaxed seedlings from the thawed soil, Celenneth mended tools and tack, and Brynja tended the baby in the way only a child with a heart that size could—singing him nonsense songs, holding his small hand with solemn pride. For a season it had seemed the world's greater troubles could not find them here.

But peace rarely holds unchanged, and fate seldom knocks before it enters.

It was early morning when Celenneth first marked the figure on the horizon. She stood finishing her tea at the window while Linnea hung fresh linen outside; and beyond the budding branches a lone traveler came on with measured steps, tall and lithe, wrapped in a cloak of deep blue that caught the light in slow ripples, like waves on a far shore.

Her heart gave a curious flutter. This was no wanderer of mortal lands. The bearing was fluid and sure, steeped in an elegance that spoke of centuries and not decades—one of the Elves, and not of the woodland kind but of Lindon, a people shaped by western seas and long memory. As the visitor neared, Celenneth glimpsed sea-grey eyes beneath the hood, and silver-streaked hair strung with small shell-like beads that shimmered when they moved.

Linnea stepped back inside, wiping her hands, her brow lifting in question.

"Someone comes," Celenneth said, low. "An Elf of Lindon, I think. Not from these parts—she carries herself as one who has journeyed far. And I think she comes for me."

Linnea drew to the window, thoughtful, unafraid. They both knew Celenneth's past held more than the simple tale of a ranger who had settled down. Her hand brushed Celenneth's. "Then let's meet her."

They stepped out into the warming day, Brynja trailing behind, Linnea balancing Hallas on her hip. The Elf halted gracefully and drew back her hood, revealing a face sharp and yet kind, timeless and strong, her eyes the grey of a distant storm at sea. She wore fitted leather chased with patterned sweeps, and at her collar a silver clasp shaped like a star.

"I am Eryndil of Lindon," she said, her voice steady and resonant as far-off tides. "I come bearing counsel from Elrond of Rivendell. I journeyed east to seek his wisdom, and he sent me here—to you, Celenneth. He spoke of your bloodline, and of a part you might play in a matter of great weight." From beneath the blue cloak she drew a shard of adamant, carved with fine symbols that glowed faintly with a soft, oceanic light. "There is a place long lost, west of these lands, hidden by time and tide. This relic may open its truth—but only if one of Númenórean blood stands beside me. Elrond believes that one is you."

Celenneth felt the ground shift beneath her. She had come here to find peace, and yet destiny had found her out again. "I understand what you ask," she said, her voice steady though her heart was not. "But I can't answer in haste. My family is here—my whole life. I must speak with my wife, and then with you. This isn't a thing I decide alone."

Eryndil inclined her head. "I will wait beneath the birches. Come to me when you are ready."

That night, once the children slept—Brynja curled protectively around Hallas's cradle—Celenneth and Linnea sat at their table in the hearth-glow, fingers laced.

"She needs me," Celenneth said softly. "But what she asks feels enormous. I left the wandering life behind. Here we found peace."

"And the past still lives in you," Linnea answered, her eyes not wavering. "If this relic could turn fate itself, how do we ignore it? I fear staying more than going—fear we'd miss something larger than ourselves. If you walk this road, I walk it beside you. The children too. We chose this life together; we'll meet what comes together."

They sat a long while, the fire and the children's breathing steadying them both. Slowly the resolve settled. "We'll go," Celenneth said at last. "But not tomorrow. We prepare, we pack what we can carry, we see the children safe—and we close this chapter of our life here with care."

At dawn they found Eryndil among the birches, patient as the tide. "We'll go," Celenneth told her. "But as a family. Brynja, Hallas, Linnea, and I—we won't be parted. We'll need time to ready ourselves."

Respect moved in the sea-grey eyes. "Then I shall wait, and help where I may. The road is long, but shared burdens grow lighter."

There remained the relic's riddle—its glowing marks unlike any craft Celenneth or Linnea had seen. For that there was one nearby who might help: Almedra, the lore-keeper. Celenneth had sat at her hearth once before, the autumn past, and left in her keeping the silver-bound Lorebook of Arnenir carried out of the ice; and word had reached the Angle over the winter that old Berthor, Almedra's husband and fellow keeper of tales, had died as the snows lay deepest. Celenneth went softly, then, as one approaching a fresh grief.

The willow-shrouded cottage was quieter than she remembered it, the second chair by the fire empty. Almedra rose on her carved stick, thinner than before, her eyes older than her years—and yet at the sight of Celenneth, and then of the tall silver-braided stranger at her side, something in her lifted from its mourning.

"Almedra," Celenneth said gently, "this is Eryndil of Lindon. She bears a relic of Númenor's making, and we've come for your lore, if you can spare it." She paused. "I was grieved to hear of Berthor. He was kind to us."

The old woman's mouth pressed thin, and she nodded. "He passed as the snows were deepest. I've carried on alone." Then her gaze went to Eryndil, and wonder eclipsed the grief—for never in her long life of reading of Elves had she looked one in the face. "You are… of the Firstborn," she breathed. It was not a question.

"I am, lore-keeper." Eryndil inclined her head. "And it is a solemn thing to meet one whose memory guards the tapestry of mortal lives. You have my sorrow for your loss."

Celenneth drew the adamant into the lamplight, careful of its glow. Almedra leaned close, fascination overtaking her reserve, tracing with her eyes the swirls of waves and stars and ships carved into its surface. "Adamant," she murmured. "Harder than diamond, and rare. The craft is of the old tales—Númenor, and ships crossing the wide seas, and gifts between friends." Her voice steadied as it found familiar ground. She reached past her grief to the shelf where the Lorebook of Arnenir now sat among her own, and to a slim, worn notebook beside it—Berthor's hand, tidy and delicate on every page. "He copied these from an old source," she said, stopping at a set of verses. "They speak of a threshold in the West—a place where land and sea meet, where something long overlooked may be revealed under starlight. Take them. For old friendship, and for the hope that your road brings light to dark places."

Celenneth bowed her thanks; Linnea took the old woman's hand a moment, wordless. As they left, Almedra stood in her doorway on her staff, the dusk gathering, and called after them softly: "May your journey bring meaning—and your return, peace."

Eryndil looked back and smiled, and their steps carried them away into the twilight, guided now by new fragments of lore and the memory of a kind woman who had never thought to meet an Elf—and, in doing so, had helped shape a fate that might reach far beyond her quiet corner of the world.1

2A Stop in Bree

Súlimë 24, 2971

Bree's market hummed with spring—merchants hawking early greens, children weaving between the stalls, the smell of warm bread and damp earth. Linnea tried to move through it unremarked, and knew it was hopeless. Beside her Celenneth carried Hallas; Eryndil trailed close in her serene composure; and Brynja clung to Linnea's hand, eyes darting at every curious stare.

It did not take long. Evanor—one of her cousins—saw them first and nearly spilled her basket of apples. "Linnea? You've come back." Her gaze traveled over Celenneth, the Elf, the baby, and her face said plainly it was a great deal to take in. Her younger siblings Pelle and Mareth gawked openly. Then Tharen and Jocelyn arrived, drawn by the buzz—Tharen amused, Jocelyn's eyes sharp and cold.

The questions came fast and pointed. Where had she been. Who was the father. Don't tell me it's the elf. Did she truly think she could stroll in, say nothing, and have them all be fine with it.

Brynja's fingers tightened. "Mama," she whispered, "why are they so angry?" The small voice cut straight through Linnea's chest. This was meant to be home. It did not feel like home.

Celenneth stepped forward, her voice level and carrying over the noise. "The child bears his father's name," she said. "Hallas. That is all you need to know." The quiet firmness of it shut Tharen up for a moment—but Jocelyn's eyes only narrowed.

"Hallas," she repeated, dripping insinuation. "As in that Ranger? So you've been playing house with rangers now."

Then Merla and Harvin pushed through—older kin who had never troubled to hide their disapproval. "What's the meaning of this?" Merla demanded. "Strolling into Bree with strangers and a baby, making a scene in the market?"

Linnea had meant to be civil. She had wanted to show them she'd grown, that she was happy and safe. She tried, her voice measured. "Merla, Harvin—please. I know how this looks. But I came to tell you that I'm alright. Yes, I have a child, and yes, Celenneth and Eryndil travel with me. That doesn't change who I am—"

"You think waltzing in after months of silence, with an elf and a ranger's baby, is meant to make us feel better?" Merla cut across her. "We're the ones left with the questions and the rumors. You've embarrassed us before. Now this? What will folk think?"

And there it was. Something in Linnea's chest, held so carefully, let go.

"What they think?" Her voice rose, fury and hurt at war. "Since when is that worth more than your own blood? Is your pride worth more than my happiness?" She flung a hand toward Hallas, safe in Celenneth's arms. "This is my son. He's named for a good man—a better one than you'd credit. And Celenneth is more family to me than you lot have ever been."

Jocelyn's lip curled, and she lunged—an open hand swinging for Linnea's face. The crowd gasped. But the blow never landed: Celenneth caught the wrist mid-arc, her grip firm and controlled.

"No one touches my wife," Celenneth said quietly, and every word rang clear. Jocelyn froze. The market went utterly still.

Linnea laid a hand on Celenneth's arm. "Let her go," she said. "She's not worth it." Celenneth opened her hand, and Jocelyn stumbled back, glaring but cowed.

Linnea gave them all one last look. "Bree isn't home. Not anymore. Home is where my family is—Hallas, Brynja, Celenneth. We're leaving, west to the Grey Havens. If I never set foot here again, that's too soon." And she turned her back on them and walked.

They took a little respite at the Silver Lily before quitting the town. Lantern-light and the low hum of talk soothed her frayed nerves, and Eira found them at their corner table with a curious grin. "Look who's the talk of Bree," she teased. "Haven't had this much excitement in years." She dropped into the bench with the ease of an old friend, and for a while the barbs of the market eased. A merchant raised his mug in salute; a shy young woman asked Eryndil about the Havens and near-swooned at the soft-spoken answer.

Then a timid man hovered near, fiddling with his cloak-hem, and finally spoke barely above a whisper. "I only wanted to say—I admire what you're doing. Being open about who you are. It's not something I've managed yet." He glanced about him. "What you said out there—it means a great deal. Maybe one day I'll find that courage too."

Linnea's heart tugged. "I hope you do," she said gently. "I believe it's in you."

He nodded quickly, eyes bright, and slipped back into the crowd. And she thought that perhaps that alone had been worth the coming.

Come morning they made for the western gate. They had nearly cleared the town when footsteps hurried up behind—Pelle and Mareth, red-faced from running, contrite rather than angry.

"We're sorry about yesterday," Mareth said. "We didn't handle it right. We should've spoken up for you." Pelle held out a small cloth bundle. "For Hallas. A traveling blanket—Mum's charm for luck." Mareth produced a carved wooden horse for Brynja. "So you'd not leave without something good from home."

Brynja's whole face lit as she took the horse, and Linnea felt tears prick at her eyes. "Thank you," she managed. "It means more than you know that you came." She watched them go, and the weight she had carried into Bree lifted a little from her chest. Bree would stay a memory—some of it bitter, some of it, to her surprise, sweet. But she was done letting it name her. Her home rode west with her.1

3A Shelter in a Storm

Víressë 2, 2971

The morning after Bree was crisp with lingering dew, but a thread of warmth ran through it. Celenneth guided Nimroch at a steady westward pace, Linnea and Eryndil close behind—Brynja in the saddle before Linnea on Eorlin, Hallas dozing in a wrap against Celenneth's chest. The road stretched ahead in long, gentle curves, trampled earth and loose gravel worn by years of travelers.

For all the easy warmth, Bree still sat heavy behind them. Celenneth watched Linnea let out a slow breath and set the old resentments down, one mile at a time.

"Are the people in Bree still angry with us?" Brynja asked.

Linnea tucked a strand of hair behind the girl's ear. "Some, maybe. Not all. We met kindness too. That's how towns are, love—some will judge you for who you are, and others will open their arms."

Eryndil said nothing, her sea-grey eyes combing the horizon, the dappled mare Silmë matching Nimroch's stride. She seemed to sense the family needed a measure of quiet after the upheaval, and gave it to them.

They passed rolling fields dotted with early wildflowers, and midday came and went in peace. But as the sun tipped toward late afternoon the clouds thickened to a heavy grey mass, the breeze sharpened, and the light drained too fast from the day.

"Storm?" Linnea asked, frowning.

"It looks that way," Celenneth said. "We'd best find shelter."

It was no gentle spring shower that broke over them but a driving downpour, roaring across the sky, churning the road to soup. In moments the world shrank to a murky veil of rain, and the path turned slick beneath the horses' hooves. Hallas's fussing rose to an anxious wail; Brynja pressed her face to Linnea's shoulder, tears mixing with rain. "Mama, I'm cold," she whispered.

They needed shelter, and soon—the baby could not stay long in this. Celenneth strained through the murk and caught it: a faint orange glow, lamplight behind a window. "There's a house ahead!" she called over the wind.

Step by careful step they climbed a rain-treacherous incline to a small farmhouse, and Celenneth swung down and rapped harder than she meant on the door. It cracked open on a middle-aged woman, worry etched on her face; she took in Celenneth's drenched state, the children, the slender Elf behind them—and compassion won out over caution. "Oh, my goodness—come in, come in, you'll catch your deaths!"

The warmth hit them like an embrace. "Luthan!" the woman called over her shoulder. "Blankets—and stoke the fire!" A broad-shouldered man appeared, his eyes widening at Eryndil before softening at the sight of a trembling child. "You're safe here," the woman said. "I'm Martha. This is my husband, Luthan. I'll not turn away a family in need."

Celenneth peeled back the soaked wrap to check Hallas's face; his cries were already easing. "I'm Celenneth. My wife, Linnea. Our children, Brynja and Hallas. And this is Eryndil of Lindon." An odd hush met the Elf's name—an Elf beneath one's roof was no small thing—but Martha only cleared her throat. "An elf's no more welcome or unwelcome than any other soul soaked to the skin," she declared, practical to the bone. "Let's get you dry."

Luthan built the fire to a roar. Warmth flooded the modest room, and the color came back into Hallas's cheeks. Linnea sank onto the couch with Brynja, tears of exhaustion and relief in her eyes. When Martha set about stew, Eryndil quietly offered to help—"I've some small skill cooking on the road"—and followed her to the little kitchen, to Luthan's frank delight at an Elf chopping his vegetables.

By the time the bowls were filled a second storm raged outside, thunder booming, rain hammering the roof—but within, the travelers had found a small island of warmth. Martha and Luthan had farmed here twenty years and rarely hosted a soul; they had no children of their own, though half a dozen distant nieces and nephews called them godparents. "Spring storms come fierce in these border-lands," Martha said, "though rarely this early. You were lucky to find us."

They laid out blankets by the hearth, and Martha would not hear otherwise. Brynja's breathing steadied into sleep; Hallas stirred once and settled to a whispered lullaby. Eryndil spread a bedroll in the far corner, as if ready to rise at a word. In that snug farmhouse, cocooned against the storm, the memory of Bree's anger loosened its grip.

They woke to a hush; the storm had blown out before dawn, and the land shimmered under a watery sky. Martha pressed them to linger till midday, until the road dried and the children might have a gentler start, and Celenneth—weighing the long errand ahead against a kindness freely given—let herself accept it. They passed a leisurely morning, Luthan and Eryndil at the stewpot again, Brynja hopping through the puddles in the yard.

When at last they made ready to leave, Brynja ran and hugged Martha about the legs, and the woman bent to ruffle her hair, her voice thick. "You be safe." While she was distracted with the goodbyes, Celenneth drew from her pouch a small Númenórean brooch, worked with swirling wave-patterns, and set it quietly on the mantel just inside the door—no word said. She would rather they found it later, a thanks past speaking.

"We owe you much," she told Luthan, her voice catching.

"You owe us nothing," he said firmly. "We only did what decent folk do."

They rode away with the sun climbing and the road glistening, and when Celenneth looked back once, Martha and Luthan stood waving, their house a small beacon of warmth amid the wide, rain-soaked fields. The storm had tested them; it had also left a reminder to carry west—that compassion could be found in the least expected places.1

4Briars

Víressë 10, 2971

After some days the land changed, gentle farmland giving way to rolling slopes. The company skirted the fringes of the Barrow-downs—an old country spoken of in hushed voices, its grass-grown mounds and lingering shadows—and though the sky stayed clear, a tension crept back into the air, as if the breeze carried the echo of some older dark.

On a mild afternoon, with Brynja singing a nonsense tune to amuse Hallas, Eryndil reined in without warning and lifted a hand. Celenneth followed her gaze to a snarl of brambles sprawled across a bend in the path—thorns twisted netlike over one another, faintly shimmering with a sickly sheen, and swaying, almost, in a wind that was not blowing.

"These are tainted," Eryndil said quietly. "By the old darkness that lingers near the Downs, perhaps."

"They block the road entirely." Celenneth kept Hallas well back. "Is there a way around?"

"There may be. But left unchecked, brambles like these spread."

They would not leave a growing danger for the next travelers to blunder into. "Fire," Eryndil said, when Celenneth asked how. "What is tainted so recoils from flame." So they gathered what dry wood they could—scarce after the storm—and Eryndil advanced with a torch. Each step seemed to stir the vines. When the flame at last licked the thorns, a hiss went up like a thousand serpents spitting at once; the fire caught, the tangle writhed, and a cloud of acrid black smoke rolled skyward. They fell back from it, and stayed only long enough to be sure the fire held, until the twisted vines collapsed to cinders and the taint was gone.

Brynja peered out from behind Linnea's arm, eyes watering. "Are there more of those?"

"There could be," Linnea admitted gently. "But we know how to handle them now."

They pressed on, watchful. The next morning came clear, the Downs a line of silent mounds to the north—and by late morning Linnea, adjusting Brynja's saddle-blanket, spotted scraps of cloth snagged on the low rocks, and a faint ruddy stain. Blood, and recent. Eryndil crouched over the shallow drag-marks. "Someone came through not long ago—wounded, and half-carried."

Hallas chose that moment to wake and wail, and the sound carried in the stillness. "They'll know we're coming," Eryndil murmured. Celenneth nodded. "Then better we come openly." She loosened her sword in its sheath but did not draw.

The trail led into a shallow ravine, and there they found them: a man and a young woman, weary and taut with fear. "Stay back," the woman warned, though she bore no weapon.

Linnea raised an open hand. "We saw blood on the road. We came to see if you needed help." The man's leg was bound in bloodied cloth, and he leaned hard on his companion. "Something attacked us," he ground out. "Small, twisted—goblin-like, but worse. Fast. It took a bite from my leg and vanished." He and Eryndil traded a look; a tainted thing, then, of the Downs.

Suspicion warred with desperation, but the sight of the baby in Linnea's arms softened the woman's resolve, and she gave a short nod. Linnea knelt to the work—"My name's Linnea; what's yours?"—and the man managed, through his teeth, "Torric." His companion was Sera. Linnea unwound the cloth: the wound was deep and ragged, hot at the edges. She cleansed it, ground fresh herbs to a poultice, and bound the leg anew. "It's bad, but not fatal if he rests. No more dragging him about—he needs days off that leg."

Sera's rigid posture eased at last. "Thank you. We didn't think anyone would stop."

Then a ragged shriek carried from beyond the ravine's lip—no traveler's cry but the guttural rasp of something tainted and hungry. "It's back," Sera hissed, pulling Torric behind a rock.

Celenneth signed for Linnea to stay with the children and the wounded. "We'll be back." Eryndil moved like a wisp of smoke, and together they slipped beyond the bend. Linnea, Brynja, and the strangers could only wait and listen—the snarling, the clash of steel—until the sounds died into an eerie stillness, and the two returned, blades in hand, grim but unhurt.

"It won't trouble anyone again," Celenneth said simply.

Whatever new fear the couple had held of the Elf was gone, replaced by plain gratitude. "We owe you," Sera murmured. "If there's anything we can do—"

"Only rest, and travel safe from here," Linnea said, adjusting the bandage a last time. "Not at night, especially near these lands. The shadows here are treacherous." And so they saw Torric and Sera settled with water and supplies, and rode on, the specter of the Barrow-downs receding behind them—one more step in a journey guided by courage, unity, and small acts of kindness.1

5Traveling West

Víressë 20, 2971

The early light gilded the wide fields, and Bree lay well behind them now—painful, sweet, and complicated all at once. Celenneth, Linnea, and Eryndil pressed westward with the children, Hallas dozing in his sling against Celenneth's chest, the road firm enough underfoot for decent travel.

Eryndil, astride Silmë, took point—and drew rein, lifting a hand. "A bear," she murmured. "Off in the brambles to the left. Large, brown. A young adult, I think. It isn't hunting, but it's restless. We pass calmly—no sudden sound, no threat."

Celenneth's arms tightened round Hallas. She knelt to meet Brynja's frightened eyes. "We walk the horses past, slowly. No loud voices, no running." Brynja swallowed. "I'm brave," she whispered, gripping Linnea's cloak all the same.

They went at a crawl, and the bear rose into clearer view—a sturdy creature, its size awe-inspiring even at that distance. It huffed, turned its head, and for a long heartbeat weighed them with dark, intelligent eyes. Hallas whimpered; Celenneth stroked him to a hush. Then the bear, more curious than troubled, ambled off into the underbrush. When they had put distance behind them, Celenneth leaned over to Brynja. "You did well." — "I wasn't scared," the girl insisted, though her grip said otherwise. Linnea ruffled her hair. "Bravery isn't never being scared. It's doing right even when you are."

By noon the tension had eased, and Celenneth, riding at the front, marked a line of weathered stones running diagonally across the grass—too patterned to be nature. Eryndil brushed the moss from one and revealed a faint carving: a swirl ending in a stylized star. "Númenórean. Very old—a mariner's motif." Celenneth's pulse quickened; her own blood ran back to those lines. They followed the mossy road into a stand of trees, and there, half-swallowed by roots and vine, stood the husk of a small Númenórean tower.

They made camp beside it and searched it by morning light—crumbling steps, cool stone, ferns in every corner, the star-and-wave crest worn into the walls. "The Guild of the Star and Sea," Eryndil said softly, tracing a carved ship beneath a star. "Explorers who tied Lindon to Númenor, sailing by the stars and the sea's own lore. They set outposts far inland. Whatever they left, though, is long eroded or looted." A thorough search turned up no trove, no library—only the carvings, and the hush of lost centuries. "Treasure can be knowledge, too," Linnea told a disappointed Brynja. And Celenneth agreed: they had learned a little of the old ties between Lindon and Númenor, and that alone was worth the stopping.

Their next day's march ran fair, and near its end Eryndil's sharp eyes found a faint side-path bordered by smooth stones. It led to a hidden glade—a clear spring welling cold and pure beneath tall oaks, wildflowers swaying at its rim, the air sweet with something like mint and lavender. "No ordinary path," Eryndil said, cupping the water. "The Elves of Lindon often blessed places such as this for weary travelers." A calm settled over them all; even Hallas laughed, reaching for the flowers. Celenneth drank deep and felt a gentle warmth flow through her, washing the road's ache away. They lingered a day and a night in that quiet haven, and woke revitalized, as though they had stolen the hours from some timeless realm.1

The day after leaving the spring, something changed—a weight in the air, an oppressive hush over the land. Eryndil reined in and signed for silence, her elven senses catching what the others could not. The horses grew skittish, Eorlin's ears twitching, Nimroch stamping. "We are not alone," Eryndil murmured. "Movement, in the distance. Too deliberate for wanderers."

Hallas, jarred from Celenneth's chest, began to cry in earnest—a plaintive wail that carried like a beacon. "The baby will give us away," Celenneth said, low and hard. "Linnea, get the children to cover." Linnea lifted Brynja and Hallas into a hollow beneath a great tree's roots and draped a cloak over them; Brynja's face was white, but she nodded her understanding.

Then dark shapes came through the scrub—man-sized or a little smaller, moving with an unnatural coordination, a guttural muttering on the wind. Some were men; others twisted, goblin-like things. They angled toward the baby's cries. "It's now or never," Celenneth whispered. "We can't let them near the children."

She and Eryndil stepped into the open and loosed. Celenneth's shaft took the lead assailant in the chest; Eryndil's struck another through the throat. Linnea's arrow went wide into the mud, and she reached for another with shaking hands as the enemy hissed back a ragged volley of their own.

"They're closing," Celenneth muttered, slinging her bow and drawing the Warden Blade. "I'll hold them in melee. Keep firing." Two shadowed men in ragged leathers darted to meet her, spitting curses in a twisted Westron. She parried a scything blow, sparks leaping, and pivoted—the reforged edge biting deep into one man's side. He reeled but did not fall. A taller foe in heavier armor came at her; she hammered his blade aside, the ring of steel echoing.

Their leader was a towering man, darkly corrupted, his eyes blazing with an old shadow's hate. His heavy weapon came down in a vicious arc; Celenneth twisted clear, but the force clipped her and jarred her to the bone—a bruise, or a cracked rib. Anger flared, and she pressed on. Linnea's and Eryndil's arrows struck him again and again, staggering him, while the lesser foes fell one by one. When the ring thinned, Celenneth drove forward and found a gap in his armor, and with a roar the man toppled, wounded past repair.

One remained—a shadowed man who had hung back, and now turned to flee. Celenneth was on him in a heartbeat, knocking his weapon spinning, pressing him to a tree with her sword at his throat. "Yield." Slowly he raised his hands.

Linnea ran to the roots and drew the children out—Brynja clutching Hallas, tears streaking her face. "Mama!" Eryndil moved among the fallen, gleaning what she could; little but crude arms and twisted steel.

"Why do you track us?" Celenneth demanded of her prisoner, cold and clear.

"There was something we were told to find," he rasped. "We track a presence—that's all I know. We found you… or maybe it found you. I don't ask questions." He would say no more.

"He could bring more of them, if we let him go," Eryndil said flatly.

Celenneth's lips tightened. "We are not butchers." Despite the sting in her side she held firm, and Linnea laid a hand on the Elf's arm—"She's right. We won't kill an unarmed prisoner." So they bound him, led the children and horses well away from the carnage, and only then did Celenneth loosen his bonds. "Leave. Don't cross our path again." He bolted, half-limping, into the undergrowth.

They gathered themselves and rode on, eager to leave the place of death behind—and at the bend Eryndil lingered a moment, her horse poised. In one fluid motion she notched an arrow, aimed at the fleeing figure far off, and loosed. A soft hiss; a faint cry; then silence. She replaced her bow without expression and rode to catch them up. If Celenneth suspected anything, she said nothing. They went on west in a tense hush, the children kept close, hearts laden with the knowledge that shadows, once roused, do not easily give up their prey.2

6Arrival in the Grey Havens

Lótessë 4, 2971

The wind through the canopy carried a hint of salt now; Celenneth could taste it on her lips, faint but sure—a whisper that the sea, and the Grey Havens, lay not far ahead. They had put many leagues behind them since the ambush, and the miles told on all of them.

Late one morning Brynja's humming faded, and she asked in her small, serious voice, "Mama Celenneth—what happened to the bad people?"

Celenneth hesitated. How did one explain the grim arithmetic of violence to a child of five? "They won't harm us anymore," she said gently. "We had to fight them to keep you safe."

"Did you hurt them?"

"Yes." She would not lie to her. "Sometimes, to protect the ones we love, we have to stop those who mean us harm. But it's never an easy choice."

"We do it," Linnea added softly, an arm around Brynja's middle, "so that you and Hallas grow up safe and free." Brynja nodded slowly, her brow furrowed, and leaned back against Linnea's chest. "I'm glad you're here," she murmured. "And that Hallas is safe too."

The forest gave onto a ridge, but by afternoon the going grew hard again—thorny undergrowth, low branches—and Linnea, scouting ahead, led them down a track that dwindled into a thicket and cost them a half-league doubling back. She dismounted at their camp that evening heavy with apology. "I'm sorry. We lost so much time."

Celenneth touched her cheek. "It happens. We'll manage. We have to trust tomorrow will be kinder."

Dawn came mild, and they set out briskly to redeem the lost day—until, without warning, a distant rumble disturbed the morning. Celenneth felt the hairs rise on her arms; Nimroch quivered beneath her. Then the rumble deepened to a thunder that seemed to rise from within the earth, and the slope above them gave way.

"Rockfall!" Eryndil cried, swinging off Silmë. "This way—move!"

The horses screamed as clods of earth and then boulders came crashing down. "Overhang!" Linnea shouted, spotting a shelf of rock jutting from the base of the slope. They drove for it—Eryndil half-dragging the panicked Silmë, Celenneth spurring Nimroch with Hallas crushed to her chest, stones smacking the ground mere yards behind. Linnea reached the shelter first and yanked Brynja down just as a chunk of hillside tumbled across the path they'd stood on. One last boulder crashed where they had been a heartbeat before. Then the roar faded to a grinding rumble, and at last to silence.

For several breaths no one spoke. Brynja clung to Linnea, tears cutting tracks through the dust on her cheeks; Hallas shrieked in Celenneth's arms. "You were brave, love. We're safe now," Linnea murmured, and turned, trembling, to Celenneth. "Are you hurt? Hallas?"

"We're fine. Shaken." Celenneth coughed the dust from her throat. "You saved us with that overhang. A moment slower—" She did not finish.

The trail behind lay buried under rock, the slope above still treacherous. They picked their way clear and made an early camp beyond the next ridge, everyone rattled, the horses worst of all. Only that night, with Brynja finally asleep against Linnea's lap, did the tension ease into a shared gratitude for survival. "Truly," Eryndil said quietly, looking on them all, dust-smudged and weary, "each day with those we love is a gift. This day's terror only reminds us how fleeting such gifts can be."1

A little luck found them the next day: a faint side-trail, marked with subtle Elven signs, that skirted the worst of the broken slopes and saved a day's detour. And as twilight neared, the last bend of the path opened onto a wide vista—the land dipping to a sea that glimmered under the dying sun, and the Grey Havens, Mithlond as the Elves named it, sprawled along the shore: spires and domes and graceful quays, lights twinkling like stars come down to earth.

They reached the gate near sunset, the air rich with salt and kelp and flowering vine. "Oh!" Brynja breathed at her first true sight of open water. Hallas stirred at the cry of gulls; Linnea's lips parted at the towers shining gold and silver in the dusk. Elven sentinels nodded them through in serene welcome, and the streets within were pale, smooth stone, gleaming under soft lanterns—at once ancient and ageless. Eryndil pointed out a tower crowned with silver mosaic. "The Tower of the Stars. From its top one can see all the way to the wide sea beyond."

She led them by a winding side-street to a modest ivy-framed door. "This is mine," she said, a note of apology in it. "I travel often, so it's small. But it's safe." Inside, the room was cozy—carved shelves of books and shells, nautical charts pinned to one wall in flowing Elvish, a faint scent of cedar. "Make yourselves at home. You'll have my bed; I'll manage on the sofa." She would hear no argument.

That night they lay close in the narrow bed—Brynja curled at their feet, Hallas snug between them—while outside the city seemed never quite to sleep: soft Elven song rising and falling through the hush of waves, a gull's cry now and then to mark the nearness of the sea. Celenneth found herself turning over how far they had come—from the quiet cabin in the Angle, through Bree's scorn and the road's storms and shadows, to the moment the hillside had nearly buried them—and now to this, a place so far removed from fear it felt half a dream. A small tear pricked the corner of her eye. In the morning, Eryndil had said, they would go to meet Círdan the Shipwright. For now she breathed the cedar and the salt and let the city's soft magic cradle her family to sleep.

7Morning by the Harbor

Lótessë 8, 2971

Dawn broke over the Grey Havens in a wash of gold, and the winding lanes and silver spires took on a glow that seemed almost otherworldly. Within Eryndil's small house the family stirred awake one by one. Linnea rose first and found Brynja already at the window, watching the tall sails in the harbor as if they might vanish were she to look away. Celenneth lifted Hallas to nurse, and Eryndil came from the kitchen alcove with bread, cheese, and tea. "Best keep our strength," she said. "We'll be busy once we reach the docks."

"Is today the day?" Brynja breathed. "Do we go on the ship now?"

"Soon, love," Linnea laughed. "We have someone to meet first."

Eryndil led them up a winding path along the cliffs, seabirds crying overhead, the wind briny off the water, to a stone terrace above the Gulf of Lune—and there stood Círdan the Shipwright, gazing out to the horizon as though he read fortunes in it. He turned at their approach, his silver beard catching the light.

"My lord Círdan," Eryndil said, and bowed her head. "Allow me to present Celenneth, a ranger of the North, and her wife Linnea, with their children, Brynja and Hallas. They have come through many trials—a rockfall on the road, shadowed hunters, mistrust in Bree. And"—her voice grew reverent—"they survived the Frost Maw."

Círdan's thoughtful gaze moved over the small family. "Elrond named Celenneth, and not the more renowned rangers," he mused. "His counsel is rarely misplaced—yet I know little of you. Tell me, Celenneth: why should you, above others of the Dúnedain, be chosen for this?"

Celenneth met the ancient eyes. "I've been a ranger most of my life, my lord. Brandor and Orlec—mentors among our people—took me in at nine, when I was orphaned. By fourteen I roamed the wilds, aiding travelers against orcs and trolls and worse. Near two decades of such work." She spoke of the Greenway and the Weather Hills and the outskirts of Fornost's ruins, and then of the north. "But it was in the Frost Maw that everything changed. We found an ancient site—a bound evil of an elder age, its chains failing. Linnea and I helped strengthen those wards, if only for a time, and there I came upon fragments of Númenórean lore long buried beneath the ice."

"She risked her life there," Eryndil said. "Both of them did. What they found went beyond rumor—ward and relic tied to the Faithful of Númenor."

Círdan questioned her further, and she answered steadily: Elendil, who raised Arnor from Númenor's ruin and made Annúminas his seat; the kingdom's sundering into Arthedain, Cardolan, and Rhudaur; the long division of the Faithful and the King's Men that ended in Ar-Pharazôn's pride and the drowning of the isle. "Orlec insisted I learn our history," she said. "My family's oath has ever been to remember that doom—and to learn from it."

Círdan let a slow breath escape. "Many of your folk hold these tales in fragments. You speak them whole." His gaze dropped to the sword at her hip. "And that blade you carry. May I?"

She drew it and laid it across her hands, and Círdan bent close, and something shifted in his ancient face. "Brandor gave me this," Celenneth said. "A warden-blade, he called it—out of Arnor, and broken. I had it reforged. But its full name I never learned; he died before he could tell me, if he knew it himself."

"I know it," Círdan said quietly. He traced a rune along the fuller with one long finger. "This is Nurhael's Warden Blade—forged by Nurhael, a smith of the Faithful, in the last years of the North-kingdom, to guard the line of Arnor's kings against the malice of the Enemy. It was thought lost when Fornost fell." He straightened, and looked at her with new weight. "Brandor gave more into your keeping than he knew how to name. It has come to the right hand."

Celenneth's breath caught, and her fingers closed round the hilt of a thing she had carried all these leagues without knowing what it was.1

Círdan led them to a chamber facing the sea, where a broad map lay spread upon a polished table. "There is a place," he said, tracing a path to a small unmarked spot in the western waters, "far to the west and north, some three weeks by ship. To the eye it is a lonely rock—barren, windswept. But my people found within it a ruin unlike any we have seen, Númenórean beyond doubt, and warded: the very stones turn away all who are not heirs of Arnor. No one has passed its doors."

Celenneth's hand brushed the hilt at her side. "And you believe I can."

"Your heritage runs deep. Only one strong in that lineage might pass those wards. Within may lie knowledge, or artifacts, that could illuminate much we have lost." He met her eyes. "Your presence is the key. I cannot ask it lightly—the sea is no tame road—but we have the best of ships and sailors."

Brynja tugged Linnea's sleeve. "Mama… are we going?"

"We'll decide as a family," Linnea said, crouching to smooth the girl's hair.

That evening Celenneth walked the shore with Eryndil, weighing it against the hush of surf. "An island none frequent, warded to my blood—there could be dangers there for my family." Eryndil's voice softened. "The ruin has stood silent and sealed for ages. Whatever waits within is unlikely to stir until it is opened. If anyone has the best chance of success—and safety—it is you." And so Celenneth carried her resolve back to the firelit house, and took Linnea's hand. "I want you with me. All of us—together." Relief and a startled joy crossed Linnea's face; she had thought she'd be told to stay. "I've never sailed," she admitted, with a laugh that shook a little. "But if you say we should, then yes. We'll face the sea and whatever follows."

In the days that followed, Círdan showed them the ship that would carry them: a sleek vessel of silvery-white wood, three masts rigged with sails of sturdy Elven cloth, her bow lettered in flowing script. "This is Eärlindë," he said. "Her timbers are woven with subtle enchantments to steady her in rough water." She was captained by Calion, tall and composed, and navigated by Ithildur, whose star-etched instruments lay by the helm; the rest of her dozen crew moved with the ease of centuries at sea. Below, Círdan showed them two small, well-appointed cabins lit by gently glowing stones, one wall fitted with a pivoting cradle to rock Hallas as the ship rolled; and deeper still, a clean, straw-strewn hold with stalls for Nimroch and Eorlin—and, Círdan added lightly, for Silmë as well.

Eryndil startled. "My lord?"

"I cannot lead this voyage myself," Círdan said, with a faint smile. "You will be my eyes and ears. You sail in my stead." He had had a bunk readied for her in the crew quarters—but Linnea would not hear of it. "Stay in the passenger quarters with us. We've faced too many roads together to keep you below deck." Celenneth nodded. "Consider yourself family. Brynja adores you, and Hallas falls asleep in your arms." A rare flicker of vulnerability crossed the Elf's face, and she inclined her head. "Thank you. I will."

They passed their last days in the quiet splendor of the Havens, exploring streets they might never see again—Brynja enthralled by silver trinkets and glowing lanterns, Linnea trading a highland tea-recipe to an elven apothecary for salves against seasickness. And then the morning came. The crew stowed the last crates and water-barrels; the horses were walked carefully aboard and calmed in the hold. On deck the crew assembled to greet them, and Calion's voice carried over the lap of the waves: "Welcome aboard, travelers. By Círdan's command we sail under clear purpose—to bring you safely to your destination, and home again." Galaniel, the ship's cook, pressed a small sweet biscuit into Brynja's hands—"to calm your first sail"—and her face lit with delight. Ithildur showed Celenneth the star-chart pinned near the wheel. "We navigate by stars and currents. Elven methods, refined over many ages."

At midday a horn sounded, one long low note. The moorings slipped, the sails unfurled with a whisper, and the wind caught them. "We're really moving!" Brynja cried, gripping the rail on tiptoe. High on the Havens' wall Círdan raised a hand in stately farewell; Eryndil returned the salute, and Celenneth, cradling Hallas, bowed her head in silent thanks. They carried the blessing of the ancient Shipwright now, and it gave them courage.

Beyond the harbor's arms the sea grew tangible, the deck rising and falling under each swell. "Flow with the ship," Eryndil counseled, showing them to bend their knees. "Rather than resist it." — "Like dancing," Brynja decided, giggling as the deck tilted. Song-stones set in the railings threw faint prisms across the boards, and the low hum of Elven work-songs rose from the crew. By evening the coast was a distant line behind them, and the family had begun to find its rhythm aboard the Eärlindë. That night they lay close in their narrow cabin, Hallas swaying gently in his cradle, Eryndil bedded in the corner, the strains of Elven singing drifting through the porthole—bound, all of them, by the vow to face whatever came as a family.2

8Brynja Meets the Crew

Lótessë 15, 2971

Quietly, Brynja made her way around belowdecks. No one seemed to mind her questions; most gave her a kind smile, amused by the eager child who wanted to know everyone's story. By the time she was done, the Eärlindë felt less like a mere ship than a roaming village of unusual friends.

She found the captain first, a tall Elf studying a star-chart near the helm, his lined face marked with old wisdom and faint scars. "Are you Calion?" she asked, tilting her head. He turned, and his eyes shone like distant stars. "I am. And you must be Brynja—it's an honor to have you aboard." He tapped the charts under his arm. "I'm a starseer. Some say the stars speak to me more than any compass; they've guided me through many a storm." — "So you see the future in them?" — "Not precisely. But sometimes they offer hints, if you've traveled as long as I have." An easy calm surrounded him, and it made her feel at once secure.

At the table beside him sat a dark-haired Elf, intricate tattoos coiling down his arms—dragons and beasts and strange fierce symbols. "You're Ithildur?" she asked in a smaller voice. "Aye. The navigator; I work close with the captain. You've noticed my markings." Brynja nodded, curious and a little nervous. "They're big. And scary. But kind of amazing." He ran a hand over a coiled beast on his forearm. "They mark foes I've faced—things touched by shadow, as I once was. But fear not," he added, softer. "The shadows I know cannot truly stand against the light." There was a sadness in it, but he gave her a kind nod, and she bobbed her head and moved on.

Past the helm she nearly walked into an Elf-woman hoisting a crate of supplies as though it weighed nothing. "Whoa," Brynja whispered. "You're really strong." The Elf grinned and set it down. "Call me Lindarë. Quartermaster of the Eärlindë—someone's got to track every rope, nail, and biscuit aboard, and haul them besides." Brynja's eyes went to the grappling hook at her belt. "You fight with that?" — "In close quarters. Surprises many a foe." She laughed, a warm friendly-giant sort of laugh.

Not far off an elderly Elf with honey-pale hair lounged against a rail, swirling a small flask, and swept Brynja a dramatic bow. "Good day, little lady. I'm the ship's surgeon and inveterate gossip—Nithrandir." She giggled. "Surgeon? For broken bones?" — "I set bones, stitch wounds, dabble in the apothecary's arts. A little skill and a gentle word both go far." He winked. "And the flask's for emergencies." Something in his easy banter made her shoulders relax.

She met the boatswain next, a slim Elf hung with rings, a vivid green bird on his shoulder. "What kind of bird is that?" — "Her name's Tuila. She keeps watch on the rigging with me. I'm Eärion—ropes, sails, anything that keeps us afloat. And discipline, though I mostly let Tuila squawk at the lazy ones." Tuila squawked on cue, and Brynja laughed. There was a lithe Elf-woman on a barrel with a pipe, an earthy smoke curling around her—"Name's Celebrin, deckhand; I can climb a mast faster than you can blink, and sing a tune that'd make a troll blush"—who warned with a wink that she had a colorful vocabulary that might shock Brynja's mothers. And from an open hatch, wreathed in the smell of fresh bread, a plump Elf in an apron waved a wooden spoon: "Ah, a little traveler! Galaniel, the cook. Fancy a taste?" He gave her a cup of stew and promised stories enough to lull anyone to sleep mid-sentence—starting with himself, he confided, who could doze off just about anywhere. She nearly choked on the stew laughing.

In a shady corner an Elf with an eyepatch watered a row of potted herbs, more gardener than sailor. "Are you growing plants?" — "I am. My name is Thalandir. Mostly hardy herbs—I keep something growing whenever I'm at sea. It reminds me life always finds a way." She thought that was beautiful. High in the crow's-nest she glimpsed a figure scanning the horizon, and later met the same Elf-woman swinging down the mast: "Voriel, the lookout. Nothing escapes these eyes, day or night—though I might play a prank or two if the voyage gets dull." Across the deck a thoughtful Elf sat scribbling in a small journal beside a set of carpenter's tools. "I keep a journal of omens and runes—hints of what may come. But mostly I do the carpentry, not the fortune-telling. Sometimes mending a thing is the best fortune-telling there is." His name was Mallor, and his calm sincerity soothed her.

On the foredeck a slender Elf-woman with silvery hair inspected a half-folded sail, her face grave. "Did you make these?" — "I did. My name is Elenmir. Mind the edges, child—these sails can't afford flaws in a storm." When Brynja ventured that she seemed strong, a fierceness flickered in her eyes and vanished. "I'd rather be alone with my stitching than raging in a battle. But if forced, I don't hold back." And last, near the prow, a tall Elf-woman moved through sword-forms with two runed blades, fluid and lethal. "Silivren, deckhand," she said, lowering them. "And you must be Brynja." — "Are those swords special?" — "Heirlooms, generations old. More than mere steel. But I keep them sheathed unless there's real danger." — "I hope we won't need them." Silivren gave a small chuckle. "So do I."

After meeting them all, Brynja retreated to her favorite nook near the bow, thoughts awhirl—a whole new world of friends, each with their own gifts and quirks and stories. Her parents found her there, swinging her legs, and Celenneth tousled her hair while Linnea lifted Hallas a little so Brynja could see him blink awake. "You've been busy," Linnea teased. "Making new friends?"

Brynja's eyes shone. "Yes! I'm so glad they're all here!"1

9The Reef

Lótessë 24, 2971

Celenneth stood at the rail of the Eärlindë, watching the Grey Havens dwindle and vanish behind them as the ship cut into open water. She had roamed wild hills and dark forests all her life, yet the ocean's vastness felt new and unsettling. Ahead lay three weeks and more of salt wind and rolling swell.

At first the sailing was calm enough to let her ease. Brynja perched along the deck's edge with Linnea close at hand, and Hallas drowsed in a sling across Celenneth's chest, his soft breathing a gentle counterpoint to the crew's movements. Calion kept a watchful eye on the horizon while Ithildur bent over his charts, the star-etched instruments glinting. All seemed in order—for a time.

Then, one morning, the weather turned fast: dark clouds marching across the sky, the swell rising to a heave that rocked the Eärlindë harder with every hour. Celenneth braced at the rail, swallowing down the queasiness, and caught sight of Linnea holding a pale, frightened Brynja close, and Eryndil moving calm from soul to soul with quiet words and water. Her own stomach knotted with each lurch and plunge; she told herself she had endured worse on land—orcs, the bitter storms of the Frost Maw—but here there was no ground, only water that obeyed no mortal will. At last the seas calmed, and she joined Linnea in soothing Brynja that the worst had passed.

Not two days later a new peril rose. Ithildur marked an ominous pattern in the water—a reef beneath the surface, betrayed only by the strange breaking of the waves. The crew fell into a tense, practiced dance: Calion slowed the ship; Eryndil took the bow, her sharp eyes hunting hidden rock; Lindarë set the deckhands to sail and line to weave a course between the teeth of it. Celenneth handed Hallas to Linnea and rushed forward to help with the ropes, sweat at her temple, fixed on each shouted command. For a while it seemed they might pass clean through—until the wind shifted without warning, and the ship jolted against a submerged rock. A long scraping shriek tore through the hull, the deck shuddering beneath her boots, and she gripped the rail hard as cries went up around her.

The Eärlindë steadied, but the harm was done. Calion and Ithildur wore grim faces as they confirmed it, and Mallor came up from below with a carpenter's plain verdict: the repairs could not be made safely out on open water. Calion gathered the crew and spoke with quiet resolve—they would have to find a harbor to mend the hull. It meant a detour, and lost time, but there was no help for it if they wished to sail on without risking a deadly leak.

Celenneth exhaled, aware of Linnea steady at her side, of the worry in Brynja's eyes and the confusion in Hallas's small whimper, and of the calm resolve in Eryndil's face. When the ship came about she felt a pang of frustration—every day's detour meant slower progress toward the distant island Círdan had set them to find. But after a few tense days land rose on the horizon: rocky cliffs cradling a natural bay, sheltered enough for the Eärlindë to anchor without fear of the wound in her hull. Celenneth let out a slow breath. Linnea lifted Brynja high to see the coastline, and Hallas woke from his nap to gaze about with wide, wondering eyes.1
