1Eorlas

Úrimë 2, 2965

They set out under a cool morning sun with the dew still bright on the grass, and the land rose gently ahead of them into rolling hills stood about with ancient trees. Nimroch went steady, his pale coat gleaming, and for a while the road was kind — birdsong, an unmarked way that asked Celenneth's sense of direction, a narrowing of the path where the slopes went to loose stone and bramble and she dismounted to lead the horse around the worst of it, losing little time. It was the sort of travelling she liked, and she had a companion now who was learning to like it too; Linnea rode easier every day, her eye going to the land the way Celenneth's did, picking up the habit of watchfulness without being taught it.

In the afternoon they crested a hill and saw a figure far off — a lone traveller in a patchwork cloak, walking with a weary, steady gait, a staff tapping out his pace. Linnea glanced at Celenneth, curious and wary both. But the traveller saw them and raised a hand in plain greeting, and when they came together he proved an old man with sharp eyes and a kind mouth, who named himself Eorlas, a wandering herbalist out after rare plants for his healing salves.

They slowed to his pace and fell to talking, and there was more to his wandering than herbs. He was bound for Dale with a message — the villages along the forest's edge had suffered raid on raid from goblins, and were desperate for supplies and allies, and he hoped the folk of Dale might answer. When they stopped by a shaded brook he unwrapped a bundle of dried herbs and showed Linnea how to know the useful ones, and his knowledge of the country ran deep: bogs in the north-east that swallowed the careless, ruins where the dead did not rest, and — this caught Celenneth's ear — a rumour of an orc camp near the south-western eaves of Mirkwood.

She pressed him on the orcs, for it fit too well with what Radagast had warned of. He had seen their smoke, he said, and heard foul chanting on his night-walks after herbs; frail as he looked, there was a sharp and steady competence in him, and Celenneth marked it. It was Linnea, taken with his herb-lore, who asked if he would travel with them a while — and then looked to Celenneth, half-afraid she had overstepped. But Celenneth only nodded, and Eorlas said, "I accept," and it was done.1

Before they went on, the old man pressed a sprig into Celenneth's hand — narrow leaves, a clean sharp scent. "Take this. Athelas — kingsfoil, some call it. No common weed. Bruised and steeped in boiling water, the very smell of it can lift the heaviness of despair from a heart the Shadow has touched." She thanked him and stowed it careful, and thought of the dreams, and of the cold the deep and the road had left in her, and was glad of it.2

As they walked he went on teaching — Elvenstar Blossom and how to harvest it, a thin spiky leaf that would dull pain if handled right and sting worse than a wasp if not — and handed Linnea a small satchel to gather samples under his eye. Linnea drank it in, her face alight, and Celenneth watched her and saw what it did for her: with every leaf named, every small competence gained, she was becoming a contributor to this road and not only a passenger on it, and she knew it, and it straightened her back.

The hills gave way to denser wood, the air cooling, the smell of moss coming up around them. Eorlas crouched over broken earth. "Boar," he muttered, reading the deep prints. "We'd best keep clear — they're ill-tempered this time of year." By dusk they came to the ruined watchtower he had spoken of, crumbled and overgrown but sound enough to shelter in, its stones still holding the day's warmth, and they laid their bedrolls in its lee while Eorlas coaxed a fragrant tea from the day's gathering.

Around the fire he spoke of his hopes for Dale. "There's strength in that people. But they must see what's at stake. Let the Shadow get a foothold in the south, and it won't stop there."

Celenneth nodded into the flames. "Dale must be warned. But Radagast spoke of more than goblins. Something greater's stirring."

The camp settled, the fire fell to embers, and Eorlas sprawled snoring gently on his bedroll while the wood rustled and the stars threw their faint silver over the broken stones. And Linnea moved quietly closer to where Celenneth sat working her whetstone along the blade, and knelt, and hesitated — and then, with a shy downward glance, leaned in and stole a kiss, brief and soft, barely a brush of lips. Celenneth's surprise melted to warmth, and her hand rose to Linnea's cheek.

"I'll take first watch," Linnea whispered, quiet but resolved. "You've carried so much already. Rest. I'll wake you at the first whisper of trouble."

The old instinct pulled at Celenneth — to keep the watch herself, as she always had — but she looked at the woman's steady face and let it go, and nodded. "Wake me if you hear even that much," she said, her eyes staying on Linnea a moment longer than they needed to. Linnea smiled, sat straighter for the trust of it, and took her place at the edge of the ruins; and she watched the ranger drift down into a sleep she had not had to guard alone, and the forest hummed its quiet night around them both.

2A Bloody Discovery

Úrimë 3, 2965

The camp stirred at dawn — Eorlas stretching and wincing at the stone bed, Linnea humming as she repacked her herbs, Celenneth already up and reading the treeline out of habit. They ate quick and went on, the air cool and clean, and Eorlas led them onto a faint deer-trail that promised a swifter way through the wood. It kept its promise; the trail slid around the hard ground, Nimroch went easy, and the morning smelled of pine and wet earth as they wove between the trees. They passed an old stone marker whose weathered runes spoke of a road that had once joined Mirkwood to Dale — the bones of a busier age — and Eorlas stopped to name bright flowers against the green while Linnea gathered samples under his eye, surer with every lesson. By dusk the forest thinned and the rolling plains showed gold in the distance.

They camped in a quiet glade with the plains glowing in the last light and the wind bringing wildflower-scent off them, and while Eorlas set a small fire and hummed some old tune, Linnea came to Celenneth with a question in her.

"You've carried that bow since we met." She nodded at it. "I've watched how you use it. Could I learn?"

Celenneth smiled, the warmth breaking through the sternness. "Every ranger must know the bow. Come."

She named its parts, and let Linnea feel its weight, and drew an arrow and ran her fingers down the fletching. "The shaft straight, the feathers true. Everything working together, or it fails in the air." In the fading light she set the woman's stance with gentle hands. "Feet apart, steady. You don't just hold the bow — it should feel like an extension of your own body." Linnea nocked and drew slow. "Elbow high. Breathe with the tension. Don't think of the arrow — think of where you want it to go."

Linnea sighted a distant tree, breathed out, loosed — and the arrow sailed wide and buried itself short in the earth. She huffed; Celenneth laughed, low and kind. "No one strikes true the first time. It wants patience. Again." And again, and with each try the woman's form smoothed and settled, until Eorlas called from the fire, amusement in his eye, "A good teacher, and a stubborn student both," and at last an arrow bit the trunk with a solid thunk, and Linnea spun round grinning. "I did it!"

"You did." Pride, plain in Celenneth's face. "Tomorrow again. And the day after. Until it's as natural to you as your own hands."

The next morning began wrong. The birdsong was gone, and in its place black birds wheeled and cried overhead, their harsh caws breaking the dawn, and the sound of them put a shiver down Linnea's back as she rolled her bedroll. Eorlas sat with his staff across his knees and watched them, and the calm went out of his face. Celenneth came to his shoulder, hand at her sword. "What do you see?"

"Carrion crows." Grim. "But not just feeding. See how they hold the circle? Something in the land ahead is drawing them."

They broke camp quick and subdued and went on toward the birds, Celenneth ahead with her bow strung, Linnea and Nimroch close, Eorlas at the rear with his eyes on the sky. Where the path bent toward the wheeling crows she stopped and put out a hand. "Wait here. I'll see what draws them."

Linnea's face pinched, but she nodded. "Be careful."

Celenneth stroked Nimroch's neck once and slipped into the trees, and the crows' crying grew louder as she came to it. The wood opened on a small clearing and a shattered wagon lying overturned, its frame splintered as though struck by something with no patience — and around it three bodies, two men and a woman, clothes torn, the ground gone dark with old blood, the earth churned with the signs of a struggle. She crouched among them and read it. A few days dead, faces gone pale. And the wounds were wrong — not brigand-work, not beast: jagged, deep, deliberate, savage. And there was a deeper wrongness under that, one that raised the hair on her arms: the scavengers would not touch them. The crows cried over the dead and would not come down to feed.

She rose slow, and her eye went to the clearing's edge, where tracks led into the brush — orc-prints, unmistakable in their crookedness, and mixed among them something larger, clawed and heavy, and drag-marks in the disturbed earth where something, or someone, had been pulled away alive. The crows were not mourning. They were warning.

She went after it careful, all her senses up, following broken foliage and deep prints deeper into the wood until the canopy closed and the crows' cries fell behind. The trail brought her to a thicket, and she looked through it onto a crude camp: three orcs around a dying fire, harsh with laughter, and a great warg prowling restless at the edge of the light, its yellow eyes hunting the dark. And bound and gagged against a tree — a boy and an older man, bruised but living, their eyes wide and casting about the camp.

She breathed, and steadied, and marked the warg for the first shaft — the one threat that might scent her or run her down — and while the orcs laughed on, oblivious, she loosed. The arrow went through the beast's skull clean, and it dropped near the fire without a sound, and the orcs never turned. She held her place. One rose and moved off from the fire, and she drew again and put the shaft through his throat, and he crumpled, silenced. Then she came in on soft feet, and drawing her sword she stepped up behind the second and drew the edge across its throat, clean — and the last one knew her at last and snatched up an axe and swung it heavy at her, and she slipped the blow, the blade whistling past, and pivoted and let her sword arc round and find its mark. The orc fell, and the wood went still but for the fire's small crackle.1

The captives stared at her out of desperate eyes. She went to them and cut the bonds and looked over their hurts. "You're safe now. Can you walk?" The boy nodded, legs trembling; the older man grimaced at his ribs but rose with her arm under him.

She knelt by the man as he caught his breath. "Who were they? Why take you?"

"Orcs." A rasp. "Out of the hills. We were bound for Dale — merchants, my brother and his wife and the two of us." His voice broke; he gestured weakly at the clearing. "They killed them. Took what they wanted, dragged us here. I think they meant to — to eat us."

The boy, barely lifting his eyes, said, "They spoke of others. Bigger plans. Said their leader would pay them for bringing captives." He shuddered. "We thought we were dead."

Celenneth's frown deepened. Orcs this far east was ill news enough; orcs taking prisoners, for a leader, spoke of something larger behind them.

She had no time to ask more, for Nimroch nickered soft behind her, and she turned to see Linnea and Eorlas come into the clearing — and Linnea's eyes went wide at the sight of it, three orcs dead and the warg's bulk among them and the blood on the ground. "Celenneth." Horror in it. "What have you done?"

Eorlas took it in grim and went straight to the boy and the man, low-voiced, his hands already at their hurts. But Linnea came straight to Celenneth, pale with fury. "You went in alone. You didn't tell us. You could have been killed. What were you thinking?"

Celenneth sheathed the sword, and the guilt showed in her even as she answered. "I couldn't wait. They had prisoners. If I'd hesitated—" she nodded at the two survivors.

"That's not the point." Linnea's voice shook. "You gave us no chance to help. And if you hadn't come back? What would we have done then?"

The clearing went quiet but for the fire and the murmured thanks of the rescued. Eorlas glanced at the two women and kept to his work. Linnea's hands trembled where she crossed her arms. "You can't carry the whole weight of it alone, Celenneth. Let us help you."

And Celenneth met her eyes, and the hardness went out of her. "You're right," she said, low. "I acted rashly. But I couldn't let them die."

The anger bled out of the moment, and she took charge again, calm and sure. "Eorlas — take these two to safety. Make for Dale. They need care, and you'll go faster on foot."

Eorlas hesitated, glancing at the horse. "And Nimroch?"

She laid a hand on his neck. "He stays with us. We may need to move quick, if this turns dire."

Eorlas nodded, reluctant, and helped the boy find his feet while the older man leaned on his staff; and with a last look at Celenneth he began to guide them away. "Don't linger long," he said.

3The Orcs’ Plot

Úrimë 5, 2965

Linnea turned to Celenneth, her anger giving way to determination. "So," she said firmly. "What’s the plan?"

Celenneth adjusted her cloak, checked her bowstring. "We track the rest of them. Quietly. Look for signs of their leader, their numbers. If they’re preparing for something bigger, we need to know before Dale."

Linnea nodded, her fingers brushing Nimroch’s mane, drawing comfort from the steed’s calm presence.

The two women, with Nimroch trailing, moved carefully through the forest. Celenneth led the way, her instincts guiding them as they followed the faint trail of clawed footprints and broken foliage. Nimroch moved with surprising quiet, his steps barely rustling the underbrush.

The deeper they went, the darker the forest grew. Trees stood gnarled and twisted, their branches clawing at the sky. The air felt heavy, as if the land itself recoiled from the presence of the Shadow.

After nearly a few hours of tracking, they found another clearing. A crude totem dominated it—a grisly structure of bones and tattered banners, with orcish runes scrawled across its base. The stench of decay hung thick in the air.

Celenneth crouched, examining the ground. "This isn’t just a raiding party," she muttered. "This is a staging ground. They’re organizing."

Nearby, the faint sound of guttural voices drifted through the trees. Celenneth gestured for Linnea to stay low as she edged closer, peering through the underbrush. A small group of orcs sat around a crude map laid out on the ground, their conversation animated but unintelligible.

Nimroch lowered his head, his ears flicking at the sound, sensing the danger yet trusting his companions.

From her concealed position, Celenneth listened intently. Though the words were harsh and fragmented, her limited knowledge of orcish helped her piece together the general meaning.

The orcs spoke of an upcoming raid—aimed at a nearby village to capture more prisoners. They mentioned a leader named Grahnak, amassing forces in the region. Their orders were clear: capture, spread fear, leave survivors to tell the tale.

One word stood out like a sharp blade: Dol Guldur. The orcs spoke the name with reverence and fear, suggesting their orders came from the corrupted stronghold itself.

Celenneth retreated to Linnea and Nimroch, her expression grim. "They’re planning a raid. Soon. Their leader is Grahnak, and..." She hesitated, the weight of her discovery heavy. "They spoke of Dol Guldur. If that place is involved, this isn’t just about Dale—it’s about shadow spreading over the entire region."

Linnea’s face paled, but she squared her shoulders. "What do we do?"

Celenneth glanced back toward the orcs. "We can’t let them warn their leader. If we strike now, we might delay their plans. If we’re lucky, we might find something that points to Grahnak’s location."

Linnea shakes her head. "Let's avoid direct conflict. Take their map, if you can."

Celenneth nodded and moved carefully through the underbrush, her steps light and deliberate. Her breathing was steady, her instincts guiding her every move. The orcs had moved away, and the map, drawn hastily but detailed enough to be useful, lay within reach. With one swift motion, she scooped up the parchment and retreated into the shadows, the orcs none the wiser.

Back with Linnea and Nimroch, Celenneth unrolled the map, scanning its contents. It marked several locations: the staging ground they had found, a larger camp further east—a potential hideout for Grahnak—and a crude drawing of a village, likely their next target.

Linnea looked at her with a mix of admiration and lingering worry. "What now?"

Celenneth’s expression was determined. "We find the village first, and warn them; otherwise they'll be defenseless. Then we take this to Dale. They need to understand the threat."

Linnea nodded, mounting Nimroch swiftly behind Celenneth. The ranger spurred the horse into motion, and they plunged into the forest at a gallop. Nimroch’s hooves thundered against the earth, his swift strides eating up the distance as branches whipped past.

Urgency filled the air as they raced through the woods. The map’s markings suggested the village wasn’t far—perhaps an hour’s hard ride. The terrain grew rougher, exposed roots and rocky patches forcing Celenneth to guide Nimroch carefully even at speed.

4Defense of the Village

Úrimë 5, 2965

The smell of smoke reached them first, carried on the wind. Cresting a hill, they saw the village ahead: a cluster of cottages surrounded by fields. Farmers moved about, unaware.

Celenneth and Linnea rode straight into the village center, their urgency drawing the attention of the villagers. A stout man with a hoe slung over his shoulder stepped forward, frowning. “What’s this about?”

Celenneth dismounted, her voice sharp. “You’re in danger. Orcs are coming. They’ll be here soon.”

The villagers exchanged nervous glances, murmurs rippling through the crowd. The man frowned. “Orcs? Here? We’ve heard rumors, but they’ve never come this far.”

“They are coming,” Linnea added, her voice steady. “You need to prepare—barricade your homes, arm yourselves.”

The man, Aldric, nodded grimly. “We’ve got a few old swords and hunting bows, nothing much. But we’ll fight if we have to. What should we do?”

Celenneth nodded. “We don’t have time to get reinforcements. We’ll help you prepare to defend the village.”

Linnea dismounted and immediately set to work alongside Celenneth, her worry turning into determination. Celenneth quickly assessed the village layout—scattered homes, a small central square, nearby fields. Modest, but the people were resolute.

Celenneth stepped into the role of leader, her voice steady. “Gather anything that can be used as a weapon—axes, pitchforks, knives. Create barricades at the edge of the square and block open paths between the buildings.”

She scanned the fields, pointing to a line of low hedges at the village perimeter. “Dig shallow trenches behind those hedges and put in some sharpened stakes. It won’t stop the orcs, but it’ll slow them.”

Linnea helped fortify homes, nailing shutters closed and bracing doors. Her gentle words calmed frightened children, and her steady hands guided those overwhelmed by the preparations.

Celenneth led a group of able-bodied villagers to set traps along the likely approach routes. Using rope, spikes, and fallen branches, they hid snares and snap-traps. The work was hard and hurried but efficient.

Aldric returned with a small collection of weapons: two rusted swords, a few hunting bows, and a quiver of mismatched arrows. Other farmers had brought a mixture of pitchforks and scythes. Celenneth inspected them grimly. “Not much,” she muttered. “But it’ll have to do.”

As the sun dipped below the horizon, the preparations were complete. The villagers gathered in the square, their faces pale but resolute. Celenneth moved among them, offering quiet words of encouragement.

Linnea stood beside her, holding a hunting bow, her grip tight. “Do you think it’ll be enough?” she asked softly.

Celenneth glanced at the darkening forest. “It has to be.”

As the villagers finalized preparations, Celenneth approached Linnea, her expression a mix of resolve and concern. “You don’t have to stay for this,” she said. “You can take Nimroch, get a head start for Dale."

Linnea met her gaze, eyes fierce. “No. These people need all the help they can get. I’m staying.”

Celenneth studied her for a moment, then nodded. “Very well. But stay out of harm’s way. You can make a difference with a bow. Use what you've learned.”

Linnea took one of the hunting bows Aldric brought. “I’ll stake out on that roof,” she said, nodding toward a sturdy cottage near the village square. “It’ll give me a clear view of the square and the approaches.”

The villagers, armed with their mismatched weapons, stood in tense groups behind the barricades. The air was thick with anticipation, every rustle of wind or crack of a branch setting them on edge.

Then, from the forest, a guttural roar broke the silence, followed by the crash of heavy footsteps. The orcs had arrived.

The orcs emerged from the darkness, hulking and grotesque, their crude weapons glinting in the torchlight. The villagers gasped, some stepping back, but Celenneth’s sharp command steadied them. “Hold! Wait for my signal!”

Linnea watched from the roof, her bowstring taut. As the orcs charged the barricades, Celenneth gave a sharp cry. “Loose arrows!”

Linnea released her shot, the arrow flying true into the fray below. Her vantage point gave her an advantage, allowing her to target orcs advancing on weaker points in the defenses.

As the orcs entered the village, Linnea kept a sharp eye on the chaos from her perch. Her training with Celenneth served her well; several of her arrows struck their intended targets, harrying the orcs as they navigated traps and barricades. The villagers fought bravely, but gaps formed in the line.

Linnea saw an orc break past, charging toward a young boy who had armed himself with a pitchfork. The boy froze, terror in his eyes as the brute raised a crude axe.

Not having time to think, or aim, Linnea drew her bowstring, releasing an arrow. Her sheer intensity of focus on the orc saw the arrow fly true, striking the orc squarely in the chest, giving it pause, before Celenneth struck it down. The child scrambled back, sobbing but alive.

Linnea exhaled shakily, her heart pounding. From the square, Celenneth looked up and gave a nod of approval before turning back to the fight.

Near the center of the barricade, Celenneth commanded the villagers with precision, her sword flashing in the torchlight. The orcs were relentless, but the barricades had mostly held—until a massive orc, clad in scavenged armor and wielding a brutal spiked mace, charged forward.

With a roar, the orc champion smashed through a section of the barricade, sending villagers scattering. Celenneth stepped forward to meet the beast, her blade steady. The clash was fierce—each swing of the orc’s mace crashed against Celenneth’s sword, forcing her to dodge and counter quickly.

The villagers rallied behind her, keeping lesser orcs from swarming her position. With a calculated feint, Celenneth sidestepped a powerful swing and drove her sword deep into the champion’s side. The orc staggered, roaring in pain, before collapsing with a heavy thud.

Her victory sent a wave of renewed determination through the villagers, who pushed harder to repel the remaining attackers.

As the fight raged on, the orc numbers dwindled. The traps, barricades, and villagers’ courage—bolstered by Linnea and Celenneth’s leadership—turned the tide. The remaining orcs, realizing their losses, began to retreat into the forest.

The villagers, too spent from the fight, and their numbers diminished by the wounded, did not give chase, but instead slowly gathered in the square, their faces a mixture of exhaustion and disbelief.

Linnea stepped forward, on the roof lowering her bow and addressing the crowd. Her voice was steady, carrying warmth and encouragement despite the chaos. “You stood your ground,” she began, her tone firm but kind. “You fought with courage and strength. You protected your home and each other.”

The villagers, still catching their breath, looked to her with growing confidence. She continued, gesturing to the barricades and battlefield. “The Shadow will think twice before coming here again. Your bravery will echo in the hearts of anyone who dares threaten you.”

A cheer rose, hesitant at first, then building into a triumphant cry. The villagers clapped one another on the back, the fear and tension of the night melting into pride and relief.

From the edge of the square, Celenneth watched with a faint smile. She nodded to Linnea, her approval clear. The young woman’s words had brought the villagers a sense of closure and strength, solidifying their resolve.

Night settled over the village, quieter now after the battle. The villagers offered Celenneth and Linnea a place to rest—a modest but comfortable room in one of the cottages. Despite the aches from the fight and the weight of their mission, sleep came quickly, the exhaustion of the day washing over them.

Linnea, still processing the events, laid close to Celenneth. “I never thought I’d face something like this,” she whispered as they lay side by side. “But you... you made it seem possible. Do you think this truly made a difference? Do you think the orcs will return?”

Celenneth reached out, taking Linnea’s hand. “You raised their spirits with your speech,” she replied softly. “I don't know if the orcs will return; I think they'll certainly think twice about it. But what's more important is what you've done for the village: these people will remember what they accomplished—what we helped them achieve.”

5Catching up with Eorlas

Úrimë 6, 2965

At dawn, after a quick meal and farewells to the villagers, Celenneth and Linnea mounted Nimroch. The villagers sent them off with hopeful smiles and thanks, their spirits lifted despite the Shadow looming beyond the forest.

The pair rode hard, the path bringing them closer to the dense trees of Mirkwood with each passing hour. By midday, they spotted Eorlas on the road ahead. When he heard Nimroch’s approach, he turned, relief evident.

“You made it,” he said, his voice warm. “And you’re in one piece. What of the village?”

“Safe,” Celenneth replied, dismounting and clasping his shoulder. “The orcs were repelled, but there's more to their plans than we thought. We need to reach Dale quickly.”

Eorlas nodded, his expression darkening. “Then let’s not waste time.”

The open countryside was left behind as Mirkwood's canopy enveloped them. The air was heavy, carrying the scent of decay. Shadows stretched long, even at midday, the dense forest muffling all sound except for Nimroch’s hooves and the occasional snap of a twig.

Eorlas paused at the forest’s edge, his staff tapping the mossy ground. “Mirkwood,” he murmured. “Alive—but never comforting.”

Nimroch moved carefully over gnarled roots and uneven ground, while Eorlas paused to inspect strange fungi growing along the path.

The path became more treacherous, the forest floor slick with moss. Gnarled roots twisted like buried giants' fingers. Celenneth reined in Nimroch as the trail descended into a swamp filled with stagnant water. The air thickened, and the buzzing of insects grew louder.

Eorlas knelt at the water's edge. “The forest shifts. Paths change. We must tread carefully.”

Celenneth took the lead, guiding Nimroch over the firmest ground and avoiding deep puddles. Eorlas followed, his staff probing ahead. Linnea kept close, her bow ready.

“Where is the trail?” Eorlas murmured, noting how it had seemed to fade into the stagnant water.

“I see where it picks up,” Celenneth replied quietly. “Stick to me.”

When they finally cleared the swamp, they found themselves on a small rise, the canopy parting to reveal a sliver of darkening sky. The party took a moment to rest, the air still heavy but the ground mercifully dry. Linnea sat beside Nimroch, running her fingers through his mane, while Eorlas inspected his mud-caked boots.

“Well done,” Eorlas said to Celenneth, respect clear. “Many would be lost back there.”

Celenneth nodded, her gaze already scanning the path ahead. “Mirkwood doesn’t suffer the unprepared.”

As twilight fell, the group made camp in a small, dry clearing. Linnea sat cross-legged near the flames, frowning at the mud on her boots. Eorlas joined her, carrying strange, twisted plants he'd picked during their trek.

“You’ve been learning well,” Eorlas said, his tone conversational but purposeful. “But there's more to herbs than healing. Sometimes, what seems harmful can be useful.”

Linnea looked up, intrigued but skeptical. “You mean poisons?”

Eorlas chuckled softly, selecting a dark, thorny plant. “Yes, and no. Poison is only poison when misunderstood. Watch.”

Eorlas laid out the plants, pointing to each. “This one,” he said, holding a dark leaf. “Eaten raw, causes pain and fever. But boiled with water and mixed with honey, it soothes pain.”

Linnea leaned closer, her skepticism fading. “How do you know how much is safe?”

“Experience,” Eorlas replied simply, plucking a plant with red berries. “Deadly in large quantities. But dried and ground, a pinch helps the heart.”

He demonstrated, grinding leaves and boiling them in a tin pot over the fire. Linnea watched intently, mimicking his movements with her own samples.

When the lesson ended, Eorlas poured the cooled liquid into a small wooden cup and offered it to Linnea. “Here. Drink.”

Linnea hesitated, eyeing the concoction. “You’re sure this won’t kill me?”

Eorlas smiled. “Quite sure. Bitter, but it eases fatigue.”

Trusting him, Linnea sipped, her face scrunching at the taste. “Awful,” she muttered, but moments later, she sat straighter, her weariness lifting. “But it works.”

Celenneth reined Nimroch to a halt, her hand instinctively going to her sword. Linnea dismounted cautiously, her bow ready. "Who are you?" Celenneth called, her tone firm but not unkind.

The figure lifted his head, revealing a young man with sunken eyes and a pale, sweat-slicked face. "Help me," he gasped, his voice hoarse. "Please... they're coming."

Celenneth's eyes narrowed as she scanned the horizon. "Who's coming?"

The man's gaze darted behind him, his expression wild. "Wolves," he whispered. "Not... not normal wolves."

The sound of howls echoed across the plain, corroborating his report. Linnea glanced nervously at the man, then at the horizon.

"What do we do?" Linnea asked, her voice steady but tense.

Celenneth didn't hesitate. "Linnea, get him on Nimroch," she commanded, her voice sharp and steady. "We'll move fast."

Linnea nodded and hurried to the man's side. He groaned in pain as she helped him to his feet, but with her support, he managed to climb onto Nimroch's back. Celenneth steadied the horse, murmuring soothingly as the man slumped forward, gripping the saddle weakly.

The howls grew louder, closer, their unnatural tone unmistakable. Shadows flickered along the distant hills as the wolves approached.

"We ride hard," Celenneth said, mounting Nimroch behind the man. She reached down, pulling Linnea up behind her with practiced ease. "Hold tight, and don't look back."

Nimroch surged forward, his powerful strides eating up the ground as the group raced away from the approaching wolves. The wind whipped past them, carrying the sound of snarling and snapping jaws. Linnea clung to Celenneth, her heart pounding as she risked a glance over her shoulder.

The wolves were gaining.

They were large, lean creatures with glowing red eyes and dark, matted fur. Their howls echoed like a chorus of nightmares, driving terror into the hearts of those who heard them. But Nimroch's speed was unmatched, his hooves pounding the earth in a steady rhythm.

Celenneth encouraged Nimroch onward, her voice firm and calm. The horse responded with unwavering strength, pushing harder even as the weight of three riders took its toll. The wolves were relentless, but the open ground offered no place to trap the group, and Nimroch's endurance began to wear them down.

Gradually, the howls faded into the distance, the wolves abandoning their chase as the group entered a rocky incline. Nimroch slowed, his breath heavy but even. Celenneth dismounted first, steadying the horse as Linnea helped the injured man down.

The man collapsed to his knees, gasping for breath. "Thank you," he croaked. "I thought... I thought I was done for."

Linnea knelt beside him, offering her waterskin. "What were those things?" she asked, her voice trembling slightly.

"Wargs," Celenneth said grimly, her sharp eyes scanning the horizon for any sign of pursuit. "They were hunting him—and now they know we're here."

Celenneth crouched beside the man, her expression sharp and focused. "You've got some explaining to do," she said firmly. "Who are you, and why were those wargs after you?"

The man drank deeply from Linnea's waterskin, his hands shaking as he handed it back. "My name is Taron," he said, his voice hoarse. "I'm just a merchant—or I was. I was traveling south, hoping to avoid the toll roads, but I took a wrong turn near the forest."

He paused, his face pale and drawn. "That's where I saw them. Not just the wargs—there were orcs too. They had a camp, they arguing about something. I tried to stay out of sight, but... I think they saw me."

Linnea frowned, her brow furrowed. "What were they arguing about?"

Taron shook his head. "I don't know, but it sounded like they were waiting for something—or someone. One of them mentioned Dale."

At this, Celenneth's expression hardened. "Dale?" she pressed.

Taron nodded, his gaze darting between them. "I didn't hear much else before the wargs started sniffing around. I ran, but they followed me all the way out of the forest. If you hadn't come along..."

Celenneth exchanged a look with Linnea, her jaw tightening. "If they're looking toward Dale, it means trouble."

Linnea nodded, her voice steady. "We need to get there fast. If they're planning something, we might be the only ones who can warn them in time."

Taron tried to rise but stumbled, his face pale with exhaustion. "I'll go with you," he said weakly. "I owe you my life."

"You'll slow us down," Celenneth said bluntly, though not unkindly. "If you want to help, tell us everything you know now and head east toward the river. It's safer there."

Taron hesitated, then nodded reluctantly. "There's not much more to tell. Just... be careful. They were organized and well armed."

Celenneth helped Taron to his feet, her expression firm but understanding. "Head east," she said, pointing toward the faint outline of a river in the distance. "You'll find safer roads there. Keep to the open lands and avoid the forest."

Taron hesitated, his gratitude evident in his weary gaze. "Thank you," he said earnestly.

Linnea handed him a small pouch of dried provisions. "This should keep you until you reach the river," she said. "Be careful."

With a final nod, Taron limped away. Celenneth watched him go until she was satisfied he was out of immediate danger. Then she turned back to Linnea, her resolve hardening.

"We ride for Dale," she said. "No delays."

Celenneth swung into Nimroch's saddle, pulling Linnea up behind her. The horse snorted, sensing the urgency in their movements, and set off at a swift pace. The open lands stretched out before them, the Lonely Mountain growing larger with every mile.

They rode through the night, breaking only to give Nimroch respite.

11Audience with the King

Úrimë 15, 2965

As the sun dipped above the horizon, the walls of Dale came into view, sturdy stone rising against the backdrop of the Lonely Mountain. Smoke drifted lazily from chimneys, and the distant hum of voices carried on the wind. The city appeared calm—for now.

Linnea exhaled sharply, relief mingling with urgency. "We made it," she said, her voice tight. "But what now?"

Celenneth slowed Nimroch as they approached the gates, her sharp gaze scanning the surroundings. "We find someone who will listen," she said. "And quickly."

The guards stationed at the gates watched them with curiosity and caution. The travelers' worn appearance contrasted with the bustling calm of the city, but Celenneth’s commanding presence gave them pause.

“We seek audience with the king,” Celenneth said firmly. “It’s urgent—Dale may be in danger.”

The guards exchanged wary glances. “And who might you be?” one asked.

“A ranger of the north, a Dúnedain” Celenneth replied, her tone brooking no argument. “This is Linnea, my companion. We’ve traveled far to bring this warning.”

The second guard frowned but nodded. “King Bain, holds court today. If your business is urgent, I’ll see that you’re brought before him. But tread carefully—he doesn’t take kindly to false alarms.”

The guards escorted Celenneth and Linnea through the gates and into the streets of Dale. The city bustled with activity: merchants peddled wares, children chased each other through alleys, and smiths hammered steel in preparation for winter. The sight of the Lonely Mountain towering in the distance served as a reminder of Dale’s resilience.

Linnea glanced around, her expression a mix of wonder and unease. “It’s so... alive,” she murmured. “After Mirkwood, it feels like another world.”

Celenneth nodded but remained focused. “Let’s hope it stays that way.”

They were led to a grand hall at the heart of the city. Inside, Bain, son of Bard, sat upon a modest throne, his presence commanding despite his youth. He rose as Celenneth and Linnea approached, his piercing gaze taking in their travel-worn state.

“You come with urgency,” he said. “Speak plainly—what brings you to Dale?”

Celenneth stepped forward, her posture steady. “Orcs,” she said without preamble. “And worse. They’re moving, and they’ve spoken of Dale. Whatever they’re planning, it involves this city.”

Bain’s brow furrowed, his expression darkening. “Orcs near Dale are not uncommon,” he said. “But your warning implies something greater. What evidence do you have?”

Linnea stepped forward, her voice steady despite her nerves. “We encountered a merchant—a man named Taron—who overheard their plans while fleeing from wargs. He spoke of orcs camped near Mirkwood, arguing about Dale. Their traps and numbers were organized, not random.”

Celenneth added, “This wasn’t a raid. They were waiting for something—or someone.”

Bain considered their words. “If what you say is true, Dale must be prepared,” he said. “But I need more than warnings to act. I cannot call my people to arms on fear alone.”

Celenneth reached into her pack and retrieved the map they had taken earlier. She stepped forward, unrolling it with deliberate care before Bain, her eyes meeting his with unwavering resolve.

“This is not mere rumor,” she said firmly. “This map was taken from orcs we encountered in Mirkwood. It details movements and paths leading toward Dale—and worse, toward Dol Guldur.”

At the mention of Dol Guldur, Bain’s expression tightened with concern. He leaned forward, studying the map intently. “Dol Guldur...” he murmured. “That name carries a weight no one in this land can ignore.”

Linnea stepped forward, her voice steady despite the gravity of the moment. “We overheard their talk—of Grahnak, their leader. Whatever they’re planning, Dol Guldur is at the heart of it.”

She glanced at Bain, her expression earnest. “We’ve seen their organization firsthand. They are preparing for something large, and this city is a target.”

Bain straightened, his gaze moving between the map and the two travelers. “Your words carry truth,” he said gravely. “And this map... if it is from Dol Guldur’s agents, then the threat is greater than I imagined.”

He paused, his hands gripping the arms of his chair. “Dol Guldur’s shadow has not stretched this far in years, but if it stirs again, Dale must be ready. I cannot ignore this.”

Bain rose, his presence commanding. “I will call my council and begin preparations for defense. But I need more information about the size and strength of this threat. If you are willing, I would ask you to scout their movements near Mirkwood. A small force will follow, should you find proof of an advancing host.”

He looked to Celenneth and Linnea, his gaze steady. “You’ve proven yourselves resourceful and brave. Will you take this task? Dale may depend on it.”

Linnea stepped forward, her voice clear but laced with weariness. “We are willing to take on this task, my lord,” she began, respectful but firm. “But we’ve ridden hard to reach you, and the road has tested us greatly. Both we and our horse need time to recover. If we were to ride out immediately, we would risk failure, and Dale cannot afford that.”

Bain regarded her thoughtfully, his expression softening. “Your honesty does you credit,” he said. “You’ve already gone to great lengths to deliver this warning. It would be unwise to send you out in such a state.”

He gestured to one of the guards. “See that they are given proper lodging and their horse is tended to. They will have what they need to recover.”

Turning back to Linnea and Celenneth, Bain added, “Rest well, for when you ride out again, the fate of Dale may rest on your shoulders.”

The guard led them through the streets of Dale to a modest but comfortable inn near the city center. The innkeeper, a stout woman with kind eyes, greeted them warmly after a quiet word from the guard. “You’ve rooms ready and a warm meal waiting,” she said. “And your horse will be seen to by the stablemaster.”

Celenneth nodded her thanks, her weariness beginning to show now that the immediate danger had passed. Linnea, though tired, looked around the bustling inn with quiet curiosity, unused to such comforts after weeks on the road.

After their meal—a hearty stew with fresh bread and ale—they retired to their room. Nimroch was already being tended to, and the sight of the horse comfortably stabled brought a measure of peace to Celenneth.

Linnea sat on the edge of her bed, her gaze drifting to the window overlooking the lively streets. “It feels strange,” she murmured, “to rest here after everything we’ve seen. Like the Shadow is just waiting for us beyond the walls.”

Celenneth placed a hand on her shoulder. “We're not just buying them time. We're stoking a fire that can dismiss the shadow."

12Dale

Celenneth and Linnea spent several weeks in Dale, using their time there to recover from the long journey and prepare for what lay ahead. The quietude of the city offered a rare respite, and they made full use of their comfortable lodgings to focus on the map they had taken from the orcs. Spread across a sturdy table, the map became their obsession, each line and symbol revealing new pieces of a puzzle that demanded solving.

Celenneth’s experience as a ranger proved invaluable. She traced the network of trails branching out from Dol Guldur, identifying paths that led to places of strategic interest—Dale, Lake-town, even Erebor. Some trails were old, worn by years of use, while others were fresh, suggesting recent activity. “These are supply lines,” she mused, her eyes narrowing as she pointed to one particularly bold trail. “But this one... it's too direct. They’re moving something—or someone—important along this path.”

Linnea sat beside her, her brows furrowed as she examined symbols etched near the map’s southern border. “What about these?” she asked, pointing. “They’re not roads, but they look deliberate.” Celenneth leaned closer. “Hidden camps, perhaps. Or strongholds.” Linnea nodded slowly, tracing her fingers over the parchment. “If they’re planning something against Dale, they’re using these places to gather strength. We could use that against them—if we know where to strike.”

As they worked, the map revealed a grim picture. Trails converged, forming a pattern that pointed northward. Celenneth’s expression darkened. “These aren’t just roads—they’re staging points. The orcs are organizing, and Dale is their target.” Linnea glanced at her, her voice quiet but resolute. “Then we need to stop them.” Celenneth nodded, rolling up the map with care. “And we will.”

During their time in Dale, Linnea focused on sharpening her skills. She visited the markets and local herbalists, learning to identify medicinal plants and create tinctures for common ailments. She practiced what Eorlas had taught her, gaining confidence in her abilities, and relayed his message to Elenna. Under Celenneth’s guidance, she also began to understand strategy—how to think critically about positioning, timing, and leveraging small advantages. Bain’s council meetings provided insight into the strategies for defending a city, and Linnea absorbed all she could, eager to grow.

Linnea’s horsemanship also improved. She spent time caring for Nimroch in the stables, learning from the stablemaster. Her riding became smoother, more in tune with the horse’s movements. She practiced archery in the training yards, competing with local archers and honing her precision under Celenneth’s watchful eye. Her aim grew sharper, her draw faster, until she could land her shots with accuracy even under pressure.

In the evenings, after the city had quieted, they often found themselves alone, sitting under the open sky or by the hearth in their shared quarters. Linnea hummed melodies she’d picked up from the locals, and Celenneth listened with a faint smile, her guarded expression softening. One night, as they gazed at the stars, Linnea leaned her head against Celenneth’s shoulder. “Do you ever wonder what it would be like,” she asked softly, “to live without all this? The Shadow, the danger... to just stay in one place?” Celenneth hesitated, her hand brushing lightly against Linnea’s. “I’ve never known that kind of life,” she admitted. “But... I think, with you, I might be able to imagine it. I'm just not sure how long I would be able to stay put.”

Linnea found herself taking more risks in expressing her affection. She brought Celenneth small surprises—flowers from the market, or a song to lift her spirits. Celenneth, though more reserved, returned the affection in quieter ways—checking Linnea’s gear before practice, ensuring she had enough rest, or simply being a steady presence when Linnea struggled with self-doubt. On Linnea’s more challenging days, Celenneth would place a reassuring hand on her shoulder and remind her, “You’ve come further than you think.”

Despite the looming threat, lightness was allowed to seep into their relationship. Linnea teased Celenneth for her seriousness, trying to coax laughter from her, while Celenneth countered with dry humor that often caught Linnea by surprise. One evening, Linnea stole Celenneth’s cloak while she was distracted, draping it over her shoulders and striking a mock-serious pose. “What do you think?” she joked. “Do I look like a ranger?” Celenneth chuckled softly. “You’d need more scowling for that.”

But as the weeks passed, the peace of Dale weighed heavily on Celenneth. With Bain’s blessing and a few of his most trusted scouts, she set out to confirm the locations of the orc rendezvous points marked near Mirkwood and Dol Guldur. The small group moved quietly through the wilderness, Celenneth leading them through thick forests and rocky terrain without detection. Bain’s scouts quickly deferred to her expertise.

They found the first rendezvous point nestled in the shadow of a craggy hill. Crude tents and a burnt-out fire lay scattered across the ground. Celenneth knelt, running her fingers over the trampled earth. “They moved recently,” she murmured, pointing to tracks leading east. “Perhaps a day or two ahead of us.” At another site near the forest’s edge, the group found signs of a hurried departure—discarded equipment and the faint, acrid smell of old fires. One of Bain’s scouts picked up a broken blade. “They’re rallying,” the scout said grimly. “These aren’t just raiding parties..”

Celenneth’s unease deepened as they moved south. Tracks converged in that direction, drawing lines of power back to the ancient stronghold. One night, while camped near an abandoned watchtower, Celenneth spotted movement in the distance—orc scouts, perhaps a dozen or more, traveling eastward. Their guttural voices carried through the stillness of the night, and Celenneth and her companions watched from the shadows, silent and unmoving. “They’re scouting us as much as we’re scouting them,” she whispered. “We’re running out of time.”

After several weeks of tracking, the group returned to Dale with their findings. Celenneth shared her report with Bain, spreading the map across the table in his hall. “They’re gathering strength near Dol Guldur,” she explained, tracing the converging paths. “If we don’t act, they’ll move north in force. Dale and Erebor will be their first targets.” Bain nodded solemnly, his brow furrowed. “We’ll prepare our defenses,” he said. “But this is larger than Dale. We need to reach out to our allies.”
