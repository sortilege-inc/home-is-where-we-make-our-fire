1Departure / Feystone's Gift

Narquelië 19, 2965

They left the woodland halls at first light, with the forest still under its dawn haze. Eryndor and Arannis walked them to the edge of it, their farewells quiet and warm; Arannis set her hands on Celenneth's shoulders and held her gaze. "The forest keeps you in its care, even as we let you go." Eryndor clasped Linnea's hands in both of his, his amber eyes bright with fondness and a little grief. "May your road run clear and your arrows fly true. We'll meet again when the winds bring you."

Thalion himself came leading a sleek chestnut mare. "Linnea. This is Mareth. She'll carry you swift and sure — and when your road passes beyond the forest, she'll come home to us with my scouts." Linnea brushed the mare's soft muzzle and murmured her thanks, and mounted; Celenneth swung up onto Nimroch, and the scouts fell in ahead.

They were two — Ellarien, lithe, her deep chestnut hair bound back, her armor woven in the muted greens and browns of Elvish craft so that she all but vanished among the trunks; and Thalanar, tall and rangy, his midnight hair in a loose braid over a young face gone prematurely weathered, a dark grey cloak at his shoulders worked with silver in the pattern of running water. The four of them found a rhythm quickly, and the scouts' quiet competence was a comfort on a road that gave few.

They had gone some way when Celenneth reined in. "Is it true there's a great hart in these woods?"

The scouts traded a glance, and Ellarien answered, almost reverent. "It is. Antlers wide as a king's crown. None have taken it."

Linnea grinned and nudged her. "A challenge?"

"A challenge," Celenneth agreed, already sliding from the saddle. She crouched to the tracks, read them, and rose sure of the line. For hours she followed it — the trail thinning and vanishing and surfacing again under her eye — until they came on it at last in a sunlit glade: a great hart at graze, its antlers hung with moss and small flowers, as though the wood itself had crowned it. She signed for silence, drew, breathed, and loosed. The arrow flew true. The hart faltered and fell, its death clean and quick.1

The scouts knelt beside it with words of respect. Celenneth laid a hand to its coat. "A worthy quarry." The meat they shared among them for the days ahead; the great rack of antlers they gave to Thalanar, a gift to carry back to the woodland.

Night drew the wood quiet, and by the fire Ellarien produced a worn leather pouch and a carved pipe, its bowl stained dark with years. "Time you saw how the Elves mark a good hunt," she said, tapping in a pinch of something fragrant — earthy, with a sharp citrus edge that cut through the smell of roasted meat.

"Pipeweed?" Linnea asked.

Ellarien laughed, rough and easy. "Not your Dale leaf. This is feystone's gift. Breathe it deep, and it takes you places you've never walked." She lit it, drew, and passed it round. Thalanar took a pull and exhaled with a grunt, his sharp features easing. "Not for the faint-hearted," he warned, handing it to Linnea.

Linnea drew tentatively, coughing, then found the rhythm of it, and the world tilted bright around her. The fire leapt in impossible shapes and she laughed, giddy. "The flames — they're alive."

"Good, isn't it," Ellarien said, and offered the pipe to Celenneth. "Watch your step, though. The visions can be kind, or they can be cruel."

Celenneth drew deep. At first it was warmth: the trees seemed to hum, their branches weaving patterns that hinted at some language just past knowing, and stars bloomed in the dark overhead like flowers. Then the warmth turned.

The stars go out. The dark comes swirling in to fill the space they leave, and the laughter she has been hearing curdles into screams. She is on her feet and no longer by the fire. The trees loom hostile, their gnarled limbs reaching for her like claws, and something moves between them — a shape she cannot fix, a presence that bears down on her like a weight she cannot get out from under. "Celenneth." Linnea's voice, far off and echoing — but when she turns there is no Linnea, only more shadow. A guttural whisper crawls the air, saying her name in a voice that lifts the hair on her arms, and the shape swells until it blots out the sky. "You cannot outrun the dark," it tells her, and it is not a threat but a certainty.

She came back to herself gasping, the firelight harsh on her tear-streaked face, her hand fisted white in Ellarien's sleeve. "The Shadow," she rasped. "It's coming."2

Across the fire Linnea's giddy laughter faltered. "What shadow?"

Ellarien's smile had gone. She guided Celenneth down to sit, her grip firm and kind. "Whatever you saw, it hasn't happened," she said. "Feystone's gift doesn't lie. But it doesn't quite tell the truth, either."

Celenneth stared into the flames, the warmth of the smoke gone, and only a cold certainty left where it had been: the dark they were running from was nearer than she had let herself believe.

Linnea reached for her, her hand hovering at Celenneth's shoulder. "It wasn't real. It was the smoke. You're safe here."

Celenneth went rigid under the touch and shrugged it off as though it burned. "I don't need comfort," she said, harder than she meant. "Real or not — what I saw was a warning. I can't set it aside."

Linnea drew back, her mouth thinning. "Fine. But you don't have to face it alone." When Celenneth gave no answer she sighed and went to her bedroll and lay down with her back to the fire.

The night dragged. Celenneth stayed by the flames, the whisper going round and round in her — you cannot outrun the dark — poking at the embers until they swirled up and died in the black. After a long while Thalanar broke the quiet without looking up from the blade he was sharpening. "You won't find answers in the fire."

"No," she said. "But I might in keeping watch."

He snorted. "You'll find exhaustion. A thing like what you saw feeds on it. Sleep, ranger."

"Not yet. I'll take the first watch."

His mouth twitched. "Stubborn," he muttered. "Fine. Just don't let it eat you alive."

She sat the watch alone, turning the vision over for meaning, and when Thalanar came to relieve her hours later she only said "your turn" and brushed past him to her bedroll, her shoulders tight. Sleep came slow, and thin, and she knew her strength would not hold forever against nights like this. But for now the fire burned, and Thalanar stood over it, and that was watch enough to close her eyes to.

2The Scouts' Paths

Narquelië 20, 2965

Morning came with an unnatural hush, the wood slow to stir after the uneasy night. Ellarien broke camp first, quick and quiet; Thalanar grumbled at the stiffness in his back; and Celenneth rose carrying the same tension she had lain down with, her eyes shadowed but sharp as she saw to Nimroch's tack. Linnea glanced her way and thought better of speaking, and saddled Mareth in silence.

The scouts took the lead, weaving through the underbrush with practiced ease, and the air felt lighter than the day before, as though the forest had granted a small reprieve. By midday the trees thinned and the ground began to climb, and Ellarien stopped and gestured them up a narrow trail that wound between ancient boulders. When they gained the ridge, the sight took their breath: the whole vast roll of Mirkwood spread below like a dark sea, and far off beyond it the faint gleam of Dale's towers under the sun, the horizon washed in pale gold and silver. For a moment the weight of the forest felt like something they had already left behind.

Celenneth stood a little apart, her hand on Nimroch's flank, her thoughts still snarled in the vision. Linnea came to stand beside her without a word and held out a piece of dried fruit. After a long moment Celenneth took it. "Thank you," she said quietly. Linnea only nodded — an acknowledgment that asked for nothing more.

The descent was steep and slick, and the deeper they went the darker it grew again, until by the time they reached level ground the full weight of Mirkwood had settled back over them. But the night passed in a rare calm, and Celenneth lay awake a while turning over the small kindnesses of the day — Linnea's, and the scouts' — their weight unfamiliar and not unwelcome, before sleep took her at last, and for once it was dreamless.

Narquelië 21, 2965

Ellarien led them off at a game trail that cut west through the densest part of the wood. "The deer know the way," she said. "If we hold it, it saves us a day." The trail was narrow and root-choked, and Thalanar warned them to watch their footing — this near the heart of the forest, the ground had a way of swallowing careless feet — but the scouts moved silent and sure, and the party made good time. At midday they watered the horses at a stream, and Linnea passed Celenneth a waterskin, and the exchange was wordless and easy. The shortcut held its promise, cutting a full day from their road, and they made camp at dusk in a clearing Thalanar judged good ground.1

Narquelië 22, 2965

By mid-morning the game trail failed them: a wall of bramble and low branch, an hour's backtrack at least. Thalanar cursed and hauled at a thorned vine that would not give.

"Wait," Celenneth said, and dismounted. She read the ground — the subtle dips in it, the faint places where deer-tracks veered sharply left — and a slow smile came. "There's a way through. Narrow, but it saves us the hours."

Ellarien tilted her head, approving. "Lead on, ranger."

They led the horses through by hand, brambles clawing their cloaks, and came out on the far side onto the main trail with the day barely dented. "You've an eye for what the wood hides," Thalanar allowed.

"You learn to see what it wants you not to," Celenneth said, and swung back up.

They made camp that evening in a hollow ringed by old oaks, and while the others saw to the fire Celenneth came to where Linnea sat working a whetstone along her blade, and stood a moment gathering herself before she spoke.

"I owe you an apology," she said. Linnea looked up, brows drawing together. "For how I've been. Cold. Shut against you. It wasn't fair."

Linnea set the blade aside. "You've carried a lot. I understand."

"No." Celenneth held her eyes. "You've been patient. Kind. More than I've given back. And I'm grateful for it. Truly." Before Linnea could answer she stepped in and gathered her into her arms, and Linnea stiffened, and then melted into it, her hands coming to rest on Celenneth's back. They held each other with the firelight moving over them, and the shadows of the wood drew off a little.

When they parted Linnea's eyes were bright. "You've nothing to apologize for," she said. "Just — don't shut me out again."

"I won't," Celenneth promised, and meant it, and slept that night lighter than she had in days.

3Rhovanian Hazards

Narquelië 23, 2965

The wood met them again with its grim silence, the trail winding on through darkened undergrowth. Ellarien led; Thalanar held the rear, his mood as cold as the morning; Celenneth and Linnea rode between them, their mounts picking over ground that grew steadily worse.

"We're making good time," Ellarien said, in the cautious tone of one who knew Mirkwood too well to trust its favors. "Push through to sundown and we cut another half-day."

"If the forest doesn't swallow one of us first," Thalanar muttered, nodding at the roots crossing their path.

The ground turned treacherous on a steep descent, slick moss over jagged stone, and they dismounted to lead the horses down. Celenneth took the front with Nimroch; Linnea and Mareth came behind. It was halfway down that the mare balked at a patch of loose rock, her hooves skidding — and then her legs went from under her with a sharp whinny, and Linnea, thrown, hit the ground hard and rolled to a stop against a jutting root.

"Linnea!" Celenneth was at her side in an instant. Ellarien swept the ground for threats; Thalanar caught the mare, who had come away with only a shallow scrape. Linnea had not. Her arm was bent at a wrong angle, her face gone white, her teeth set. "I'm fine," she hissed, and the tremor in her voice gave the lie to it.

"You're not," Celenneth said. "Don't move."

Ellarien crouched and read the break with a practiced eye, already drawing a length of sturdy cloth from her satchel. "It's clean. Painful, but it'll knit. Hold her steady." She set the bone — Linnea's muffled cry rang through the quiet wood — and bound it fast in a splint. "That holds till we can do better. But rest the arm."

Thalanar got Linnea to her feet, his grip firm and not unkind, and Celenneth walked her back to the mare, guilt heavy on her. "I shouldn't have let you ride so near the edge."

Linnea shook her head and found Celenneth's hand with her good one. "It's not your fault. I'll be more careful."1

The rest of the day went in tense quiet, Linnea riding with her arm cradled to her chest and Ellarien choosing the gentler ground. And that night Celenneth watched her suffer it — the way the splint drew a wince with every small shift, the ragged edge to her breathing, the sleep that would not come and hold. "Do you need anything?" Celenneth asked into the dark.

"I don't know what I need," Linnea said through her teeth. Celenneth settled back against her pack, and did not close her eyes, and kept a kind of watch over her through the worst of the night.

Narquelië 24, 2965

Morning came with a rare clear sky, the sun cutting the haze, and by degrees the wood began to loosen its grip — the great trunks thinning to shorter, sparser growth, the air turning fresher, carrying the first faint scent of open grass.

"Rhovanion, just ahead," Ellarien said, relief in it. At the forest's edge Thalanar drew rein and dismounted. "This is where I turn back," he said, gruff and, for once, gentle. His gaze went to Linnea, then lingered on Celenneth. "Keep her safe. Both of you." He turned to Ellarien. "Ride with them. See them to the Riddermark."

"I'll see it done," Ellarien said.

Thalanar mounted, swept the thinning trees once more as if for a last threat, and with a final nod rode back into the shadow of Mirkwood and was gone.

The step from the tangled dark of the forest to the open roll of Rhovanion was almost a shock. The plains ran gold to the horizon, and Linnea breathed deep of wildflower and open air after so long in the damp decay of the wood. That first night on the grass brought a vast quiet, broken only by the wind, and a sky thicker with stars than they had seen in days.

Linnea sat by the fire, her splinted arm cradled, the pain plain in the tightness of her jaw for all she tried to hide it, picking without appetite at her food. Celenneth knelt beside her and unwrapped a small parcel: a slender vial of shimmering, faintly golden liquid.

"What is it?" Linnea asked.

"Elrond's draught. He gave it to me before we left Rivendell — to heal, to strengthen. It won't take the pain, but it'll mend the arm faster." Celenneth's mouth tightened. "I should have thought to give it to you yesterday."

"You've been holding onto this." Linnea studied her. "Why now?"

"Because you need it. And because I can't stand to watch you hurt." Celenneth uncorked it; the scent of wild herbs and honey rose into the cold air. Linnea drank, and exhaled slow as the warmth of it spread through her, the tension easing out of her body. "It's strange," she murmured. "Like sunlight on my skin — even here in the dark."2

"Rest," Celenneth said, a hand at her shoulder. "Let it work." And Linnea sank into a deep and untroubled sleep, and Celenneth kept the fire and watched over her, and the stars seemed to burn a little brighter above the wide dark of the plains.

Narquelië 25, 2965

They made swift, easy miles the next day, the horses glad of the open ground, Linnea's arm much eased and her seat steady again. But toward dusk the land turned against them — the smooth ground breaking into rocky outcrop and stubborn bramble. Celenneth raised a hand and stopped.

Ellarien came up beside her, her face going grim. "Sinkholes. The ground looks solid till you set weight on it, then it takes you whole." She dismounted and tapped the earth with the butt of her spear; the hollow sound of it put a chill through them all. "This whole stretch is rotten. We pick our way, slow."

They had barely begun to skirt it when Celenneth caught a faint sheen on the grass ahead, like dew where the air was dry, and stopped them again. Ellarien narrowed her eyes. "Not dew. Wet earth — a hidden spring." Celenneth pressed her palm to the soil and it gave, and the reek of stagnant water rose. "A marsh. Shallow, most likely. But it could swallow a horse if we're unlucky."

They edged the marsh's rim with care, hooves sinking in the soft ground, and at one point Mareth's forelegs went into the mud and Celenneth caught the reins and hauled her free with a sharp pull. By the time they reached firm ground the sun was down, and they made camp weary and whole. "We nearly lost her," Linnea said, shaking her head at the dark line of the marsh behind them.

"We were lucky," Ellarien said. "These plains are as dangerous as they're beautiful." And the wind moved through the grass with a sound like a reminder that even the open country kept its hidden teeth.

4Following the Teeth

Narquelië 26, 2965

Dawn brought a bitter wind off the plains, an edge that cut through the thickest cloak. They moved swiftly against it, Celenneth at the front reading the horizon, Ellarien at the rear. At midday they paused at a shallow stream to water the horses and check their stores — and it was then that Ellarien pulled open one of Mareth's satchels and held up an empty sack.

"Gone," she said flatly. "The seam's torn. The marsh, most likely — we've lost most of the rations to it."

Celenneth crouched to the other bags and felt the truth of it settle cold: a handful of dried meat and hard biscuit, barely a day's food between the three of them.

Linnea let out a breath but her jaw set. "We're not turning back. We'll make do."

Celenneth was already slinging her bow across her shoulder. "I'll find game. Settle the horses and wait." She went off into the tall grass, silent and quick, and it was not long before a rabbit broke cover; she nocked, tracked, loosed, and it dropped clean, and she murmured her thanks to the land as she took it up. Back at the stream Linnea knelt over a cluster of smooth brown mushrooms, glancing to Ellarien. "Are these safe?"

Ellarien crouched to look. "Fieldcaps. Common enough. They'll keep you alive, if not delight you." By the time Celenneth returned with the rabbit the fire was going, and they made a thin soup of the mushrooms and the last of the stores, the roasted rabbit turned over the flames. It was a lean meal, but it held. "We'll be fine," Celenneth told Linnea, who sat close and weary. "This is the one thing I do know."1

Narquelië 27, 2965

The next day carried them easily over smooth ground, even Linnea managing the reins one-handed with growing confidence. Shortly after midday a rise revealed a strange formation ahead — a jagged cluster of black rock jutting from the flat plain, weathered and cracked by centuries of wind. Celenneth drew rein, studying it. "I've seen that before. But I can't place it — north of the Riddermark, or south."

Ellarien came up beside her. "The Stone Teeth. We're south of them, which puts the Riddermark ahead — eight days' riding, maybe less, south and a little west." She nodded at the landmark. "Travelers steer by the Teeth the way sailors steer by stars. We're on the right road."

The tension went out of Celenneth's shoulders. "Then we have our bearings." They pressed on, the Teeth falling away behind them, the grass growing shorter and the soil harder underfoot — the land itself promising the Mark drew nearer.

Narquelië 28, 2965

They rode on under a sky that smelled of distant rain, and made camp at dusk in the lee of a cluster of boulders. The fire warmed their tired faces, and Linnea leaned into Celenneth's side, her good arm draped across her waist — and then wrinkled her nose and drew back.

"Ugh. Is that us?"

Celenneth arched a brow. "What do you mean?"

Linnea sniffed pointedly. "I mean we smell like a barn a month past its last clean straw. Between the riding and the smoke, we're ripe."

Celenneth laughed, the tiredness giving way to it. "You're not wrong. But unless you're hiding a hot spring out here, we'll have to bear it a while yet."

From across the fire Ellarien glanced up from cleaning her blade. "There's a stream half a day south," she offered, dry. "Bear yourselves till then, and you might smell of grass and flowers again."

Linnea grinned and nudged Celenneth. "You hear that? Grass and flowers. That's the goal."

Celenneth shook her head, the smile lingering. "Fine. And if you behave, I might even change that splint for you."

5Dangers on the Plains

Narquelië 29, 2965

The stream Ellarien had promised came into view by mid-morning, cutting a silver line through the grass. Celenneth led Nimroch down to drink and swung off, and Linnea slid from her saddle with a sigh of pure relief. "We've earned this," she said, already pulling off her boots.

The water was shallow and wide and cold enough to make them gasp, and they waded in to rinse the road from their clothes and lay them out on the rocks. Even Ellarien's stoicism softened; and when Linnea splashed Celenneth with a daring grin, Celenneth answered with a wave of water that broke over Linnea's head, and Ellarien was dragged laughing into the fray besides. For a while the three of them played like children, and the weight of the journey fell clean away.

A deep, resonant bellow stopped them. Celenneth froze, her hand going for a bow that lay on the bank. Across the stream, majestic shapes came out of the tall grass — a herd of great elk, antlers crowned with moss and small blooms, their leader a towering stag that stepped forward and regarded the three of them in the water with dark, unhurried eyes.

Ellarien lifted a hand. "Still. They're no threat unless we startle them." They stood motionless as the elk came down to drink, deliberate and serene, and the stag lifted its dripping muzzle and held Celenneth's gaze a long moment, as though some understanding passed unspoken between them, before it turned and led the herd away into the grass.

The three stood quiet in the wake of it. "I've never seen anything like that," Linnea breathed.

"Nor have I," Celenneth said, softer than her wont.

Narquelië 30, 2965

The day after unfolded like something out of a good dream — crisp air, endless blue, the horses light beneath them. Linnea's splinted arm no longer dragged at her, and she rode easy at Celenneth's side. And then, near midday, she slowed Mareth and tilted her head as though catching a sound too faint for the others.

"What is it?" Celenneth asked, low.

Linnea closed her eyes a moment. There was — something. Not a threat; the opposite of a threat. A presence, close and warm, brushing at the edge of the new sense the plains had woken in her. "Someone," she said. "Near. But there's no danger in it."

They crested a low rise, and a figure stood upon it, haloed by the sun at its back — tall, slender, its garments shifting between earth-tones and silver as though woven of the light itself. As they came near, warmth reached out from it to meet Linnea, not heat but a deep and settling peace, and its features clarified: a being of unearthly beauty, its skin luminous, its eyes holding a kindness old as the land, hair the color of turning leaves falling down its back, a slender staff crowned with a single glowing crystal in its hand.

Linnea reined in, her breath caught. Behind her, Celenneth's voice came tight with wariness. "Stay back. We don't know what it is." And Ellarien had dismounted slowly, reverent, one hand near her blade but making no move to draw it. "An emissary," she murmured. "Or something greater. Tread carefully."

But Linnea felt no fear, only the peace of it, and she slid down and walked forward with her good hand raised in greeting. "You're not of this world," she said, gentle, "are you?"

The being inclined its head. "Few see beyond the veil." Its voice was like the far rustle of leaves, layered with a quiet, ancient wisdom. "But you carry a light within you, Linnea — one that sees past shadow."

She startled at her own name, and yet felt no alarm, only that abiding calm. "If I carry any light," she said, "it's because others have shared theirs with me."

The spirit's mouth curved. "A rare humility. It is such that binds the broken threads of the world." It reached into the folds of its shifting garments and drew out a ring — delicate, silver, gleaming with an otherworldly sheen — and held it out to her. "A gift, for your kindness and your trust. It will help you know the hearts and minds of others, if you wear it wisely."

Linnea took it in both hands, and slipped it on, and a subtle warmth spread through her, invigorating and strange. "Thank you," she said simply.

The spirit bowed its head once more, and began to fade, its form dissolving into the golden afternoon until it was as though it had never stood there, and the air let out a held breath.

Celenneth came to her, brow furrowed. "You shouldn't have trusted it so easily. It could have been a trap."

Linnea turned the ring on her finger, and met her worry with a small, certain smile. "Maybe. But sometimes trust is worth the risk. And this —" she lifted her hand "— this feels right."

Ellarien watched them both. "You may have made a friend today," she said. "Or something greater than a friend."1

Hísimë 1, 2965

The next day the sky was clear, but the wind carried an unease, and Celenneth rode with her gaze sharper than ever. When the foreboding took shape it took it fast: dark forms moving low and quick across the plain, a dozen or more, closing with a relentless purpose. She raised a hand and stopped the others. "Orcs."

The shapes clarified — crude armor, jagged iron, and at their head a hulking brute in a spiked black helm. He flung up a hand and bellowed in the Black Speech, pointing them out. Celenneth's blood went cold. "Dol Guldur," she said. "That's who sent them."

"That's a hunting party," Ellarien said, tight. "Not a raid. Us they're after."

The open plain gave no cover, and outrunning them on tiring horses was no sure thing. But the land might serve where speed would not. Celenneth's eye caught a shallow depression to the west — a dried streambed cut into the plain, its high banks narrowing to a bottleneck. "This way!" She spurred Nimroch toward it, and the others followed, the orcs' guttural cries rising behind them.

At the streambed's edge she swung down and strung an arrow. "Ellarien — take Linnea and the horses into the tall grass. Slow, low, circle back east. I'll lead them into the cut."

Linnea's eyes went wide. "You can't take them alone—"

"I won't have to. The banks bottleneck them — they'll have to come single file, chasing one target. I climb out the far end and lose them, and meet you east." She loosed before Linnea could argue; the shaft took a lead orc in the shoulder and he howled, and the brute bellowed, "After her! She's alone!"

She darted into the cut, drawing them in, the high banks forcing them to string out behind her, and fired again — a leg this time, slowing them further. When she reached the narrowest point she vaulted up the bank, hauling on a fistful of grass and root, and loosed a last arrow that rang off the leader's helm. "Up here, fool!" she goaded, and vanished over the ridge, and the broken ground slowed their scramble after her enough that she was clear and mounted and away before they crested it. The orcs boiled out of the depression too late, their leader roaring, to see the three riders already small against the horizon.2

They slowed at last, breaths ragged. Linnea turned to her, anger and awe at war in her face. "That was reckless. But it worked."

Celenneth gave a tired smile. "Sometimes the best fight is the one you don't have to finish."

Ellarien scanned the country behind. "They'll find us again if they've a mind to. But we've bought time." And they rode on into the lengthening light, the plains no longer feeling quite so open, the memory of the hunting party lingering at the edges of the day.

6The Riddermark

Hísimë 2, 2965

Dawn spilled over the plains, the golden grass silvered with frost. Despite the day before, the group rode lighter for the nearness of their goal. Celenneth led, reading the ground, and by late morning she called a halt and crouched over the dry, trampled turf. "Tracks. Horses — many of them."

8Yule in the Riddermark

They found a rhythm quickly enough among the Rohirrim — training on the field, riding out with the border patrols or alone, their skills honed against the best horsemen in the North; and each night the warmth of the hall to come back to, a comfort rare on the long road they had known so long. 10Winter in the Riddermark

Narvinyë 20, 2966

Winter lay over the Riddermark like a quiet blanket, the golden grass dusted white, the mornings sharp enough to sting. Linnea divided her days between the warmth of Freowyn's hearth, where Brynja grew stronger week by week, and the training paddocks, where she pushed herself to master the horse-craft of Rohan under a stern horsewoman named Eadwyn.

At Freowyn's fire she would sit with Brynja against her chest, rocking her and murmuring the lullabies she was learning from Freowyn's own lips. The child had thrived since the borderlands — her cries robust now, her dark eyes bright with curiosity, tiny fingers closing hard around Linnea's. She found herself enchanted by the small warm weight of her, by the coos that could set even Celenneth to soft laughter.

But Linnea was not made to idle, and once Brynja was settled she would pull on her riding gear and go out to where Eadwyn waited — tall, sharp-featured, and utterly without patience for coddling.

"Mount with purpose," Eadwyn barked one frost-bright morning, her steely gaze appraising. "A rider commands the horse. Not the other way round."

The lessons were grueling. Linnea had always been sound in the saddle, but the Rohirrim asked far more than soundness — balance, the guiding of a horse by the lightest press of the knee, the seamless shift of weight through a gallop. "Your horse is an extension of you," Eadwyn lectured as Linnea worked a tight turn. "Feel her as you feel your own body. Trust her, and she trusts you." Linnea set her jaw and burned brighter under each correction, and by winter's deep she could ride bareback with confidence — a thing Eadwyn allowed, grudging, was "not hopeless."

"High praise, from you," Linnea shot back, and earned the ghost of a smile. She had no horse of her own to school — her Woodland mare had gone back to Thalion with the scouts — and so she rode the Mark's, learning each new mount as Eadwyn set her to it.1

Linnea came out of Freowyn's house bundled in a borrowed cloak and stopped at the sight of it: Celenneth standing tall beside Nimroch, finishing the last of the bindings that held Brynja snug against her chest, the baby swaddled in wool with only her small face showing, pink and wide-eyed with wonder at the shining world.

"You look a proper mother-wolf with her cub," Linnea teased.

Celenneth gave her a faint smile, a hand resting on Brynja's small back. "I thought today was a good day to ride. The snow, the air — it feels peaceful. Like the earth holding winter close one last time before spring." She swung up onto Nimroch, the baby secure against her, and Linnea climbed up behind, arms circling Celenneth's waist, her cheek to her shoulder.

"Ready?" Celenneth asked.

"Always."

They rode out onto the white plains, Nimroch's hooves crunching soft, the cold air sweet with pine and distant hearth-smoke, Brynja's small gurgles threading through it all. And when they crested a rise and the whole Riddermark opened below them — the hills like frozen waves, the snow unbroken but for the tracks of wandering deer, the sky an endless blue — Celenneth slowed the horse and let him breathe.

"This," she said quietly, "is why I wanted to ride today. To remember the world isn't all shadow and fear."

Linnea rested her chin on her shoulder, and looked at the two of them before her — the woman she had bound her life to, and the child they had pulled from the cold — and felt, for the first time in a long road of them, something that was not quite like anything she had lost, and better than anything she had dreamed. "You always find a way to surprise me," she said. "And to remind me why I followed you out here."
