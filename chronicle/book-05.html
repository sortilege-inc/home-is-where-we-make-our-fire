1Departure / Feystone’s Gift

Narquelië 19, 2965

The morning dawned crisp and clear, the forest still wrapped in the gentle haze of dawn. Celenneth and Linnea stood at the edge of the woodland halls, flanked by Thalion’s scouts. Eryndor and Arannis lingered with them, their farewells imbued with quiet tenderness. Arannis pressed her hands to Celenneth’s shoulders, her gaze steady and warm. “The forest will hold you in its care, even as we must let you go,” she murmured.

Eryndor stood before Linnea, his amber eyes filled with a blend of admiration and gentle sorrow. “May your path be clear and your arrows strike true,” he said, clasping her hands with a firm grip. He stepped back, and added, “We will meet again when the winds bring you.”

Thalion approached, leading a sleek chestnut mare whose coat gleamed in the morning light. “Linnea,” he said, “this is Mareth. She will carry you swiftly. When your journey leads beyond the forest, she will return to us with our scouts.” Linnea nodded, her hand brushing over the mare’s soft muzzle as she murmured her thanks. The party mounted—Celenneth astride Nimroch, Linnea on Mareth, the scouts riding ahead.

The scouts, Ellarien and Thalanar, moved with the natural grace of those born to the forest. Ellarien was lithe with her deep chestnut hair, . Her armor, woven with fine elvish craftsmanship in muted greens and browns, rendered her nearly invisible among the trees.

Thalanar, tall and rugged tied back his midnight-black hair in a loose braid, revealing a youthful face, prematurely weathered. He wore a dark gray cloak, with silver embroidery resembling flowing rivers.

They soon fell into a rhythm, Celenneth and Linnea adapting quickly to their new companions. The elves' quiet competence was a relief.

Suddenly, Celenneth pulled up on Nimroch. "Isn't it true that there is a story of a great hart in these woods?" she asked the scouts. The scouts exchanged brief glances, and Ellarien murmured, “It's true; a great hart roams this part of the forest. Its antlers span wide as a king’s crown, none have caught it.” Linnea grinned, nudging Celenneth. “A challenge?”

Celenneth’s eyes gleamed with determination. “A challenge,” she agreed, sliding from Nimroch’s back. She crouched low, studying the tracks with an experienced eye. “This way,” she said, her voice certain. The party followed as she moved with practiced ease, her senses sharp and attuned to the forest’s subtleties.

Hours passed in pursuit, the trail twisting and fading, only to reappear under Celenneth’s discerning gaze. Finally, they found it—a magnificent hart grazing in a sunlit glade. Its antlers gleamed, adorned with moss and small flowers, as if the forest itself crowned the creature. Celenneth motioned for silence, her bow already in hand. With measured breath, she nocked an arrow, aimed, and loosed. The arrow flew true, striking cleanly. The hart faltered and fell, its death swift and merciful.

The scouts stepped forward, murmuring words of respect as they knelt beside the creature. Celenneth approached, her expression a mix of triumph and reverence. “A worthy quarry,” she said softly, her hand brushing over the hart’s coat. Linnea dismounted, clasping Celenneth’s arm. “You never cease to amaze,” she said with a grin.

The hart was dressed with care, its meat divided among the party to sustain them in the days ahead. Its rack was given to Thalanar, the trophy a gift to the Woodland elves.

The night had grown quiet, the only sounds the crackle of the fire and the soft murmurs of the forest. The scouts, Ellarien and Thalanar, sat cross-legged near the flame, their silhouettes sharp against the flickering light. Ellarien, ever watchful, produced a worn leather pouch from her satchel, a faint smile tugging at the corners of her lips.

“Time to show you how the elves celebrate a good hunt,” she said, her voice low but rich with a hint of mischief. She tapped a pinch of fragrant pipeweed into a finely carved wooden pipe, its bowl stained dark from years of use. The scent of the herb was earthy, with a sharp, citrusy undertone that cut through the lingering aroma of roasted meat.

“Pipeweed?” Linnea asked, raising an eyebrow as she adjusted her cloak.

Ellarien laughed, the sound unexpectedly coarse and unguarded. “Not your Dale crop, if that’s what you’re thinking. This is feystone’s gift—burn it, breathe it deep, and it’ll take you places you’ve never been.” She struck a spark and lit the pipe, drawing in a deep pull before passing it to Thalanar, who took it without ceremony.

Thalanar exhaled a plume of smoke with a grunt, his sharp features softening momentarily. “It’s not for the faint-hearted,” he said, handing the pipe to Linnea with a smirk.

Linnea took a tentative draw, coughing at first before finding a rhythm. The smoke filled her lungs, and the world around her seemed to tilt, colors growing impossibly vibrant. She laughed—a high, giddy sound—as the fire seemed to leap and dance in impossible shapes. “The flames,” she said, wide-eyed, “they’re... alive.”

Ellarien chuckled, taking the pipe and offering it to Celenneth. “Good, isn’t it? Watch your step, though. The visions can be kind... or cruel.”

Celenneth took the pipe and inhaled deeply. At first, the effect was warm and intoxicating. The trees seemed to hum with life, their branches weaving into patterns that hinted at some unknowable language. Celenneth saw stars blooming in the sky like flowers and heard the laughter of unseen voices.

But then, the warmth twisted. The stars dimmed, replaced by swirling darkness. The laughter turned to screams, sharp and haunting. Celenneth stumbled to her feet, her breath ragged. She was no longer beside the fire. The trees loomed tall and hostile, their gnarled branches reaching for her like claws. A shadow moved between them—a figure she couldn’t quite see, but whose presence bore down on her like a weight she couldn’t escape.

“Celenneth!” Linnea’s voice broke through the vision, distant and echoing. Celenneth turned, but Linnea wasn’t there—only more shadows. A guttural whisper filled the air, repeating her name in a tone that made her skin crawl. The shadow grew, its formless shape blotting out the sky. “You cannot outrun the dark,” it said, the words a dreadful certainty.

Back at the camp, Linnea sat transfixed, giggling at a vision of dancing animals that only she could see. Ellarien and Thalanar watched her with bemusement, occasionally exchanging their own puffs of the pipeweed. Thalanar glanced at Celenneth, who now stood rigid and trembling near the fire.

“What’s wrong with her?” Thalanar asked, his voice gruff.

Ellarien’s smile faded. “Not everyone sees joy,” she said, standing and moving to Celenneth. “Sometimes the pipeweed shows truths we’d rather not face.”

Celenneth gasped as she came back to herself, the firelight harsh against her tear-streaked face. Her hand gripped Ellarien’s arm like a lifeline. “The shadow,” she whispered. “It’s coming.” Her voice was hoarse, raw with fear.

Linnea’s laughter faltered as she looked at Celenneth, her own vision fading. “What shadow?” she asked, her voice filled with concern.

Ellarien guided Celenneth to sit, her grip firm but kind. “Whatever you saw, it hasn’t happened yet,” she said quietly. “Feystone's gift doesn’t lie, but it doesn’t exactly tell the truth, either.”

Celenneth stared into the flames, her mind racing. The warmth of the pipeweed had vanished, replaced by a cold certainty: the darkness they sought to outrun was closer than she’d thought.

Linnea reached out to Celenneth, her hand tentative as it hovered just above her shoulder. “Celenneth,” she said softly, her voice carrying both concern and confusion. “Whatever you saw, it wasn’t real. It was the pipeweed. You’re safe here.”

Celenneth stiffened under Linnea’s touch, her body rigid and unyielding. She shrugged off the hand as if it burned her, her eyes locked on the flickering fire. “I don’t need comfort,” she snapped, her voice harsher than intended. “It doesn’t matter if it was real or not. What I saw—what I felt—was a warning. One I can’t ignore.”

Linnea stepped back, her lips pressed into a thin line. “Fine,” she said, her tone edged with frustration but tempered by worry. “But you don’t have to face it alone.”

Celenneth didn’t answer, her gaze unwavering as the flames danced in the darkness. The silence stretched between them, thick and uncomfortable. Linnea sighed and moved to her bedroll, lying down with her back to the fire. Sleep came fitfully, her dreams punctuated by the occasional crackle of burning wood and the faint murmur of voices.

As the hours dragged on, Celenneth remained by the fire, her thoughts churning. The shadow’s words echoed in her mind, heavy and relentless. You cannot outrun the dark. She poked at the fire with a stick, sending embers swirling into the night air, their brief light consumed by the oppressive blackness beyond.

Thalanar, who had been sitting quietly nearby, finally broke the silence. “You won’t find answers in the flames,” he said, his voice rough and low. He wasn’t looking at her, instead sharpening the edge of his blade with slow, deliberate strokes.

Celenneth glanced at him, her expression weary but sharp. “No, but I might find them in vigilance.”

Thalanar snorted, setting his blade aside. “You’ll find nothing but exhaustion. The shadow you saw—if it’s anything like the things I’ve seen—feeds on that. Sleep, ranger.”

Celenneth shook her head. “Not yet. I’ll take the first watch.”

Thalanar’s mouth twitched into something that might have been a smile. “Stubborn,” he muttered. “Fine, but don’t let it eat you alive.”

The night deepened, the forest around them settling into uneasy quiet. Celenneth sat alone, the fire casting sharp shadows across her face. Her mind wandered, replaying every detail of the vision in search of meaning. She didn’t look up when Thalanar approached hours later, his boots crunching softly on the forest floor.

“Your turn,” she said flatly, not waiting for him to reply before standing and stepping away from the fire. She brushed past him, her shoulders tight with unspent tension.

Celenneth found her way to her bedroll, lying down with her back to the camp. Sleep didn’t come easily, but she knew that her strength wouldn’t last forever. For now, the fire still burned, and Thalanar stood watch, his presence a quiet assurance against the shadows pressing in.

2The Scouts’ Paths

Narquelië 20, 2965

The morning came with an unnatural hush, the forest seemingly reluctant to stir after the uneasy night. Ellarien was the first to break camp, her movements efficient and quiet, while Thalanar grumbled at the stiffness in his back. Celenneth rose with the same tension she’d carried the night before, her eyes shadowed but sharp as she busied herself with Nimroch’s tack. Linnea glanced her way, hesitant, but said nothing as she saddled Mareth.

The scouts took the lead as the party set off, weaving through the dense underbrush with practiced ease. The air felt lighter than it had the day before, as if the forest had granted them a rare reprieve. The trees thinned gradually, their gnarled branches giving way to smoother, younger growth. By midday, they reached the foothills of the mountains that skirted Mirkwood’s heart.

Ellarien stopped abruptly, gesturing for the others to follow her gaze. “There,” she said, her voice almost reverent. Ahead, the trees broke open to reveal a narrow path climbing steeply. The scouts exchanged a glance and led the party upward, the trail winding between ancient boulders and tufts of stubborn moss.

When they reached the summit of the ridge, the sight stole the breath from their lungs. Before them stretched the vastness of Mirkwood, its endless canopy rolling like a sea of green and black. Beyond the forest, far in the distance, faint glimpses of Dale’s towers shimmered under the sun. The horizon was painted with pale golds and silvers, and for a moment, the oppressive weight of the forest seemed a distant memory.

Celenneth stood slightly apart from the group, her hand resting on Nimroch’s flank. Her eyes drank in the vista, but her thoughts remained tangled with the shadow of her vision. Linnea approached, standing beside her without a word. She extended a piece of dried fruit, her expression calm, almost expectant.

After a long pause, Celenneth took it, the barest smile flickering across her lips. “Thank you,” she said quietly. Linnea nodded, a silent acknowledgment that needed no elaboration.

Ellarien and Thalanar exchanged hushed words, their sharp eyes scanning the treetops below. “We’ll lose the light too early if we linger,” Thalanar said, louder now, his tone gruff but lacking its usual bite. “Enjoy the view while you can. It’s all shadows from here.”

The descent was steep, the path slick with damp moss and loose soil. The deeper they went, the darker the forest became, the shadows lengthening as the sunlight above was swallowed by the canopy. The air grew thick and humid, the sounds of unseen creatures echoing faintly around them. Celenneth found herself more receptive to the occasional jest from Thalanar or the steadying hand Linnea offered when the ground became treacherous.

By the time they reached level ground again, the oppressive weight of Mirkwood had returned in full.

The night passed in a blessed calm, the forest holding its breath as if in silent truce. The campfire burned low, casting faint shadows across the bedrolls as Celenneth lay awake for a time, her thoughts swirling. The kindnesses offered by Linnea, Ellarien, and Thalanar lingered in her mind, their weight unfamiliar but not unwelcome. Eventually, the steady rhythm of the forest lulled her into a dreamless sleep.

The game trail proved narrow and uneven, twisting through dense thickets and over roots as thick as a man’s thigh. Celenneth rode Nimroch at the rear of the group, her sharp eyes scanning the surroundings for any sign of movement. Linnea followed close behind Ellarien, her borrowed horse stepping carefully over the uneven ground. Thalanar took up a middle position, his watchful gaze sweeping from side to side, ready for trouble.

Despite the trail’s challenges, the group made swift progress. The scouts moved with practiced ease, their steps silent and sure. Ellarien paused occasionally to examine faint marks on the trail, nodding to herself before pressing on. “The deer know the way,” she said once, half to herself, half to Linnea, who smiled faintly in reply.

By midday, the forest opened slightly, revealing a stream running parallel to the trail. They stopped briefly to let the horses drink, the cool water a welcome relief from the humid air. Celenneth dismounted, her hand brushing Nimroch’s flank as she stretched her legs. Linnea offered her a waterskin, which she accepted without hesitation, their exchange wordless but companionable.

The shortcut proved effective, cutting a day from their journey as promised. As the sun dipped low, painting the treetops in fleeting gold, the party emerged into a small clearing, the trail ahead winding back into the thicker part of the forest.

“We’ll camp here tonight,” Thalanar said, his tone brooking no argument. “Good ground, good visibility.” He knelt to examine the soil, nodding with satisfaction. “Nothing’s been through here in days.”

Ellarien began unpacking their supplies, her movements efficient. “It’s not often the forest gives us such a gift,” she said. “We’ll take it and rest well.”

Celenneth and Linnea exchanged a glance, the progress of the day lifting their spirits. Though the shadows pressed close around them, the air felt lighter, the camaraderie among the group holding the darkness at bay.

Celenneth adjusted Nimroch’s saddle with a practiced hand, her sharp gaze scanning the forest ahead. The game trail had thinned, its winding path swallowed by creeping roots and dense underbrush.

By midmorning, the way forward seemed blocked entirely—a tangle of brambles and low-hanging branches forming an impassable wall. Thalanar cursed under his breath, tugging at a thorny vine that resisted his strength. “We’ll lose hours if we backtrack,” he muttered, his frustration evident.

“Wait,” Celenneth said, her voice cutting through the tension. She dismounted, her boots crunching softly against the forest floor. With narrowed eyes, she studied the obstruction, noting subtle dips in the ground and faint impressions where deer tracks veered sharply to the left. Her lips curved into a slight smile. “There’s a way through. It’s narrow, but it’ll save us time.”

Ellarien tilted her head, watching Celenneth with an approving glint in her eyes. “Lead the way, ranger,” she said simply.

The passage was tight, forcing them to lead the horses by hand through the winding path Celenneth had discovered. Brambles clawed at their cloaks, and roots snagged their boots, but the way remained passable. The scouts followed Celenneth’s lead without question, their movements smooth and silent. Linnea brought up the rear, her sharp eyes scanning their flanks for any sign of danger.

By mid-afternoon, the forest opened slightly, the oppressive canopy above giving way to shafts of pale sunlight. The group rejoined the main trail, their progress unhindered. “You’ve got an eye for the unseen,” Thalanar said with a gruff nod toward Celenneth. “Could’ve been hours lost, maybe more.”

Celenneth shrugged, her expression calm but pleased. “You learn to see what the forest wants to hide,” she replied, mounting Nimroch with practiced ease.

Ellarien smiled faintly. “And the forest was kind to us today. A rare gift.”

They pressed on, the shadows lengthening as the day waned. When the sun dipped low, painting the treetops in fleeting gold, they found a small hollow shielded by ancient oaks and made camp. The fire burned low, casting flickering light on the weary travelers as they settled into a comfortable silence.

As the others busied themselves with their evening routines, Celenneth approached Linnea, who was seated near the fire, sharpening her blade. Celenneth hesitated for a moment, her hand brushing against Nimroch’s saddle as she steadied herself. Then, with quiet resolve, she stepped closer, her shadow falling across Linnea.

“I owe you an apology,” Celenneth said, her voice low but steady. Linnea looked up, her brows furrowing slightly in confusion. “For the way I’ve been. Distant. Cold. It’s not fair to you.”

Linnea set her blade aside, her expression softening. “You’ve been through a lot,” she said gently. “I understand.”

“No,” Celenneth said firmly, her eyes meeting Linnea’s. “You’ve been patient. Kind. More than I deserve. And I... I’m grateful. Truly.”

Before Linnea could reply, Celenneth stepped forward and embraced her, her arms wrapping tightly around her companion. Linnea stiffened momentarily, then melted into the embrace, her hands resting lightly on Celenneth’s back. The firelight danced across their faces as they held each other, the forest’s shadows retreating in the warmth of their shared connection.

When they pulled apart, Linnea smiled, her eyes glistening. “You’ve got nothing to apologize for,” she said softly. “Just don’t shut me out again.”

Celenneth nodded, her expression lighter than it had been in days. “I won’t,” she promised. That night, as the campfire burned low and the forest settled into silence, Celenneth felt a quiet peace settle over her, the weight of the shadows easing, if only for a while.

3Rhovanian Hazards

Narquelië 23, 2965

The fifth day began with the forest greeting them in its usual grim silence, the trail ahead winding through dense, darkened undergrowth. Ellarien led the group, her sharp gaze fixed on the path, while Thalanar brought up the rear, his gruff mood unchanged from the morning’s cold. Celenneth and Linnea rode in the middle, their mounts stepping carefully over the uneven terrain.

“We’re making excellent time,” Ellarien said, her tone even but laced with the cautious optimism of someone who knew Mirkwood well enough not to trust its favors. “If we push through until sundown, we’ll cut another half-day.”

Thalanar snorted, gesturing at the gnarled roots crisscrossing their path. “If the forest doesn’t swallow one of us first,” he muttered.

Celenneth narrowed her eyes, scanning the path ahead. The ground was becoming treacherous, the faint trail they’d been following swallowed by creeping roots and loose rocks. “Careful,” she warned. “This part of the forest doesn’t look forgiving.”

The terrain had grown harsher. They dismounted to navigate a steep incline, the horses struggling to keep their footing on the slippery moss and jagged stones. Celenneth took the lead, guiding Nimroch with a steady hand, while Linnea and Mareth followed behind.

It was halfway down the slope when disaster struck. Linnea’s mare balked at a particularly loose patch of rock, its hooves skidding. The animal let out a sharp whinny as it stumbled, its legs giving way beneath it. Linnea tried to steady the horse, but the weight of the fall threw her off balance. She hit the ground hard, rolling a few feet before coming to a stop against a jutting root.

“Linnea!” Celenneth shouted, scrambling to her side. The others rushed forward, Ellarien already scanning the area for threats while Thalanar checked the mare. Mareth, though shaken, had escaped with only a shallow scrape along its flank. Linnea, however, wasn’t as lucky.

Celenneth knelt beside her, her hands hovering over Linnea’s arm, which was bent at an unnatural angle. Linnea gritted her teeth, her face pale but resolute. “I’m fine,” she hissed, though the tremor in her voice betrayed the pain she was in.

“You’re not,” Celenneth said sharply, her voice tinged with worry. “Don’t move.”

Ellarien crouched beside them, her nimble fingers already pulling a length of sturdy fabric from her satchel. “It’s a clean break,” she said, examining the injury with a practiced eye. “Painful, but it’ll heal.” She glanced at Celenneth. “Hold her steady.”

Linnea let out a sharp breath, her eyes narrowing. “Just do what you need.”

Ellarien worked quickly, her hands deft as she set the bone and bound the arm with a makeshift splint. Linnea’s muffled groan echoed in the quiet forest, her breath ragged by the time Ellarien finished. “That should hold until we can get proper treatment,” Ellarien said, her tone calm and steady. “But you’ll need to rest that arm.”

Thalanar helped Linnea to her feet, his grip firm but not unkind. Celenneth guided Linnea back to her horse, her worry etched into her face. “I shouldn’t have let you ride so close to the edge,” she said quietly, guilt heavy in her voice.

Linnea shook her head, her uninjured hand reaching to squeeze Celenneth’s. “It’s not your fault. I’ll be more careful.”

The rest of the day passed in tense silence, Linnea riding with her arm cradled against her chest, and Ellarien taking more conservative routes.

The night passed slowly for Linnea, the firelight flickering weakly against the encroaching darkness. Though she tried to rest, the pain in her arm throbbed with each small movement, radiating up to her shoulder and leaving her breath ragged. She shifted in her bedroll, wincing as the splint pressed awkwardly against her skin.

Celenneth, ever vigilant, stirred at the slightest sound. “Do you need anything?” she asked softly, her voice carrying across the quiet camp.

Linnea shook her head, though her face twisted with discomfort. “I don't know what I need,” she said through gritted teeth. Celenneth watched her for a long moment, her expression unreadable, before settling back against her pack, though she never fully closed her eyes.

Ellarien, who had taken the first watch, crouched near the fire, her gaze steady on Linnea. “You’re stubborn, I’ll give you that,” she said, her tone not unkind. “But you’d do better to wake one of us than suffer in silence.”

Linnea managed a faint smile, but the rest of the night passed in fits and starts, each jolt of pain dragging her from uneasy sleep. By the time dawn broke, casting faint light through the thinning canopy, she looked pale and drawn but determined as ever to press on.

Linnea inhaled deeply, the scent of wildflowers and open air a welcome change from the damp decay of Mirkwood.

Ellarien rode close to Linnea, her presence steady and reassuring. “Mareth will stay with you until we reach Rohan,” she said, patting the mare’s neck. “Once you’ve arrived safely, she’ll know the way back with me.”

Celenneth rode ahead, her eyes scanning the horizon. The open land felt foreign after days in the suffocating grip of the forest, but it also brought a sense of freedom.

Linnea sat cross-legged near the fire, her splinted arm resting gingerly across her lap. Despite her attempts to hide it, the pain was evident in the tension around her eyes and the tightness of her jaw. She poked halfheartedly at the meal Ellarien had prepared, her appetite dulled by discomfort.

Celenneth approached, a small, well-wrapped package in hand. She knelt beside Linnea, the firelight catching the edges of her face, softening her expression. “This should help,” she said quietly, unwrapping the package to reveal a slender glass vial filled with a shimmering liquid. Celenneth’s lips pressed into a thin line. “Because you need it,” she said simply. “And because I can’t stand to see you suffer.” She met Linnea’s gaze, her voice gentling. “It’s what Elrond intended—for moments like this. I don't know why I didn't think to give it to you yesterday.”

The liquid was warm and soothing as it slid down her throat, leaving a gentle heat in its wake. Linnea exhaled slowly, the tension in her body easing as the draught began to take effect.

Ellarien watched from across the fire, her sharp eyes reflecting the flames. “Elven magic's effects sneak up on you,” she said, her voice low but approving. “You’ll feel the difference by morning.”

Linnea leaned back, her good hand pressing against her stomach as the warmth of the draught spread through her body. “It’s... strange,” she murmured, her voice tinged with wonder. “Like sunlight on my skin, even here in the dark.”

Celenneth smiled faintly, her hand brushing against Linnea’s shoulder. “Rest,” she said. “Let it do its work.”

As Linnea drifted into a deep, untroubled sleep, the stars above seemed to shine brighter, the vastness of the plains a little less daunting under their light.

Celenneth took a deep breath, her voice steady as she began. “Marshal Eofred, I wish to formally petition for the right to adopt the child we found in the wilderness. I would raise her as my own, teach her the skills of a ranger, and prepare her for the dangers of the world.”

Eofred’s brows furrowed, his gaze narrowing. “You speak of raising a child in the ways of the wilderness. That is a hard life, far from the safety a child needs.”

“It is,” Celenneth admitted, her tone firm but respectful. “But it is also a life of strength, of resourcefulness, of survival. And I would not put her at unnecessary risk. When my path becomes too dangerous, Freowyn has agreed to care for her.”

Freowyn stepped forward, her voice soft but resolute. “It is true, my lord. I would gladly give this child a home when the road is unsafe. Celenneth and Linnea have shown they would do anything to protect her. I believe the girl would be loved and cared for, in their arms as in mine.”

Eofred leaned back in his chair, his expression unreadable. “And what of Rohan?” he asked, his gaze sharp as he looked at Celenneth. “You would raise her as a ranger, but she was found within the Riddermark. She should also know the ways of our people—the bond between rider and horse, the traditions that run through our blood. If you are to be her mothers, then Rohan shall be her father.”

Celenneth looked to Linnea and nodded, her posture unwavering. “We would not deny her that,” she said. “The strength of the Rohirrim is legendary, and their ways are noble. We would welcome your people’s traditions as part of her life.”

Eofred regarded them for a long moment, the weight of the decision clear in his expression. Finally, he stood, his commanding presence filling the room. “Very well,” he said, his tone measured. “Celenneth, you may adopt the child, on the condition that she is raised in both the ways of the rangers of the North and the ways of the Rohirrim. She will know both the wilds and the plains, and she will be prepared for whatever future awaits her.”

A flicker of relief passed over Celenneth’s face, though her expression remained composed. Linnea was beaming. They both spoke. “Thank you, Marshal. We will honor this agreement.”

Freowyn smiled, stepping closer with the baby in her arms. “And her name?” she asked gently, her gaze shifting to the women. “Have you chosen one?”

Celenneth hesitated for a moment, then stepped forward, placing a hand lightly on the child’s swaddled form. “Her name will be Brynja,” she said softly, her voice carrying a mix of strength and tenderness. “She was protected by us, and we will raise her to protect others.”

Linnea grinned, her eyes sparkling with warmth. “Brynja,” she repeated, testing the name. “It suits her.”

As Freowyn placed the baby in Celenneth’s arms, the room grew quiet, the gravity of the moment settling over them all. Celenneth held Brynja close, her heart swelling with a fierce, protective love. Linnea rested a hand lightly on her shoulder, her smile soft but proud.

10Winter in the Riddermark

Winter in the Riddermark settled like a quiet blanket over the rolling plains, the golden grasses dusted with frost and the air sharp with the bite of cold mornings. Inside the warmth of Freowyn’s home, Linnea found herself dividing her days between tending to the now-thriving Brynja and pushing herself to master the equestrian arts of Rohan under the guidance of a stern but skilled horsewoman named Eadwyn.

At Freowyn's hearth, Linnea often sat with Brynja nestled against her chest, rocking her gently as she murmured lullabies learned from Freowyn’s lips. The child had grown stronger since they found her, her cries now robust, her bright eyes filled with curiosity. Linnea found herself enchanted by the tiny fingers that gripped her own and the soft coos that could make Celenneth giggle.

Yet, Linnea was not one to idle for long. After ensuring Brynja was settled with Freowyn, she would don her riding gear and stride out to the training paddocks, where Eadwyn waited. A tall, imposing woman with sharp features and an unyielding manner, Eadwyn was not one for coddling.

“Mount with purpose,” Eadwyn barked as Linnea approached one frosty morning, her steely gaze appraising. “A rider commands their horse, not the other way around.”

The lessons were grueling, the techniques complex. Linnea had always been competent in the saddle, but the Rohirrim’s methods demanded more than basic competence. Eadwyn drilled her on the finer points of balance, on controlling a steed with the lightest pressure of her knees, on shifting her weight seamlessly during a gallop.

“Your horse is an extension of you,” Eadwyn lectured as Linnea guided her mount through a tight turn. “Feel her movements as if they were your own. Trust her, and she will trust you.”

Linnea clenched her jaw, her determination burning brighter with each correction. By the end of the first month, she could ride bareback with confidence, a skill Eadwyn praised grudgingly. “You’re not hopeless,” the older woman remarked, her tone almost teasing.

Linnea responded with a smirk. “That’s high praise coming from you.”

Evenings brought Linnea back to Freowyn’s hearth, where she would collapse into a chair with an exaggerated groan. Freowyn would chuckle softly, setting a bowl of stew before her as Brynja babbled happily from her cradle.

“She missed you today,” Freowyn said one night, handing Linnea the baby. “She knows your voice, you know. She lights up whenever you’re near.”

Linnea grinned despite her weariness, cradling Brynja close. “She’s lucky she doesn’t have to deal with Eadwyn. That woman could scare the frost off the plains.”

Freowyn laughed, shaking her head. “Eadwyn is harsh, but she means well. You’ll thank her when you’re riding as well as the best of the Rohirrim.”

Linnea kissed the top of Brynja's head, her heart full despite the ache in her muscles. “Maybe,” she said softly, “but for now, this is the best part of my day.”

The sound of boots crunching on the frost outside drew Linnea’s attention, and her heart quickened as the door creaked open. Celenneth stepped inside, her frame illuminated briefly by the cold blue light of the evening before the door closed behind her. She looked winded but radiant, her cheeks flushed from the ride, and her auburn hair escaped in wild strands from beneath her cloak.

“You’re back!” Linnea exclaimed, a smile lighting up her face. She scooped up Brynja, standing to meet Celenneth. “I wasn’t expecting you for another two days.”

Celenneth smiled softly, a rare tenderness in her eyes. “I rode hard,” she said simply, her gaze flicking to Brynja. “I missed you both.”

After shedding her cloak and boots, Celenneth approached Linnea, who held Brynja securely in her arms. She placed a hand on Linnea’s shoulder and leaned down to kiss Brynja's head, her expression softening even further as the baby cooed in recognition.

Linnea chuckled, shifting Brynja to her other arm. “She missed you too, though she’s been perfectly spoiled by Freowyn.”

“I’m sure she has,” Celenneth said, her tone light. “But tonight, it’s your turn to be spoiled.”

Linnea blinked, tilting her head in curiosity. “What do you mean?”

Celenneth reached into the pouch at her belt, retrieving something carefully wrapped in cloth. She handed it to Linnea, her lips curving into a faint smile. “Open it.”

Linnea adjusted Brynja in one arm while unwrapping the parcel with the other. Her breath caught as she uncovered its contents: a delicate necklace of braided silver, its centerpiece a small, gleaming stone that caught the firelight like a fragment of a star.

“Celenneth,” Linnea murmured, her voice soft with wonder. “It’s beautiful. Where did you...?”

Celenneth’s smile widened slightly. “I had a smith in an outlying settlement make it for me, to repay me for a favor owed. I thought... you deserved something to remind you of the stars, even when the sky is hidden.”

Linnea’s cheeks flushed, and she looked up at Celenneth with a warmth that reached her eyes. “You rode for days, fought gods-know-what alongside the Rohirrim, and still thought to bring me this?”

“I did,” Celenneth said simply.

Linnea set Brynja in her cradle by the fire and stepped closer to Celenneth, her fingers brushing the necklace. “Put it on me,” she said, her voice a soft command.

Celenneth obliged, her fingers deft as she fastened the necklace around Linnea’s neck. The cool weight of the silver settled against her skin, and she turned to face Celenneth fully, her smile widening.

“Thank you,” Linnea said, her voice low and full of meaning. She reached up, her hand resting lightly against Celenneth’s cheek. “You’re full of surprises, you know that?”

Celenneth just smiled knowingly, as Linnea rose onto her toes, pressing her lips to Celenneth’s in a kiss that was slow and deep, filled with the affection and familiarity that had bonded them.

Linnea laughed softly, her forehead resting against Celenneth’s. “You’ve set the bar awfully high, you know. I’ll have to think of something remarkable for your next return.”

Celenneth smirked, her arms slipping around Linnea’s waist. “I look forward to it.”

The morning air was sharp, biting but invigorating, as the sun cast its golden light over the snow-draped fields of Rohan. The snow glittered like stars fallen to earth, a quiet blanket softening the world. Celenneth stood beside Nimroch, the stallion’s breath clouding the frosty air, as she finished tying the last of the bindings that secured Brynja snugly to her chest. The baby was swaddled warmly in layers of wool, her tiny face peeking out, pink from the cold and wide with wonder at the bright, shimmering landscape.

Linnea emerged from Freowyn’s house, bundled in a thick cloak borrowed from their hosts. She paused at the sight before her: Celenneth, standing tall and resolute, the baby nestled close, and Nimroch pawing the snowy ground impatiently. Linnea smiled, her heart warming despite the chill. “You look like a proper mother wolf with her cub,” she teased, adjusting her cloak.

Celenneth gave her a faint smile, her hand resting on Brynja's small back. “I thought today would be a good day to ride. The snow, the air—it feels... peaceful. Like the earth is embracing winter one last time before spring.”

With a practiced motion, Celenneth swung up onto Nimroch’s back, the bindings holding Brynja secure against her chest. The baby cooed softly, her tiny fingers brushing against the fabric of Celenneth’s cloak. Linnea climbed up behind them, her arms wrapping around Celenneth’s waist, her cheek pressing lightly against her shoulder.

“Ready?” Celenneth asked, her voice steady and calm.

“Always,” Linnea replied with a grin.

They rode out into the snowy plains, Nimroch’s hooves crunching softly with each step. The crisp air filled their lungs, carrying with it the faint scent of pine and distant hearth fires. Brynja's soft gurgles mixed with the quiet sounds of the journey, and Linnea occasionally leaned forward to murmur nonsense to the baby, her voice warm and light.

As they crested a small rise, the full expanse of the Riddermark spread out before them. The hills rolled like waves frozen in time, the snow unbroken except for the faint tracks of wandering deer. Above, the sky was a clear, endless blue, the sun casting long shadows that danced over the ground.

Celenneth slowed Nimroch, letting the horse breathe as she took in the sight. “This,” she said quietly, “is why I wanted to ride today. A moment to remember that the world isn’t all shadow and fear.”

Linnea rested her chin on Celenneth’s shoulder, her eyes softening. “You always find a way to surprise me,” she said. “And remind me why I followed you out here.”
