1Heading North

Nénimë 7, 2970

Winter was loosening its grip on the Angle. Most of the snow had gone to slush and mud, the trees still bare but the air softening, and the scattered Dúnedain families were out mending fences and clearing ditches against the coming spring. Inside their cottage near the settlement's edge, Celenneth stood at the window while Linnea coaxed the hearth-fire to a steady glow and Brynja — four years old now, and full of questions — darted about arranging wooden figures along the sill. Where do the birds sleep at night? Why does the snow melt when the sun comes? They answered what they could, amused by her endless curiosity.

They had made a life here, among these quiet, resilient folk who shared their harvests and asked no questions about the past. It was good. But Celenneth had felt, all winter, an old restlessness she could not name — a habit of staring north as though listening for voices carried on the wind. She had told Linnea some of it: the rangers who had raised her, Brandor and Orlec, the men who taught her to track and hunt and read the shape of clouds, whom she had left at fifteen to walk her own road. She had not seen them in years. She did not know if they still lived.

It was Linnea who had first asked, one lamplit evening after Brynja slept: Have you ever thought about going north? Back to your kin? And Celenneth had turned the word over — kin — and admitted she had, and admitted too that she was afraid of what she'd find. Or wouldn't. If you ever want to go, Linnea had said, covering her hand, I'll go with you. You won't face it alone. And Celenneth had said, not yet.

The not yet had lingered, and ripened, until at last she could no longer bear the not-knowing. She needed the truth of them — whether the men who raised her still walked the wild roads, or whether she was chasing ghosts. It was time.

The question of Brynja settled itself. Four was too young for a hard winter trek that might end in sorrow and could stretch to weeks, so they turned to their closest friends in the Angle — the Aldions: Eadric, a steady, kind tracker; Lira, wise and gentle with her herb-lore; and their daughter Elys, Brynja's own age and inseparable friend. Eadric and Lira agreed without hesitation; they understood the pull of an old question. Brynja was uncertain why her parents would go without her, but the promise of a long stay with Elys — a "special sleepover," as Elys called it — soothed her.

They spent a few days making ready — cloaks and blankets, dried food, flint and tinder, Celenneth's blade sharpened and her bow checked — and their last quiet evenings with Brynja, explaining as best they could. We're going to find some old friends of Mama Celenneth. We'll be gone a while, but we'll come back. Brynja nodded, though Celenneth doubted she understood; children felt time differently.

At dawn they rose, and the Aldions came with Elys, and the two girls ran off to chase sparrows by the fence. Lira pressed a pouch of dried herbs into Linnea's hands; Eadric gripped Celenneth's shoulder in quiet solidarity. When it came time to mount, Brynja stood at the cottage door and held first Celenneth's hand, then Linnea's, serious for a child so small.

Celenneth crouched and kissed her brow. "You be good. Listen to Lira and Eadric, and we'll bring you something from our travels." And then Brynja reached up and patted her cheek, a small grave gesture that said, plain as words, I trust you — and Celenneth had to look away a moment to keep her composure.

They mounted Nimroch and Eorlin and set out north, and the Aldions waved until the riders were small on the horizon, the two girls already hopping into some new game. Winter's hold was loosening but not gone; snow clung in the hollows, and a chill rode the wind, but here and there a bird sang, and a few early wildflowers dared to bloom near sheltered rock. They kept off the main routes, taking the quiet scouting-paths the Dúnedain used.

"Thank you," Celenneth said after a while, the words carried on the breeze. "For coming with me."

Linnea smiled. "I promised I would."

"I remember. I just never thought I'd find the courage to actually go."

That night they camped by a stream, and over dried fruit and hard bread spoke low of where to start. "Rangers meet in certain hidden camps — places I half-remember," Celenneth said, poking the embers. "We'll try one of those old haunts, and ask in the villages between. Rangers leave a memory, if not a footprint." When Linnea asked, quietly, whether Brynja would be all right, Celenneth found she could answer it honestly: she was in good hands, and there was a home to come back to. "For a long time I never stayed anywhere long enough to call it that," she said. "And now we leave her with friends I trust. That feels like a gift."

Linnea found her hand in the firelight. "It is one. We earned it."1

2The Silver Lily

Nénimë 16, 2970

Late-afternoon light stretched across the muddy lanes of Gelden Hollow, turning the puddles to pale gold. Celenneth rode Nimroch at an easy walk, Linnea beside her on Eorlin, both scanning the shabby fronts of a town tucked away from the world — timber frames, thatched roofs, crooked signs, a blacksmith's forge smoking in the distance. They had come here to find someone Celenneth once knew, someone who might have word of Brandor and Orlec.

She led them to a low, unassuming building, shutters half-drawn, a small painted lily etched into the lintel: the Silver Lily, a house of pleasure she remembered from younger years — more discreet and more respectable than most, and a place where secrets were sometimes kept, and sometimes shared for good reason. Linnea had raised an eyebrow at the notion on the road, but held her tongue and trusted Celenneth's judgment.

They stabled the horses behind and stepped inside, into warm lamplight and the scent of incense and something floral, soft drapes screening intimate lounges, low conversation and muted laughter and the occasional clink of glasses. No seedy dive, but a quiet haven. The madame came to meet them — confident, sharp-eyed, wary beneath her smooth welcome. "Need a spot to rest? Some company?"

"Not that business," Celenneth said. "Information. Two rangers — Brandor and Orlec — used to come here. We hoped one of your people might remember them."

The madame's eyes narrowed a fraction, but her smile held. "Brandor and Orlec. I remember. Rangers leave impressions."

"They raised me," Celenneth said. "I'm trying to find them."

Something flickered in the woman's face, as if she saw a younger Celenneth beneath the years. "Wait here." She returned a while later with another woman — tall, with short black hair and a loose blouse cut low enough that it seemed always a breath from spilling her free. She wore it, and herself, with an easy, unbothered confidence, at home in her own body and its effect. At the sight of Celenneth her expression shifted to surprise, and something like regret.

"Celenneth," she said softly. "It's been a long time. I never thought I'd see you again."

Celenneth stood. "Life takes strange turns. I'm sorry to appear like this, but I need your help. Brandor and Orlec came through here last. We have to know what became of them."

The woman — Eira — glanced at Linnea, then nodded, and led them to a curtained corner with a low couch and dim lamplight. When they had settled, she spoke, her eyes falling. "They came through last summer. Excited, but nervous. Orlec talked of a discovery — something old and powerful. Brandor was tense, like he sensed danger. I warned them it sounded risky, but they insisted it mattered. They left at night, and never came back."

"Did they mention being followed?" Linnea asked. "Suspicious folk?"

Eira frowned, thinking. "Not outright. But Orlec said that knowledge like theirs draws trouble — treasure-hunters, cultists. They knew the risk, and went anyway; there was a desperation in them. He spoke of a watchtower, a stream that forked, a landmark to guide them north. Something about old Númenórean script carved in stone." She looked up. "They're gone, aren't they?"

"We don't know," Celenneth said. "That's why we're going to find out."

Eira held her gaze. "Take me with you."

Celenneth blinked, and Linnea with her. "Eira, it's dangerous. Wild lands, ruins, bandits — worse. You don't owe them this."

Eira smiled sadly. "I owe them more than you know. Brandor and Orlec were kind to me when few were — they respected me. If I can help find the truth, I want to. I can ride, I can use a dagger, and I know these lands. I've business of my own up north, besides, and you're safer to travel with than most." Celenneth weighed it, and after a moment nodded. "We leave at first light. Pack what you need."1

They took a room at a quiet inn down the street, and over a plain supper spoke low. "A watchtower and a forked stream," Linnea said. "We start there." Celenneth stirred her stew. "From there, maybe tracks, or an abandoned camp. They couldn't have vanished without a trace." And in the grey of dawn Eira came to the stable, cloak and pack ready, hair braided back, a dagger at her hip. "I'm ready." Celenneth nodded. "Then let's move before the town stirs."

3The Broken Branch

Nénimë 20, 2970

The days after Gelden Hollow went quietly, Celenneth, Linnea, and Eira making steady progress north and west toward the Evendim hills along narrow lanes and old tracks. Eira proved good company — quick with small advice, patient on the long trail, and a comfort to Celenneth simply as another who had known Brandor and Orlec and felt the weight of their absence.

Tarnath was a different country from the Hollow. A crossroads town with a hard name, it clustered crooked around a muddy central lane, thick with the smell of stale ale and smoke; wagons creaked past, and weathered faces watched from shaded doorways without a word of greeting. Celenneth had warned them: it was a place mercenaries and cutthroats passed through — but Brandor and Orlec had stopped here too, and the tavern called the Broken Branch might hold a name, or a map.

They tied the horses behind the tavern and went in. The Broken Branch stank of old ale and cheap pipeweed and sweat, its lamplight throwing shadows over men in battered leather nursing their drinks. The noise dipped as the three women entered, and eyes turned — a large man with a scarred cheek marking Linnea with open interest, another giving Eira a sidelong smirk. Celenneth took the lead, guiding them to the bar where a grizzled, sharp-eyed keep dried a mug.

"What'll it be?"

"Information, mostly," Celenneth said. "And ale for three, if it's decent." When the mugs were down she went on, low. "We're looking for two rangers — Brandor and Orlec. They came through chasing rumors of old Númenórean relics. Ever hear the names?"

His gaze sharpened. "Aye. Hard to forget men like them — rangers have a presence. They came through months back. Gathered supplies, asked about the hills. Smelled of trouble, to be honest."

"They raised me," Celenneth said. "I'm trying to learn what happened."

He tilted his head, and something eased at the mention of the trail she'd followed. "They headed north, toward Evendim. Claimed they'd found signs of old watchtowers and inscriptions. In a hurry — looked over their shoulders a lot. Brandor was tense; Orlec tried to seem casual and couldn't hide the edge." He lowered his voice further as a few nearby mercenaries went quiet to listen. "They asked about the old roads that vanish in those hills, a ruin said to hold a relic. I warned them not to trust anyone out there. Too many folk smell gold and go mad." He shook his head. "Never came back through. But if you're set on it, I'll mark a rough path."1

As he sketched a scrap of parchment, one of the mercenaries — the scarred one — swaggered over, mug in hand, and looked the three of them up and down. "What's a trio like you doing in a place like this? Lost your way?"

Celenneth turned to face him without flinching. "We're not lost. We're talking with the barkeep. Go back to your table."

He chuckled, glancing at his fellows. "Feisty. Maybe we'd like to join your conversation." His eyes lingered where they had no business, and Celenneth felt Linnea stiffen beside her.

Eira's voice came low and even. "We're not interested, friend. Step back." She did not raise it, but the steel in it was plain.

The barkeep looked up, bored and firm. "Leave them be. They're paying, and I'm busy." The mercenary's grin faltered; perhaps he read the set of Celenneth's shoulders, or the quiet in Eira's eyes, and decided it wasn't worth the trouble. He shrugged, muttered a curse, and wandered back.

The barkeep pushed the parchment across. "This line's the path they likely took — toward a broken ridge near an old logging camp, then into rough country. Hardly a road anymore. If they found that watchtower or those carvings, they'd have gone deeper into the hills." He would not wish them luck. "Don't trust anyone easily. And don't linger in old ruins after dark."

Celenneth tucked the parchment away and left coin on the bar, and they went out into the cleaner light of dusk. "That could've gone worse," Eira sighed. Linnea checked Eorlin's girth. "But we got what we needed." They rode out of Tarnath — the townsfolk giving them sidelong looks but no trouble — and made camp in a patch of low trees beyond, where a fallen log broke the wind. Linnea built the fire, Celenneth tended the horses, Eira scouted and came back shaking her head: no one following.

By the firelight Celenneth studied the barkeep's scrawl. "From here, the logging camp, then the watchtower Orlec spoke of. With luck we'll find sign of their passing." Linnea took first watch; at midnight she woke Eira and lay down beside Celenneth, who stirred and brushed her shoulder before sleep took her again. At dawn they rode on, the party of three grown quietly sure of itself, into the wild hills where rangers moved like whispers and Brandor and Orlec had vanished.

4The Ranger Station

Nénimë 24, 2970

The northern wind was merciless, hurling needles of ice as Celenneth guided Nimroch across a barren field of snow, Linnea hunched beside her on Eorlin, Eira a short way behind with her hood pulled tight. Rumors, half-remembered, had led them here — to a ranger station huddled against a rocky bluff, half-buried in drifts, a thin thread of smoke rising from its chimney. Shelter, if not welcome.

Celenneth pried the door open, and warmth spilled out — woodsmoke, old stew, well-worn leather — and inside, rangers at a long table let their hands drift from their hilts as the three women entered. A tall man with a salt-streaked beard stepped forward, one hand near his sword from habit. "You've come far."

Celenneth pushed back her hood, auburn hair tumbling free. "We need shelter. And answers."

He measured them, then jerked his chin toward the hearth. "Warm yourselves. I'm Gaidric. This station's mine." They settled by the fire, and when Celenneth said she sought Brandor and Orlec, a hush fell over the hall, and Gaidric's face went grave.

"You knew them?"

"They raised me. They vanished. I need to know what happened."

He folded his arms. "They went north, beyond reason. Toward the Frost Maw." At the name, a chill went through the room. "No one returns from the Frost Maw," Gaidric said, low. "Not Brandor, not Orlec, not any who've tried. It's a ruin older than the kingdoms of Men, its stones fused with ice that never melts. Some say it whispers — that it calls travelers in. We searched after the thaw. Found nothing. The snow buries all traces."

"You assume they're dead," Celenneth said.

A younger ranger, Aldric, muttered, "If they entered the Maw, they might as well have walked into a grave."

Celenneth's fist clenched. "I won't accept that without proof."

Gaidric's voice cracked. "They were good men. But the Maw takes all."

"That I'll determine for myself," Celenneth said, and though Linnea's head came up in alarm, she did not argue — only said they should sleep on it and make a plan. Eira folded her arms. "I owe Brandor and Orlec a debt. But I'm loath to run into some ruin that eats the living. I'm with Linnea." Celenneth met their eyes. "I understand. And if I find I can't leave without seeing it myself, I'd not begrudge either of you for staying back." Linnea's shoulders sagged; she had never been able to stop her. "I'm not leaving your side. I'll make sure we come home together."1

Night settled over the station, the rangers keeping to themselves, sharpening blades and staring into cups of bitter ale, the doom of that frozen ruin thick in the air. Eira watched Celenneth and Linnea draw close by the fire, and thought the tension in this place wanted breaking. She spotted Gaidric and Aldric murmuring at a side table — grim, but with strength in them, and want beneath the grimness — and rose, and crossed to them with an easy smile.

"You two have been helpful," she said lightly. "Could be I owe you something. A fair exchange."

Gaidric raised a brow; Aldric smirked. "And what are you offering?"

Eira leaned in, her voice dropping. "A bit of warmth, in this cold place." She turned toward a side chamber, and the heavy door creaked shut behind the three of them, muffling the hall and the howling wind.

She knew this game; she had played it with harder men. She let the silence draw out, enjoying the anticipation, and shed her cloak slowly. "Planning to stand there all night?"

Aldric chuckled and stepped close. "Didn't want to assume."

"Assumptions are risky." She reached out and grazed Gaidric's jerkin with her fingertips, and he caught her wrist, testing her boldness; she did not pull away. In a moment the balance tipped, and she pressed into Gaidric and claimed a fierce kiss that drew a low growl from his chest, while Aldric came round behind, his breath hot at her ear. "We share," he rasped.

She tilted her head, baring her throat. "Prove it."

They fell together onto a narrow cot piled with furs — a tangle of hands and low sounds, Gaidric's calloused palms strong against her, Aldric's lither frame pressing at her back. But it was never surrender, not for her; it was a negotiation of touch and will, and she twisted free of Gaidric's grip just enough to make him work for it, spun and pinned him against the cot's edge with a thigh between his, her hands splayed on his broad chest. A low, approving sound rumbled in him — not anger — and he seized her hips and pulled her down onto him, and she welcomed it with a gasp, riding the intensity even as she stayed the one in command. Aldric's mouth found her throat; she reached back and freed him from his belt, drew him round, and took him in her mouth while Gaidric moved beneath her, and set the rhythm of all three of them by the roll of her own hips.

She pushed them to their edges and pulled back at the brink and gave more, until both men were past their calm and begging in their way, and only then did she let her own release take her, crying out as it broke. In the main hall, the muffled sounds through the chamber wall reached Celenneth and Linnea — moans, a creaking cot, low laughter. Linnea rolled her eyes, cheeks coloring. "I swear she does that on purpose."

Celenneth set aside her whetstone and leaned back. The dread of the Maw still weighed on her, but there was something oddly steadying in Eira's unabashed pleasure — that in a place of ghosts and doom, someone claimed a piece of life. Linnea leaned closer, her voice dropping. "Hearing them… it's hard not to think of our own."

Celenneth's gaze warmed and darkened at once. "I remember when we had fewer worries. More time for this." She hooked a finger under Linnea's cloak. "Come here."

They slipped to a quiet corner of the hall where the shadows lay deep and no one marked them, and Celenneth pressed Linnea against a sturdy support beam and kissed her, slow and thorough and then not slow at all, her fingers tangling in Linnea's thick hair. Linnea's hands found the buckles of the battered breastplate and worked them loose, and the armor thudded soft onto a bench, and her hands slid beneath the damp linen beneath to trace the familiar lines of Celenneth's stomach and ribs, pausing at the old scar above her hip to press a kiss there. Celenneth caught Linnea's face and lifted her into another hungry kiss, pushing the cloak from her shoulders, and then the shirt beneath, baring her to the firelight.

"Your turn," Celenneth breathed, and peeled the last of it away, and let her calloused palms map Linnea's sides, her thumbs brushing the underswell of her breasts until Linnea gasped. "You're being noisy," she teased against her throat.

"Maybe I don't care," Linnea shot back, arching into her.

"Bold." Celenneth loosed the ties of her own breeches and pressed close, pinning Linnea to the beam with the heat of her, and slid a thigh between Linnea's legs, and Linnea bit her lip to stifle a moan. "Quiet," Celenneth warned at her ear, "unless you want the whole station listening." Her hand slid between them and found Linnea slick and ready, circling the aching peak of her until Linnea's nails raked down her back. "Please," Linnea gasped. "Say it again," Celenneth murmured, and when Linnea did — broken, breathless — she pressed two fingers up into her and swallowed the cry with her mouth, and Linnea shattered against the beam, trembling, held steady by Celenneth's body until the waves let her go.

But Celenneth was not done. She pinned Linnea's wrists above her head with one hand and dropped to her knees, her intent plain, and set her mouth to Linnea where her fingers had been, and Linnea's head fell back against the wood, every breathless sound swallowed by the storm outside, until she broke a second time with a sob, unravelling into fire and starlight. Celenneth held her through it and rose to gather her close, and Linnea kissed her, soft and reverent now, tasting herself. "I've missed this," she whispered.

And then Linnea turned the tide, guiding Celenneth back until her shoulders met the long bench, straddling her hips, and worked her tunic higher to bare her breasts and set her mouth to them until Celenneth growled low. She kissed a slow trail down every scar of Celenneth's stomach, tugged the loosened breeches from her hips, and settled between her parted thighs, and let her tongue glide through her, unhurried and relentless, her fingers joining her mouth and curling to find the spot that made Celenneth buck. Celenneth's hands shot to Linnea's hair, holding her there, her thighs clenching, and when she begged — don't stop, please — Linnea drove her over the edge, and Celenneth shattered with a cry muffled only by the crackling fire and the howl of the wind, arching and then going boneless and spent.

Linnea crawled up to cradle her, and Celenneth pulled her into a fierce, soul-deep kiss. "You," she rasped, still breathless, "are dangerous."

Linnea smirked. "I warned you." The fire burned low, but their shared heat was enough to drive back even the coldest wind; and for now, in that stolen corner, nothing else weighed on them at all.

After a time Eira slipped back into the hall, flushed and pleasantly weary, and found Celenneth and Linnea holding each other close by the dying fire, trading slow kisses with a tenderness that tugged at her chest. She grinned to herself — pleased her own indulgence had sparked their closeness — and settled on a stool to warm her hands. Tomorrow they would speak of the frozen depths ahead. Tonight, at least, they had all claimed a measure of life. She wondered how long so fragile a peace could last.

5Leaving the Ranger's Station

Nénimë 25, 2970

Grey dawn seeped into the station, and the rangers moved about stoking the fire and brewing weak tea. Gaidric and Aldric gave Eira a small, knowing nod; she returned a lazy wink. Celenneth and Linnea rose stiff but warm, the night's closeness lingering in the glances they traded, and Eira joined them at the hearth.

"Well, Eira," Linnea said, husky with sleep, "you had a productive night."

Eira smirked, unabashed. "I did. You two seemed inspired yourselves." Then the levity thinned, and Celenneth's face turned serious. "Linnea and I are heading out. Thank you for what you've shared. We'll send word if we can." Eira embraced them both, and Gaidric and Aldric nodded solemn farewells, and the two women mounted and rode out into the biting wind, leaving Eira to her own road north.

The wind off the Evendim hills was sharp enough to steal breath. The station sat at the very edge of the known trails; beyond it lay only broken hills and thin forest, and somewhere ahead, the frozen ruin the rangers called the Frost Maw — a country of ice-choked valleys and strange stones. They pulled their cloaks tight and set out, Celenneth guiding their route along faint old game-trails, Linnea keen-eyed at her flank. The weather worsened the deeper they pressed, snow reaching the horses' chests, and at length the ground grew too rough for riding; they tethered the horses in a sheltered hollow and went on afoot.

In a narrow pass Linnea raised a hand: fresh disturbance in the snow, large paw-prints. A predator. They crouched, and Linnea's eye caught it — a white shape low behind a ridge of ice, near-invisible against the snow. A warg, big and silent. The gorge behind was too steep to slip back through unseen, and flight would only invite a chase, so they set to meet it from strength: Celenneth climbed a rocky ledge for the height, Linnea angling behind a boulder for a clear line.

When Celenneth stepped into a patch of weak sun the warg snarled and launched itself up the icy rise. She swung down hard, but the blade skittered off its thick hide; below, Linnea's arrow slipped under its raised shoulder and bit deep, and the beast howled. It vaulted at Celenneth and its weight bore her down onto the ice, teeth snapping inches from her face — her armor turned the bite — and she wrenched free and rolled clear. Linnea's second shot went wide as the maddened beast twisted; Celenneth found her feet and drove a precise thrust deep into its flank, and it stumbled, blood black on the snow. When its teeth sought her throat she took the lunge on her buckler and thrust upward into its chest, and drove the steel home until it collapsed with a last ragged sigh.1

"Hurt?" Linnea hurried to her.

Celenneth tested her ribs. "Bruised. The armor took it. Let's move."

They retrieved the horses and pressed on, and by dawn — after a blizzard that drove them to huddle a night beneath a rocky shelf — they crested a rise and saw the ruins: tall pillars of runed stone rising like broken teeth from the ice, the silence broken only by a strange hum that came and went with the wind. And among the fallen blocks, two figures moved — men, tall and lean, cloaked to blend with the winter gloom, studying the old carvings. Rangers, by the way they moved.

Celenneth stepped from behind a pillar, one hand raised, Linnea half-hidden with an arrow ready. "Rangers! Be at ease. Friends approach."

Both spun, weapons half-drawn — and the taller, dark-skinned and stern-eyed, let his gaze fall on the old ranger's star-token Celenneth held out, and eased his blade back into its sheath. "Name yourself."

"Celenneth of the Dúnedain. This is Linnea, my companion. We mean no harm."

"I am Damrod," the man said. "That's Hallas, my partner." The fair-haired ranger a pace behind kept a hand on his hilt. "We patrol these hills. Few sane folk wander this far north in winter. What brings you?"

"We seek the way into the Frost Maw," Celenneth said. "But these ruins caught our eye, and your cloaks — I hoped you might be kin, or friends of it. Allies are scarce out here."

Hallas snorted softly, brushing frost from his beard. "Anyone bound for that frozen hell has good reason. The Maw's known for swallowing travelers whole."

Damrod raised a hand. "Ice-beasts have been terrorizing the folk of the Lone-lands, and we traced them to here. There's a power stirring beneath these stones. If we don't root it out, the North keeps suffering."

Celenneth nodded slowly. "Then we'll help each other. Gladly we'd see these beasts weakened — and in turn, I ask that you come with us into the Maw, where my guardians went. Brandor and Orlec."

Damrod's jaw tightened, and Hallas looked at Celenneth more closely. "Wait — you were their protégé, were you not?"

"I was," Celenneth said, grateful for the recognition. "They ventured into its mouth. Whether we find them, or only their bones, I have to know."

Hallas crossed his arms. "We'll come — on one condition. We settle what's cursing this land first. A fair bargain?"

Celenneth looked to Linnea, who nodded slowly. "A fair bargain," she said. "We'll stand with you."2

Before the shadows fully claimed the ruins, they set to work. Damrod and Linnea kept watch while Celenneth and Hallas gathered at a cracked pillar etched with faded runes — delicate, intricate work, chipped and frost-blurred. "These were made to seal or guard something beneath the ice," Celenneth murmured. "Binding sigils. But corrupted — a whole line chipped away, so the sequence no longer holds."

"Carefully made, once," Hallas said, low, as if afraid to wake what lay below. "Can it be restored?"

"We can try. If the runes are stabilized, it might weaken whatever's spreading these beasts. At least for now." From her belt she drew a small chisel, a hooked knife, a wrapped stub of charcoal, and set to re-inscribing the missing segments while Hallas — who had studied a little old lore under older rangers — compared the intact pillars and called soft corrections: that stroke should curve; the binding runes ended in a spiral, not an angle. Following his guidance she etched the corrected lines, and faint blue sparks danced where the symbols joined, as though the old magic knew her intent. A tremor passed through the stone under their hands, and they shared a startled glance — something below had stirred — but Hallas only said, "Don't stop. We've come this far." She finished the last strokes, and the runes glowed and then settled to a steady shimmer, and the ruins' ominous hum mellowed. "That should hold it a while," she said, wiping sweat from her temple. "Not forever. But it makes this place less volatile."3

Hallas ran a gloved hand over the new-cut lines. "Well done." And as night fell fast, they went to find a place to camp before descending any deeper.

6The Frozen Depths

Nénimë 28, 2970

They built a modest fire in the lee of the ruins, and sat close, knees near touching, sipping hot water — tea-leaves were scarce this far north. Linnea broke the quiet. "We've fought beasts and survived storms, and still know little of each other beyond names. Perhaps we should change that, before we go below."

Celenneth spoke first, of Brandor and Orlec who had vanished into the Maw, of the life she and Linnea had built near Bree and the family waiting on them. Damrod took his turn — "warden of these wilds, though my path has wandered close to Angmar's old bones" — telling how he and Hallas had come north tracking the frost-beasts to nip a budding threat. And Hallas, quieter: "I hail from a line that served the Dúnedain for generations. I've known cold winters and hungry wolves. But these frost-beasts are something else." A long silence followed, filled with the crackle of twigs and the moan of wind through broken arches — strangers a few hours ago, now bound by fate's whim. They arranged watches, and a fragile trust settled over the small circle, and before she slept Celenneth met Linnea's eyes and found acceptance there. They were not alone.

The next day they found the entrance — a narrow opening behind half-buried stones, a dark tunnel angling down into the earth, the air near it tight as though the world held its breath. "Down we go," Damrod said quietly. Linnea unstrung her bow to pass the cramped mouth, then strung it again inside; Celenneth led with a hooded lantern, its light glistening off ice-slick rock.

The passage descended, runed and weathered, silent but for dripping meltwater and their soft footfalls. Where it narrowed to single file, Celenneth's lantern showed ice-crusted bones half-buried in frost — and Linnea touched her shoulder and pointed: fresh tracks, heavy and clawed. "Frost hounds," Hallas breathed. They dimmed the lantern and crept on, and around a bend Celenneth glimpsed low, silent shapes blending into the gloom — three hounds, their leader rimed with frost crystals, its eyes a dull hungry blue.

They struck without warning. Damrod met the leader's lunge with his shield; Celenneth pivoted and slashed at its flank, but the frozen pelt turned her blade. From the rear Linnea's bowstring sang, her arrow sinking into a smaller hound's chest, and Hallas drove his spear up beneath its jaw with a sickening crack. The leader clamped onto Damrod's shield and tried to wrench it free; Celenneth seized the moment and drove her sword deep into its exposed flank, and Damrod finished it with a strike to the neck. The last hound charged Hallas in a bid to break free and impaled itself on his braced spear, and he heaved and twisted until it fell limp. They stood panting in the gloom, and Celenneth knelt to be sure the beasts were dead — and found faint runes carved into their frozen hides. "They're being guided," she murmured. Damrod nodded grimly. "This place grows more accursed the deeper we go."1

The tunnel widened, and after a hundred paces more they emerged into a vast chamber, its high ceiling glittering with ice, its walls carved with ancient reliefs — cloaked figures battling misshapen creatures of ice; warriors forging a great blade beneath starlight; hooded figures inscribing runes into pillars. At its center stood a stone altar half-encased in ice, faint runes glowing on it. Hallas ran a hand over the reliefs, awe in his voice. "These warriors — Númenórean rangers of old. They were sealing something. Binding it." Linnea frowned. "Then who corrupted it? Something here is twisting the old wards."

The temperature dropped sharply, their breath clouding, and Linnea stiffened. "Do you feel that?" A pressure in the air, a sense of watching eyes — and then shapes drifted out from behind the icy columns: four wraiths, pale and silent, cloaked in rime, wielding blades of hoarfrost, their hollow eyes glowing dull and hungry.

They attacked in eerie silence. Linnea's arrow passed clean through the first's misted torso, scattering frost, and the thing re-formed a pace away in a soundless mockery of a laugh. Steel found scant purchase; Celenneth's sword swept through robes and empty cold. She realized blades alone would not serve — and memory sparked, a curse from the old ranger archives, a Númenórean malediction meant to unsettle spirits bound to the ancient wars. As a wraith lunged she stepped back and raised her voice in words that scraped like iron on stone: "Basannórë maitanëa, durëa hlaruvalyë!" The ancient words rolled through the chamber, and a tremor passed through the wraiths, as if they knew them.

Damrod caught the sense of it and repeated the words, stumbling on the syllables but slowing his foe as though it waded through thicker air; Hallas muttered them too, and then Linnea, hesitant and then stronger, until all four voices chanted the old curse and the wraiths shrank from the weight of remembered magic. Then Linnea wound oil-soaked cloth around an arrowhead and sparked it aflame, and shot a wraith weakened by the chanting; the fire caught in its nebulous form, and it shuddered, and Hallas's spear — meeting something more solid now — scattered it into drifting motes. One by one they broke the others, steel and flame and the old words together overwhelming the self-mending spirits, until a last gust of cold swept the chamber and the final wraith came apart into empty silence.2

They stood breathing hard in the frosty gloom. "That curse," Hallas said. "You knew it would help?"

Celenneth wiped her brow. "I remembered fragments of old spells our ancestors used against spirits bound to ancient places. I wasn't sure."

Linnea set a hand on her shoulder. "Your lore saved us, love." Celenneth managed a thin, relieved smile.

They were too deep to retreat easily and too worn to press on, so they made a small camp in an alcove of the chamber, Linnea tending Hallas's wound, Damrod checking the shadows, a low fire coaxed to life from a folding brazier. In hushed voices they agreed the truth of it: this chamber was no separate place but an antechamber of the Maw, and the corruption ran deep — and Celenneth understood that her guardians' fate and this cursed power were bound together, that Brandor and Orlec had perhaps stood at this very altar, trying to hold back the dark. For now they would recover, bound by a fragile alliance and tested together in battle, and ready themselves for the frozen heart of the Maw beyond.

7Entering the Frost Maw

Nénimë 30, 2970

They slept fitfully near the old altar, and when they rose it was hard to mark the passage of time in the absolute dark, lit only by lantern and the faint glow of runes. They shared a scant meal, and then Damrod tested the weight of his shield. "We've lingered long enough. This place is tied to the Maw, and we've seen what haunts it. If we mean to end it, we press on." Hallas nodded. "You lead. We stand with you." And Celenneth, her heart squeezing at the thought of Orlec and Brandor, said, "We go together."

A single wide corridor led deeper, framed by ice-crusted pillars, and they stepped into it with steel and the old curse ready on their tongues. The air grew colder still, their breath hanging longer, and half-buried carvings suggested a path once well-trodden in ancient days. Then, at a sharp bend, Linnea halted them and pointed to a drift of snow against a collapsed pillar: heavy boot-prints, not hound-paws. Celenneth's heart leapt painfully. "Rangers have been here." They followed the trail through winding, ever-smaller chambers, until they reached a cramped space where ice cascaded from the ceiling like frozen curtains — and there, huddled against a stone block beside a nearly-spent torch, lay a figure, wounded, breathing raggedly.

She knew him at once. "Orlec." Older than her memory, gaunt with pain, but unmistakable. She rushed to him and dropped to her knees, pressing two fingers to his neck, and his dull eyes opened.

"Celenneth?" he rasped, trying to sit, wincing — his cloak torn, his arm in a bloodied sling. She gave him water, and he drank in gasping swallows.

"We found you," she whispered. "Brandor — where—?"

"Still deeper," Orlec managed. "We tried to seal it. The source. He stayed behind, fighting." His eyes shut against the pain. "I thought I'd never see a friendly face again."

Tears stood in Celenneth's eyes. "I'm here. And there's someone I want you to meet." She drew Linnea close. "My partner. My love. Linnea. And we have a daughter — Brynja. Safe, at home. You would love her."

Orlec's eyes shone. "Linnea… Brynja," he echoed, savoring the names, a ghost of joy crossing his ruined face. "A daughter. Such hope." He coughed, breath hitching. "You've grown so much, Celenneth. I'm proud of you."

She squeezed his hand. "Where's Brandor? What happened?"

And then his expression twisted, terror flaring stark in his eyes, as if he saw something behind them or in his own last moments. "He's — no — you must—" His voice cut off. His body went rigid. A final sharp breath rattled in his throat, and he stared at her as though trying to warn her, and no words came — and a cold stillness settled over him, and his heart stopped.

"Orlec!" Her cry tore down the icy corridor. She cradled his worn face, tears freezing on her cheeks, and Linnea gripped her shoulder — a steady warmth against unimaginable grief.1

Before any of them could grieve, Damrod's head snapped up, and Hallas spun with his spear raised. Dark shapes emerged from a shadowed corridor — twisted forms in ice-covered armor, eyes burning with unnatural light, bearing old frost-crusted swords: Guardians of this place, and their silence was worse than any roar.

The nearest caught Celenneth in the side before she could raise her guard, steel and ice biting deep, warm blood seeping beneath her cloak. Damrod rushed to cover her; another Guardian smashed his shield-arm; Hallas braced against a third and was driven to one knee. Celenneth's blade glanced off the first's icy breastplate, and the ice smoothed over the shallow scratch instantly — the armor almost alive, refreezing around every wound. Steel alone would not do it.

It was fire that turned it. When Damrod's harder blow fractured a chestplate to reveal the hollow within, Linnea drove a flaming arrow into the breach before the ice could seal it, and the Guardian's core shattered with an echoing crunch, and it collapsed into shards and drifting mist. One down — but the others struck back with fury, the first's blade slicing Celenneth's guard, the third tearing again into Damrod's arm. Weary and bleeding, they fought on: Celenneth hammering a spot she had cracked while Linnea's flame melted the ice fighting to reseal it, Damrod and Hallas distracting and repositioning, until the second Guardian's plating split apart to bare frail bone and old rags, and Celenneth drove her blade into its core. The last stood alone against four, and their combined assault — spear and sword and flaming arrow together — kept the ice from mending, and with a drawn-out crack its armor burst, and the unnatural life within sputtered and died.2

The cavern fell silent but for ragged breathing. Celenneth slumped against a stone, clutching her wounded side, tears returning for Orlec, and Linnea wrapped an arm around her. Damrod traced the runes still visible on the shattered armor. "Once Númenórean wardens," he said quietly, "twisted by dark magic into eternal tools." What had Orlec seen, to fear these things so at the last?

They could not linger. Celenneth closed Orlec's eyes and whispered a promise to return, and they pressed deeper, following faint bootprints and signs of struggle, until a distant flicker of dying torchlight drew them into a small chamber cluttered with ice and rubble. Slumped near the guttering torch, half-buried in frost, lay another ranger, clutching something that gleamed faintly.

Celenneth's breath caught. Even before she reached him she knew: Brandor. She dropped to her knees and found the worst wound beneath his cloak — a deep, frozen gash near the chest — and, faint though it was, a pulse. She worked swiftly, cleaning and binding it and whispering reassurance, and stabilized his breathing though his life hung by a thread. His eyes fluttered, and focused on her with recognition and relief. "Celenneth," he managed, his cracked voice reaching across decades.

She gave him water, and his gaze drifted to what he clutched — a fragment of an ancient blade, its hilt bearing Númenórean runes that glimmered faintly, as if caught between worlds. His trembling hand lifted it toward her. "Take it," he whispered. "Its fate is yours now."

She accepted it gently, feeling the weight of memory and power in it.3 "We're taking you out of here, Brandor," she said. "We found Orlec. He's gone." Her throat closed, but she pressed on.

Grief twisted his features. "Orlec… gone," he repeated, splintering like brittle ice. "I feared as much." Then his eyes sharpened with urgency. "You can't leave. Not yet. There's something still here. We swore to stop it. Promise me." Word by forced word he told it: an altar beneath the ruins, something ancient trapped but not fully; the wards must be reforged, or the altar destroyed. "That blade was our key. Orlec fell buying me time. You must finish it. End what we began."

Celenneth bowed her head, resolve hardening. "We will." And they lifted him carefully between them and bore him toward a more sheltered spot, Linnea and Hallas guarding the flanks, the ancient hilt gripped tight in Celenneth's hand and the last of her guardians breathing shallow against her shoulder.

8The Altar

Súlimë 1, 2970

At last they came to the final chamber — a small circular vault where a terrible cold seeped through the bones and the hum had grown to a low resonant moan. At its center stood a pedestal of black stone, cracked and weeping icy water, runes flickering wildly across walls and ceiling, half-distorted by corruption.

Celenneth advanced with the ancient blade in hand, recalling the patterns she had studied above, and the sword's own runes to guide her. Damrod and Linnea stood guard; Hallas kept close, murmuring the old curse to hold any lurking spirits at bay. As she neared the pedestal a surge of foul magic lashed out — shadows of frost flickering at the chamber's edges — but she raised the blade and chanted the old words more firmly now, weaving in fragments of what her guardians had taught her long ago. The runes on the hilt flared bright, and she traced the missing lines on the pedestal's surface with the blade's broken tip, blue fire dancing over the cracked stone, and the darkness shrank back with a keening wail. Linnea loosed an arrow at a half-forming shape; a soundless pressure built — and then thinned, and settled into a calm hush. Celenneth pressed the broken edge to the last key rune, completing the pattern, and a soft radiance rose from the stone and dimmed, and the threat was contained. No grand explosion, no final roar — only peace returning to old bones of the earth.1

They swept the chamber before they left. Behind a curtain of ice Celenneth found a Númenórean reliquary, intact after untold centuries, holding ceremonial tokens once meant as charms or offerings; and Hallas, clearing a half-blocked hollow, found an iron-bound chest untouched by frost — ancient scrolls in oilskin, old coins, a silver-bound book of forgotten lore. They divided the burden and returned for the two rangers, Damrod lifting Orlec's body as one might a sleeping friend, Celenneth supporting Brandor, and bore relics and memories and their beloved guardians out through the frost-rimed corridors and up at last into the open air — pale sky, clean cold wind, the malevolent whisper below left behind them.

They made camp beneath a rocky ledge, hollowed out by grief but alive. That night, by the small fire, Brandor found the strength to speak of it — how he and Orlec had pushed deeper than they should, trusting old lore and scattered runes; how they'd glimpsed a sealed and ancient malignance and tried to piece it together with too little time. "We knew it had to be stopped," he said. "Orlec believed the wards could be restored, with the right place and the right tools. By the time we saw how much we were missing, it was too late." Celenneth gripped the relics at her side. "You gave everything to hold the line." His eyes glimmered. "Knowing you finished what we began — it eases the weight on my heart."

He spoke, too, of the past. "You must know how unexpected it was, for Orlec and me to raise a child. We were rangers, warriors — not caretakers. When we found you, after your family was lost, we could have turned away. But Orlec said, she needs us. That was all it took." Linnea reached to squeeze Celenneth's hand. "You gave me a reason to live beyond the blade and the hunt," Celenneth said softly. Brandor nodded. "Raising you was never easy — we knew how to track beasts, not soothe a frightened child. But you gave us purpose. You made the endless fight mean something more. And now you've made a family of your own. Brynja — a spark of a future we never dared hope for."

In the pale light of the next morning, as Celenneth knelt to change his bandages, Brandor caught her hand and held it, his voice almost serene. "I've chosen my path, child. Last night I took certain roots — they'll let me slip away quietly, without pain."

Her eyes went wide, tears pricking. "Brandor — no — why—?"

He hushed her gently. "Seeing you alive, with Linnea, knowing Brynja waits for you — it's more than I dreamed I'd witness. But I can't leave Orlec alone in this cold earth. My work is done. I fought my battles, I raised you, I tried to seal these horrors, and you finished it. My soul's companion is waiting. I would never leave him behind. Let me rest here, beside him."

Celenneth's tears slipped free. "If that is your wish," she whispered, "I won't deny it."

He smiled softly. "You've given me peace. Thank you." And he closed his eyes and breathed slow, waiting for the quiet end he had chosen, and Celenneth wept and held him, Linnea steady beside her, Damrod and Hallas bowing their heads. There would be no arguing it; he was tired, his purpose fulfilled, and he belonged at Orlec's side in death as in life.2

They dug the grave under the frozen sky, the hard earth yielding at last to their blades, and laid Orlec and Brandor side by side with their hands clasped, tokens and sigils arranged with care, weapons crossed over them in a last honor guard of steel. Linnea whispered a prayer through her tears; Hallas set down a small token from the reliquary; Damrod bowed stiff and jaw-clenched; and Celenneth laid a woodcarving of the old blade's runes upon them, a memory of the darkness they had bested. Then they stepped back, hearts heavy but calmer, and left the two rangers to sleep in the wild hills they had died to protect.

After a long silence Celenneth cleared her throat. "We'll go home. To Bree, to Brynja. There's nothing more we can do here."

Damrod rested a hand on his sword-hilt. "We'll guide you by a safe route — swing east, follow the quieter valleys. Less direct, but it avoids the harshest passes."

Hallas inclined his head. "You deserve an easier road out of this wilderness. And we'd be honored to see you two home." Celenneth dipped her head, grateful past saying, and they gathered their gear — the reliquary, the chest, the broken Warden Blade — and settled in for one last night by the fire, survivors bearing old relics and new grief, and turned their faces, at last, toward home.
