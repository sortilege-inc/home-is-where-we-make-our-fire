1Heading North

Near the end of winter, 2970, the Angles stood in a state of quiet transition. Most of the snow had melted, leaving patches of slush and muddy paths. The trees were still bare, but the air had begun to soften. There was a sense of waiting, of leaning toward spring. Birds called early in the mornings, and the Dúnedain families who lived scattered across these gentle hills were busy repairing fences, clearing ditches, and preparing fields for the warmer seasons ahead.

Inside a small cottage near the outskirts of the Angles, Linnea knelt beside the hearth, coaxing a low fire into a steady glow. The warmth slowly spread through the main room. Celenneth stood by the window, looking out at a horizon still tinged with winter’s gray. Brynja, now four years old and full of questions, darted from one corner of the room to the next, collecting a handful of wooden figures she liked to arrange along the windowsill. Every so often, she’d ask something like, “Where do the birds sleep at night?” or “Why does the snow melt when the sun comes out?” Linnea and Celenneth answered what they could, amused by her endless curiosity.

They had made a life here in the Angles. Among these Dúnedain families—quiet, resilient folk who shared their harvests and their stories—Linnea and Celenneth had found something close to home. Brynja had friends to play with, neighbors dropped by with bread or honey, and no one asked too many questions about the past. It was a life of small comforts and daily chores: gathering herbs, helping neighbors mend a fence, trading gossip at the well. Simple, and good.

But Linnea knew that Celenneth carried a lingering restlessness. She’d seen it in the way Celenneth sometimes stared into the distance, as though listening for old voices carried by the wind. Celenneth had told her stories—of the rangers who’d raised her when she was young, of running beneath endless skies and learning to track in silent forests, of leaving them behind at fourteen or fifteen to forge her own path. They were part of Celenneth’s past, but she hadn’t seen them in years. She didn’t even know if they still lived.

Linnea recalled a quiet evening a few weeks ago, after Brynja had fallen asleep. The two of them had sat at the table, lamplight dancing on their faces, a comfortable hush between them. Linnea had asked softly, “Have you ever thought about going north? Back to your kin?”

Celenneth had paused, blade and whetstone in hand. “My kin,” she said, as if tasting unfamiliar words. She looked at the sword, then at Linnea. “I have thought about it, yes,” she admitted. “But it’s been so long. I left them when I was so young. I’m not even sure they’re alive.”

Linnea remembered how her heart had tightened at that uncertainty. “Don’t you want to know?” she’d asked gently.

Celenneth had studied her face, then looked at her empty palm as if searching for something that wasn’t there. “Sometimes I do,” she said. “But maybe I’m afraid of what I’ll find. Or won’t find. Life out there is hard. Rangers move like ghosts, leaving few traces unless they want to be found.”

Linnea had reached across the table and covered Celenneth’s hand with her own. “If you ever want to go,” she’d said, “I’ll go with you. You won’t have to face it alone.”

Celenneth’s eyes had grown distant, but grateful. “Thank you. But I’m not sure I could bear the answers. Not yet.”

That “not yet” lingered.

Now, weeks later, that moment had ripened into a decision. Celenneth had finally admitted she did need to know, or at least try. The uncertainty weighed on her too heavily. She wanted to see if the men who raised her still walked the wild roads. Even if they were gone, she needed the truth. It was time.

Linnea supported this wholeheartedly. She saw how Celenneth wrestled with her past, and she believed that facing it could give Celenneth peace—help her feel settled in their life here, or guide them toward something new. For them to truly live in the Angles, building a future with Brynja, Celenneth needed to understand her roots, or at least close that old chapter with honesty.

So they decided: they would travel north. The journey would be long, and not without risk. The winter roads were muddy, and who knew what lurked between the Angles and the far northern reaches where rangers sometimes gathered.

The question of Brynja arose immediately. The child was strong and curious, but four years old was too young for such a trek. They’d be moving through rough country, and this journey wasn’t just a holiday—it was a search that might end in sorrow or uncertainty, and it could take weeks, maybe longer.

They turned to their closest friends in the Angles, the Aldion family: Eadric, Lira, and their daughter, Elys. Eadric and Lira had welcomed Linnea and Celenneth warmly since they settled here. Eadric, a skilled tracker who often patrolled the region’s outskirts, was steady and kind. Lira was known for her herbal remedies and calm wisdom. Their daughter, Elys, was Brynja’s age, and the two girls had become inseparable, spending afternoons playing in the fields, chasing insects, and splashing in puddles.

When Linnea and Celenneth asked if they might leave Brynja with them, Eadric and Lira agreed without hesitation. They understood the pull of the past and the need to face old questions. Brynja, of course, was uncertain why her parents would go without her, but the promise of staying with Elys—her best friend—soothed her. Lira knelt before Brynja and explained softly that her parents had important errands, but would come back soon, and that in the meantime, Brynja and Elys would have many adventures together. Brynja’s worried frown eased at the mention of her friend.

The final preparations took a few days. Linnea and Celenneth sorted their gear: sturdy cloaks, warm blankets, dried foods, simple medicines. Celenneth sharpened her blade and checked her bow. Linnea packed flint and tinder, extra wool socks, and a small tin of salve for sore muscles. They gathered what coin they had, though money might not matter much if they ranged far off the beaten track.

In the evenings before their departure, they spent quiet moments with Brynja. They explained as best they could. Linnea said, “We’re going to find some old friends of Mama Celenneth. We’ll be gone a while, but we’ll come back.” Brynja nodded, eyes bright, though Linnea could see she didn’t fully understand. Children felt time differently—“a while” could mean anything.

The night before they were to leave, Linnea and Celenneth sat by the hearth after Brynja had gone to sleep. Eadric and Lira had stopped by earlier, confirming all was ready. Elys couldn’t wait for Brynja’s “special sleepover,” as she called it. Everything was in place.

Linnea studied Celenneth’s face in the firelight. “Nervous?” she asked softly.

Celenneth took a moment before answering. “A little,” she said, voice quiet. “I keep wondering what we’ll find. Maybe they’re alive and well, or maybe I’m chasing ghosts.” Her gaze dropped to the floor. “But not knowing feels worse than anything else.”

Linnea nodded, lacing their fingers together. “We’ll face it together,” she said. “And when it’s done, we’ll come home.”

Celenneth smiled at that word, “home.” It had taken time for this place—the Angles, this cottage, these neighbors—to feel like that. But it did now, thanks to Brynja’s laughter echoing in the yard, thanks to shared meals with Eadric and Lira, thanks to the sense of belonging they’d built piece by piece. If a journey north could help Celenneth settle the last unanswered questions in her heart, it was worth it.

At dawn, they rose early. Brynja was up, too, sleepy-eyed but excited because Elys and her parents would come soon. When Eadric and Lira arrived with Elys, the two girls ran off to chase a flock of sparrows near the fence. Lira handed Linnea a small pouch of dried herbs “for the road,” and Eadric gripped Celenneth’s shoulder in quiet solidarity.

Brynja returned as they saddled the horses. She stood at the cottage door, holding Celenneth’s hand tight and then Linnea’s. She looked between them, serious for a child her age. Linnea bent down, stroking Brynja’s dark hair. “We’ll be back,” Linnea promised, voice gentle. “And while we’re gone, you’ll have fun with Elys, right?”

Brynja nodded slowly. “I’ll show her how to catch beetles by the stream,” she said matter-of-factly, as if that sealed the deal.

Celenneth crouched down, pressing a kiss to Brynja’s forehead. “You be good,” she said softly. “Listen to Lira and Eadric, and we’ll bring you something special from our travels.”

Brynja’s eyes brightened at that. Then she did something that made Linnea’s throat tighten: she reached up and patted Celenneth’s cheek as if to say, “I trust you.” It was a small gesture, but so full of understanding and love that Celenneth had to look away for a moment to keep her composure.

They mounted their horses, Eorlin and Nimroch. The morning air felt crisp on their faces, and the muddy roads gleamed in the early light. They set out northward, following a path that wound through low hills and sparse woodlands. The Aldion family stood waving until the riders were small figures on the horizon. Brynja and Elys hopped and laughed, already making new games.

The landscape opened up before them. Winter’s hold was loosening, but not yet gone. Patches of snow clung in hollows, and the wind carried a faint chill. Still, birds sang here and there, and they passed a few early wildflowers daring to bloom near sheltered rocks. They avoided the main routes where possible, preferring quieter paths that the Dúnedain used for scouting and travel.

As they rode, Linnea’s thoughts drifted to their goal. They were heading toward lands where rangers moved like whispers, men and women who guarded roads and borders but seldom revealed themselves. Finding the two men who raised Celenneth might be tricky. They might need to ask quietly among scattered communities, track rumors, follow signs of ranger presence. It wouldn’t be simple. But Celenneth’s determination was solid, and Linnea’s loyalty was unwavering. They had time and patience.

Celenneth broke the silence after a few hours. “Thank you,” she said, her voice carried by the breeze. “For doing this with me.”

Linnea smiled, adjusting her cloak. “I promised you I would, remember?”

Celenneth glanced sidelong at her, warmth in her eyes. “I remember. Just didn’t think I’d ever gather the courage to actually go.”

Linnea shrugged lightly. “Sometimes you just need someone to push you a little. Besides, I’m curious, too. I’ve heard so much about these men, these ranger-guardians of your past. I want to know what shaped you into who you are.”

Celenneth’s expression grew thoughtful. “They taught me to track, to hunt, to read the weather in the shape of clouds. They were harsh at times, but kind too. I was so young, I didn’t understand half of what they said about duty and sacrifice. I wonder if I’ll understand it now.”

Linnea listened, picturing Celenneth as a wiry teenager, following two tall, weathered men through secret forest trails. She could almost see it: the stern lessons, the quiet smiles, the patient corrections. It had formed Celenneth’s character—her steady hand, her keen senses, her quiet strength. Linnea felt gratitude toward these unknown men, whoever and wherever they were, for helping make Celenneth who she became.

They rode through the remainder of the day and camped by a small stream that night. Linnea gathered tinder and built a fire while Celenneth scouted the perimeter. The world felt vast and quiet, with no sound but the flow of water and the rustle of dry reeds. Linnea missed Brynja’s chatter, but the memory of her daughter’s trust filled her with a warm resolve.

Over a simple dinner of dried fruit and hard bread, they spoke in low voices. “Any idea where to start looking?” Linnea asked.

Celenneth poked at the fire’s embers. “I know that rangers sometimes meet in certain hidden camps—places I remember vaguely. We could head toward one of those old haunts I recall, see if anyone’s there or left signs. If not, we’ll ask at small villages along the way. People might have seen them. Rangers usually leave a memory, if not a footprint.”

Linnea nodded, satisfied with this loose plan. “We’ve got time,” she said, stretching out under her blanket. Above them, stars scattered across a deep sky. She wondered if Brynja was asleep by now, curled up with Elys, or telling stories in her high-pitched voice until Lira coaxed them to rest.

“Do you think Brynja’s all right?” Celenneth asked quietly, echoing Linnea’s thoughts.

“Of course,” Linnea replied, confident. “She’s in good hands. And if there’s any trouble, Eadric can handle it. Besides, Brynja’s excited to be with Elys. I think she’ll hardly notice we’re gone once they start having fun.”

Celenneth chuckled softly. “You’re probably right.” She fell silent for a moment, then added, “I’m glad we have a home to come back to. For a long time, I never stayed in one place long enough to call it that. And now we leave Brynja with friends I trust. That... feels like a gift.”

Linnea reached out, found Celenneth’s hand in the flickering light. “It is a gift. One we earned by building a life there.”

They said no more, letting the soft crackle of the fire lull them. The journey had just begun. Ahead lay uncertainty, old memories, and perhaps difficult truths. But they were not alone. They had each other, and a place to return to. That steadied them both.

2The Silver Lily

Late afternoon light stretched across the muddy lanes of Gelden Hollow, turning puddles into pale gold mirrors. Linnea guided her mare, Eorlas, at an easy walk, scanning the shabby fronts of shops and houses. Celenneth rode beside her on Nimroch, the white horse’s coat dusty from travel. They’d arrived just as the sun dipped low, hoping to find someone Celenneth once met—someone who might know more about Brandor and Orlec’s trail.

Gelden Hollow felt tucked away from the world, neither large nor important. The buildings were simple—timber frames, thatched roofs, with crooked signs above a few doors. A blacksmith’s forge smoked in the distance, and somewhere a soft lullaby drifted from a closed window. Nothing grand, but not exactly hostile either.

They were looking for the Silver Lily, a brothel known to Celenneth from younger years. She hadn’t said much about it on the road, only that it was more respectable than one might expect, and that the people there sometimes knew things others didn’t. Linnea had raised an eyebrow at that but held her tongue. She trusted Celenneth’s judgment. If Brandor and Orlec had passed through these parts, maybe someone at the Lily could shed light on their disappearance.

They turned a corner and spotted a low building that matched Celenneth’s memory: unassuming from the outside, shutters half-drawn, no flashy signage. Just a small painted lily etched into the lintel. “There,” Celenneth said quietly.

Linnea glanced around. The street was calm—an old man shuffled by with a sack of oats, barely acknowledging them. A woman sweeping her stoop gave them a faint nod. Good. They didn’t need attention.

They dismounted near a modest stable behind the building. Linnea patted Eorlas’s neck, while Celenneth led Nimroch inside. The stableboy wasn’t around, but they found empty stalls and fresh hay. With horses settled, they returned to the Silver Lily’s front door.

Linnea studied Celenneth’s face. “You sure we’ll find something here?”

Celenneth shrugged, voice low. “If not direct answers, then at least a name or a lead. Brandor and Orlec spent time here. They trusted these people.”

Linnea nodded. Brandor and Orlec: two rangers who raised Celenneth years ago. Their trail had gone cold, and now Linnea and Celenneth were piecing together what happened. Rumor said the men had chased old Numenorian relics in the Lone-Lands. Finding their fate mattered deeply to Celenneth.

They stepped inside. The Silver Lily’s door opened onto a room lit by warm lamplight, scented with subtle incense. Linnea inhaled a blend of spices and something floral. The place felt unexpectedly cozy, not the seedy dive one might imagine. Soft drapes sectioned off lounge areas, plush seating invited patrons to relax. The atmosphere hummed with low conversation, muted laughter, and an occasional clink of glasses.

Men and women chatted softly, some leaning close over small tables, others heading upstairs arm in arm. It was calm, intimate. Linnea relaxed a bit. This was no raucous tavern but a discreet haven, a place where secrets might be kept and shared for the right reason.

A woman approached—clearly the madame, by her confident posture and assessing gaze. She wore a well-fitted dress, her eyes sharp. “Welcome,” she said smoothly, but Linnea sensed caution beneath the greeting. “Need a spot to rest? Some company?”

Celenneth offered a polite smile. “Not that kind of business,” she said softly. “We’re looking for information. About two rangers—Brandor and Orlec—who used to come here somewhat regularly. We hoped one of your staff might remember them.”

The madame’s eyes narrowed slightly, but her smile remained. Linnea watched as the woman weighed the risks. “Brandor and Orlec,” she repeated. “I remember them. Rangers leave impressions.” Her voice was cool but not unfriendly.

Celenneth nodded. “They raised me. I’m hoping to find them out in the wilds.”

A flicker of recognition crossed the madame’s face. She studied Celenneth more closely, perhaps recalling a younger version of her. “Wait here,” the madame said, gesturing to a nearby table draped in soft fabric. “I’ll check my records.”

They sat as instructed, Linnea and Celenneth settling onto a low couch. The patrons paid them little mind. This felt like a place where people respected privacy. Linnea took in the gentle glow of lanterns behind colored screens, the soft laughter from nearby alcoves. She glanced at Celenneth, who watched the entrance with focused calm.

Brandor and Orlec: their names lingered like ghosts. Linnea could sense Celenneth’s impatience and worry, even if her partner’s face remained composed.

Before long, the madame returned with Eira. Eira was tall with short black hair. Her blouse was loose and her breasts seemed to perpetually be on the verge of falling out of it. She wore a simple, elegant dress. Meeting Celenneth’s eyes, Eira’s expression shifted to surprise and something like regret.

“Celenneth,” Eira said softly, stopping a few steps away. “It’s been a long time. I never thought I’d see you again.”

Celenneth stood. “Life takes strange turns,” she said simply. “I’m sorry to show up like this, but I need your help. Brandor and Orlec were here, right? We must know what happened to them.”

Eira hesitated, glancing at Linnea briefly before focusing back on Celenneth. Finally, she nodded. “Come,” Eira said, voice quieter. “Let’s talk somewhere private.”

They followed Eira to a secluded corner draped with heavy curtains. Inside was a low table and plush couch, lamplight dim. Eira closed the curtains behind them. Linnea perched beside Celenneth, Eira sitting opposite, hands folded in her lap.

For a moment, silence. Celenneth broke it. “Eira, it seems like you might have been the last one they saw when they came through last. Did they say anything to you before they left?”

Eira’s eyes dropped. “They were excited but nervous. Orlec talked about a discovery—something old and powerful. Brandor was tense, like he sensed danger. I warned them. I said it sounded risky, but they insisted it was important. They left at night and haven’t returned. This was last summer.”

Linnea leaned forward. “Did they mention being followed, or meeting suspicious people?”

Eira frowned, thinking. “Not outright. But Orlec hinted that knowledge like theirs attracts trouble—treasure hunters, cultists, bandits. They knew the risks. But this time, I sensed desperation. They believed they had to go. Orlec mentioned a watchtower, a stream that forked, a landmark to guide them north. Something about old Numenorian script carved into stone.”

Celenneth’s jaw tightened. That was more than they’d known before. A watchtower and a stream—a place to start searching. “They never sent word after?” she asked, though the answer was obvious.

Eira shook her head. “No. I waited, hoping they’d return. They promised they would, said they’d share their findings.” Her voice grew softer. “They’re gone, aren’t they?”

Celenneth hesitated. “We don’t know. That’s why we’re going to find out.”

Eira’s gaze lifted, meeting Celenneth’s eyes. “Take me with you.”

Linnea blinked, surprised. Celenneth likewise seemed taken aback. “Eira, it’s dangerous. We’re heading into wild lands, hunting for clues in ruins. There could be bandits, beasts… worse. You don’t owe them this.”

Eira smiled sadly. “I owe them more than you think. Brandor and Orlec treated me kindly when few did. They weren’t just patrons; they respected me. If I can help find the truth, I want to. I can ride, I can handle a dagger if needed, and I know these lands. Let me help. Besides, I have business to attend to up north, and you’re safer to travel with than most.”

Linnea studied Eira’s face. She seemed sincere. Another ally could be valuable, especially one who knew the region. Celenneth weighed the risk, lips pressed thin. After a moment, she nodded. “Alright. We leave in the morning. Pack what you need. We’ll head north at first light.”

Eira let out a quiet breath, relief mixed with resolve. “Thank you. I’ll speak to the madame. She’s used to my comings and goings, though not usually for reasons like this.”

Celenneth stood and offered Eira a faint smile. “We appreciate it.”

Eira slipped out, leaving Linnea and Celenneth momentarily alone behind the curtains. Linnea reached over, touching Celenneth’s arm lightly. “Think this is a good idea?”

Celenneth shrugged. “We need all the help we can get. Eira knew them, can guide us. I trust her enough to give it a chance.”

Linnea nodded. “Agreed. Let’s find a place to sleep and leave at dawn.”

They emerged into the main room. The madame caught their eye and raised a brow. Celenneth gave a small nod of thanks. The madame inclined her head, as if to say she hoped they found what they sought.

Outside, the sky was shifting into dusk, a soft glow lingering. Linnea and Celenneth found a modest inn down the street. The inn was quiet, run by an older couple who barely asked questions. Perfect for a short stay. They took a small room with two narrow beds and simple furniture. After stabling their horses again for the night, they ate a simple meal in the inn’s common room—stew, bread, and a mug of weak ale each.

Over dinner, they spoke in low tones. “We have a direction now,” Linnea said. “Eira mentioned a watchtower and a forked stream. We’ll start there.”

Celenneth stirred her stew thoughtfully. “From there, maybe we’ll find tracks or abandoned camps. They couldn’t have vanished without leaving a trace.”

Linnea understood Celenneth’s determination. These men were important to her, a link to her past. Uncovering their fate mattered not just for closure, but to ensure Celenneth could move forward unburdened by old shadows.

They finished quietly, then went upstairs to their small room. Linnea lit a candle on the bedside table. Celenneth sat on the bed, removing her boots.

“Eira joining us changes our dynamic,” Linnea said softly, not complaining, just stating a fact.

Celenneth nodded. “It does. But she might know shortcuts, safe routes. She has a reason to help. And maybe this journey will help her too.”

Linnea trusted Celenneth’s judgment. They blew out the candle and slept. The night passed without trouble. In the early dawn, they rose and packed, ate a crust of bread, and went out to the stable. Eorlas and Nimroch were rested, nickering softly as they saddled up.

A soft step behind them: Eira appeared, cloak and pack ready, her hair braided back, a dagger at her hip. She gave them a tentative smile. “I’m ready.”

Celenneth nodded. “Good. Let’s move before the town stirs.”

They led the horses onto the quiet main street. Gelden Hollow still slumbered, only a few distant figures moving about. The smell of dew on the thatch roofs, the hush of waking birds—peaceful, yet charged with purpose.

3The Broken Branch

The days after leaving Gelden Hollow had been quiet but purposeful. Celenneth, Linnea, and Eira made steady progress north and west, edging closer to the Evendim hills. Though no great roads led there, they found narrow lanes and old tracks, following scraps of knowledge gleaned from the Silver Lily.

The journey so far had been calm enough—no sign of trouble beyond the usual wary glances of passing travelers. Eira proved a pleasant companion, quick with small bits of advice: where to find cleaner water, which shrubs hid biting insects. She could ride, handle a dagger, and had a patient way of bearing the long hours on the trail. Linnea appreciated her presence more than she’d expected. Celenneth seemed relieved to have another person who knew Brandor and Orlec, someone who understood the weight of their absence.

As they crossed into Tarnath’s outskirts, Linnea noticed the shift in atmosphere. This wasn’t a peaceful hollow like Gelden, nor a friendly hamlet. Tarnath leaned closer to the rough side of the world. Crooked buildings clustered around a muddy central lane, the smell of stale ale and smoke drifting through the air. Wagons creaked by, and weathered faces watched from shaded doorways. No one offered greetings.

Celenneth had warned them about Tarnath: it was a crossroads town with a hard reputation, a place mercenaries and cutthroats passed through. But word had it that Brandor and Orlec once stopped here too. They needed confirmation, maybe even a map or name from the tavern known as the Broken Branch.

They led their horses—Nimroch for Celenneth, Eorlas for Linnea, and Eira’s borrowed mare—toward the old tavern at the end of Tarnath’s main row. The Broken Branch sign hung crookedly, paint chipped, barely readable. Even from outside, laughter rang out, harsh and rowdy, mixed with curses and the slam of mugs on tables. The late afternoon light did little to soften the place’s rough edges.

Linnea’s shoulders tightened. She remembered the Silver Lily’s warmth and discreet comfort. This would be nothing like that. Celenneth caught her eye, giving a small nod of reassurance. Eira remained quiet, but Linnea noticed her hand hovering near her belt, where a dagger was sheathed.

“Let’s tie the horses around back,” Celenneth said softly, voice steady. “We won’t linger long.”

They found a rail behind the tavern. A few other horses stood there, thick-shouldered animals that looked well-used by hard riders. Linnea patted Eorlas, whispering a soothing word. Eira scanned the alley with a wary gaze. Satisfied, they made their way to the front door. The building’s weather-beaten planks creaked underfoot.

Inside, the Broken Branch smelled of old ale, cheap pipeweed, and sweat. Lamplight cast shadows over worn tables, and the hearth’s smoke hung in the air. Mercenaries in battered leather armor crowded around small tables, nursing drinks and grunting through broken-toothed smiles. The noise dipped slightly as the trio entered. Eyes turned their way: Linnea’s slender frame, Celenneth’s confident posture, Eira’s calm watchfulness. Three women who didn’t fit the usual mold of this place.

Linnea felt the weight of stares. These weren’t friendly travelers. The silence stretched a heartbeat too long, then broke into muttered whispers. A few low chuckles—nothing friendly there.

Eira stepped closer to Linnea, as if to say, “I’ve got your back too.” Celenneth took the lead, guiding them toward the bar at the far end of the room. They passed a table where a large man with a scarred cheek eyed Linnea with open interest. Another with a face like a weathered rock gave Eira a sidelong smirk. Linnea kept her gaze forward, heart steady. She’d seen this sort of attention before, but it never got easier.

As they reached the bar, the tavernkeeper glanced up. He was an older fellow, grizzled, with sharp eyes. He dried a mug with a rag that looked no cleaner than the floor. Linnea noticed that he, at least, seemed unimpressed by the display of interest from his patrons—perhaps he’d seen it all.

He raised a brow. “What’ll it be?” he asked, voice low and gruff.

Celenneth answered quietly. “Information, mostly. A drink wouldn’t hurt, though. Ale for three, if it’s decent.”

The barkeep gave a curt nod, poured ale into three chipped mugs, and placed them on the counter. “What kind of information?” He didn’t offer a smile, just met Celenneth’s eyes with steady patience.

Celenneth took a mug, sipped, not grimacing at the taste. “We’re looking for two rangers: Brandor and Orlec,” she said. “They passed through here some time ago, chasing rumors of old Numenorian relics. Ever hear those names?”

At that, the barkeep’s gaze sharpened. “Brandor and Orlec. Aye, I remember. Hard to forget men like them. They had a presence—rangers tend to.”

Eira slipped onto a stool beside Linnea, sipping her ale warily. Linnea took a cautious taste—bitter, but drinkable. She watched the barkeep’s face, seeing if he’d recognize Eira. He didn’t seem to, at least not openly.

The barkeep continued, voice quieter now that he knew he was dealing with serious inquiries. “They came through… oh, must have been some months back. Gathered supplies, asked questions about the hills. Thought they smelled of trouble, to be honest.”

Celenneth nodded. “They raised me, long ago. I’m trying to find out what happened to them. We’ve followed hints and rumors from Gelden Hollow and beyond.”

The barkeep tilted his head, and Linnea glimpsed a flicker of recognition at the mention of Gelden Hollow. Perhaps he understood the chain of contacts they’d tapped. “So you’ve got a trail. They headed north, didn’t they? Toward Evendim, I heard. Claimed they’d found signs of old watchtowers and inscriptions. They were in a hurry—looked over their shoulders a lot.”

Eira leaned forward slightly, voice soft. “Did they mention anyone following them, or show fear of certain people?”

The barkeep gave Eira a measured look, as if noting her short black hair and the dagger at her hip. “They didn’t name anyone, but Brandor was tense. He kept scanning the room as if expecting trouble. Orlec tried to act casual, but he couldn’t hide that edge.”

A few mercenaries at a nearby table grew quiet, listening with thinly veiled curiosity. The barkeep noticed and lowered his voice. “They asked me about the old roads that vanish in those hills, about a certain ruin that was said to hold a relic. I warned them not to trust anyone out there. Too many folk smell gold and go mad.”

Celenneth’s eyes narrowed. “They never came back here, I take it.”

He shook his head. “No. Haven’t heard a peep since. But if you’re set on following them, I can mark a rough path on a scrap of parchment. Not a proper map, mind you, but it might help. Could be they took a route that passes by Tarnath’s old logging camp—abandoned now—then north into the wilder stretches.”

5Leaving the Ranger’s Station

The wraiths attacked in eerie silence. One rushed Linnea, raising its icy blade high. She leapt back, loosing an arrow that passed through its misted torso, scattering frost crystals. The creature only re-formed a pace away, twisting its head in a soundless mockery of a laugh.

Hallas met another wraith’s charge, spear poised. The spear’s tip met an odd resistance, neither flesh nor empty air. He felt a biting cold spread through the spear’s shaft into his hands. Damrod and Celenneth stood back-to-back, blades ready. A third wraith lunged at Damrod’s flank. Damrod blocked, but the spirit’s icy sword left a crust of frost along his shield rim.

Celenneth struck at the nearest phantom. Her sword passed through the wraith’s robes, finding scant purchase. It hissed, drifting aside. She realized steel alone was not enough. Memory sparked—a curse taught in old Ranger archives, a Númenórean incantation meant to unsettle spirits tied to the ancient wars.

As another wraith lunged at her, Celenneth stepped back, raised her voice, and spoke in old words that scraped like distant iron on stone. Her tone was low and forceful: “Basannórë maitanëa, durëa hlaruvalyë!” The ancient curse rolled through the chamber, echoing strangely. A faint tremor passed through the wraiths, as if they recognized the words.

Linnea and Hallas exchanged startled glances. Damrod, eyes narrowing, understood that Celenneth might have tapped into old lore. Another wraith advanced on him, blade raised. He repeated the words he’d just heard from Celenneth, stumbling slightly on the syllables: “Basannórë maitanëa…” His voice lacked her fluency, but the effect was there—he saw the wraith’s form flicker and slow, as if wading through thicker air.

Hallas caught on, muttering the curse under his breath, each repetition lending it power. Linnea’s voice joined, hesitant at first, but growing stronger. Together, they chanted this old Númenórean malediction, each voice adding weight to the ancient words. The wraiths shrank back slightly, forced to contend not just with steel, but with the heavy presence of old magic remembered.

Seizing the advantage, Linnea tried another tactic. She wrapped oil-soaked cloth around an arrowhead and sparked it aflame with quick strikes of flint. The arrow’s flame danced in the gloom. She shot at a wraith weakened by their chanting. The flame found a hold in its nebulous form, and the wraith shuddered, its silent shriek like a ripple in the air.

Hallas took the cue and struck at the same wraith. This time, his spear met something more solid, as if the curse and the flame had given the spirit just enough substance to be broken. The wraith’s form scattered into drifting motes of ice.

The remaining three grew frantic, blades chopping at the air. Damrod braced his shield as one hammered at it with furious strokes. Celenneth kept chanting, voice more certain now, her sword raised. She timed her strike as the wraith slowed under the curse’s weight. Her blade cut a faint line of frost-fire through its torso. Linnea fired another flaming arrow, and Damrod lunged, cutting horizontally. The wraith flickered and burst apart in a shower of tiny ice shards.

Two left. One hovered near the altar, trying to draw strength from the runes there. Hallas and Linnea advanced on it, chanting softly, their voices rough but insistent. The wraith tried to dart forward, but stumbled mid-lunge, trapped by old words it could not outrun. Hallas’s spear cracked into its center, and Linnea’s arrow pierced its head, scattering the phantom into formless frost.

The last wraith circled Celenneth and Damrod, wary now. They pressed it, chanting the curse in unison. Celenneth’s pronunciation was the clearest, her voice steady with remembered lore. The spirit strained against the sound, each syllable like a chain dragging it down. Damrod struck high, Celenneth low. The wraith shivered, form collapsing inward. A final gust of cold air swept over them as the wraith broke apart, leaving only empty silence.

In the battle’s aftermath, they stood panting, breath misting in the frosty gloom. Celenneth lowered her sword, heart pounding. She met Damrod’s gaze. He nodded, respect in his eyes. Linnea rubbed her stiff fingers, still holding her bow, and Hallas breathed heavily, leaning on his spear.

“That curse,” Hallas said softly, voice echoing, “you knew it would help?”

Celenneth shrugged, wiping sweat from her brow. “I remembered reading fragments of old spells used by our ancestors against spirits bound to ancient places. I wasn’t sure it would work.”

Damrod let out a short, breathy laugh. “Well, it certainly did.” He flexed his shield arm, the numbness receding now that the wraiths were gone. “Without it, I’m not sure we’d have managed.”

Linnea stepped closer, placing a hand on Celenneth’s shoulder. “Your lore saved us, love,” she said quietly, voice warm despite the chill. Celenneth offered a thin, relieved smile.

The chamber’s silence returned, but now they were alone with the altar and the old reliefs, no phantoms lurking. Tired and shaken, they decided to find a sheltered corner of the cavern to rest. Though none liked the idea of lingering in this tainted chamber, they had no choice. They were too deep to retreat easily, and too worn to push deeper without recovery.

They found a small alcove along the chamber’s perimeter, partly shielded by fallen stone blocks. Linnea treated Hallas’ wound. Damrod cleared debris as he checked for threats. Celenneth set up a makeshift camp, using spare cloaks and a folding brazier from Damrod’s pack. With careful strikes of flint, they lit a low fire. The warmth felt like a gift from a kinder world.

As they settled, Linnea boiled water for tea—just enough to fend off the biting chill. Damrod breathed easier now, gaze drifting to the carved reliefs that surrounded them. Hallas stared at the altar with troubled eyes, as if hoping to understand what they had found. Celenneth rubbed her aching shoulder, recalling how close those wraith blades had come.

In hushed voices, they conferred. It seemed this chamber was not separate from the Frost Maw, but an antechamber of sorts, leading them deeper into its icy bowels. Damrod and Hallas had hoped to address whatever cursed power plagued the region without venturing too far. But these battles with frost hounds and wraiths hinted that the corruption ran deep. Celenneth and Linnea realized that their path toward the Frost Maw and the older rangers’ guardians lay inextricably tied to solving these mysteries. Perhaps the guardians had once stood here, at this very altar, trying to hold back the dark.

For now, they would recover. Bound by a fragile alliance and tested in combat, the four rangers huddled close to the fire, tea steaming faintly in battered cups. They might not have chased away the shadows that haunted this place, but they had survived its first ordeals together. And in the quiet crackle of the small fire, amidst runes and lost histories, they prepared to face whatever lay beyond, deeper in the Maw’s frozen heart.

7Entering the Frost Maw

They slept fitfully in that makeshift camp near the old altar. The brazier’s coals glowed dimly, reflecting on the ice-slick stone and the half-faded reliefs etched long ago. Linnea dozed lightly, one hand near her bow, while Hallas, after tending his injuries, took first watch with quiet determination. Damrod sat against a fallen block, eyes half-shut but ears keen for the slightest echo. Celenneth rested close to Linnea, exhaustion tugging her into brief pockets of uneasy slumber, her mind drifting through old memories of Brandor and Orlec, and the haunting knowledge that deeper below awaited answers—or more horrors.

When they rose, it was hard to mark the passage of time. The darkness underground remained absolute except for their lantern and the faint glow of runes. Celenneth stretched, rubbing a kink out of her neck, and Linnea shared a scant meal of dried fruit and hard bread with the others. Damrod poured lukewarm water into wooden cups, and Hallas paced the perimeter, spear tapping lightly against stone.

No one spoke much at first. They understood what lay ahead: further descent into the Frost Maw’s bowels, drawn by the threads of old magic and duty. Damrod cleared his throat. “We’ve lingered long enough,” he said quietly, testing the weight of his shield. “We know now this place is tied to the Maw. We’ve seen what haunts it. If we want to end this—” he glanced at Hallas, “—we must press on.”

Hallas nodded gravely. “Aye. Much as we feared. Let’s see what fate holds below.” He turned to Celenneth and Linnea. “You lead. We stand with you.”

Celenneth’s heart squeezed at the thought of Orlec and Brandor. Somewhere ahead, their story had ended or continued in unimaginable ways. She would learn the truth. “We go together,” she said quietly, voice firm. Linnea touched her arm lightly, silent support.

They doused the fire and gathered their belongings. A single corridor led deeper into the earth, a wide passage framed by pillars crusted with ice. They stepped into that yawning darkness, lantern held high, curses and steel ready on their tongues and blades.

They traveled for a time through a corridor slanting downward. The air grew colder yet, and their breath formed thick clouds that hung longer than before. Occasionally, faint carvings or runic marks caught Celenneth’s eye, half-buried under ice. They suggested a path once well-trodden, maybe in ancient days, by those who guarded or studied this place.

As they rounded a sharp bend, Linnea raised her hand, halting them. Her keen eyes picked out disturbances in a bank of drifted snow that had settled against a collapsed pillar. Tracks again—heavy boot prints, not hound paws. Celenneth’s heart leapt painfully. “Rangers have been here,” she breathed. Damrod and Hallas exchanged looks. If rangers had passed this way, perhaps Orlec and Brandor had descended into these very halls.

They followed the trail, quiet as ghosts themselves. The prints led them through a winding series of chambers, each smaller than the last, until they reached a cramped space where ice cascaded from the ceiling like frozen curtains. There, huddled against a stone block, a figure lay, wounded and breathing raggedly. A faint torch stub flickered nearby, almost spent.

Celenneth recognized him instantly—Orlec’s gaunt face, etched with pain and weariness, older than her memory, but unmistakable. Her throat tightened. She rushed forward, Linnea right behind her. Damrod and Hallas fanned out, cautious, weapons half-raised in case something lurked unseen.

“Orlec,” Celenneth said, voice trembling. She knelt, slipping her glove off to press two fingers to his neck. He stirred, opening dull, half-focused eyes.

“Celenneth?” he rasped, voice cracked and faint. He tried to sit up, wincing. His cloak was torn, his right arm bound in a makeshift sling. Blood darkened the fabric. She offered him water, and he drank greedily, each swallow a gasp.

Linnea checked the shadows—no movement. Damrod and Hallas maintained a perimeter. The corridor beyond was silent but for Orlec’s ragged breathing.

“Orlec,” Celenneth murmured again, relief mixing with fear. “We found you. Brandor, was he—?”

Orlec’s eyes widened in sudden panic. “Brandor… still deeper,” he managed, voice hitching. “We tried to seal it… the source… he stayed behind… fighting…” He closed his eyes, pain twisting his features. “I thought… I’d never see a friendly face again.”

Tears welled in Celenneth’s eyes. She had imagined this moment countless times—finding Orlec and Brandor here or deeper in. She took his hand gently. “I’m here,” she whispered. “And there’s someone I want you to meet.” She beckoned Linnea closer. “This is my partner, my love; Linnea.”

Linnea knelt, taking Orlec’s other hand. His gaze moved to her, puzzled but curious. Celenneth’s voice trembled. “We have a daughter… Brynja. She’s safe, waiting for us at home. You would love her.”

Orlec’s eyes shone, tears forming despite the cold. “Linnea… Brynja,” he echoed softly, savoring the names. A ghost of joy passed over his features. “A daughter… such hope.” He coughed weakly, breath hitching. “You’ve grown so much, Celenneth,” he murmured. “I’m proud of you.”

Celenneth squeezed his hand, heart aching. “We’ve been searching for you and Brandor. Where is he? What happened?” Urgency crept into her voice. She had to know.

Orlec’s expression twisted, shadows passing across his face. Fear flared in his eyes, sudden and stark. He tried to speak: “He… he’s… no… you must—” His voice cut off. His body went rigid. Terror shone in his gaze as if he saw something behind them or in his mind’s last moments.

Then his breath caught, a final sharp intake that rattled in his throat. He stared at Celenneth as if trying to warn her, but no words came. A cold stillness settled over him. His heart stopped.

“Orlec!” Celenneth’s cry tore through the silence, echoing down the icy corridor. She cradled his worn face, tears freezing on her cheeks. Linnea gripped Celenneth’s shoulder, her presence a steady warmth against unimaginable grief.

Before any of them could process this loss, Damrod’s head snapped up, eyes narrowed. Hallas spun, spear raised. Linnea released Celenneth and stood. The air tensed.

Something stirred in the gloom. Dark shapes emerged from a shadowed corridor—twisted forms encased in ice-covered armor. Guardians of this place; their eyes burned with unnatural light, their armor creaking as they moved with halting steps. They bore old swords and shields crusted with frost, and their silence was more terrifying than any roar.

Celenneth, at the front, raised her sword too slowly. The nearest Guardian’s blade caught her in the side. She hissed in pain as steel and ice bit deep, warm blood seeping beneath her cloak. Damrod rushed forward to cover her, but another Guardian smashed into his shield arm, forcing him back and nearly numbing his shoulder. Hallas, bracing his spear defensively, tried to hold the third Guardian at bay. The impact drove him to one knee, spear jarring in his hands.

Linnea hung back, bow in hand, heart pounding. Three foes, all deadly and strange. She nocked an arrow, searching for an opening.

Celenneth gritted her teeth, shaking off the pain. She surged forward, sword raised, determined to strike back. But as her blade struck the first Guardian’s icy breastplate, it merely glanced off, leaving a shallow scratch that the ice seemed to smooth over instantly. She cursed under her breath. The armor almost felt alive, shifting and refreezing around each wound.

Damrod tried to drive his sword into the second Guardian’s flank. He swung wide, but the Guardian twisted awkwardly, its movements stiff yet unpredictable. The blade cut only empty air.

Hallas, grunting with effort, lunged at the third Guardian’s joints. His spear struck true beneath a shoulder plate, chipping away a chunk of ice and exposing what looked like old bone. For a moment, hope flared—then the ice crackled and re-formed, sealing the gap. Hallas’ stomach sank. They could wound these creatures, but the wounds wouldn’t last.

From the rear, Linnea released her arrow toward the second Guardian. Her aim was true and the arrow sank into a crack between the helm and collar. There was a sharp, echoing crack. The Guardian staggered, its inner cold light dimming. This one was wounded, at least. Linnea drew a sharp breath. Maybe not all strikes were futile.

The group realized they had to focus on the wounded Guardian before it recovered. Celenneth hacked again at the second Guardian’s armor, but her blow skittered harmlessly over the icy plating. Damrod tried next, sword flashing downward. This time, the ranger’s blade hit harder, and the Guardian’s chestplate fractured. Pieces of ice chipped away, revealing a hollow cavity within.

Hallas spotted an opening and drove his spear forward, trying another piercing thrust at the weakened Guardian. He aimed at the exposed core—but again, ice surged inward, sealing the gap before the spear could land a killing blow. Frustration gnawed at him. These creatures’ armor responded to every strike.

Linnea steadied herself. She decided to up the stakes. She grabbed a torch from the ground and quickly wound oil-soaked cloth around an arrowhead. A spark, a flame—and now she had a flaming arrow. She loosed it at the same wounded Guardian. The arrow hissed through the air, flames dancing. This time, it struck home. Fire licked through the icy breach Damrod had created, and the Guardian’s core shattered with a final, echoing crunch. The creature collapsed into shards, dark mist dispersing into the cold air.

One down. But Celenneth barely had time to draw breath before the remaining Guardians retaliated. The first Guardian lunged at her with renewed fury, catching her off guard. Its blade slammed into her guard, slicing painfully. She staggered, blood staining the ice at her feet. At the same moment, the third Guardian took advantage of Damrod’s focus on the fallen foe, swinging its blade in a brutal arc that tore into Damrod’s shield arm yet again. Damrod gasped at the jolt of pain.

Celenneth’s endurance flagged. Damrod, too, felt his strength wane. Hallas’ arm shook with fatigue, and Linnea’s heart pounded in her throat. They were all weary now. The fight had turned desperate. Hallas tried to hold a defensive stance, spear raised to protect his companions. Damrod positioned himself more carefully, balancing offense and defense. Linnea stepped further back, preparing to light another arrow, while Celenneth gritted her teeth and stayed on the offensive, refusing to give ground.

Celenneth struck at the first Guardian again, this time with careful precision. Her sword cut deep into its chest, forcing out chunks of ice and bone. She felt some resistance give way. Still, the creature did not fall. Damrod tried to help her but missed his mark. Hallas attempted another thrust, but the Guardian parried or twisted aside each time, leaving only scuffs on the ice plating.

Linnea, now with a lit arrow ready, aimed carefully at the first Guardian. The flaming arrow arced through the darkness and embedded itself in a crack Celenneth had made. Flame spread across the Guardian’s chest, melting and weakening the ice structure. Celenneth saw a chance and pressed harder, hammering away at the softened spot.

But the ice fought back, crackling and refreezing, resisting every blow. For a moment, Celenneth feared they would never bring it down.

Then Damrod lunged low, distracting the Guardian. Hallas used the brief opening to reposition. Linnea fired again, another flaming arrow whistling in the gloom. This time, her arrow found a gap in the Guardian’s shoulder. Fire and steel combined to overwhelm the creature’s self-repairing armor. The icy plating sizzled and split apart, revealing frail bone and old rags beneath. With a final blow, Celenneth drove her blade into the Guardian’s exposed core. Ice and bone fragments showered the floor as it collapsed, defeated at last.

Only one Guardian remained. The third had taken hits from spear and sword, but each wound had sealed quickly, leaving them no lasting advantage. Now, however, it stood alone against four determined foes. Linnea’s flaming arrows had proven effective. She lit another one and aimed for the Guardian’s center mass. The arrow struck, flame licking at the armor.

Hallas took a step forward, teeth gritted. He lunged again, and this time the combined damage—spear thrusts, sword cuts, and flaming arrows—prevented the ice from fully resealing. Damrod swung down hard with his sword, cleaving through a half-frozen arm. The Guardian tottered, struggling to maintain its form.

Linnea fired once more, the arrow’s flame dancing in the cavern’s chill. The shot punched into the Guardian’s fractured torso. With a drawn-out crack, the armor shattered, leaving the unnatural life force to sputter and fade. The final Guardian crumbled, a pile of icy shards at their feet.

The cavern fell silent, save for their ragged breathing. The floor was littered with chunks of ice and scattered arrows, stained with dark ichor and bone fragments. Celenneth clutched her wounded side, pain pulsing with each heartbeat. Damrod rotated his shield arm slowly, wincing at the ache. Hallas leaned on his spear, shoulder heaving. Linnea lowered her bow, exhaustion washing over her.

A hush fell over the corridor, broken only by the rangers’ heavy breathing. Celenneth slumped against a nearby stone, tears returning as she remembered Orlec. Linnea hurried to her side, wrapping an arm around her shoulders. Damrod and Hallas stood nearby, weapons lowered, respecting the moment of grief.

They had found Orlec, only to lose him in the same breath. His final moments, the joy at hearing of Brynja, and the terror that took him—what had he seen that would have him fear these guardians so? Celenneth closed her eyes, letting Linnea’s presence steady her. Damrod and Hallas exchanged grim looks. This place offered them no mercy.

Damrod traced the runes still visible on the shattered guardians’ armor. They were once Númenórean wardens, twisted by dark magic into eternal utensils.

After a time, Celenneth cleared her throat, voice raw. “We must press on,” she said softly. “Orlec mentioned Brandor. He must be ahead.”

Linnea nodded, reluctant but resolute. Damrod hefted his shield, and Hallas retrieved what he could of his spear’s splintered haft. They had no time for a proper burial, no means to carry Orlec’s body back now. Celenneth knelt to close Orlec’s eyes gently, whispering a promise to return if fate allowed.

They pressed deeper through the winding corridors, guided by faint bootprints and subtle signs of recent struggle. Celenneth moved quietly, her footsteps muffled by frost-covered stone, keen eyes searching the gloom. Damrod followed close behind, senses alert. Linnea and Hallas trailed them, forming a protective rear guard, weapons ready but hearts heavy from recent losses.

As they advanced, Damrod’s gaze sharpened, spotting scuffed patches of snow, partially obscured tracks leading onward. Celenneth recognized the faint trail as clearly as if etched into the tunnel, guiding the group through twisting passages with an uncanny precision.

Then, a distant flicker—a suggestion of light ahead, maybe a torch or a dying campfire. Damrod signaled caution. Celenneth slowed her pace, senses straining. She sensed movement, a weak presence lingering near that faint glow. The air tasted of desperation and old pain.

Linnea nocked an arrow, Hallas tightened his grip on his spear. Damrod edged forward silently. As they rounded a final bend, the corridor widened into a smaller chamber cluttered with ice and rubble. The faint light came from a discarded torch sputtering weakly against a stone. Slumped near it, half-buried in drifting frost, lay a wounded ranger clutching something that gleamed faintly in the half-light.

Celenneth’s breath caught in her throat. Damrod’s eyes narrowed; he recognized the worn cloak, the old ranger’s gear. Hallas and Linnea held position, scanning for threats, but none emerged.

“Cover me,” Celenneth said quietly to Damrod, her voice steady despite the sudden rush of emotion.

Before Damrod could reply, Celenneth advanced, boots skidding slightly on ice-slick rock. She reached the fallen figure and dropped to her knees, heart pounding. Even before she confirmed it, she knew: Brandor. One of her guardians. The man’s breathing was shallow, ragged clouds against the cold air. His cracked lips moved soundlessly, eyes half-lidded.

With practiced urgency, Celenneth’s hands found the worst wound beneath his cloak—a deep, frozen gash near his chest. Though faint, his pulse still flickered. She worked swiftly, using the limited supplies she carried, cleaning the wound, applying pressure, and whispering reassurances. Her efforts stabilized his breathing, though his life still hung by a thread.

Damrod knelt beside her, ready to fend off any ambush. Hallas stood guard, spear leveled at the darkness, while Linnea kept an arrow on the string. They formed a protective circle around Celenneth and Brandor, expecting danger at any moment.

Brandor stirred weakly, eyelashes fluttering. He focused on Celenneth’s face with recognition and relief. His cracked voice emerged like a whisper across decades. “Celenneth…” he managed, barely audible.

She offered him water, tilting it carefully to his lips. He swallowed with difficulty. As he steadied, his gaze drifted downward to something clenched in his bloodied grip. Celenneth followed his eyes and saw a fragment of an ancient blade. Its hilt bore Númenórean runes, faintly glimmering as if caught between worlds.

Brandor’s trembling hand lifted the hilt, offering it to her. “Take it…” he whispered, voice rough with pain and meaning. “Its... fate is yours now.”

Celenneth accepted the blade gently, feeling its weight of memory and power. Damrod watched silently, understanding that this relic mattered greatly. Linnea and Hallas remained vigilant, scanning the corridor behind them, muscles tense.

With measured steps, Celenneth and Damrod carefully lifted Brandor, supporting his weight between them. Linnea and Hallas guarded their flanks as they moved him away from the drafty intersection, seeking a more secure spot to tend him further. Brandor’s ragged breathing caught in his throat as Celenneth spoke quietly, pressing a bandage more firmly over his wound.

“We’re taking you out of here, Brandor,” she said, voice steady but layered with concern. “We’ve found Orlec’s body… he’s gone.” Her throat tightened, but she pressed on. “We’ll leave this cursed place after we finish what must be done.”

Brandor’s eyes closed at the mention of Orlec, grief twisting his features. His fingers tightened weakly on Celenneth’s arm. “Orlec… gone,” he repeated, voice splintering like brittle ice. “I feared… as much.”

He drew a trembling breath, eyes reopening, now sharper with urgency. “You… can’t leave… not yet,” he rasped, forcing each word. “There’s something… still here. We swore… to stop it. Promise me… you will.”

Celenneth’s jaw set, tears threatening her eyes. She nodded firmly. “We’ll face it,” she said. “Tell me what awaits us.”

Brandor swallowed, voice low and grave. “The altar… beneath the ruins,” he said. “Something ancient… trapped, but not fully.” He paused, struggling for breath. “We thought… we could seal it. The wards… must be reforged. If… you can’t… destroy the altar.”

He nodded toward the hilt Celenneth held. “That blade… was our key,” he murmured. “Orlec fell… buying me time.” His eyes shimmered. “You must finish it. End what we began.”

Celenneth bowed her head, resolve hardening. “We will,” she said quietly. Linnea stepped closer, placing a comforting hand on Celenneth’s shoulder. Damrod and Hallas exchanged looks, their grim determination matching Celenneth’s resolve.

Brandor’s gaze lingered on Celenneth, a mixture of pride and sorrow etched into his worn face. “Go,” he said softly, voice steady despite the pain. “I can hold on. But don’t tarry.”

Celenneth rose, the hilt of the ancient blade gripped tight. Linnea stood by her side, love and support shining in her eyes. Damrod and Hallas took up their weapons, ready to face whatever lay beyond.

8The Altar

At last, Celenneth, Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas approached the final chamber. The passage narrowed, and a terrible cold seeped through their bones. The hum had grown into a low, resonant moan. When they emerged into a small, circular vault, they saw a pedestal of black stone at its center, cracked and weeping icy water. Runes flickered wildly over walls and ceiling, half-distorted by corruption.

Celenneth advanced slowly, the sword in hand. She recalled the patterns she had studied and the efforts she and Hallas had made above. Now she would do the same, guided by the sword’s runes. Damrod and Linnea stood guard, Hallas beside her, murmuring the curse softly to keep any lurking spirits at bay.

As Celenneth approached the pedestal, a surge of foul magic lashed out—shadows of frost and dark shapes flickered at the edges of the chamber. Hallas gripped his spear, Linnea and Damrod raised weapons, but Celenneth held the sword high, chanting the old words more firmly now, combined with fragments of what she remembered her guardians teaching her long ago.

The runes on the sword’s hilt flared bright. She traced the missing lines on the pedestal’s surface with the blade’s tip, careful and deliberate. Sparks of blue fire danced over the cracked stone, and the darkness shrank back. A keening wail rose from unseen throats.

Linnea gasped as spectral shapes tried to form—more wraiths, perhaps—and she fired an arrow at one, but the shadows danced around it. A soundless pressure built around them, and for a moment Celenneth feared a final monstrous foe would appear. But no beast or wraith emerged. Instead, the hiss of escaping magic thinned, settling into a calm hush.

She pressed the blade’s broken edge against a key rune on the dais, completing the pattern. A soft radiance emanated from the stone, and then dimmed, leaving only silence. The threat was contained.

Linnea lowered her bow, exhaling. Damrod and Hallas let out quiet sighs. Celenneth stepped back, blade still in hand, tears on her lashes. It was done. No grand explosion, no final roar—just peace returning to old bones of the earth.

Before leaving this place, they made a careful sweep of the area. Celenneth spotted a sealed alcove behind a curtain of ice. With Hallas’s help, she chipped away at the frost and discovered a Númenórean reliquary within—ornately carved, still intact after untold centuries. Inside were ceremonial tokens that once might have served as protective charms or offerings. Celenneth lifted them reverently, feeling echoes of a proud past lingering in the silent carvings.

Hallas, venturing further along the edge of the hollow, discovered a hidden chamber, its entrance half-blocked by ice. He cleared it, working steadily with the butt of his spear. Inside, he found an iron-bound chest, untouched by frost. Its hinges creaked softly as he pried it open. Ancient scrolls lay folded in oilskin, their ink barely faded, alongside old coins and a silver-bound book. Hallas ran a gloved finger over the book’s cover, marveling at the intricate script. Lore and knowledge from a distant age rested here, waiting to be carried back into the light.

With quiet efficiency, they divided the burden. Hallas took the reliquary, its silent weight pressing against him like a reminder of the past they had fought to preserve. Linnea balanced the iron-bound chest, her muscles straining but her will unyielding. They returned to Brandor and Orlec, and Damrod moved gently to lift Orlec’s body, cradling his fallen comrade as one might a sleeping friend. Celenneth supported Brandor, who leaned weakly against her but managed a stiff nod of gratitude. Together, they bore relics, memories, and their beloved guardians’ remains outward, steps careful as they retraced their path through frost-rimed corridors.

The journey back up through the Maw’s passages was grim and slow. Celenneth led, her memory guiding them surely. Damrod struggled with Orlec’s weight, shifting the body around.

At last, they emerged from the icy depths. The open sky greeted them, pale and distant, as they found a spot beneath a rocky ledge to make camp one final time before deciding their ultimate route home. They were exhausted, hollowed out by grief and hardship, yet alive and free of the malevolent whisper that had lurked below.

Celenneth gently set down the relics, eyeing the blade that had helped seal the old evil. Damrod and Hallas took up watch positions, ensuring no lurking threat followed them to the surface. Linnea coaxed a small fire into life, offering warmth and comfort. The wind here was still cold, but clean, no longer tainted by old magics.

As the night deepened and the small fire crackled softly, Brandor leaned his back against a stone, exhaustion weighing down his voice. Still, he mustered enough strength to speak.

Brandor cleared his throat, voice quiet but steady. “We never fully understood what lay below, only pieces. Orlec and I... we pushed deeper than we should have, trusting old lore and scattered runes.” He lifted his gaze to Celenneth. “We found carvings that hinted at a sealed presence, something ancient and malignant. We glimpsed frost beasts lurking in corridors of ice, wraiths that drifted without form... we tried to piece it all together, but we had no time.”

Damrod, arms folded, frowned. “So you entered knowing it was dangerous, but not how it all fit?”

Brandor managed a weary smile. “Exactly. We knew it had to be stopped. Orlec believed the wards could be restored if we found the right place and the right tools—old blades, old runes—but we only learned fragments. By the time we realized how much we were missing, it was too late.”

Celenneth listened, gripping the relics at her side. “You and Orlec gave everything to hold the line, didn’t you?”

Brandor’s eyes glimmered with grief. “We tried. I only wish we could have understood more before we ventured so far. Still, knowing you finished what we began... it eases the weight on my heart.”

Hallas tilted his head. “Did the runes offer any clue on what might have happened if it broke free?”

Brandor shuddered. “We suspected unspeakable horrors. Worse than the wraiths, the beasts, or the guardians you’ve seen. A darkness that would spill beyond these hills. That’s why we dared so much with so little.”

Celenneth closed her eyes a moment. “I’m sorry you had to face it half-blind,” she said gently. “We understand more now, thanks to your sacrifice.”

Hallas opened the reliquary, carefully revealing ceremonial tokens. Linnea unlatched the chest, unrolling a single scroll to glimpse cramped Númenórean script and delicate illuminations. Damrod marveled at the silver-bound book, wondering at its forgotten lore. Together, they pieced hints of history, power, and duty, each item a puzzle piece of ages long gone.

Linnea pulled her cloak tighter and smiled softly at Celenneth. “We never expected to become adventurers again,” she said. “After Bree, we built a life—simple, kind. Brynja’s laughter... it echoes in my mind even here. She’s so curious, always asking why the sun rises or where the birds sleep.”

Celenneth nodded, voice warm and fond. “She dances in the meadow behind our home, invents stories about foxes and giants. I miss her voice so much.”

Damrod, listening, allowed a faint grin. “Sounds like a life worth fighting for. Hard to imagine such peace from here.”

Hallas lowered his spear, leaning closer. “And you plan to return to that life once we’re free of these icy lands?”

Celenneth’s eyes shone. “Yes. Brynja deserves to know we faced these shadows and came back.”

Brandor’s voice was subdued but steady. “Celenneth,” he began, “you must know how unexpected it was for Orlec and me to raise a child. We were rangers—warriors, not caretakers. When we found you after your family was lost... we could have turned away, but Orlec said, ‘She needs us.’ That was all it took.”

Linnea reached out to squeeze Celenneth’s hand. Celenneth’s throat tightened. “You gave me a reason to survive beyond the blade and the hunt,” she said softly.

Brandor nodded. “Raising you was never easy. We knew how to track beasts, not soothe a frightened child. But you gave us purpose. You made the endless fight mean something more.” He managed a faint smile. “Now you’ve made a family of your own. Brynja... a spark of the future we never dared hope for.”

The next morning, as pale light seeped into their icy shelter, Celenneth knelt beside Brandor, changing his bandages. He caught her hand and held it gently.

“Celenneth,” he said calmly, his voice almost serene. “I’ve chosen my path. Last night, I took certain herbs—roots that will let me slip away quietly, without pain.”

Celenneth’s eyes widened, tears pricking. “Brandor... no, why—?”

He hushed her gently. “Listen to me. Seeing you alive, with Linnea, knowing Brynja waits for you... it’s more than I dreamed I’d witness. I can’t leave Orlec alone in this cold earth. My work is done, child. I fought my battles, raised you, tried to seal these horrors. You finished what we started.”

He glanced toward where Orlec lay buried. “My soul’s companion awaits. I... would never leave him behind. My life’s purpose ended with this victory and with seeing your happiness. Let me rest here, beside him.”

Celenneth’s tears slipped free, voice quavering. “I understand,” she whispered, grief and love mingling. “If that is your wish... I won’t deny it.”

Brandor smiled softly. “You’ve given me peace, Celenneth. Thank you.” He closed his eyes, breathing slowly, waiting for the quiet end he had arranged.

Celenneth wept openly, embracing Brandor. Linnea stood beside her, offering silent strength. Damrod and Hallas bowed their heads. They understood. There would be no arguing against Brandor’s choice. He was tired, his purpose fulfilled. He belonged at Orlec’s side, in death as in life.

They found a suitable place and dug the grave under the frozen sky, the earth hard but yielding eventually to their blades. They laid Orlec and Brandor side by side, hands clasped, tokens and sigils arranged with tender care. Weapons crossed over them, a final honor guard of steel. Celenneth closed her eyes as the others lowered the soil gently, snowflakes drifting down to mark the solemn rite.

Linnea whispered a prayer, her voice catching on tears. Hallas set down a small token from the reliquary. Damrod bowed stiffly, jaw clenched. Celenneth placed a woodcarving of the old blade’s runes, a memory of the sealed darkness, the foe they had bested. Together, they stepped back, hearts heavy but calmer, knowing that Orlec and Brandor slept peacefully in these wild hills.

After a long silence, Celenneth and Linnea exchanged glances, voices hushed as they stood with Damrod and Hallas near the cairn that sheltered Orlec and Brandor’s remains. Celenneth cleared her throat, voice steady despite tears drying on her cheeks. “We’ll go home,” she said, “to Bree, to Brynja. There’s nothing more we can do here.”

Linnea nodded, relief softening her features. “We’ve lost so much. A gentler path would be a mercy now.”

Damrod stepped forward, resting a hand lightly on his sword hilt. “We’ll guide you by a safe route,” he offered, sincerity plain in his tone. “We can swing east, follow valleys less observed. It’s less direct, but we’ll avoid the harshest passes.”

Hallas inclined his head. “You deserve an easier journey out of this wilderness. We know these lands—enough to steer clear of further dangers. And we would be honoured to accompany you two.”

Celenneth dipped her head gratefully. “Thank you,” she said, voice steady if sad. “Your help means more than I can say.”

With that understanding, they began to gather their gear. Hallas secured the reliquary, Damrod shouldered his pack, Linnea adjusted the chest’s weight, and Celenneth checked the straps that held the old blade. They would rest fully today, letting hearts and bodies mend a little more, and tomorrow they would start the journey home, guided by memory, loyalty, and love.

They settled in for one final night’s rest, huddled close around the fire. Sleep claimed them gradually, one by one, as the wind whispered lullabies through stunted pines. Beneath the stars, far from the horrors below, they were survivors bearing old relics and new scars. Tomorrow, they would shoulder their burdens and head for home, Brynja’s laughter and a warmer hearth waiting in the days to come.
