1The Bandits at Sarn Ford, and the Cursed Diadem.

Not quite spring, in Bree.

Nénimë 6, 2965.

The heavy oak door of the Prancing Pony creaked open, letting in a gust of cold wind and a solitary figure wrapped in a travel-worn cloak. Celenneth stepped into the warmth of the inn, the door swinging shut behind her with a dull thud. She lowered her hood, revealing auburn hair tangled from the road, and took a slow breath, letting the warmth seep into her bones. The firelight flickered on her armor, still marked with the dust of long trails and faint scuffs of past battles.

The inn was bustling as always, filled with the scent of roasted meat and the tang of ale, the hum of laughter and chatter a welcome contrast to the silence of her last few days on the road. She scanned the room, her eyes resting momentarily on a familiar figure—Orlen, seated at a table near the hearth, his head thrown back in laughter at some jest. The sight of him brought a pang, a feeling that she kept buried beneath layers of practicality and caution. Celenneth never lingered, never allowed herself to entertain what-ifs. She was always coming or going, and Bree was just another stop on the journey.

She turned her eyes away before he could notice her, stepping further inside, her hand instinctively resting on the hilt of her sword as she moved through the crowded common room. The innkeeper, Butterbur, caught sight of her and waved, his round face lighting up with recognition.

"Celenneth! Back again, are ye? I swear, you're in and out like the wind itself!" Butterbur's voice boomed over the noise, drawing a few curious glances. Celenneth smiled faintly, nodding as she approached the counter.

"Aye, Barliman, it seems I can't stay away for long," she replied, her voice carrying a warmth she seldom allowed herself on the road. Butterbur filled a tankard, setting it before her with a knowing smile.

"Well, you look like you've come a long way. Sit, rest. The road can wait a while longer, eh?"

Celenneth was already distracted. Her ears caught snippets of conversation from a nearby table—rumors of brigands harassing travelers near the Baranduin. The talk was hushed, but the concern in the voices was unmistakable. She straightened, her focus shifting. As the talk continued, it seemed more sure that these were not mere speculations.

She approached the table, her presence commanding enough to make the men pause in their conversation. "Pardon me," she said, her voice even but firm. "I couldn't help but overhear. These brigands, near the Baranduin—how many, and how often?" The men exchanged glances, and then began to share what they knew—scraps of information about attacks on travelers, sightings of shadowy figures along the riverbanks. Celenneth listened intently, and made up her mind. Orlen would have to wait, not the road.

She set the tankard down with a decisive clink and nodded to Butterbur. "I'll be needing provisions again, Barliman. It seems there's no rest for me tonight."

Butterbur sighed but gave her a nod, already turning to gather what she needed. "Off again, eh? Well, you be careful, Celenneth. The roads are dark these days."

With her provisions in hand, Celenneth left the warmth of the inn behind. The chill of the night bit into her as she made her way through Bree, her steps purposeful. Celenneth set out alone, her feet carrying her swiftly along the darkened road. The sky above was clear, stars glimmering coldly as she moved with the practiced ease of one who had spent years navigating the wild.

Celenneth made excellent time coming down from Bree, first following the Greenway, then cutting across the edge of the Barrow Downs towards Sarn Ford. She moved quickly, her senses sharp and alert, the landscape shifting from rolling hills to the shadowy edges of the Downs, where the air held an eerie stillness. As she traveled, she spotted the crumbling silhouette of a tower across a ravine, its ruins standing stark against the darkening sky.

The wisp of smoke caught her eye, curling lazily above what appeared to be an abandoned highway robber camp. Curiosity piqued, she crept closer, her steps careful and silent. She caught sight of movement—a figure slipping into a nearby cave. A woman, dressed in unusual clothes that bore the look of Tharbad's exiles.

Celenneth narrowed her eyes, her instincts kicking in. This woman might have answers, or at the very least, she could be a scout for the brigands. Celenneth moved swiftly, closing the distance without a sound. She lunged, her arm wrapping around the woman's throat as she attempted to wrestle her into submission, reaching for a length of rope to tie her hands. But the woman was wiry and quick; her hands slipped free, struggling fiercely before Celenneth could secure a knot.

Thinking quickly, Celenneth drew her sword, her heart pounding. The woman twisted, her eyes wide with fear, and in that moment, Celenneth acted. She thrust the blade forward, the steel piercing the woman's chest. Celenneth clamped a hand over her mouth, muffling the cry that escaped as the woman slumped against her.

The forest fell silent once more, the only sound her own ragged breathing. Celenneth lowered the woman gently to the ground, her chest tightening as she stared at the lifeless form. She had done what she thought necessary—yet, in the cold aftermath, she knew this act of self-preservation would leave a mark on her soul. This was not a clean kill in the heat of battle; it was something darker, something that would bear its own shadow in her heart.

She wiped her blade clean, her eyes cold as she scanned the camp. There was no time for regret—not now.

After a brief survey of the camp and retrieving her rope, Celenneth headed towards the cave. She approached cautiously, her eyes scanning for any movement. As she neared the entrance, she encountered a broken old stone bridge spanning the ravine, with a makeshift rope and wooden bridge beside it. The crumbling stone structure looked unreliable, and the makeshift bridge, hastily put together, wasn't much better. She knew time was of the essence.

As she approached the bridge, she spotted a shadow—a figure on the opposite side. The man, with a frantic look in his eyes, held a knife, and with a sharp movement, began cutting the ropes holding the bridge together. Celenneth's heart raced, and without hesitation, she sprinted forward. The bridge swayed under her feet as she ran, her boots pounding the wooden planks. She leapt just as the ropes snapped, the bridge collapsing beneath her. For a breathless moment, she hung in the air before her feet hit solid ground on the other side.

The figure stumbled back, his eyes wide with surprise. He hadn't expected her to make it. Without waiting, he turned and darted into the next chamber, disappearing into the darkness. Celenneth steadied herself, her breath coming fast. She could feel the adrenaline coursing through her veins, her body trembling with the thrill of survival.

Lighting a torch, she moved into the debris-filled chamber. The air was damp, the walls lined with broken stones and shattered wood. Water trickled somewhere nearby, echoing through the cavern. Large doors stood across the room, their surface worn with age, and what looked like a small closet or cupboard door was barely visible amidst the rubble. Celenneth moved carefully, her eyes darting from shadow to shadow, her senses alert for any movement.

Suddenly, the man burst out of a pantry, wielding an axe. He swung at her with a wild cry, the blade slicing through the air. Celenneth barely dodged, the axe missing her by inches. She countered with a thrust of her sword, but the man twisted away, her blade scraping against the stone wall.

He swung again, his movements frantic but strong. Celenneth raised her buckler, the impact of the axe jarring her arm. She gritted her teeth, pushing back against him. With a swift movement, she twisted, bringing her sword up and driving it into his chest. The man's eyes went wide, his breath escaping in a ragged gasp. Celenneth yanked her sword free and kicked him back, his body crumpling against the wall before sliding to the floor.

She stood over him for a moment, her breathing heavy, her muscles tense. The echoes of the fight faded, leaving only the soft trickle of water. There was no time to search the room thoroughly; she needed to move quickly, and the body needed to be dealt with. She sheathed her sword and grabbed the man's collar, hauling him towards the edge of the ravine. With a grunt of effort, she hefted the body over the side, watching as it disappeared into the darkness below.

Celenneth took a deep breath, steadying herself. She turned back towards the chamber, her torch casting long shadows across the rubble-strewn floor.

Knowing she'd need to find another way across the ravine later, Celenneth pressed on, pushing through the larger doors. A sense of dread washed over her as she entered what appeared to be a pillared hall, now flooded. The air felt thick and stuffy, as if weighted.

She noticed inscriptions and carvings on the walls, ancient symbols and worn figures whose meanings eluded her, but whose presence felt heavy with history. Across from the entrance, a stairway beckoned, half obscured by shadows and the reflective surface of the water. She stepped further in, her boots splashing softly in the shallow flood, her eyes scanning the murky water for any sign of danger.

As she moved deeper into the hall, surveying her surroundings, a flicker of movement caught her attention. She spotted a dwarf—a brief glimpse of a small figure in dark clothing—running off into a side passage. Her heart skipped a beat, her instincts screaming a warning. Then, echoing through the chamber, she heard it: the toll of a bell, deep and resonant, reverberating through the pillars and the water.

The sound seemed to wake something, and as Celenneth's gaze snapped back to the water, she saw them—two ghoulish creatures, rising from the depths, their gaunt forms dripping and twisted. Their eyes were hollow, their mouths curled into grotesque snarls as they moved towards her, intent on attack.

Celenneth's grip tightened on her bow. She backed up, nocking an arrow and drawing it back, aiming for the closest of the creatures. She loosed the arrow, but the movement of the water made her misjudge her aim; the arrow sailed past, striking a pillar behind them. The creatures closed the gap quickly, their movements jerky and unnatural.

One lunged at her, its teeth bared, but it bit down on her leather armor, unable to pierce through. She twisted away, deflecting a clawed hand with her buckler, the impact rattling her arm. She swung her sword in retaliation, the blade slicing through the air. The first creature dodged, hissing, while the second one lunged, its bony fingers wrapping around her arm, holding her fast.

Celenneth grunted, struggling against the creature's grip. She dropped her torch, the flame sputtering as it hit the water. The creatures shied back, their eyes flickering with something that resembled fear. With a burst of effort, she wrestled against the one holding her, but its grip was iron-tight, and she failed to break free. The creature leaned in, its teeth gnashing, trying to bite through her helm.

Desperation clawed at her mind, and then she remembered the torch. Forcing herself towards it, she strained against the creature's hold, her fingers brushing the wooden handle. She grasped it, the flame flaring slightly as she lifted it, thrusting it towards the creature's face. It released her with a screech, recoiling from the fire, its companion following suit, both of them retreating hastily into the water, disappearing beneath the murky surface.

Celenneth panted, her heart pounding in her chest. She stood there, torch held high, watching the water until it was still once more. Only then did she lower the torch, her eyes shifting to the center of the chamber. There, amidst the flooded hall, were several sepulchers—ancient stone coffins, some intact, others broken open, their contents long gone or disturbed.

She moved cautiously through the water, fording across the chamber, her eyes on the sepulchers. She attempted to explore the area, searching for anything of note, but found nothing—only the cold, empty echoes of the dead. It seemed whatever secrets this place held, they were not ready to reveal themselves to her.

Celenneth took a deep breath, the sense of dread still clinging to her as she turned her gaze towards the passage ahead. There was no going back now, not with the bridge collapsed behind her. She pressed forward, her torch casting flickering light on the ancient stone walls as she moved deeper into the darkness.

Celenneth took the staircase up, her feet echoing softly on the worn stone steps. As she ascended, the air grew colder, and she emerged onto what seemed like another world—a vast expanse of ruins floating in a sea of dense mist. It was dark, and the sun hung low, a pale disc setting beyond the horizon. The mist clung to the shattered remnants of stone, the silence almost deafening. Nothing rose more than a few yards, most of the structures broken and crumbling, as though a great force had razed it all to the ground.

A tower stood amongst the devastation, its silhouette rising above the shattered remnants, the only structure left somewhat intact. Celenneth made her way towards it, her eyes scanning the ruins warily, her footsteps muffled by the mist. The walls of the tower were covered in graffiti, dark symbols scrawled hastily, marking it with a strange and unsettling presence. She approached cautiously, narrowing her eyes to make out the inscriptions.

The letters were Elvish, though written in a twisted and ancient mode. The words themselves, however, were unmistakable—the Black Speech of Mordor, used here to convey a powerful curse. Celenneth frowned, her heart heavy with unease as she deciphered the script. She could feel the oppressive weight of the words, the malevolence behind them seeping into the air around her. The curse was laid by the Witch-king of Angmar himself, his rage evident in every line. Furious at how strongly the Dúnedain had opposed him, he had cursed these ruins, binding them to his wrath as long as the torment of Lord Arveleg continued.

She did not know much of this Lord Arveleg, but it was clear enough—this place had once been a stronghold of the Dúnedain, now twisted and corrupted by dark power. The knowledge left a cold feeling in her chest, a sense of futility at the evil that had befallen this once-proud city. The weight of the curse lingered, pressing on her spirit, and she felt the Shadow grow within her. Yet, as she turned her gaze away from the curse, she took a deep breath, shaking off the heaviness. She had deciphered enough to understand, and in doing so, managed to lessen the impact on herself—though she still felt its dark tendrils wrap around her soul.

Celenneth moved away from the inscriptions, her eyes scanning the area. The air was still and silent, the ruins a stark reminder of what had been lost. As she walked further, she found an area that felt secure—a hollow beneath a broken archway, sheltered from the chill wind that swept across the ruins. She settled there, taking a short rest, her body weary from the battles and the journey. She closed her eyes, letting her guard down just for a moment, allowing herself to breathe and regain some strength.

After a time, Celenneth stood, her muscles stiff from the cold. She began to search the place, her eyes catching a glimpse of something unusual amidst the rubble—a rusty iron gate, half buried beneath stone and debris. She pulled it open, her curiosity piqued as she stepped through. Beyond lay a patch of greenery, a small oasis in the midst of the desolation—an orchard, overgrown but still bearing signs of life.

It was the remnants of an herbalist's garden, the plants thriving despite the ruin around them. Celenneth knelt, her eyes scanning the herbs, her fingers brushing over the leaves. She recognized a few—a healing herb that could prove useful. She carefully harvested some, tucking them away, and decided to use them on herself. The herbs, however, did little to ease her wounds; the fatigue and strain of the journey ran deeper than the plants could heal. She sighed, shaking her head as she gathered a bit more to take with her. It was not much, but it was something—a small glimmer of hope amidst the darkness.

Celenneth turned back towards the tower, her eyes narrowing as she considered her next move. The ruins lay quiet around her, but she knew better than to let her guard down. There were still secrets to uncover, dangers to face, and she could not afford to rest for long.

Celenneth turned away from the herb garden, her body aching from the strain and her mind heavy with the dark weight of the curse she had read. The ruins around her seemed to groan and whisper, their ancient secrets echoing in the thick mist. She knew that this place had been twisted by evil, a stronghold now shrouded in a dreadful past. And yet, there was something here—some truth or relic worth uncovering, though hidden beneath layers of dark enchantment.

Celenneth climbed the remnants of a stone stairway, each step cautious. She moved closer to the tower, its imposing silhouette still rising defiantly amidst the rubble and ruin. She hoped it might offer a vantage point, something that would give her a better sense of where to go next.

The paint-strewn walls stared back at her, the sinister letters now burned into her memory. She gritted her teeth and pushed open the heavy, splintered wooden door at the base of the tower. It groaned on rusted hinges, a deep sound that reverberated in the stillness of the ruins.

Inside, the air was thick with dust, and the fading light of her torch flickered across worn stone. The room she entered was circular, the walls lined with shelves of ancient books, their spines cracked and covers faded with age. Most of the books were beyond use, their pages crumbling to touch, but one stood out. Bound in blackened leather, it bore no title—only a sigil she vaguely recognized, a symbol of some ancient order long forgotten.

Celenneth took the book, tucking it carefully into her pack. It felt significant, though she could not say why. Something about this place seemed to be nudging her towards the past—towards history that had been twisted, lost, and buried.

Suddenly, a noise echoed from above—a faint shuffle, like someone or something moving in the upper floors. Celenneth tensed, her hand instinctively going to her sword. She took a deep breath, her eyes narrowing as she moved towards the narrow spiral staircase winding upwards along the wall. With each step, her boots made a soft scrape against the stone, the only sound breaking the oppressive silence of the place.

As she left the orchard, a figure emerged from the mist—a wraith-like woman, her flesh almost transparent, her eyes glowing with an eerie light. A hood covered most of her face, but thin strands of white hair were visible, drifting like cobwebs. The wraith spoke in a strange, lilting accent, her voice echoing unnaturally.

"Why have you come? There is nothing here. Only death. Leave this place or join me in my torment."

Celenneth studied the wraith, her mind racing. She pieced together enough fragments of old tales and the markings she had seen to believe this could be Lady Elwin—a tragic figure from the old histories of the Dúnedain.

"Are you the lady of this place? Who do you defend it against?" Celenneth asked, her voice steady, though her heart pounded.

The wraith did not answer. Instead, its face twisted in anger, and it lunged at her, its form shifting like mist. Celenneth backed away, scrambling up the stone steps to gain higher ground. She tried to draw her bow, hoping for a shot, but the wraith floated effortlessly upwards, closing the distance. Desperate, Celenneth drew her sword and slashed at the spirit. The blade passed through the wraith's head, and it let out a bone-chilling scream, its voice like a winter wind cutting through her. A cold dread gripped her, but she gritted her teeth, striking again. This time, the wraith dissipated into the air, its form scattering like fog in the morning sun.

Celenneth shivered, the cold still lingering in her bones. She took a moment to gather herself, drawing on her inner strength and the hope of her Fellowship to shake off the chill of despair. The Miserable feeling lifted, but the weight of the Shadow remained.

Celenneth looked around, her gaze returning to heights of the tower above. She moved up the stairs, her hand brushing against the rough stone walls for balance. At the top of the tower, she found a grim sight. A mummified corpse lay propped upon a rotting wooden throne, its skeletal form tied to the chair with rusted chains. A metal crown, nailed into the skull, adorned its head. The blue gem set into the crown gleamed brilliantly, its light casting eerie shadows across the room.

Celenneth stepped closer, examining the crown. She could see that the nails pierced the skull, fixing it in place. This was no symbol of honor—this was a punishment, a cruel mockery of kingship. She bowed her head in apology for what she was about to do. Dead or not, the figure before her had once been a ruler, perhaps even of the Dúnedain, and it deserved respect.

Carefully, Celenneth began to pry the crown from the corpse's head, her hands steady but her heart pounding. As the crown loosened, she felt a shift in the air, a presence behind her. She turned her head slightly and saw the wraith of Lady Elwin, her form looming but motionless. The wraith watched silently, her hollow eyes fixed on the crown.

With a final effort, Celenneth removed the crown. The mummified body collapsed into dust, the chains falling slack. For a brief moment, the wraith's form changed, her spectral features softening, her tattered robes transforming into the garments of a noblewoman. She looked at Celenneth, a silent gratitude in her eyes, before she too crumbled into nothingness, the mist that had bound her dissipating.

The blue gem's light faded, the glow that had filled the tower vanishing. Though Celenneth did not fully understand the significance of what had occurred, she felt a shift—a release. The curse that had bound this place had been broken, at least in part.

Exhausted, Celenneth decided to make camp in the tower. She barricaded one of the doors and set a simple trap on the stairs, then settled at the foot of the throne. She allowed herself a respite, her body aching from the battles and the journey.

Celenneth rested through the night, though her sleep was fitful, haunted by dreams of shadows and whispers. She woke with an unnerving feeling of being watched, her eyes darting around the room, but there was nothing there. Her trap was undisturbed. She took a deep breath, steadying herself, and prepared to move on.

Descending the tower, Celenneth noticed a change. The supernatural mist that had hung over the ruins was gone. Morning had come, and the sun shone bright, casting long shadows but illuminating the once-dreadful place. The shadows had been cast away, and with them, some of the darkness that had haunted these ruins.

She decided to explore further, her eyes catching sight of something amidst the rubble—a trapdoor, half-hidden beneath debris. She cleared the stones away, revealing an entrance to an underground chamber. The air was musty as she descended, her torchlight revealing a small hoard—treasure left behind, perhaps forgotten by those who had abandoned this place.

Among the treasure, her eyes fell upon a pair of boots. They were made of fine leather, surprisingly well-preserved. The craftsmanship was Númenórean, unmistakable in its quality, and there was a rune etched into the leather—a symbol of flight, though not in the literal sense. These were herald’s boots, blessed for travel and healing. Celenneth smiled faintly, quickly exchanging her worn boots for these. This was an auspicious find—something that would aid her in her journeys.

As she exited the chamber, Celenneth found herself face to face with a dwarf. He spoke quickly, his words in a language she did not understand, his gestures agitated. He seemed upset, perhaps angry at her presence. Celenneth, in an attempt to show goodwill, reached into her pack and withdrew a portion of the treasure she had found. She offered it to him, her gesture one of courtesy.

The dwarf eyed her, then the treasure, before nodding and stepping back. He accepted the offering, his demeanor softening slightly. He turned, gesturing for her to follow, and Celenneth did so, her curiosity piqued. The dwarf moved confidently through the ruins, leading her past a flooded chamber and into a maze of narrow corridors. There was a sense that something lay below—perhaps a hidden society of dwarves, still lingering in the depths.

The dwarf led her to a small side room, slipping through a cleverly concealed crevasse. On the other side was a staircase that ascended to a narrow ledge overlooking the ravine. Celenneth tied her rope securely, lowering herself down the ledge to cross the ravine from another side. The dwarf pointed towards the opposite edge, and Celenneth's eyes narrowed as she spotted movement. The brigands had returned, lingering near the place where the bridge had once stood. They appeared to be waiting for her, perhaps hoping to finish what they had started. But now, she had the advantage of an alternate route and a guide who knew these ruins better than anyone.

Once she reached the bottom, the dwarf untied the rope and tossed it down to her, vanishing into the shadows without a word. Celenneth gathered the rope and quickly assessed her surroundings. The terrain offered her an opportunity to sneak around the brigands, to avoid a confrontation altogether.

She moved with care, each step deliberate, the soft crunch of gravel beneath her feet the only sound. But despite her caution and the favor granted by her new boots, fate did not favor her. Her foot struck loose stones, sending them clattering down the rocky slope. The noise echoed sharply in the stillness, drawing the attention of the brigands above. Celenneth's heart pounded, her instincts screaming at her to move. She could see the figures turning, their shouts carrying across the ravine.

Without hesitation, Celenneth broke into a run, her boots barely touching the ground as she sprinted through the narrow passage. The brigands' shouts grew fainter as she put distance between them, her breath coming in quick bursts. She ran until her legs burned, her body propelled by sheer determination and the will to survive. Finally, when the sounds of pursuit faded into silence, she allowed herself to slow, her gaze scanning the surroundings for any sign of danger.

Having escaped, Celenneth took a moment to steady herself, her eyes turning towards the path that would lead her back. She knew the way well enough now, the shortcuts and the hidden routes that would take her safely to Bree. With her new boots lending her speed and stamina, she embarked on the journey, moving swiftly and with purpose. The road was long, but she felt the favor of fortune at her back, each step bringing her closer to the safety of the town.

Celenneth returned to Bree, her body weary but her spirit unbroken. The familiar sight of the village brought a sense of relief, the warmth of its lights a beacon after the cold, shadowed ruins. She made her way to the Prancing Pony, where she was able to relay a message to the other Dúnedain about the brigands, detailing their numbers and the routes they had been taking. It was important that the others knew what she had found, that they could be prepared.

After delivering the message, she allowed herself time to recuperate. She rested in Bree, letting her wounds heal and her strength return. Each day, she grew stronger, and as she did, she couldn't help but think of Orlen. She sought him out, hoping to find him at the market or perhaps at his usual place by the hearth. But Butterbur informed her that Orlen had traveled west for a market, and would not be back for a few weeks.

Celenneth hid her disappointment behind a faint smile, nodding her thanks. She knew the road would call to her again soon, but for now, she would rest, recover, and wait for the chance to see Orlen again.

2Gandalf’s Request

While in Bree, Celenneth heard rumors of Gandalf the Grey passing through. The news stirred something within her, a sense of urgency that would not allow her to sit idly by. She hurried to catch him on the road, leaving Bree with only her pack and her instincts to guide her. It was near midday when she finally spotted him—a tall figure in grey, riding a wagon drawn by a stout pony, headed towards the Shire.

Celenneth approached the wagon cautiously, her footsteps crunching softly on the road. Gandalf turned his head slightly, his sharp eyes noticing her long before she called out. He slowed the wagon, and she fell in step beside it, walking alongside as he resumed his journey.

"Celenneth," Gandalf greeted, his voice tinged with curiosity. "It is not often I am pursued by a ranger, especially one with such urgency."

She smiled faintly, keeping pace with the wagon. "I heard you were passing through and hoped to speak with you, Gandalf. There are things I have discovered—secrets buried in the ruins to the south, and... well, I have found these." She gestured towards her feet, the fine Númenórean boots that had carried her swiftly across the treacherous landscape.

Gandalf glanced down, his eyes narrowing thoughtfully as he examined the boots. "Ah, indeed," he said, nodding. "A remarkable find, Celenneth. They bear the mark of Númenor—crafted for travel and resilience. They will serve you well, I think."

As they walked, Celenneth recounted her recent journey—the brigands, the ruined city, the haunted tower, and the curse she had uncovered. She spoke of the wraith, of the heavy weight of Shadow that seemed to cling to her still. Gandalf listened in his characteristic silence, his gaze fixed on the road ahead, only occasionally nodding or making a low sound of acknowledgment.

When Celenneth finished, Gandalf remained quiet for a long moment, his brow furrowed in thought. Eventually, he spoke, his voice calm but with an edge of gravity. "You have grown much in the year since we last met. I believe you were but a child then, albeit one with responsibilities upon your shoulders. Now, you've matured in years, wisdom, and ability."

He paused, his eyes meeting hers. "I have a discreet task that may suit you well—a message that needs relaying. Balin the dwarf attempted to make contact with the dwarves of Moria, and I've not heard back from him or these potential allies. Should they be well, I ask that you relay this message to them. If they've met some untoward fate, I ask that you return and inform me."

Celenneth's breath caught at the mention of Moria, a place wrapped in both legend and darkness. Gandalf continued, his tone grave. "Moria is not for the faint of heart—it is perilous. Exercise discretion; do not charge in headlong. If I haven't heard from you in four months' time, I will seek you out."

She was awestruck that Gandalf would entrust her with such a mission, her heart swelling with both pride and apprehension. She nodded, her voice steady as she replied, "I will deliver the message to Balin or to the dwarves of Moria, whatever state they may be in."

Gandalf smiled, though there was a hint of sadness in his eyes. He reached into his cloak and produced a small, bound parchment, sealed with wax and marked with symbols that shimmered faintly with a magic she could not understand. He handed it to her carefully. "Take this. It bears words for Balin. Guard it well, for it must not fall into the wrong hands."

Celenneth accepted the message, tucking it safely into her pack. They continued on together for a while longer, their conversation turning to lighter topics—the Shire and its hobbits, the simple joys Gandalf spoke of with a glimmer of fondness. Eventually, their paths diverged, Gandalf continuing towards the Shire, and Celenneth turning back towards Bree.

She returned to the village with renewed purpose. Over the next few days, she prepared for the journey ahead. She sharpened her sword, checked her supplies, and gathered what provisions she would need for the long road south and then east, towards the Misty Mountains. The thought of Moria filled her with a mixture of dread and determination, but she knew this was her path.

3The Journey to Moria

The Númenórean herald’s boots had already proven their worth. With each stride, Celenneth moved swiftly and with a lightness she had never known before. She headed east from Bree, her path taking her to Weathertop, its lonely silhouette rising against the horizon. The wind swept over the ancient hill, carrying with it whispers of the past, and Celenneth paused for a moment at its summit, feeling the weight of history around her.

From Weathertop, she cut southeast towards the South Downs. The land here was untamed, filled with rolling hills and hidden valleys, and it was during this leg of her journey that she came upon a remarkable sight—a battle of stags. Two great beasts, their antlers locked, clashed in a majestic struggle for dominance. Celenneth crouched low, watching in awe as they battled, their powerful forms moving with a grace and ferocity that reminded her of the wild beauty of the world.

Once the battle was over, and the victor had claimed his territory, Celenneth moved on, her path taking her further southeast until she neared the banks of the Greyflood River. It was here that she stumbled upon something unexpected—ruins that seemed Elvish in origin, hidden amidst a copse of trees. The stonework was masterful, the carvings delicate and intricate, and though the place was too small to be a village, it was far too grand to be a mere roadside shrine. Celenneth wandered through the ruins, her fingers brushing against the cool stone as she wondered about its purpose. Perhaps it was erected in memory of some long-forgotten event? She made a note of the location, thinking that one day she might return to learn more.

Crossing the Greyflood was no easy task, but Celenneth managed, her progress slowed by the strong current. Once across, she found a secluded path that seemed to offer a shortcut, and though it did, it also revealed signs of recent use—tracks that sent a chill down her spine. Wargs. The large paw prints were unmistakable, and as she examined them more closely, she realized they were headed in the same direction she was—towards Moria.

Three days she followed their trail, her unease growing with each passing hour. Finally, she found them—four wargs resting in a valley that blocked her path. She kept her distance, her eyes narrowing as she studied the creatures. They were fierce and dangerous, and she knew better than to engage them directly.

Her mind turned to the lore she had learned over the years. She knew better than to drink the waters of Moria, for it was said that orcs had poisoned them long ago. With that knowledge in mind, she filled several bags with water from a nearby stream, ensuring she would not be left without clean water when she ventured into the dark depths of the dwarf kingdom.

The valley where the wargs rested was bordered by stone buildings—ancient structures that Celenneth recognized as the mansions of Thráin I. Rumors spoke of treasures hidden here, buried by Thráin before he journeyed east. The buildings, though crumbling, still held an air of majesty, and Celenneth couldn’t help but wonder what secrets they held.

She waited until the wind shifted, carrying her scent away from the wargs, and then she moved, her steps careful and deliberate as she tried to sneak past the resting beasts. The tension was palpable, her heart pounding in her chest as she edged along the rocky slope. But fortune favored her, and she managed to slip by unnoticed, her form blending into the shadows as she moved towards the camp of the Stair Falls.

To her astonishment, she found not an empty camp, but a small group of dwarves—four of them, their beards long and their eyes weary. They were part of Balin’s company, sent out to relay messages to their kin, but now stranded, unable to move on due to the presence of the wargs. They eyed her with suspicion as she approached, their hands resting on the hilts of their weapons.

“I come with a message for Balin,” Celenneth said, her voice calm, though her heart raced. “Sent by Gandalf the Grey.”

The dwarves exchanged glances, their expressions a mixture of surprise and wariness. One of them, a stout dwarf with a thick, braided beard, spoke up. “Gandalf, you say? It is unlikely that Balin will want to hear what Gandalf has to say.”

Celenneth frowned, sensing the tension in their words. “I can help you,” she offered. “I can lure the wargs away, give you the chance to move on. In return, I ask for safe passage into Moria.”

The dwarves shook their heads, the leader speaking again. “We will not risk Balin’s wrath. He is determined, and we cannot afford to act without his blessing. We will wait, as long as it takes.”

Seeing that they would not be swayed, Celenneth nodded, respecting their decision. She continued up the Stair Falls alone, the path steep and treacherous, her eyes scanning for any sign of danger. The grove of holly trees was dense, their dark leaves whispering in the wind as she made her way across the Sirannon. The river was swollen, but she managed to ford it, her body aching from the effort as she finally stood before the sheer mountainside that hid the gates of Moria.

For five long days and nights, Celenneth searched for the door. Each day, she grew more frustrated, her fingers tracing over the rock, her eyes scanning for any sign of an opening. On the second day, she returned to the dwarves, hoping to persuade them once more, but they were gone, vanished into the wilds. It wasn’t until the fifth day, as the sun dipped below the horizon, that she finally found it—a faint outline, a doorway hidden beneath the moonlight.

The inscription was in Ithildin, shimmering faintly under the pale light of the crescent moon. It read: “Sleeps until it is touched by one who speaks words long forgotten in Middle-earth.” Celenneth stared at the inscription, her mind racing as she tried to decipher its meaning. She deliberated, wracking her brain for the right words, until finally, she stepped forward, her hand brushing against the crescent moon.

“I come in peace and bring tidings to the dwarves,” she said, her voice echoing in the stillness.

The silence that followed was heavy, and then, with a low rumble, the great doors of Moria began to open inward, the ancient stone shifting to reveal the darkness beyond. Celenneth took a deep breath, steeling herself as she stepped forward, her torch held high. The air was cold, the scent of damp stone and decay filling her senses as she ventured into the depths of Moria.

4The Journey across Moria

The air inside Moria was cold, a darkness so thick that even the flickering light of Celenneth’s torch seemed to struggle against it. She took a moment to let her eyes adjust, her heart pounding as she looked down the long, dim corridor that stretched before her. The scale of the dwarven halls was overwhelming, the arches rising high above her head, carved with intricate patterns that spoke of the skill and artistry of their makers.

With each step, the echo of her boots against the stone seemed to grow louder, reminding her of just how alone she was. But there was beauty here, even in the silence and the gloom. The stonework was masterful, columns carved with great care, statues depicting the proud history of the dwarves. Celenneth could almost hear the hum of their forges, the laughter of their halls, though now only silence remained.

She moved cautiously, her eyes scanning the shadows for any sign of danger, her senses alert. The halls were vast, and as she walked, she found herself awed by their grandeur. Yet, despite the awe, she remained focused—her task was to find Balin, and she could not afford distractions. There were many things she might have explored, but her path was singular, her purpose driving her forward.

The days blurred together, her journey through Moria taking her deeper and deeper into the ancient kingdom. She passed through grand halls and winding passages, crossing narrow stone bridges that spanned chasms too deep for her torchlight to penetrate. She encountered gates adorned with three orc skulls, a grim warning of what had befallen those who had dared to challenge the dwarves. She navigated tricky staircases and hurried through flooded halls where water reflected the shadows on the walls, making the vast space seem alive with movement.

She knew she was in the upper mines when she found evidence of an orc camp in one of the great halls—fire pits long extinguished, the bones of small animals scattered across the floor. She pressed on, her path leading her out of the mines and onto a straight road, a great corridor that seemed to stretch endlessly. She made camp at one of the way stations, a small alcove where travelers of old had rested, the walls marked with runes of protection and hope.

It was on her seventh day within the depths of Moria that she finally found something worth noting. She had just passed through a broken gate when she entered a massive chamber—the King’s Hall. Her breath caught at the sight, the vast space filled with towering pillars, their surfaces carved with scenes of dwarven history. She tried to commit every detail to memory, the grandeur of the architecture, the way the light of her torch seemed to dance across the stone, illuminating the past.

But there was no time to linger. She moved east along the King’s Road, climbing the molten stairs that led to the Second Hall. This hall was astounding in its own right, massive pillars marking it as a crossroads. As she made her way through, the sound of movement caught her ear—a scuffle, the quiet clash of metal against metal. Celenneth’s instincts kicked in, and she doused her torch, slipping into the shadows as she approached the source of the noise.

What she found was a group of dwarves, their forms barely visible in the dim light as they attacked a small detachment of goblins. The goblins, surprised by the sudden rush, were quickly overwhelmed, their cries silenced before they could raise an alarm. Celenneth waited until the fighting had ceased, then raised her torch once more, stepping forward cautiously.

“I come as a friend,” she called out, her voice echoing through the hall. “I seek Balin.”

The dwarves turned, their eyes widening at the sight of her. They exchanged astonished glances, and one of them—a broad-shouldered dwarf with a braided beard—stepped forward, his eyes narrowing. “Who are you to seek Balin?” he demanded, his voice gruff but not unkind.

“I am Celenneth, of the Dúnedain,” she replied, lowering her torch slightly. “I bring a message from Gandalf the Grey, meant for Balin himself.”

The dwarves looked at one another, their expressions a mix of surprise and uncertainty. Finally, the leader nodded. “Very well, Celenneth. We will take you to him. But know this—Balin does not take kindly to humans in dwarvish spaces. You may find him less than welcoming.”

Celenneth nodded, understanding the warning. “I only wish to deliver the message,” she said. “Nothing more.”

The dwarves seemed satisfied with her response, and they motioned for her to follow. They led her further along the path she had taken, their movements sure and confident even in the darkness of Moria. Together, they made their way through the halls, past the grand columns and the broken gates, until they reached the First Hall. It was there, amidst the remnants of an old dwarven camp, that she saw him—Balin, son of Fundin, his eyes sharp as he looked up at their approach.

5Meeting Balin

One of the dwarves approached Balin, his voice gruff as he announced, "The woman seeks Balin."

Balin looked up, his gaze locking onto Celenneth. His eyes narrowed as he studied her, a strange look passing over his features. "You have found him," he said simply, his voice carrying a weight of authority that left no room for doubt.

Celenneth stepped forward, reaching into her pack. Her hand brushed against the smooth, sealed parchment that Gandalf had given her, and she pulled it out, offering it to Balin. He took it, his thick fingers breaking the seal as he unrolled it, his eyes scanning the contents.

"What is this?" he asked, though even as he spoke, he was already reading, his brow furrowing. Whatever was written there seemed to frustrate him, his demeanor shifting as he rolled the parchment back up and stuffed it into his vest.

He looked back at Celenneth, his gaze sharp. "Well?" he prompted, his tone carrying an edge of impatience.

Celenneth hesitated, her task completed, yet something within her felt unfinished. She glanced around the room, her eyes taking in the dwarves, the worn stone of the hall, the remnants of a once-great kingdom now shrouded in shadow.

"Well, what?" Balin pressed, his irritation growing. "Is that all? Do you have any other needs? Are you going to be on your way?"

Celenneth looked around, her thoughts drifting to the wonders she had seen on her journey through Moria—the vast halls, the grand pillars, the eerie beauty of the dwarven craftsmanship. She had come all this way, and to leave now seemed almost a waste.

"I am at your service, should you need me," she said finally, her voice steady.

Balin regarded her for a long moment, his expression unreadable. Then he asked, "By what means did you enter Moria?"

"By the West Gate, sir," Celenneth replied, meeting his gaze.

Balin's eyebrows shot up, a flicker of surprise crossing his face. "And you made it all the way across to here, seeking me by yourself?"

"Indeed, sir," she confirmed.

Murmurs rippled through the gathered dwarves, their eyes widening as they exchanged glances. Balin stood, his expression softening slightly as he clapped Celenneth on the shoulder. "Well, consider yourself to have earned a place in our company here. We will be sending a group for exploration shortly. In fact, it was those very dwarves you met on your way here. We'll make a few adjustments and have you join them. How does that sound?"

Celenneth's heart leapt at the opportunity to explore Moria alongside the dwarves, but she masked her excitement. She nodded, her expression serious. "As you say, sir."

Balin nodded, seemingly satisfied. "All right then. The task ahead is no easy one," he said, his tone becoming more solemn. "We are on a mission to retrieve some captives. We need to find a way down to them. We've heard their cries from a crevasse, but we cannot reach them yet."

He gestured towards a group of dwarves standing nearby, each of them distinct in their own way.

Dagrin stood slightly apart from the others, her face partially obscured by a cloud of smoke from the pipe she held clenched between her teeth. Her eyes were sharp, and there was an edge to her presence—a certain shadow-touched quality that spoke of someone who had seen too much of the darkness within Moria.

Beside her was Ida, her crossbow slung across her back. She had a focused, steely look in her eyes, her fingers constantly twitching near the weapon's stock as if she was always ready to take aim. She was known among the dwarves as a deadeye, her accuracy unmatched.

Hanar stood with his arms crossed, his expression stern. His beard was braided tightly, and scars lined his forearms—a testament to his many battles. He was known as a goblin-slayer, his reputation forged in the depths of Moria where he had faced countless foes. Next to him was his brother, Ginar, who stood slightly apart, his eyes scanning the shadows. Ginar was a prowler, preferring solitude to the company of others, but his skills in stealth and scouting made him invaluable to the group.

Nar, a wiry dwarf with keen eyes, was adept at finding the safest paths through the dark and treacherous halls. His movements were quick, his gaze constantly shifting as if mapping out every route, every possible danger. He was the group's scout, trusted to lead them through the labyrinthine passages of Moria.

Finally, there was Regan, who stood with a sketchbook in hand, his eyes filled with curiosity. He was the artist and historian of the group, his role not only to fight but to record the wonders they encountered. His hands were stained with ink, and he seemed almost out of place among the hardened warriors, yet there was a quiet resolve in his gaze.

Balin's eyes met Celenneth's once more, a hint of a smile tugging at his lips. "And now, with you, the seven of you will retrieve those of us held prisoner below."

Celenneth nodded, her eyes scanning the group, each of them nodding back in acknowledgment. This was to be her new company, her allies in the deep and dangerous halls of Moria.

6Finding the Ledge of Woe

The echo of footsteps resonated through the ancient halls of Moria as Celenneth and her new company made their way deeper into the heart of the dwarven kingdom. The goal was clear, though daunting: to find the Ledge of Woe, the place where they believed the prisoners were held. They could hear the faint cries echoing up from far below the chasm, but the way down remained elusive, the darkness of Moria offering no clear path.

The first day of their journey took them through a maze of passages, the dwarves moving with a confidence born of familiarity with stone. It was Dagrin, her pipe smoke trailing behind her, who first spotted the forge—a modest one, not one of the mithril forges that Moria was famed for, but a proper dwarven forge nonetheless. Regan, with his keen eye for mechanisms, was able to get it working. The warmth of the forge fire was a welcome respite, its crackling flames a reminder of the dwarven spirit that refused to be extinguished.

As they rested, Celenneth took the opportunity to learn more about her surroundings from her dwarven allies. She learned that the halls they had passed through earlier were known as the Halls of the Roadwarden, and that she had already seen the grandeur of the Second Hall as well as the Doors of Durin. She took care to memorize each new piece of knowledge, annotating her mental map of the complex chambers and tunnels.

On the second day, they found themselves in a hall of records—a vast chamber carved entirely into the rock of the stone. But it was clear that the hall had suffered greatly; the walls were cracked, stone scattered across the floor, as if some immense force had shaken the very foundation of Moria. Regan was visibly distraught, his fingers tracing the broken edges of stone as he mourned the loss of so much dwarven history. Before they left, they marked the wall near the entrance, a small act to commemorate their passage.

By the third day, the shadows had grown darker, and an oppressive sense of dread settled over the company. The deeper they went, the heavier the air seemed to become, the weight of the mountain pressing down upon them. The path narrowed, a sheer wall of rock rising on one side while the other dropped away into a seemingly endless chasm. As they made their way across, a sudden gust of air whooshed up from the depths below, snuffing out their torches. They were plunged into darkness, their breaths held as they painstakingly made their way across, step by careful step. The dread clung to them, each of them feeling the touch of shadow that lingered long after they reached the other side.

On the fourth day, they kept up a good pace, but the journey was grueling. The passages twisted and turned, often leading them in circles, and they found themselves doubling back more than once. Fatigue began to set in, their bodies aching from the constant strain. It was late in the day when they finally emerged into a new chamber—an austere, solemn place that seemed to shine with an otherworldly light. The stonework was polished to a sheen, almost like glass, and the grandeur of the space suggested it had once been a forum, a place where dwarves had gathered for pronouncements or judgments. But now, it stood empty, the echoes of the past the only company they found there.

The fifth day brought them to a series of flooded passages. The water was rank, a foul smell rising from the stagnant pools that filled the halls. Some of the passages were completely flooded, while others reached only to their hips, but the going was slow and the water was freezing. They trudged through, the chill seeping into their bones. In one of the chambers, they found what remained of an old wine cellar—barrels broken, their contents long gone, the wood charred as if the place had been raided and burned.

Fatigue was wearing on them, their bodies heavy with exhaustion and their minds clouded with the effects of the Shadow. But despite the hardships, they pressed on. Somewhere ahead lay the Ledge of Woe, and the prisoners who needed them. And Celenneth knew, deep down, that they could not turn back—not now, not when they had come so far.

7The Ledge of Woe

Finally, on the sixth day, they found the passage leading to the Ledge of Woe. The cries of the prisoners grew louder, echoing off the stone walls, a haunting reminder of those they had come to rescue. Orcs moved in and out of the passage with unsettling frequency, their guttural voices and heavy steps filling the air. But fortune favored Celenneth and her allies, as the filth and grime from the flooded passages still clung to them, masking their presence and allowing them to move undetected.

The first task was clear: they needed to make it past the orc guard post. The tension in the air was palpable as the group crouched in the shadows, watching the orcs from a distance. The guards appeared preoccupied, their focus on the ledge where a group of prisoners—some dwarves and even a few orcs—had been herded. The situation was chaotic, the captors trying to incite violence among the prisoners, shoving them and jeering, forcing them to fight each other for the privilege of staying on the ledge.

Celenneth counted more than a dozen enemies: an orc chieftain, a great orc bodyguard (later discovered to be known as Grimnar the Gaoler), four orc guards, three goblin archers, and four orc soldiers, one of whom bore an insignia she later learned marked them as belonging to Udûn—a secretive and cruel order of orcs in Moria.

Their first aim was to breach the guard post and prevent any call for reinforcements. With a silent nod from Balin, Celenneth took her position, her heart pounding in her ears. She led the charge, her feet light as she moved swiftly towards the guardhouse, her sword ready. Before the last of the prisoners entered the guardhouse, she and her dwarven allies attacked, their weapons flashing in the dim torchlight.

The element of surprise was on their side, and they fought fiercely, the close quarters of the guardhouse turning the battle into a brutal melee. Celenneth found herself face-to-face with the orc chieftain, her blade striking out and drawing first blood. The chieftain roared in anger, his own weapon lashing out, and Celenneth felt the sharp pain as the blade cut into her shoulder, near her breast. She stumbled back, her vision blurring for a moment as she fought to keep her footing.

Hanar the Goblin-Slayer threw himself into the fray, his axe swinging in wide arcs, but the chaos of the cramped space caused their ranks to splinter. The dwarves became separated, each fighting their own battle amidst the confusion. Celenneth tried to call out, her voice drowned by the clash of steel and the guttural cries of the orcs. Hanar, recognizing the danger, fought his way towards the others, his blows clearing a path for the dwarves to regroup.

Celenneth, cut off from her allies, nocked an arrow and took aim. She fired, the arrow finding its mark in Grimnar the Gaoler's shoulder, staggering the massive orc. With their footing regained, the dwarves pressed their advantage, cutting their way through the orcs. On the ledge, the dwarven captives rallied, their spirits lifted by the sight of their kin. Three of the orcs were wrestled off the ledge, their screams echoing as they fell into the abyss below.

Celenneth saw her chance, her next arrow aimed with precision. It flew true, piercing Grimnar's eye, and the great orc fell with a heavy thud. The remaining goblins and orcs, seeing their leader slain, broke ranks, attempting to flee. Celenneth called out, her voice carrying over the din, "Do not let them escape!" The dwarves moved swiftly, ensuring that no orc survived to call for reinforcements.

With the battle won, they pulled the six dwarven captives back through the gate, leaving two orcs behind, bound and silenced. They couldn't stay—Moria was still filled with dangers, and they had no intention of being caught by a larger force. They quickly sacked the guardhouse, finding a few small treasures and a dwarven dagger, wickedly sharp, hidden away in Grimnar's quarters.

The band gathered what food and resources they could, the rescued captives grateful but weakened by their ordeal. They distributed the load, each dwarf taking on part of the burden to ensure they could move as swiftly as possible. They found dwarf wine and roasted meat, the only sustenance they trusted in the dark halls of Moria.

The return journey began, their path now aimed back towards the First Hall, with hopes of cutting a third off the distance by taking a more direct route. Their initial progress was promising—Celenneth's keen eyes found a plunging watercourse, a rapid descent that allowed them to use a makeshift orcish craft to navigate the waters, cutting off an entire day and several miles from their journey.

But the way was not without its challenges. Endless branching stairs and passages twisted before them, and fatigue set in as they climbed. The heights were dizzying, the narrow ledges perilous, but they managed to traverse them. The prisoners, however resilient, were showing signs of exhaustion, and eventually they were forced to camp.

Eventually they pushed on, finding an unexpected path through a network of giant pipes. The hollow echoes allowed them to eavesdrop on orc movements, avoiding several warbands. Fatigue, however, was unrelenting, and the group grew weary. They hit a dead end, forcing them to backtrack, but even with the added distance, they knew they were getting near.

The final stretch was grueling. The stairs seemed endless, each step a battle against their own exhaustion. Nearly an entire day was spent covering the last four miles, but finally, they reached the First Hall. The sight of the great stone pillars was a welcome relief, and they were greeted with cheers and rejoicing as they entered Balin's camp.

Celenneth's successful mission had earned her the respect of Balin and his company. She was given food, healing herbs, and time to recover from her wounds. Hanar was gifted the wickedly sharp dagger they had found—a token of gratitude for his bravery.

But their rest was cut short. Not long after they had settled in, an orc attack came crashing against their defenses, the harsh cries of their enemies echoing through the halls. Celenneth, though weary, rose to meet the challenge, her resolve hardened by all she had endured.

She improved her skills, her keen eyes scanning the darkness with newfound clarity. Her strength of will had grown, and her sword, now sharpened and honed, was ready to deliver piercing blows. The shadow of Moria still loomed, but Celenneth knew that she, along with her companions, would not falter.

Half a day was spent doubling back, searching for a new route. The dwarves muttered in irritation, their patience wearing thin, but they pushed on, knowing the mission was too important to abandon.

By the fifth day, exhaustion weighed heavily on them. The last few miles seemed endless, the path climbing sharply, their muscles straining with every step. The next few days were spent building out the camp, the dwarves working tirelessly to create a secure and defensible outpost. They reclaimed hope in the form of fresh water, and though the work was exhausting, there was a sense of accomplishment in what they had achieved.

Despite the challenges, they had succeeded. The new base camp was established, a haven within the depths of Moria. They had reclaimed a safe place for Balin's company, a step forward in their quest to reclaim the ancient dwarven kingdom. Celenneth knew that there were more challenges ahead, but for now, they had achieved something worth celebrating.

The second day began well, the group making phenomenal time through the winding passages. They found a collapsed tunnel, and rather than turning back, they decided to navigate through the rubble, again managing to cut time from their journey. Spirits were high as they crossed an old stone bridge, their progress uninterrupted—until they found themselves caught between two approaching parties of orcs, converging on them from opposite directions.

Hanar’s eyes narrowed, and without hesitation, he moved to cut a path forward. Ida harried the orcs with her crossbow, her bolts finding their marks with deadly precision, while Celenneth stood her ground, holding the orcs at bay. They focused on escape, pushing hard in the direction they had been traveling, rushing to get off the bridge before the orcs could block both ends. It was a tense, frantic scramble, but they managed to get all the dwarves across, hurrying up a hill and into the relative safety of another tunnel. The group worked quickly to knock loose rocks from the hill, creating a small avalanche that blocked the path behind them, cutting them off from their pursuers. It was a hard-won victory, but they succeeded, though the shadow of the encounter left its mark.

On the third day, the journey continued, though the strain of the previous days was beginning to show. Nar, one of their scouts, was visibly fatigued, his steps slower than usual. They managed to travel another six miles, but the progress was slower, the weight of their task heavy on their shoulders.

By the fourth day, they had covered another ten miles, their determination unwavering. It was late in the day when they stumbled upon a small dwarven domicile—a long-abandoned home carved into the rock. It had been ransacked, the shelves bare, the furniture overturned, but there was a strange sense of homeliness to it. A single bed, a sink, a small workspace—reminders of what life could be like, even in the depths of Moria. The sight gave the dwarves a renewed sense of purpose, a glimpse of what they were fighting for.

On the fifth day, they pressed on, their path taking them to a place known as the Cave of Lights. The walls glimmered with a strange luminescence, and far above, Celenneth spotted an old dwarven lantern, its surface dulled with age. She could tell it was no ordinary lantern—it seemed as though it could be magically relit, perhaps repaired. They made note of it, deciding to return if time allowed.

The wide stairs leading up to the Fortress of Malech were guarded, orc sentries stationed at the base. But with careful reconnaissance, they found a second staircase, one that was unguarded and winding its way up the opposite side. Moving quietly, they ascended the hidden path, their eyes scanning the darkness for any sign of danger.

At the top, they found themselves presented with a massive orc, larger than any they had seen before. He was surrounded by gleaming, polished weapons—swords and axes of dwarves, elves, men, and orcs alike; Uftak.

10Uftak’s Lair

Uftak was no ordinary orc. His hulking frame suggested trollish ancestry, his bulk and height dwarfing even the largest of his kind. He was Malech’s enforcer, a presence meant to terrify, and it was clear why the orcs respected him—or perhaps feared him. This was the creature that Celenneth and the dwarves now faced at the top of the stairs leading to the heart of the Fortress of Malech.

They struck first, taking advantage of the element of surprise. Celenneth led the charge, her bowstring drawn back, the arrow released with deadly intent. It missed, clattering harmlessly off the stone wall behind Uftak. Without hesitating, she drew her sword and rushed forward, determined to close the distance.

Uftak roared in rage as he stood, grabbing his massive axe, swinging it at Celenneth with a force that could have cleaved her in two had it struck. She ducked, the wind of the blade passing just above her head. She used the momentum of her movement to strike upward, her sword finding purchase between his ribs. Uftak bellowed, the sound echoing in the narrow corridor as he staggered back, wrenching himself free of her blade.

He swung wildly, but Celenneth dodged, the clash of steel on stone reverberating around them. From the edges of her vision, she saw movement—orc guards, drawn by Uftak's cries, coming to investigate. The dwarves, their axes and crossbows ready, moved to intercept. A fierce battle erupted, each of them fighting to keep the orcs from overwhelming them.

The dwarven archers, repositioning themselves, began to harry Uftak, arrows flying at him as Celenneth kept pressing forward. She aimed for his vitals, striking again and again, but Uftak was relentless. Just when she thought she had him, her blade poised for a killing blow, Uftak let out a guttural laugh and threw his axe at her. She barely managed to duck, the weapon spinning past her head, smashing into the wall behind her.

Before she could recover, Uftak had grabbed a spear from the wall, jabbing it forward. The spear pierced her side, the pain exploding through her, a gasp escaping her lips. She stumbled back, blood seeping from the wound. The pain threatened to overwhelm her, but she gritted her teeth, adrenaline coursing through her veins as she pushed forward. She had to end this.

Uftak fought like a cornered beast, his blows wild and powerful. Celenneth kept moving, dodging and striking where she could, but the fight was taking its toll. Just as it seemed she might have the upper hand, Uftak lunged, catching her below the collarbone with the spear. The force of the blow drove her to the ground, her vision darkening as she felt herself slipping, the cold stone beneath her a harsh reminder of her failure.

Hanar and Lif rushed forward, their war cries filling the air as they attacked Uftak from both sides. Ida, her eyes focused, loosed arrow after arrow, each one finding its mark. Lif's blade struck deep, but Uftak, in a final act of defiance, drove his spear into Lif's thigh, the dwarf crying out as he fell.

Regin and Tora, seeing their comrades in peril, grabbed spears from the wall and rushed in, stabbing Uftak through, their combined strength finally bringing the massive orc down. Uftak let out a final, shuddering breath before collapsing, his lifeless body hitting the ground with a heavy thud.

The battle was won, but the cost was high. Celenneth lay bleeding, her breaths shallow. The war band gathered around her, their faces grim as they worked to stabilize her. They managed to slow the bleeding, but it was clear she was in a bad way—too wounded to move far on her own. Ginar, ever resourceful, slipped away, sneaking further into the fortress to find supplies. They were too worn, too wounded to make the journey back without help.

Further up the tower, Ginar found an unexpected ally—a dwarf named Imri, working as a blacksmith for the orcs. Ginar returned with Yrr, bringing Uftak’s severed ear as a grisly token to persuade Imri to help them. The blacksmith, fearful and skeptical, eventually relented, the threat implicit in Uftak’s ear combined with Yrr's assurance that they would be gone as soon as possible convincing him to cooperate.

Imri led them to a small back room, hidden from prying eyes, where they could rest. He helped Ginar steal food from the larder, and together they made a cold soup, feeding Celenneth and wrapping her wounds. The war band stayed there for two days, Celenneth slowly regaining her strength. By the second day, she was able to stand, though fighting was beyond her. They knew they couldn’t stay—Uftak’s absence would eventually be noticed, and they had to decide whether to push forward or try to return to Balin.

Imri, seeing their determination, decided to throw his lot in with them. He had long since given up hope of rescue, but with Uftak dead and the orcs in disarray, he saw an opportunity. He shared what he knew about Malech One-Eye, the orc chieftain who held sway over the fortress. Malech had been at the Battle of Five Armies, using his reputation to maintain power, but Imri knew him to be weak and paranoid. Malech had relied on Uftak to keep order, and now, without his enforcer, he was vulnerable.

The decision was made. They would push forward, take the fight to Malech while they still had the element of surprise. Imri showed them a hidden staircase leading further up the fortress. They moved quietly, assassinating the orc guards they encountered, their approach swift and deadly. At the top, they broke into Malech’s chambers, catching the orc chieftain by surprise.

Celenneth, her bow once more in her hands, loosed an arrow that struck Malech in the shoulder. He roared, calling for his guards, but none came. Panic flashed in his eyes, and he grabbed his spear, lashing out as Hanar and Lif closed in. Lif, already injured, was caught by the spear, the point driving through his gut. He fell, his eyes wide with shock as the life drained from him.

Hanar let out a furious cry, his blade sinking into Malech’s back. The orc staggered, his movements becoming desperate as he tried to defend himself. Ida and Celenneth fired arrow after arrow, each one finding its mark. The fight was brutal, but eventually, one of the arrows struck true, and Malech fell, his body crumpling to the floor.

They had done it. Malech was dead, his head severed and taken as a trophy. In the aftermath, they discovered a magnificent hoard—gold and jewels, treasures from across Middle-earth. Each of them took their share, but there was more than just wealth here. Among the treasures, Celenneth found a sword, its craftsmanship unmistakable—a relic from the Battle of Five Armies. They also found the brazen head of Dero, said to whisper secrets under the moonlight, and a ring of amethyst, elven in origin, blessed with the power of courtesy and insight.

The dwarves also raided Uftak's chambers, taking the best of the weapons they found there. They wrapped Lif’s body, carrying him with them as they made their way back. Imri joined them, his eyes filled with a newfound hope as he left the fortress behind.

The journey back was grueling. On the first day, they traveled eight miles, fatigue setting in as they found a great feast hall that Imri led them to. He had been there before, accompanied by orcs to gather raw materials. On the second day, they managed another ten miles, though exhaustion was beginning to take its toll. Celenneth, still recovering, found herself weary, her strength fading. By the third day, they traveled only six miles, Tora faltering under the weight of carrying Lif, though she was eventually given reprieve.

On the fourth day, they made good time, traveling eight miles and shaving off another four by finding a hidden path. Finally, they arrived back at the new base camp, greeted with a mix of relief and sorrow.

Celenneth presented Balin with the brazen head of Dero, while Hanar brought forth the severed ear of Uftak and the head of Malech. They laid Lif’s body before Balin, the weight of their losses heavy on their hearts. They explained that they had not been able to clear the orcs entirely from the fortress, but they had dealt a substantial blow, weakening the enemy’s hold on Moria.

They also brought forth the gold and jewels they had found, though there was much they had left behind, too burdened by their wounded and the body of their fallen comrade to carry it all.

In the weeks that followed, Celenneth and the others took time to recover. Wounds healed, and the weight of shadow lifted as they took solace in their victory. Celenneth gained further insight into the lore of Moria, her understanding deepened by the treasures they had found. Together with the dwarves, she composed a lay in honor of Lif and the battle they had fought, a song to remember their fallen friend and the victory they had achieved.

But unknown to them, their actions had drawn the attention of a new enemy—Gorgol, son of Bolg, who would soon learn of Malech’s death and make his way to Moria, seeking vengeance.

11Leaving Moria

The gates of Moria shut behind Celenneth with a heavy clang, leaving her alone beneath the sprawling sky. The silence was unnerving after the constant echo of dwarven voices and the weight of stone above her. She adjusted the straps of her pack, feeling the heft of mithril within, and tightened her grip on her bow. The dwarves had escorted her to the threshold, but this next leg of her journey—to Rivendell—she would walk alone.

Before her departure, Balin had given her the brazen head of Dero, tasking her to deliver it as a gift to Rivendell, in exchange for answers to several questions he had written. He had also insisted that she leave behind the gold and jewels she had taken from the hoard, replacing them instead with an equivalent value of mithril; far lighter to carry. He had asked her to choose three dwarves to accompany her to the western exit, and she had chosen Ginar, Dagrun, and Nar.

The journey to the westernmost exit had taken three days. They traveled steadily, following a winding watercourse, until they finally arrived at the exit. Celenneth bid farewell to Ginar, Nar, and Dagrun, wishing them safety as they returned to the depths of Moria. She made camp there, resting and recovering her strength, preparing herself for the journey north to Rivendell.

The Misty Mountains loomed behind her, their peaks shrouded in dark clouds. As Celenneth began her descent into the western foothills, she kept to the hidden paths, her every step deliberate. The journey ahead was long and fraught with danger, but solitude was not unfamiliar to her. A Ranger of the North knew how to endure.
