1The Watchtower

Súlimë 3, 2970

Celenneth led the company through the rugged eastern hills, her senses tuned to every shift in the land — wind-tossed leaves, the far cry of a hunting hawk, the earthy smell of thawing soil against lingering frost. Damrod and Hallas kept pace without complaint, their vigilance matching hers; Linnea followed close, her eyes ever moving. By dusk the old watchtower marked on her maps came into view, its crumbling upper walls standing like broken teeth against the dimming sky. Celenneth signed a halt, and they led the horses to a sheltered hollow.

"We observe from here," she said. "Damrod, Hallas, take the flanks. Linnea, keep watch." A flicker of light showed in one of the tower's lower windows, and she moved ahead — until her boot struck loose masonry and sent it clattering down the incline. She froze. The old door creaked open, and a hooded figure emerged with a shielded lantern; while it moved toward the noise, Celenneth slipped through the door left ajar, sword drawn. Outside, Damrod and Hallas closed on the figure from either side like shadows made flesh; Damrod struck the blade from its grasp, Hallas drove it back against the cold stone, and they had their captive.

Inside, Celenneth found a brazier's struggling glow and, beyond an archway, worn steps descending into dark. At their foot a Númenórean ward pulsed faint in the wall — long faded, but not dead. She touched it, whispering a phrase of warding drawn from her studies of the old Adûnaic tongue and what had served in the frozen ruin of the north, and the rune flared silver, and for an instant she felt its purpose whole: a seal meant to confine something powerful below. But the enchantment was cracked, and something down there had already begun to stir.1

Hallas joined her, and together they pressed deeper. The corridor descended steep and cold, and the cursed Fangorn blade at her hip — bound in stained cloth — throbbed with a subtle hunger she tried to name imagination. A sickly red glow appeared ahead, pulsing in time with a muffled, alien chanting, and easing to the half-open stone door she looked into a vaulted chamber: three robed figures around an altar bathed in crimson, and on it a bound captive, breathing shallow under coils of shadowy mist. Celenneth met Hallas's eyes. "We end this now — quiet and fast."

She drew, breathed, and loosed, and her arrow took the nearest cultist through with a muffled crack; he dropped in silence. The other two whirled, hands sparking with malignant fire, and her second shaft skittered off the altar-stone — but Hallas burst forward, and his sword opened the second cultist's torso, and his chant died with him. The last spat a final invocation at the captive, the chamber shuddering with corrupt power — and Celenneth's next arrow buried in his chest, cutting the words short, and Hallas's blade finished it. The ritual undone, the altar still glowed with erratic, unstable pulses. She pressed her palms to its runes and forced her mind through half-remembered Númenórean formulas, Hallas's steadying hand and his own scraps of lore guiding her, and together they drained the crimson light until only a faint silver glow remained, and the shadowy bonds around the captive shattered.2

The freed woman was young, barely past twenty, pallid and bruised, her ranger's gear — runed bracers, a wood-and-bone pendant — hinting at a hard road. Celenneth knelt and took her cold hands and draped her own cloak round the trembling shoulders. "It's over. You're safe now. You're free." While Hallas searched the fallen cultists — turning up a foul rune-blade, a scroll-case of Black Speech, and an obsidian sigil-stone still crackling faintly — Celenneth coaxed the woman toward speech. "Morwenna," she managed at last. "I came from the North, seeking the Forgotten Watchers — but they found me first." The effort drained her, and she leaned weak against Celenneth as they carried her up into the night air.

Outside, by Damrod and Linnea's small fire, the bound captive stiffened at the sight of Morwenna, fury barely held. Linnea laid out the recovered relics in the firelight and pulled the gag free. "These — what purpose did they serve? What were you doing down there? Who do you serve?" The man sneered, then spat words like venom. "The Watchers stir, you blind fools. You've only delayed their rise, not stopped it. The bond endures. The Shadow waits." He laughed, hollow and brittle. Morwenna shivered, and forced out the rest of it: "The Watchers — sleeper agents of Morgoth, if the old stories hold. Ancient guardians, or fiends, meant to wake at some dark summons. Most thought them myth. But if they've stirred, someone is calling them."

Damrod's jaw set. "If he lives, he's a threat. He'll kill again." Hallas shook his head — "he might still be useful; fear loosens tongues, and we know so little" — and Celenneth weighed the burden between them. "I won't kill a bound and helpless man, however dark his deeds," she said at last. "We learn what we can. At dawn we search the tower, take what's needed, and set him free. His life isn't ours to judge." Damrod gave a reluctant nod; Linnea met her eyes in silent agreement; and they settled into an uneasy rest, the captive tied to a stout pine root, Morwenna dozing fitfully against Celenneth's shoulder, the watches shared beneath a cold and starlit sky.3

2Rituals

As morning broke, Celenneth and Damrod left the camp to Hallas and Linnea and went back into the tower, the stale air heavy with the residue of broken spells. They moved without speaking, understanding shared — though their eyes met now and then, small knowing glances passing between them. Where a carved symbol lay half-hidden beneath twisted script, Damrod brushed it clear to reveal the faint outline of a Dúnedain battle standard beneath. "The past never seems to stay buried, does it?" he murmured, dry but not unkind.

"No," Celenneth said. "But sometimes that's not the worst thing." Clearing a heap of debris uncovered a time-worn mural — Dúnedain warriors, weapons raised against some forgotten foe, their valor lingering like a distant echo. The dusty silence charged between them, and Celenneth moved close, near enough that he caught the scent of leather and travel-herbs off her. "You carry yourself like them," she said, low. "Steady and resolute — but weighed down by things unspoken." Her fingers brushed the clasp of his cloak and lingered there.

Damrod's posture stiffened, duty and something else warring in the set of his jaw. "I don't usually go in for entanglements," he managed, rough at the edges. Then, a flicker of concern: "What about Linnea?"

Celenneth held his gaze. "I love Linnea, completely. But we've always known there are parts of us the other can't fill. She understands. She wants me to find what I need, if it feels right." Her eyes held him. "Maybe this is that time." His stoic mask softened, warmth showing through the cracks, and his hand brushed hers just long enough to say her words had landed. "We'll see where the path leads," he said. "Come on — the upper floors."

They climbed the spiraling stair to a ruined observatory open to the slate-grey sky, and while Celenneth traced old wards drained but not dead, Damrod's sharp eye caught a loose stone in a cracked pedestal. He worked it, and a hidden compartment clicked open on three relics of the tower's forgotten past: a ranger's old journal in coded script; a map fragment marked with cryptic symbols along what he recognized as the far northern borders — near the frozen ruin they'd sealed; and a silver clasp bearing a watchful eye, a motif that unsettled him for reasons he could not yet name. He tucked the journal away to puzzle over later.1

As they turned back toward one another, something in the room shifted: the dulled runes flared suddenly with silvery light — whether from the items uncovered, or the honesty shared, or their resolve stirring old powers — and the dark whispers in the corners of the tower seemed to shrink back, subdued.

Celenneth went ahead to inspect a rune at the end of the lower hall, and leaned her palms against the warm, faintly pulsing stone. She felt Damrod approach behind her, steadfast and magnetic, and glanced back over her shoulder with a deliberate curve to her smile. "Some magic," she murmured, low and teasing, "can be strengthened by release. Shall we be sure that whatever slumbers beneath this tower stays buried?"

"I'd make use of it." She arched her back, pressing into the promise of him. "Wouldn't you?"

For a long moment he was silent — and then his gloves creaked as he undid the straps of his belt, and stepped close, his hands moving slow over her hips, down the ties of her breeches. "You're cold," he murmured at her ear. "Foolish, letting the chill settle into your bones."

The rune's glow settled to a steady pulse, the chamber quiet but for their ragged breath. Damrod leaned over her, his forehead brushing her shoulder, his hands lingering with an unspoken tenderness. "Perhaps," he murmured at last, "there's some truth to your rituals."

Celenneth laughed, breathless and sated, tracing lazy patterns over the cooled stone. "Consider the tower safer for it." He straightened, fastening his belt, a faint smirk on him. "Then I suppose we've done our duty." She met his gaze with a mischievous glint as she set her clothes to rights. "Until next time, ranger." — "Until then," he said.

3On the Road

Súlimë 5, 2970

They gathered at dawn around a broad flat stone, the rising sun softening the tower's silhouette behind them. Celenneth spread the map fragment and traced its faded paths; Damrod laid the coded journal beside it, frustration creasing his brow; Hallas and Linnea studied the dark relics, which still seemed to hum faintly with severed power. Morwenna, paler than the day but more present, recounted what she could of her capture — half-ruined outposts and abandoned watchtowers in the east, rumors tied to the waking of the Forgotten Watchers. The eastern hills were calling, and they agreed to follow. Morwenna settled behind Hallas on his bay, and they broke camp and rode out, the tower shrinking behind them into just another broken shape on the horizon.

After a while Celenneth guided her horse close to Linnea's, letting the others drift ahead. The land rolled green and budding beneath them, and they rode in easy quiet until Linnea caught her eye and arched a brow.

"You asked me once about motherhood," Celenneth said, low enough for Linnea alone. "About whether we might try, if the chance came." Her gaze flicked to Damrod's broad shoulders ahead, and a faint blush warmed her. "I told you what happened with Damrod in the tower. We connected, in a way I hadn't planned."

Linnea's eyes danced, curious and warm rather than jealous. "I hoped you'd tell me more." And Celenneth did — the elemental sweep of it, how it had surprised her as much as thrilled her, and the idea of a child, always too tangled in duty and danger to feel real, now stirring closer.

"So you're excited by it," Linnea said quietly.

Celenneth laughed, soft. "It's strange. I am. I never thought I would be. But there it is."

Linnea laid a hand over her glove. "If it's something you want, keep going. Damrod, Hallas — who knows what the road holds. I don't desire men that way; my heart and body are yours. But that doesn't mean I don't want to see you happy, fulfilled." Her grin turned playful. "And when you find another moment with one of them, I'd like to be there. I'd like to watch." Celenneth colored, but a pleased smile curved her lips. "You would?" — "Absolutely. Consider it my way of encouraging good fortune." Then her gaze grew earnest. "We've always trusted each other. I trust your heart. If this is a step you want, I'll stand with you." They rode on through the quiet hills, the idea settling in Celenneth like a warm and quiet thrill.

That night Linnea sat the watch, her gaze drifting from the fire to Celenneth's sleeping form, auburn hair slipped free of the hood and catching the light. A fierce, tender need swelled in her — to pull her close, to remind herself that in the vastness of the road some things were still wholly theirs. Silent, she moved to her knees and slipped to Celenneth's bedroll, her travel-roughened fingers brushing the familiar line of the scarred arm. Celenneth stirred, her hand twitching toward her blade before her eyes focused on Linnea's face above her, and she relaxed, a wry grin tugging at her mouth. "Couldn't sleep?"

"Didn't want to," Linnea whispered, and leaned in and closed the space between them. The kiss was slow and reverent, and Celenneth's arms rose to wrap around her and pull her down fully into the bedroll. Clothing loosened in practiced silence; Linnea's hands mapped scar and skin as though the night had granted them new bodies to learn, and when Celenneth's cloak slipped away and left her bare and gold in the firelight, Linnea simply looked at her a moment — the strong, capable woman who met every storm unshaken, her guard down now, her eyes dark and hazed with wanting.

"Come here," Celenneth murmured, and Linnea pressed flush against her, and the friction of bare skin sent sharp pleasure through her. She moved slow, savoring every tremor she drew, every gasped whisper of her name, and then deepened her touch — her fingers working in slow, deliberate circles, her mouth at Celenneth's throat — until Celenneth's head fell back and her body arched off the bedroll, a moan breaking from her. Linnea whispered something too soft to carry against her ear, and Celenneth came undone beneath her, taut and then trembling, her face a portrait of pure surrender. Linnea watched her through it, reverent, brushing the sweat-damp hair from her brow, and afterward the two of them curled together, limbs entwined, faces inches apart as the fire burned low.

Across the camp, Hallas stirred, his instincts pulling him from sleep — and stilled, his gaze catching the shadowed forms past the dying fire. Celenneth and Linnea, wrapped together, moving with an intimacy that spoke of trust as much as desire; the way Linnea touched her, cradled her as something precious. He should have looked away. He could not. He watched in silence, his breathing shallow, no malice in it — only a longing, deep and unspoken, that he'd buried longer than he cared to admit. When Celenneth's quiet cry carried across the camp his chest tightened, and when the two women settled at last into each other's arms he rolled carefully onto his back and stared up at the stars, his expression unreadable — save for something soft and wistful in it, like the glow of embers hidden beneath ash.

4The Wagon Trail

Súlimë 9, 2970

Under a clearing sky they pressed deeper into the eastern hills, Damrod leading over hidden trails, Morwenna riding close to Celenneth and drawing quiet comfort from the weathered Dúnedain waystones that marked the true paths of her ancestors. Hallas scouted ahead, and his diligence paid off when he found fresh tracks — boot-prints mixed with wagon-ruts, a small caravan gone through days before. They followed the trail into a narrow ravine, its rocky walls pressing close — and a metallic scrape sounded above, and an arrow cracked against a boulder beside them.

"Travelers!" a rough voice called from the heights. "Drop your weapons and stand, or the next won't miss."

Linnea raised her empty hands, her bow slung. "We mean you no harm," she called, calm and clear. "We followed these tracks hoping for safe passage. Lower your bows, and we'll talk." But the voice came back colder — fools or spies — and demanded their weapons thrown down. Reluctantly they complied, steel clattering to the rock, and Celenneth stepped forward. "We're travelers on urgent business, not brigands. Take our weapons if you must — but trust cuts both ways."

Steady footsteps, and a man came into view at the ridge, a ranger's cloak battered by years, scars mapping his face. "Dúnedain," he muttered, wary. "You don't belong here. Who sent you?"

Linnea answered before any could speak. "Rangers, escorting me and my partner south to the Angle." She indicated Celenneth without hiding their bond. "We followed the tracks hoping for safe passage. One of us is injured." Her eyes flicked to Morwenna, pale against Hallas. The leader's gaze softened at the sight of her, and he signaled his hidden bows down. "You've crossed into lands where trust is earned with hardship," he said tersely. "I see weariness in your faces, not malice. Come — we'll hear your tale. But if I sense deceit, you won't leave these hills alive."

"We are not your prisoners," Celenneth said, even. "We'll travel with you, but we keep our weapons and our freedom. We've come in peace." He weighed it long, then nodded. "Take up your weapons. Keep them sheathed. We'll see to her first. Then we talk."

By a sheltered fire, once Morwenna was tended, Celenneth and the others recounted it all — the tower's cursed runes, the ritual halted, the cultists bent on waking an old power; and Hallas told of the corrupted ruin in the far north and what they had sealed there. The rangers listened grim, their leader pacing, and at the mention of the frozen ruin his eyes narrowed with the recognition of old rumors he'd once dismissed. "You've walked paths no one should tread," he said at last. "If these shadows stir in the north and beyond, the danger runs deeper than we knew. We'll guide you to safer lands — only because of what you've done, and seen. Shadows remember those who challenge them."

In the days that followed they traveled in uneasy harmony, and Morwenna set herself to earn a place — scouting winding deer-paths, finding safe camps, asking no gift but a chance. The leader's terms were plain: prove yourself, through action. And she did, until even he no longer bristled. At a lonely crossroads deep among the slopes their paths at last diverged — the rangers east toward lands they would not name, and Celenneth, Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas south toward the Angle and home. Morwenna hesitated, looking from Celenneth to the ranger leader, and he gave her a curt nod that was neither warm nor dismissive. "You have proven yourself," he said. "These hills have a place for you." And so, with gifts exchanged, they parted, the company riding on with the wind at their backs and a cautious hope.1

5Closeness

The fire burned low in the heart of the clearing, and for once there was nothing pressing in on its light. Celenneth sat with Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas around it, the bitter ale from the Rangers' farewell passing between them a mouthful at a time — earthy, sharp, a small held-back comfort in a country that had grudged them every other.

Linnea lifted her cup, the firelight catching in her dark eyes. "To surviving what we shouldn't have. And to whatever comes next."

"To standing when others can't." Damrod tipped his cup, and a rare warmth came through the words before his reserve closed over them again.

The talk drifted into half-smiles and low laughter, and Celenneth leaned in and kissed Linnea slow, saying with it what she had no need to say aloud.

Damrod watched them go, then turned his eyes back to the flames. Beside him Hallas did the same, and neither spoke for a while.

"Farther than I thought we'd come," Damrod said at last, working a bracer loose to give his hands something to do. "It still feels unfinished. Like there's something waiting past the next ridge."

"There's always something waiting." Hallas's gaze stayed on the fire. The sounds from the tent reached them, faint, and his jaw tightened at a pull he could not have named. "We only decide how long we stay ahead of it."

Damrod read the shift in him — the set of his shoulders, the shadow behind the easy face. "Does it trouble you?" he asked, careful to leave the question room.

"Not that." Hallas watched the sparks go up and vanish. "Only — seeing what they have. That kind of closeness. It makes a man wonder what it would be to be let that far in."

Damrod let the silence sit a breath. "You're not the only one who wonders," he said, gentler than before. They looked at each other, and left it there, and sat close in the cold with the thing unspoken between them grown a little lighter for the saying.

Inside the tent the cold fell away. Celenneth sat among the winter furs with her cloak still drawn close, though it was not the chill that made her keep it.

"You've been thinking about it," she said.

Linnea's eyes opened to hers, trust in them and a knowing glint besides. She propped herself on an elbow. "You don't need my leave, Celenneth. You know that." The corner of her mouth curved. "But you want it anyway."

Celenneth didn't deny it. What lay between the two of them had been forged too hard to break with a careless hand, and they both guarded it.

"You deserve a night like this," Linnea said, softer now. "They'd follow you into Angband if you asked. Let them make much of you, for once." Her gaze moved over Celenneth, slow and frank, and there was heat in it as much as generosity. "And I'll want to watch."

The nights fell into a shape after that, as instinctive as breath. By day they moved guarded and precise across the snow-blanketed ridges; by dark the world drew back and left them something quieter and truer. However they began, they ended the same: Celenneth gathered into waiting arms, Linnea's breath in her hair or Damrod's heart steady beneath her cheek, Hallas a warm bulwark at her back — four people who had decided, for as long as the road allowed it, to be a shelter to one another.

6The Festival

Súlimë 27, 2970

After several days of travel they came down from the hills into a sun-warmed valley where spring flowers bloomed in wild profusion and faint music drifted on the breeze — flutes and drums from a small village in a hollow, banners rippling, the folk aglow with laughter and song. They had arrived in the midst of a spring festival, and before the four could make their purpose known an older woman bustled toward them, arms flung wide. "Ah, there you are! Our beloved newlyweds — we feared you'd miss the blessing! Two fine couples, just as the tales say!"

The four traded baffled looks, but the villagers gave no chance to explain, whisking them into the heart of it — travel-stained cloaks traded for floral ceremonial robes, garlands woven into their hair, carved wooden rings slipped onto their fingers for fertility and a good harvest. In the square the elder raised a cup: "May these couples' love be strong as the earth, their passion fierce as the summer sun!" Damrod straightened to correct the misunderstanding, but Linnea slipped beside him with a mischievous twinkle. "We don't want to disappoint them," she whispered. "Let's play along." Damrod sighed, a faint smile tugging at him. "As you say. For the harvest."

And when it wound down, the four were led to a modest lodge kept for "the newlyweds," two chambers off a small corridor.

Then she turned to Celenneth, her green eyes lit with promise, and drew her into the other room.1

And for once they let themselves linger over a hearty breakfast, the spring sun on the fields outside, stitching the memory of that place into something they would carry with them.

7Wolves

Víressë 1, 2970

After the festival they pressed southward, the hills softening under a gentler sky, spring warmth seeping into the land. Celenneth and Linnea rode side by side, sharing easy smiles; Damrod and Hallas kept their companionable silence, a steady rhythm at their backs. Late one afternoon they made camp beside a forgotten stone cairn, and as dusk fell a hush settled over the woodland — too pressed, as though the trees held their breath. Hallas caught movement at the clearing's edge. They were not alone.

The silence broke with a low, guttural growl, and out of the gloom came creatures shaped like wolves but warped by a darker power — eyes a feverish red, fur wavering half-there and half-not, like living silhouettes peeled from midnight. No natural beasts, but things of shadow and corruption. Celenneth and Linnea loosed at once; Celenneth's shaft vanished into the dark, but Linnea's punched into a wolf's shoulder, and it hissed like escaping steam and staggered — hurt, then, whatever it was.

The pack lunged with startling speed, flickering in and out of the real as they came. Hallas drove his spear low beneath one's ribs, and for a moment its shadowy hide thinned to reveal something black and oily beneath; Damrod's blade left a ragged gash that seeped darkness rather than blood; and Linnea's arrow, aimed true for a vital place, sank deep, and the beast lost all coherence and dissolved into a swirl of black mist. They fought in a storm of snarls and flickering shapes, the wolves' teeth snapping through empty air where flesh had been a moment before — but steel and arrow and the four of them together were enough, and one by one the creatures were driven apart into fading dark, until the last melted silently into the forest gloom.1

In the silence after, their breath loud in their own ears, Damrod and Hallas exchanged a grave look and drew a little apart to speak low. These wolves had not come by chance; there was a source, a blight hidden beyond these hills, and Hallas gestured eastward.

Over a spare breakfast the next morning, Damrod said it plainly. "Hallas and I will track this corruption east. It's best we don't ignore it — there may be worse ahead." Hallas added, "You can come, if you wish. But we won't turn aside, and it may lead us far afield."

Celenneth and Linnea traded a look. They had come so far — and home, the Angle, called them now more than ever, and Brynja with it. "No," Celenneth said gently. "Our road leads south." Linnea touched her arm in agreement. "We'd only slow you. Go, find what you must. We'll hope for your safe return." There was a moment of understanding that needed no words. Damrod inclined his head, a rare softness in his eyes; Hallas clasped Celenneth's forearm, warrior to warrior; and then they embraced, all four, before the two men turned east into the hush beneath the canopy and were swallowed by the shifting morning light. Celenneth and Linnea mounted and angled south, toward gentler valleys and home, and the forest sighed behind them as their hoofbeats faded into silence.2

8The Glade

Víressë 5, 2970

The sun dipped low as their path strayed into a forest both ancient and strangely awake. The world hushed in anticipation — not a leaf stirred, not a bird sang, and even the horses moved quietly, as if fearful of breaking a spell. Great oaks and silver beeches rose like timeless pillars, their branches woven into an emerald canopy suffused with a golden, otherworldly light, and where the faint track curved they came out into a clearing.

At its center lay a pool so calm and perfectly round it might have been a mirror set upon the earth, ringed with wildflowers long out of season. Celenneth reined in. "Do you feel that?"

Linnea nodded, her dark eyes reflecting the shimmer. "It's as though we've wandered into a tale. It doesn't feel real." They dismounted, and Celenneth knelt and dipped her fingers into water cool and comforting as fresh rain. "It's untouched. Not even wild creatures have spoiled it. 9The Return

At midday Celenneth and Linnea topped a gentle rise and saw a single curl of smoke in a shallow hollow below — a modest camp, a small fire, and bent over a leather-bound book a man in a faded blue cloak, his quill scratching furiously.
