1The Watchtower

Súlimë 3, 2970

Celenneth led the company through the rugged eastern hills, her senses attuned to every subtle shift in the landscape—the rustling of wind-tossed leaves, the distant cry of a hunting hawk, and the earthy scent of thawing soil mingling with lingering frost. The air was sharp with the lingering chill of winter, though hints of spring stirred faintly in the budding branches and thawing earth. Damrod and Hallas kept pace without complaint, their steady vigilance matching hers. Linnea followed closely, her keen eyes ever watchful.

By evening, they reached a cluster of twisted pines offering a measure of shelter against the wind. As the company unpacked their gear, Celenneth silently assessed the surroundings. The horses grazed quietly nearby, their breath visible in the cooling air.

The night passed uneventfully, and with dawn came a brisk wind that carried away the last vestiges of frost. They resumed their journey with determined focus, Celenneth's mind already considering the potential hazards ahead. Her thoughts returned to the old watchtower marked on her maps—a possible vantage point but also a potential danger.

As dusk settled, the watchtower came into view, its jagged silhouette stark against the dimming sky. The tower's crumbling upper walls stood like broken teeth, an ominous reminder of battles long past. Celenneth motioned for the others to halt. They dismounted quietly and led their horses to a sheltered hollow.

“We’ll observe from here,” Celenneth instructed softly. “Damrod, Hallas, take flanking positions. Linnea, keep watch.” Her voice was steady, betraying none of the unease that gnawed at her.

The company waited in tense silence as darkness deepened. A faint flicker of light appeared in one of the tower’s lower windows. Celenneth exchanged a quick glance with Damrod and Hallas before moving ahead, her steps careful but swift.

Halfway to the entrance, her boot struck a chunk of broken masonry, sending it tumbling noisily down the incline with a harsh clatter. She froze, breath held, listening. Moments later, the old wooden door creaked open, and a hooded figure emerged, carrying a dimly shielded lantern.

Celenneth pressed herself against the tower’s wall, melding into the shadows. As the figure moved cautiously toward the noise, she slipped through the door they had left ajar, her sword already drawn.

The dim interior was littered with broken furniture and scattered debris. A faint brazier’s glow struggled against the darkness, illuminating a threadbare tapestry and the archway leading deeper within. Celenneth caught a trace of something foul in the air—stale, bitter, and wrong.

Outside, Damrod and Hallas moved with practiced caution, keeping to the tower’s perimeter. As the hooded figure emerged from the tower, lantern held cautiously aloft, they advanced soundlessly from either side, appearing like shadows made flesh.

Startled, the figure drew a blade in a desperate, defensive arc. Damrod stepped in with ruthless precision, striking the weapon from the figure’s grasp with a sharp twist of his wrist. Hallas followed with fluid efficiency, driving the intruder back against the cold stone wall.

“Who are you?” Damrod hissed, his voice low and dangerous.The captive struggled fiercely but fell still under Hallas’s steely gaze, offering only a glare of defiance.

Inside, Celenneth advanced toward the archway, sword ready. The flickering light from the brazier cast shifting shadows across the cold stone walls. She moved past the stairs that went up to the tower, and, swiftly and silently, she slipped through the archway, descending the worn steps into the chilling dark. Her senses sharpened as the stale air thickened, carrying a bitter tang of ancient decay.

At the base of the staircase, an ancient rune etched into the stone wall pulsed faintly—a Númenórean ward, long faded but still resonant with weak protective power. There were doors on either side, with the door to the right having clearly been used recently. Celenneth touched the rune, tracing its worn lines with a gloved finger, feeling the latent strength battling against a dark, malevolent force below.

Pressing her hands gently against the cold, glowing rune, Celenneth called upon her studies of Adûnaic, the old language of the Númenóreans, and the practices that had seemed to work in the Frost Maw, whispering a ritual phrase of warding and protection. The rune flared with bright silver light, sending a faint, resonant hum through the stone walls. For an instant, she felt the ancient ward’s purpose resonate within her: a protective seal meant to confine something powerful beneath the tower. Yet, cracks in the enchantment frayed its strength. Something dangerous had already begun to stir below.

They exchanged a quiet, understanding nod—no words needed. With Hallas close at her side, Celenneth pressed deeper into the hidden passageway. They moved in deliberate silence, guiding one another with faint gestures. She led the way, her senses heightened, aware that they had left Damrod and Linnea above, guarding their camp and their horses in the fading twilight.

The corridor descended steeply, and with every step, the air grew colder, pressing in as though the walls themselves leaned forward, watching. Loose flakes of stone crunched softly beneath their boots, and the silence felt weighted, as if held tight by some unseen hand. There should have been no breeze so far below ground, yet a faint whisper seemed to thread through the darkness, brushing their faces like a chill breath.

Celenneth’s heart grew heavier as they ventured deeper. The cursed blade she carried—bound in stained cloth at her hip—throbbed with a subtle hunger. Its presence unsettled her, tugging at her spirit with insistent, venomous whispers that might have been her imagination.

Soon, a dim, flickering glow appeared at the end of the corridor. It painted the stone with weak, wavering light, a sickly red that seemed to pulse in time with the muffled, rhythmic chanting that now reached their ears. The language was twisted and alien, its tones scraping at Celenneth’s senses with a malevolent edge.

Celenneth eased forward and peered around the half-open stone door. Beyond lay a wide, vaulted chamber. Three robed figures stood gathered around an altar inscribed with pulsing runes, the entire surface bathed in a crimson sheen. Their hoods concealed their faces, and their voices rose and fell in dark harmony. At the altar’s center, a bound captive lay still, breathing shallowly under coils of shadowy mist that held their wrists and ankles tight.

Hallas crept up beside Celenneth, his eyes narrowing as he took in the grim tableau. The altar’s runes seemed to twist the torchlight into unnatural shapes, and the chanting drummed like a malignant heartbeat against the cold stone walls. Celenneth could feel the sting of that power—old, rancid magic that should have remained buried. She met Hallas’s gaze, her own tight with resolve.

They would have to act quickly. This was no place for careful diplomacy. The darkness here was alive and hungry, and every second they waited risked the captive’s life—and perhaps their own. Celenneth closed her eyes briefly, centering herself, letting the ward she had touched above guide her thoughts and steel her heart. Then, softly, she whispered to Hallas, “We end this now—quiet and fast.”

He gave a slight nod. There was no need for formal words. They understood each other well enough. Together, they slipped into the chamber’s shadowed edges, preparing for a swift and decisive strike.

Celenneth drew a slow, measured breath, her bowstring taut beneath steady fingers. She focused on the nearest robed figure, the dark hood disguising whatever grim features lay beneath. With a controlled exhale, she released the arrow. It struck hard and true, its impact shattering bone with a muffled crack. The cultist dropped at once, limbs folding in silence, as if life had been plucked away in that single moment. The other two whirled toward the disturbance, hands sparking with malignant energy as they hissed guttural words of power, their unholy rite abruptly stalled.

She nocked another arrow, but as she released, it skittered off the stone altar in a sharp rebuff, missing its mark by a hair’s breadth. Red sparks danced at the fingertips of the cultist she’d aimed for, twisted symbols taking shape in the stifling air. In that instant, Hallas burst forward, sword raised. Steel found flesh in a single, sweeping stroke, slicing through the second cultist’s torso. The wounded figure shrieked, their chant snuffed out as they crumpled to the floor. The last cultist, eyes blazing with panicked fervor, spat out a final invocation, channeling black magic toward the captive still bound on the altar. The chamber shook, thick with crackling, corrupt energy.

As the last, vile syllables left the cultist’s tongue, Celenneth’s next arrow flew straight and sure. It buried itself deep in their chest, cutting them off mid-incantation. Blood splattered across the altar’s surface, yet the cultist persisted, staggering forward on sheer fanatic will. Hallas did not hesitate. He lunged, blade flashing in the wan light, and opened the cultist’s throat with a swift, final slash. At once, the chamber trembled violently—the ritual undone but leaving behind a raging, unstable force. The altar still glowed with erratic pulses of magic, and the helpless captive on it drew a ragged breath, struggling weakly against shadowy bonds.

Celenneth pressed her palms against the gleaming runes etched into the altar’s edge. She forced her mind to recall old teachings, half-remembered formulas from Númenórean lore. Tendrils of corrupt energy flared, ready to lash out. Hallas laid a firm hand on her shoulder, steadying her, recalling bits of knowledge he’d seen etched on ancient ruins. Guided by each other’s strengths—his memory and her understanding—they channeled their will into the altar. The crimson light dimmed, draining away until only a faint, silvery glow remained. The dark restraints around the captive shattered with a brittle snap, freeing her at last.

Slumped against the cold altar, the young woman looked barely older than her early twenties. Her skin was pallid from whatever horrors she had endured, dark hair glued to her brow with sweat. Bruises, both physical and otherworldly, stained her face with lingering shadows. Beneath the grime and torn cloth, her gear hinted at a past life as a ranger, protective runes carved into leather bracers and a pendant of wood and bone still hanging at her throat.

Celenneth lowered herself beside the woman, speaking in hushed, gentle tones. She took the captive’s hands into her own, warming them, offering a fragile sense of calm after such brutality. Draping her weathered cloak around the trembling shoulders, she offered a safe harbor for this stranger swept up in dreadful magic. “It’s over,” Celenneth said softly, her words a steady light in the gloom. “You’re safe now. You’re free.”

Hallas worked methodically in the aftermath of the conflict, the chamber still echoing with the distant tremors of broken magic. While Celenneth tended to the rescued woman—still trembling—he moved among the fallen cultists with quiet purpose, searching their robes, checking beneath overturned furniture, and rapping gently at suspicious sections of stone walls. He found nothing to suggest a hidden passage; whatever dark secrets the cultists had forged here seemed contained within these cold, sealed walls. Yet, his thorough sweep yielded grim spoils: a blade twisted with foul runes that glinted hungrily even in the waning light, a leather scroll case concealing texts in the harsh angles of Black Speech, and an obsidian stone etched with intricate sigils still crackling faintly from the ritual’s abrupt end.

Placing the items aside for later scrutiny, Hallas joined Celenneth in supporting the young woman. Her skin was ashen, and her dark hair clung to her clammy forehead. She shrank at Celenneth’s gentle touch, eyes distant and unfocused, as though still ensnared by things too horrible to name. With patient murmurs and soothing tones, Celenneth coaxed her toward speech. Though she flinched at first, eventually the woman’s cracked voice emerged, trembling and barely above a whisper.

“Morwenna…” she managed when asked her name. “I came… from the North, seeking the Forgotten Watchers… but they… found me first.” That effort alone seemed to drain her, and she shuddered into silence, leaning weakly against Celenneth. Hallas nodded solemnly, offering what warmth and steadiness he could, and together they guided Morwenna back through the winding corridors. The darkness of the tower pressed close, but no new threats emerged; the shattered ritual and the broken runes had rendered these halls quieter now.

They climbed until the cold night air brushed their faces once more. Outside, beneath sparse starlight, Damrod and Linnea waited near a small campfire. Damrod’s captive—still bound, gagged, and watchful—stiffened at the sight of Morwenna. Recognition flared in his eyes, followed by fury barely contained. The ropes creaked as he tensed, but he could do nothing else.

Hallas carefully laid out the recovered artifacts before the firelight. The ceremonial blade winked with an unnatural gleam, the scroll case sagged ominously, and the obsidian stone fizzled with traces of severed magic. The captive’s eyes followed each object, hunger and fear mingling on his scarred face. He strained forward, glaring at Morwenna and the items as though he might will them back into his grasp.

Linnea stepped up, her posture straight and voice coolly authoritative. “These relics—what purpose did they serve?” She removed the gag and fixed him with a piercing stare, her voice low enough not to startle Morwenna, but firm enough to demand answers. “What were you and your kind attempting to do down there? Who do you serve?”

The captive’s first response was silence, a mocking sneer twisting his lips. Damrod hovered nearby, hand on the hilt of his knife, expression grim. Celenneth knelt beside Morwenna, murmuring calming words. Morwenna stared into the fire, still shaky, as though trying to banish lingering shadows from her mind.

When Linnea refused to yield, her eyes narrowed and her tone cutting like an archer’s arrow, the captive finally spat words like venom. “The Watchers stir… you blind fools,” he snarled. “You’ve only delayed their rise, not stopped it. The bond endures. The Shadow waits…”

He laughed, a hollow, brittle sound. Morwenna shivered at his words, clutching Celenneth’s cloak tighter. Linnea’s gaze did not waver. “The Watchers,” she repeated, glancing toward Morwenna and then Celenneth, as if seeking confirmation.

Morwenna hesitated, voice quavering as she forced the explanation forth. “They were… sleeper agents of Morgoth, if the old stories can be believed. Ancient guardians—or fiends—meant to awaken at some dark summons. Most believed them myths, little more than dusty legends. Yet… if they’ve stirred now, someone is calling them. Or something worse.”

A heavy silence fell over the camp. Damrod’s jaw tightened, his stance rigid. “If he lives, he’s a threat,” he said quietly, jerking his chin toward the prisoner. “He’ll kill again if given the chance. Perhaps letting him go is too kind.”

Hallas shook his head, considering their dire situation. “He might still be useful. These twisted fanatics sometimes slip up. Fear can make their tongues loose. Information is a powerful weapon, and we know so little about what we face.”

Celenneth looked between her companions, weighing the moral burden. Her voice, when it came, was steady but tinged with sorrow. “I won’t kill a man who’s bound and helpless, no matter how dark his deeds,” she said. “We will learn what we can. At dawn, we’ll search the tower, take what is needed… then we set him free. His life is not ours to judge.”

Damrod’s eyes narrowed slightly, but he gave a reluctant nod. Linnea’s gaze met Celenneth’s in silent agreement, and Hallas exhaled, as though relieved to see a line still drawn against the encroaching darkness.

For a time, the four of them spoke in hushed voices, planning the next steps, remembering old stories from their own distant homelands—scraps of knowledge, fragments of legends that might hint at what the Watchers were and how to stop them. They passed waterskins and a bit of dried bread around, their fire snapping softly, its sparks drifting into the chill night. Morwenna ate little but listened quietly, pulling strength from their calm resolve.

Eventually, they settled into uneasy rest. The captive stared into the flames with sullen, hateful eyes, tied firmly to a stout pine’s twisted root. Morwenna dozed fitfully, head resting against Celenneth’s shoulder, haunted by echoes of the ritual that nearly claimed her life. Damrod and Linnea took turns pacing the perimeter, their silhouettes sharp against the starlight, ever vigilant. Hallas sat close by, blade across his knees, scanning the tree line for any hint of movement.

Morning found them gathered around a broad, flat stone, the rising sun softening the harsh lines of the tower’s silhouette behind them. Celenneth unrolled the map fragment carefully, pressing it flat with one hand while the other traced faded paths and cryptic markings. Nearby, Damrod placed the old ranger’s journal beside it, a frustrated crease pinching his brow. He’d spent part of the early dawn turning its pages in vain attempts to decipher the coded entries, but the meaning still eluded him. Hallas and Linnea knelt opposite each other, studying the dark relics—relics that still seemed to hum faintly with severed power.

Morwenna, though still pale, appeared more present than the night before. Wrapped in a borrowed cloak, she leaned forward, her voice low and cautious as she recounted what she remembered of her capture and quest. She spoke of half-ruined outposts and abandoned watchtowers hidden deep in the east, of rumors that these places might be tied to the awakening of the so-called Forgotten Watchers. Her memories were fragmented but compelling, each snippet of information weaving into the puzzle Celenneth and Damrod hoped to solve.

As they listened, the group weighed their next move. The eastern hills were calling—there, the scattered clues might align. They agreed to follow that route, leaving the crumbling tower and its grim secrets behind. With the decision made, they broke camp. Morwenna settled behind Hallas on his sturdy bay, her grip tentative but steady. Damrod and Celenneth readied their mounts, and Linnea checked their saddle-straps with the careful efficiency of the Rohirrim. Before long, the small company rode out, the tower shrinking behind them until it was just another broken shape on the horizon.

After a short while, Celenneth guided her horse closer to Linnea’s, letting the others drift ahead so they’d be out of easy earshot. The landscape rolled gently beneath them—low, forested hills dotted with early-spring buds, a hint of green returning to the world. The scent of damp earth and budding leaves drifted in the wind. They rode mostly in silence, content in each other’s presence, until Linnea caught Celenneth’s eye and arched a brow, inviting whatever had been on her mind.

Celenneth cleared her throat, voice soft enough that only Linnea could hear. “You asked me once about the possibility of... motherhood.” Her gaze flickered to where Damrod rode ahead, broad shoulders set in a familiar, confident posture. She lowered her voice further. “About whether we might try, if the opportunity arose. Well...” She paused, a faint blush warming her cheeks. “I told you what happened with Damrod in the tower. We... connected, in a way I hadn’t planned.”

Linnea’s eyes danced with curiosity and warmth, though she kept her tone even. “I hoped you’d tell me more.” she leaned in, a teasing smile on her lips.

Celenneth nodded, exhaling slowly. She described the intensity of that morning, the feeling of being swept into something powerful and elemental, how it surprised her as much as it thrilled her. Linnea listened with genuine interest, her expression thoughtful rather than jealous. Linnea’s dark eyes widened slightly. She turned fully toward Celenneth, searching her face. “You think you might be…?” she began, voice gentle.

Celenneth nodded once, her jaw tightening for a moment before she spoke again. “Yes. I think I might be carrying a child.” Her voice shook ever so slightly on the final word, and Linnea’s heart twisted in sympathy and delight.

“Oh, love,” Linnea breathed, a bright warmth flooding her chest. She reached out, laying a hand over Celenneth’s arm. “A sibling for Brynja.”

Celenneth’s lips curved, a hesitant, hopeful smile. “If it’s true, yes. Another child—another life to care for. But…” She hesitated, chewing on the inside of her cheek. “I don’t know who the father might be. It could be Hallas, or Damrod. Does that trouble you?”

Linnea shook her head, her voice calm and certain. “No. Hallas and Damrod are both good men, and this child will have love, no matter whose blood runs in their veins. The important thing is that they are ours, just as Brynja is ours.” She gave Celenneth’s arm a reassuring squeeze. “This child will know nothing but love and care.”

Celenneth exhaled slowly, relief dancing in her eyes. “I’m glad you feel that way. I… I do worry. About bringing a child into a world still shadowed by old threats. Can we offer them safety? Can we promise them peace?”

Linnea’s hand slipped down to Celenneth’s, their fingers weaving together. “We can’t promise them a perfect world,” she said softly. “But we can promise them we will do everything we can. We will teach them strength and kindness. We will show them how to find beauty even when the road is hard.”

Celenneth nodded, blinking back the mist in her eyes. “Brynja grew under our protection, and she thrives,” she murmured, her voice carrying a note of wonder. “We’ll give this child the same love, the same guidance. They will have Brynja as a sister, and together they’ll face whatever comes with hearts full of courage.”

Linnea’s smile grew warm. “Exactly. And maybe one day, when all of this wandering ends, we’ll settle somewhere safe. A home. A place filled with laughter and the sound of small feet running across the floor. Isn’t that a dream worth holding onto?”

Celenneth’s shoulders eased, the tension flowing away like water under a bridge. “It is,” she agreed quietly. “It’s more than worth it.”

They rode on, side by side, the silence now rich with promise rather than uncertainty. The distant hills rolled gently, guiding them onward toward the shrine Lothair had mentioned, and beyond that, perhaps toward a life that grew beneath Celenneth’s heart.

Their journey carried them into a shaded valley, where cool breezes whispered through tall, ancient beeches. Descending between mossy trunks and stones streaked with lichen, they heard the gentle murmur of flowing water. Finally, at the heart of this quiet grove, the shrine revealed itself. A centuries-old Dúnedain platform, softened by time and vines, stood amid a stand of grey-barked trees. Symbols of renewal and guardianship, weathered yet still legible, adorned its edges. At the base, a font of crystal-clear water bubbled from a hidden spring, so pure it seemed to glow.

Celenneth dismounted first, stepping forward with careful reverence. “This was ours,” she said softly, her voice almost lost in the hush of the leaves. “A refuge my people built long ago. A place of rest and thanks.”

Linnea followed, her dark eyes roaming over the ancient carvings. “It still stands,” she murmured. “Even after all these years, it waits.”

They moved together, drawn to the font. Celenneth slipped the chalice from her pack, its silver surface reflecting the green twilight beneath the canopy. Kneeling, she dipped it into the spring. The water rippled, light and shadow dancing across its surface, then settled into a stillness that seemed to hold secrets of ages past.

She turned to Linnea, offering the filled chalice. “Let’s drink,” she said softly. “May this place’s strength run in our blood—and whoever follows us here.”

Linnea’s gaze warmed with understanding. “Together,” she agreed. They had come through so much, and more trials awaited on the road home. Any blessing was worth cherishing.

Celenneth raised the chalice to her lips. The water tasted of distant rain and mountain air, cool and invigorating. As she swallowed, a gentle warmth spread through her limbs, as though the shrine’s quiet blessings had entered her veins. She closed her eyes, feeling calmer, more certain.

Then Linnea drank in turn, tipping the chalice just enough to let the water flow. It touched her tongue like a memory of starlight, filling her chest with quiet resolve. Her fingers drifted to the pendant at her neck, the gift they had earned together in that enchanted glade. Now, amid these old stones, it felt more precious than ever—an emblem of hope passed from lost times to the present.

They set the now-empty chalice at the base of the shrine, leaving it behind. Celenneth’s voice was low but firm. “We’ll let it remain for those who come after. May it refresh their spirits as it did ours.”

Linnea’s hand found Celenneth’s, squeezing gently. For a moment, neither spoke. The air hummed with a subtle warmth, as if the shrine acknowledged their offering and their departure. Here, the past touched the present, passing on its quiet blessings to those willing to listen.

They mounted again, turning their horses south, leaving behind a silver chalice brimming with silent legacy, certain that one day, someone else would stumble upon this place and find its comfort.

The days after leaving the Shrine of the Steadfast passed in a comfortable hush. Gentle rains swept through the countryside, followed by mild breezes that carried the scent of wet earth and budding leaves. Celenneth and Linnea rode quietly, their hearts settled with a calm purpose. Both women carried the memories of that sacred water and the gifts they had chosen to leave behind; it felt as if a secret blessing walked beside them, guiding them homeward.

They bypassed Bree without fanfare, choosing lesser-known paths and old ranger trails to save time. Their thoughts dwelt now on the Angles—on home, warmth, and, most of all, Brynja. She had been safe in their absence, cared for by the Aldion family. Thinking of the Aldions—Eadric, Lira, and their daughter, Elys—brought Linnea and Celenneth a sense of gratitude. The family had welcomed them wholeheartedly when they first settled in the Angles, and now Brynja lived as if in a second home, playing with Elys in fields turned green by spring.

Soon, familiar landmarks emerged: low stone walls marking pastures, scattered cottages where smoke drifted from chimneys, and the soft glint of the Greyflood weaving through gentle hills. Celenneth felt her heart lift at each signpost of home, Linnea’s smiles growing more frequent and warm as they drew closer.

At last, they came upon the Aldion home, a modest stone-and-timber house sheltered by an old apple tree. The yard opened onto a small stretch of meadow, dotted with wildflowers and half-grown clover. As they approached, a child’s laughter broke through the quiet—a sound like bells on the breeze. Brynja’s laughter.

Linnea swung down from her horse first, hands trembling slightly with joy. “Brynja?” she called softly.

A clatter of footsteps answered from inside. The door burst open, and Brynja came hurtling out, golden curls bouncing, cheeks flushed. Hot on her heels followed Elys, grinning widely. But Brynja only had eyes for her mothers.

“Mama! Mama Linnea!” Brynja cried, throwing herself forward. Linnea knelt just in time, catching the little girl in a tight embrace. She pressed kisses onto Brynja’s temple, tears bright in her eyes. “Oh, my little star, I missed you so much.”

Celenneth came to join them, dropping beside Linnea and wrapping both in her arms. Brynja giggled, delighting in the closeness and the familiar scent of leather and hay. “You smell like horses!” she teased, wrinkling her nose at Celenneth.

Celenneth laughed, tucking a stray curl behind Brynja’s ear. “I’ve been riding for days, so I suppose I do,” she said, pressing a fond kiss to Brynja’s forehead.

Behind them, Eadric and Lira Aldion stepped onto the porch. Eadric’s steady gaze softened at the sight of the reunion, and Lira’s smile crinkled the corners of her eyes. Elys hovered behind her parents, watching the scene with shining curiosity and happiness.

“We’ve had no trouble at all,” Lira said warmly, folding her flour-dusted hands in her apron. “Brynja and Elys have kept us busy, exploring every puddle and patch of field they can find. She’s grown brighter by the day.”

“She’s been eagerly awaiting your return,,” Eadric added with a grin. “We’re glad to see you back safe.”

Linnea rose, still holding Brynja’s hand, and reached out to clasp Eadric’s forearm. “We owe you both so much. Thank you. Knowing Brynja was safe with you eased every mile we traveled.”

Elys stepped forward, smiling shyly at Celenneth and Linnea. “Brynja helped me feed the chickens,” she said, proud of their small accomplishments. Brynja nodded enthusiastically.

Inside the Aldion home, warmth and laughter enclosed them like a comforting cloak. Elys and Brynja huddled over the table, giggling and comparing their scribbled sketches of imagined quests—intrepid mothers battling fierce monsters, discovering secret groves, and racing swift horses across endless hills.

Lira poured milk, setting fresh bread within easy reach. “Stay as long as you like,” she said, her eyes kind. “Our door is always open.”

Linnea smoothed Brynja’s curls, pressing a kiss to the crown of her head. “You’re generous as ever, Lira,” she said softly. “But we should return to our own home. Brynja should sleep in her own bed tonight.”

Brynja’s head whipped around, her eyes bright but uncertain. “But it’s cold and empty there,” she said, her voice small. “You’ve been gone so long, Mama.”

Celenneth knelt beside her daughter’s chair, meeting Brynja’s gaze. “Then we’ll warm it again,” she promised gently, a smile lighting her green eyes. “We’ll make a fire, sweep out the dust, and turn it back into a home. Yours, mine, and Linnea’s—just as it was, and better.”

Brynja considered this, then nodded slowly. She slipped from Linnea’s lap and dashed to Elys, giving her friend a quick hug. “I’ll come back tomorrow,” she whispered, making Elys grin and nod.

Linnea and Celenneth thanked Eadric and Lira once more, heartfelt words passing between them. Then, hand in hand with Brynja, they stepped outside. The sun dipped low, painting the distant fields in soft gold. They followed a familiar path through the twilight, their shadows stretching behind them.

Their own cottage sat a little ways off, tucked near the gentle rise of a green hill. Its walls were quiet, its windows dark. But as Celenneth pushed open the door, Linnea moved smoothly inside, unrolling a window shutter to let in the last of the day’s light. Brynja hovered in the threshold, uncertain, until Celenneth lifted her gently into her arms.

“First, a fire,” Linnea said, a resolve in her voice that made Brynja smile. They gathered kindling from a small pile beneath the eaves, found tinder in the jar on the shelf. Soon, sparks caught, and the hearth crackled softly, sending flickers of warm light dancing across the walls.

Celenneth surveyed their tidy but dusty rooms. She found a broom where it had always hung, and set to sweeping. Linnea opened a trunk to find blankets still folded neatly, smelling faintly of lavender. Brynja, eager to help, wiped her hands along a bench, making faces at the dust motes that rose. Laughter wove between them, each contributing in small ways to chase away the quiet emptiness.

By the time full night had fallen, the cottage felt alive again. The fire’s glow revealed old comforts: a well-worn rug, a hand-carved chair, a small shelf of keepsakes lining the mantle. Brynja lay curled up beneath a thick blanket Linnea had shaken fresh, her eyes drooping. Celenneth ran a damp cloth over the window’s sill, smiling at the gentle breath of evening air that slipped through.

Linnea knelt beside Brynja, tucking her in. “See?” she whispered. “Not so empty now.”

Brynja nodded sleepily, “It smells like home again,” she murmured, and Linnea’s heart brimmed with quiet joy.

Celenneth settled beside the hearth, the soft pop of burning wood filling her ears. She caught Linnea’s eye, and they shared a tender look—understanding, gratitude, love.

Outside, the Angles lay calm under a star-scattered sky. Inside, their family had reclaimed a space of their own. The long roads behind them, the uncertain future ahead, but for now, they were safe and warm, enclosed in a circle of light, laughter, and belonging—truly home again.
