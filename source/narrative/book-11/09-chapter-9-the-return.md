# Chapter 9: The Return

*Víressë 9, 2970*

At midday Celenneth and Linnea topped a gentle rise and saw a single curl of smoke in a shallow hollow below — a modest camp, a small fire, and bent over a leather-bound book a man in a faded blue cloak, his quill scratching furiously. They came close enough to hear him mutter. "No, no… the fork must have turned west of the cairn — unless it was east? Drat this ink."

Celenneth cleared her throat. "Traveler, are you lost?"

He jolted, nearly flicking ink across his notes, and peered up over round spectacles. "Lost? Not lost, no. Merely… slightly misaligned. You're not brigands, I hope? You don't look like brigands." Assured they were neither brigands nor farmers, he brightened and rose with creaking knees. "Lothair, at your service. Chronicler of roads, seeker of ancient tracks, and occasional victim of poorly marked maps. I roam these old paths of Eriador, setting them down before they vanish. They've a nasty habit of wandering off when one least expects it."

He offered them tea of "questionable virtue," and chattered freely — ruined bridges, misleading milestones, the hawk that made off with his hat near Tharbad — while Celenneth studied his map, a patchwork of guesswork and honest effort. "May I?" she asked, and traced a thin line with her fingertip. "Your cairn should be here. Follow that ridge to a broken oak stump, west until you find stones stacked in a rough pyramid — that's your cairn. From there keep the sun on your right till midday, and you'll find the ford." Lothair watched, transfixed. "By the stars — you must be Dúnedain. That's more help than I've had in weeks." In thanks he tapped a symbol on a second, more dog-eared sheet. "There's a shrine south of here — Dúnedain work, I believe. Travelers call it the Shrine of the Steadfast. It may be on your road; some say it's a place of rest, touched by old blessings." Linnea's face lit. "We'll keep it in mind. Thank you, Lothair." And they rode on, leaving a wandering scholar set back on his way, and carrying the promise of an old Dúnedain shrine somewhere ahead.

*Víressë 15, 2970*

The late sun gilded the rolling plains, and for a time they rode in the comfortable quiet that came of deep understanding. Then Celenneth drew a breath. "Linnea. I need to tell you something." She kept her eyes on the path, but Linnea felt the shift in her tone.

"What is it?"

Celenneth chose her words with care. "I've missed my cycle." The birdsong seemed to hush, leaving only the wind and the shift of reins.

Linnea turned fully toward her. "You think you might be…?"

"Yes." Celenneth's jaw tightened, then eased. "I think I might be carrying a child." Her voice shook on the last word, and Linnea's heart twisted with sympathy and delight both.

"Oh, love." Linnea reached out and laid a hand over her arm. "A sibling for Brynja." Celenneth's lips curved, hesitant and hopeful — then she hesitated. "If it's true. But I don't know who the father might be. It could be Hallas, or Damrod. Does that trouble you?"

Linnea shook her head, calm and certain. "No. They're both good men, and this child will have love, no matter whose blood runs in their veins. What matters is that they're ours, just as Brynja is ours. This child will know nothing but love." Celenneth exhaled, relief in her eyes — though the old worry lingered. "I do fear it. Bringing a child into a world still shadowed. Can we give them safety? Peace?"

Linnea's fingers wove through hers. "We can't promise a perfect world. But we can promise we'll do everything we can — teach them strength and kindness, show them how to find beauty even when the road is hard. Brynja grew under our protection, and she thrives. This one will have the same love, and Brynja for a sister." Celenneth blinked back the mist in her eyes. "It's more than worth it," she agreed quietly, and they rode on, the silence now rich with promise.[^r-child]

*Víressë 17, 2970*

Their road carried them into a shaded valley of ancient beeches, and at its heart the shrine revealed itself — a centuries-old Dúnedain platform softened by time and vine, symbols of renewal and guardianship still legible at its edges, a font of crystal-clear water bubbling from a hidden spring at its base. Celenneth stepped forward with careful reverence. "This was ours. A refuge my people built long ago, a place of rest and thanks." Linnea's eyes roamed the old carvings. "It still stands. Even after all these years, it waits."

Celenneth drew the silver chalice from her pack and knelt and dipped it into the spring, and turned to offer it to Linnea. "Let's drink. May this place's strength run in our blood — and in whoever follows us here." They drank in turn, the water tasting of distant rain and mountain air, a gentle warmth spreading through their limbs as though the shrine's quiet blessing had entered their veins. Then they set the empty chalice at the shrine's base and left it there. "For those who come after," Celenneth said. "May it refresh their spirits as it did ours." And Linnea's hand found hers, and for a moment neither spoke, the air humming with a subtle warmth, as if the shrine acknowledged their offering.[^r-shrine]

⁂

The days after passed in a comfortable hush of gentle rains and mild breezes, and they bypassed Bree by the lesser trails to save time, their thoughts fixed on the Angle — on home, and on Brynja, safe these long weeks with the Aldions. Soon the familiar landmarks came: low stone walls, scattered cottages with smoke curling from their chimneys, the soft glint of the Greyflood through the hills. At last they came upon the Aldion home beneath its old apple tree — and a child's laughter broke the quiet, a sound like bells on the breeze. Brynja's laughter.

Linnea swung down first, her hands trembling with joy. "Brynja?" A clatter of footsteps, and the door burst open, and Brynja came hurtling out, dark curls bouncing, cheeks flushed, Elys grinning at her heels — but Brynja had eyes only for her mothers. "Mama! Mama Linnea!" She flung herself forward, and Linnea knelt just in time to catch her and press kisses to her temple. "Oh, my little star. I missed you so." Celenneth dropped beside them and wrapped both in her arms, and Brynja giggled, wrinkling her nose. "You smell like horses!" Celenneth laughed and tucked a stray curl behind her ear. "I've been riding for days. I suppose I do."

Eadric and Lira stepped onto the porch, their faces soft at the reunion, Elys hovering shyly behind. "No trouble at all," Lira said warmly. "The two of them have kept us busy, exploring every puddle and patch of field. She's grown brighter by the day." Linnea rose, still holding Brynja's hand, and clasped Eadric's forearm. "We owe you both so much. Knowing she was safe with you eased every mile."

That evening they thanked the Aldions and walked home hand in hand with Brynja through the twilight, their own cottage quiet and dark on its green rise. Brynja hovered uncertain at the threshold — "it's cold and empty, you've been gone so long" — until Celenneth knelt to meet her eyes. "Then we'll warm it again. We'll make a fire, sweep out the dust, and turn it back into a home. Yours, mine, and Linnea's — just as it was, and better." And so they did: Linnea coaxed sparks to the hearth, Celenneth swept the tidy dusty rooms, Brynja wiped a bench and made faces at the dust motes, and laughter wove between them and chased the emptiness away. By full night the cottage was alive again — the well-worn rug, the hand-carved chair, the small shelf of keepsakes on the mantle — and Brynja lay curled beneath a freshly-shaken blanket, her eyes drooping. "It smells like home again," she murmured. Celenneth settled by the hearth and caught Linnea's eye, and they shared a look full of understanding and gratitude and love; and outside the Angle lay calm beneath a star-scattered sky, and inside their family had reclaimed a space of their own — the long roads behind them, an uncertain future ahead, and a new life growing quietly beneath Celenneth's heart. Truly home again.
