# Chapter 4: The Wagon Trail

*Book 11*

*Súlimë 9, 2970*

Under a clearing sky, they pressed deeper into the eastern hills, the crisp spring air carrying hints of thawing earth and distant pines. Damrod led the way, guiding the company over hidden trails and through quiet groves known only to a few. Morwenna rode close to Celenneth, drawing small comfort from ancient waystones—slender, weathered markers etched with old Dúnedain runes—that assured her they traveled true paths of her ancestors.

Hallas scouted ahead, his gaze cutting through the underbrush and tree-shadows. At times he called out soft warnings or signaled silently, his hand raised to indicate a turn or a sudden dip in the land. His diligence paid off when he found a set of fresh tracks: boot prints mixed with wagon ruts. Perhaps a small caravan had passed here a few days prior, hugging the quiet valleys to avoid prying eyes. Following this lead, the company left the more obvious routes behind, winding along a woodland track that Halas discovered, cutting their travel time significantly.

They followed the tracks into a narrow ravine, its rocky walls pressing in on both sides. The hush of the place was broken only by the muffled clop of their horses’ hooves and the soft rustle of their cloaks. Linnea rode near the front, eyes sweeping the ridge lines overhead, though her attention drifted forward toward the bend in the trail. At that moment, a metallic scrape echoed above—followed by the sharp twang of a bowstring. An arrow cracked against a boulder beside them, flinging shards of stone across the path.

“Travelers!” a voice rang out from high above, rough and unapologetic. “Drop your weapons and stand where you are, or the next arrow won’t miss!”

Linnea raised her hands, keeping her bow slung at her back. “We mean you no harm,” she called, voice calm and clear, carrying easily in the still air. “We followed these tracks hoping for safe passage and fair trade. Lower your bows, and we’ll talk.”

Murmurs drifted down from unseen figures. The tension in the canyon mounted, strung tight as the bowstrings overhead. After a pause, the voice responded, colder than before: “Fools or spies—either way, we don’t let strangers wander unchecked. Throw down your weapons. Now.”

Exchanging a wary look, Celenneth, Linnea, Hallas, and Damrod reluctantly complied. Swords, bows, and knives clattered to the rocky ground. Celenneth stepped forward, keeping her voice level. “We are travelers on urgent business, not spies or brigands. If you must take our weapons, do so—but know that trust cuts both ways.”

Silence reigned for several heartbeats. Then, steady footsteps approached, and a man came into view at the rim of the ridge. He wore a ranger’s cloak battered by years of hardship, scars lining his face like old maps of distant wars. His sharp eyes narrowed when he saw them.

“Dúnedain...” he muttered, voice low and wary. “You don’t belong here. Who sent you?”

Linnea lifted her chin and answered before anyone else could speak. “We’re rangers escorting me and my partner south to the Angles.” She indicated Celenneth with a subtle motion, making no attempt to hide their bond. “We followed the tracks hoping for safe passage. One of us is injured.” Linnea’s eyes flicked meaningfully to Morwenna, who leaned against Hallas, still pale but alert.

The stranger’s gaze softened by a fraction at the sight of Morwenna. He raised a hand, signaling his hidden companions to lower their bows. Several rough-looking but disciplined figures emerged, weapons still at the ready, though not aimed at the newcomers.

“You’ve crossed into lands where trust is earned with hardship,” the ranger leader said tersely. “I see weariness in your faces, not malice.” His eyes strayed to Morwenna, recognition sparking briefly. “Come with us. We’ll hear your tale—but if I sense deceit, you won’t leave these hills alive.”

Celenneth’s voice cut through the tension, even and firm. “We are not your prisoners. We’ll travel with you, but we keep our weapons and our freedom.” She met his gaze without flinching. “We’ve come in peace.”

For a long moment, the ranger leader weighed her words. Hallas and Damrod stood silent beside her, and Linnea’s quiet confidence radiated from her steady posture. At last, the leader nodded. “You may pick up your weapons,” he said gruffly. “But keep them sheathed.” Turning back to Morwenna, his tone held a rough hint of concern: “We’ll see to her first. Then we talk.”

They moved together through the hills, settling by a sheltered hollow where a small fire crackled. Once Morwenna was settled and tended to, Celenneth and Damrod recounted their tale. They spoke of the tower’s cursed runes, the foul ritual halted in its final moments, and the strange cultists bent on awakening a dark power. Linnea added details, making sure their story rang true. Hallas described the Frost Maw’s corrupted presence, the unnatural forces stirring there, and how they had sealed what should have remained dormant.

As they spoke, the gathered rangers listened, faces grim and watchful. The leader paced, jaw tight, weighing every word. Morwenna, steadying her voice, confirmed the presence of these dark forces. At the mention of the Frost Maw, the leader’s eyes narrowed—a flash of recognition hinting at old rumors he’d once dismissed. Now, the evidence stood before him, undeniable.

“You’ve walked paths no one should tread,” he said at last, his voice low. “If these shadows stir in the Frost Maw and beyond, danger runs deeper than we knew.”

His wary gaze passed over them all, lingering on Morwenna. After a pause, he nodded once. “We’ll guide you to safer lands—but only because of what you’ve done and what you’ve seen. Don’t mistake my caution for hostility. Shadows remember those who challenge them. None of us can afford carelessness.”

At dawn, they set out eastward. The rangers moved with quiet efficiency, scanning the terrain for threats. Though still guarded, they asked questions: how the company sealed the dark power at the Frost Maw, what the cultists chanted, what the runes meant. Celenneth and the others answered carefully, offering what they knew without detailing their suspicions about the Watchers.

During quiet spells on the trail, Morwenna walked beside the leader, sharing her past as a tracker and scout for distant outposts. She spoke humbly but earnestly, determined to prove her worth. The ranger leader listened attentively but silently.

In time, he laid out the conditions plainly: “Joining our ranks is not just a matter of words. Prove yourself. Scout when we need a scout. Track when we need a tracker. Earn your place through action.”

Morwenna straightened, though fatigue still weighed on her. “I will,” she said simply. “I ask for no gift—only a chance.”

The leader’s eyes were flinty, but not cruel. “Then show us what you can do,” he said, voice even. “Fail, and this land won’t remember you.”

In the days that followed, they traveled together in uneasy harmony. The wild hills, though still secretive, seemed less hostile now that a fragile trust had formed. Morwenna helped find safe campsites and scouted winding deer paths. The younger rangers, once suspicious, began to accept the newcomers’ presence. Even the leader, though still reserved, no longer bristled at every question.

Eventually, at a lonely crossroads deep among rolling slopes, their paths diverged. The rangers continued east, toward lands they would not name. Celenneth, Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas turned south, toward the Angles and home’s distant promise. Morwenna hesitated, looking from Celenneth to the ranger leader. She had kept her word, done what was asked—though the future remained uncertain.

The leader gave her a curt nod. It was not warm, but neither was it dismissive. “You have proven yourself,” he said simply. “These hills have a place for you.”

With that, they parted, having exchanged gifts. The company rode on, the wind at their backs, carrying with them a cautious optimism.
