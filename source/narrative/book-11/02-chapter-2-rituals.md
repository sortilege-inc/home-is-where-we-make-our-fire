# Chapter 2: Rituals

*Súlimë 5, 2970*

As morning broke, Celenneth and Damrod left the camp to Hallas and Linnea and went back into the tower, the stale air heavy with the residue of broken spells. They moved without speaking, understanding shared — though their eyes met now and then, small knowing glances passing between them. Where a carved symbol lay half-hidden beneath twisted script, Damrod brushed it clear to reveal the faint outline of a Dúnedain battle standard beneath. "The past never seems to stay buried, does it?" he murmured, dry but not unkind.

"No," Celenneth said. "But sometimes that's not the worst thing." Clearing a heap of debris uncovered a time-worn mural — Dúnedain warriors, weapons raised against some forgotten foe, their valor lingering like a distant echo. The dusty silence charged between them, and Celenneth moved close, near enough that he caught the scent of leather and travel-herbs off her. "You carry yourself like them," she said, low. "Steady and resolute — but weighed down by things unspoken." Her fingers brushed the clasp of his cloak and lingered there.

Damrod's posture stiffened, duty and something else warring in the set of his jaw. "I don't usually go in for entanglements," he managed, rough at the edges. Then, a flicker of concern: "What about Linnea?"

Celenneth held his gaze. "I love Linnea, completely. But we've always known there are parts of us the other can't fill. She understands. She wants me to find what I need, if it feels right." Her eyes held him. "Maybe this is that time." His stoic mask softened, warmth showing through the cracks, and his hand brushed hers just long enough to say her words had landed. "We'll see where the path leads," he said. "Come on — the upper floors."

They climbed the spiraling stair to a ruined observatory open to the slate-grey sky, and while Celenneth traced old wards drained but not dead, Damrod's sharp eye caught a loose stone in a cracked pedestal. He worked it, and a hidden compartment clicked open on three relics of the tower's forgotten past: a ranger's old journal in coded script; a map fragment marked with cryptic symbols along what he recognized as the far northern borders — near the frozen ruin they'd sealed; and a silver clasp bearing a watchful eye, a motif that unsettled him for reasons he could not yet name. He tucked the journal away to puzzle over later.[^r-tower]

As they turned back toward one another, something in the room shifted: the dulled runes flared suddenly with silvery light — whether from the items uncovered, or the honesty shared, or their resolve stirring old powers — and the dark whispers in the corners of the tower seemed to shrink back, subdued.

Celenneth went ahead to inspect a rune at the end of the lower hall, and leaned her palms against the warm, faintly pulsing stone. She felt Damrod approach behind her, steadfast and magnetic, and glanced back over her shoulder with a deliberate curve to her smile. "Some magic," she murmured, low and teasing, "can be strengthened by release. Shall we be sure that whatever slumbers beneath this tower stays buried?"

"You'd make light of a dangerous ritual?" His voice was gravel-deep, and did little to mask the heat under it.

"I'd make use of it." She arched her back, pressing into the promise of him. "Wouldn't you?"

For a long moment he was silent — and then his gloves creaked as he undid the straps of his belt, and stepped close, his hands moving slow over her hips, down the ties of her breeches. "You're cold," he murmured at her ear. "Foolish, letting the chill settle into your bones."

"And here I thought you'd warm me," she countered, defiant, deliberately provoking him. He exhaled sharp, and unlaced her breeches and drew them down past her hips, his calloused palms exploring the curve of her thighs slow and reverent, lingering at the inside until she breathed his name, half warning and half plea. "You've faced far worse than waiting," he said — but obliged, his touch sliding higher to find her warm and ready, working her with firm, unhurried strokes until her gasp echoed off the cold walls. "You're ready," he said, dark with approval, and withdrew.

"And you've wasted enough time," she shot back, breathless, no venom in it. He pressed close, his weight solid against her back, and sank into her in one slow, deliberate thrust that pulled a cry from her, her hands clenching against the rune as it flared brighter. He held deep a moment, letting her feel every inch of him, then moved — slow at first, his hands guiding her hips to meet him, his pace quickening as he lost himself to the rhythm, each snap of his hips driving her toward the edge. The rune pulsed in time beneath her palms, responding to the union, her gasps climbing to moans that rang off the ancient stone.

"Is this magic enough for you?" he growled.

"Almost." Her voice broke. "Don't stop." He drove into her harder and deeper until she shattered, her cry muffled against the stone, the rune flaring blinding-bright — and he followed a breath later with a groan of her name, spilling into her as they both trembled through the aftermath.[^r-rune]

The rune's glow settled to a steady pulse, the chamber quiet but for their ragged breath. Damrod leaned over her, his forehead brushing her shoulder, his hands lingering with an unspoken tenderness. "Perhaps," he murmured at last, "there's some truth to your rituals."

Celenneth laughed, breathless and sated, tracing lazy patterns over the cooled stone. "Consider the tower safer for it." He straightened, fastening his belt, a faint smirk on him. "Then I suppose we've done our duty." She met his gaze with a mischievous glint as she set her clothes to rights. "Until next time, ranger." — "Until then," he said.
