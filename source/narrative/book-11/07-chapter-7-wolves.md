# Chapter 7: Wolves

*Book 11*

*Víressë 1, 2970*

After leaving the peaceful festival behind, the company pressed southward, the hills gradually softening under a gentler sky. Early spring warmth seeped into the land, and the air held a quiet promise of better days. Celenneth and Linnea often rode side by side, sharing easy smiles and private whispers. Nearby, Damrod and Hallas traveled in companionable silence, their presence a steady rhythm—like distant drums keeping time.

As they pushed toward the southern reaches of the hills, the forests grew thicker. Late one afternoon, they made camp beside an old stone cairn, its purpose long forgotten. A hush fell over the woodland, and as dusk settled, shadows stretched long and deep. Hallas, ever watchful, caught sight of something moving at the edge of their clearing. The quiet felt off—too pressed, as if the woods were holding its breath.

They realized they were not alone. The silence broke with a low, guttural growl that raised the hairs on their necks. Emerging from the gloom came creatures shaped like wolves but warped by a darker force. Their eyes glowed a feverish red, and their fur seemed to waver, half-there and half-not, like living silhouettes peeled from midnight. In the fading light, their forms shimmered with a strange, malevolent energy. These were no ordinary beasts, but things of shadow and corruption.

Before the creatures closed in, Celenneth and Linnea let arrows fly. Celenneth’s shot vanished into the gloom, but Linnea’s arrow found its mark, punching into a wolf’s shoulder. The beast snarled—a sound more like a hiss of steam than an animal’s cry—and staggered, shadow-fur rippling around the wound. Unnatural or not, they could be hurt.

The wolves advanced. Celenneth and Hallas stepped forward, weapons raised, bracing for impact. Damrod held a steady line, and Linnea nocked another arrow from behind, scanning for an opening. When the wolves lunged, it was with startling speed, their jaws snapping at throats and limbs, their eyes burning like coals. Their movements were wrong—fluid, but in a way that made them seem to flicker in and out of reality, blurring as they darted through moonlit branches.

Hallas met one head-on, driving his spear low, catching the beast beneath its ribs. The creature howled, and for a moment, its shadowy hide thinned, revealing something black and oily beneath. Celenneth swept her sword in a controlled arc, sparks briefly flashing against stone as she struck too low. Damrod’s blade caught another wolf’s flank, leaving a ragged gash that seeped darkness instead of blood. Linnea’s second arrow came swiftly after—this time she aimed for a vital spot. Her shaft sank deep, and with a strangled yelp, one of the wolves lost all coherence, collapsing into a swirl of black mist that dissolved into nothing.

They fought in a storm of snarls and flickering shapes. The wolves moved as if guided by something older and crueler than hunger. Their teeth snapped inches from Celenneth’s arm, tearing through empty air where her flesh had been moments before. One leapt at Hallas, but he angled his spear upward, impaling it just beneath the jaw. Its form stuttered, as though a lantern’s light had been shaken, and then it dropped, dissolving as the first had. Damrod, faced with another lunging shape, gritted his teeth and slashed hard. Though his blade cut true, the wolf twisted aside, fading for an instant like smoke caught in a gust, only to solidify an arm’s length away. Linnea rushed forward, abandoning her bow for a blade. With a precise thrust guided by grim determination, she pierced another wolf’s flank, forcing a shuddering gasp from the beast. The creature tried to snap at her, but it was too late—its strength bled away, leaving only tattered shadow drifting on the night breeze.

With each kill, the night grew a fraction lighter, the oppressive hush lifting. Two wolves remained, both wounded and staggering, their eyes now flickering from red to dull embers. Celenneth recovered her footing and struck again, her blade biting deep. Hallas followed with merciless efficiency, his spear finishing what Celenneth began. The beast vanished in a swirl of silent, fading darkness.

Now the last wolf—its form ragged and thin—lashed out in vain at Damrod, only to be met by Linnea’s cold steel and Celenneth’s relentless sword arm. Overwhelmed, it too succumbed, howling silently as it melted away into the forest gloom.

In the silence that followed, their breaths were loud in their own ears. The world smelled of damp earth and pine needles. No shadowy wolves remained, only the faint memory of their presence clinging to the roots and leaves. Damrod and Hallas exchanged a grave look, then moved a short distance from the camp to speak quietly. Whatever these creatures were, they had not appeared by chance. There was a source, a blight of some kind hidden beyond these hills. Damrod’s brow furrowed as Hallas gestured eastward, indicating trails that might lead deeper into unknown lands. The two reached a decision and returned to Celenneth and Linnea.

In the early morning light, the forest’s grim hush lifted somewhat. Birds ventured tentative songs, and a soft breeze stirred the damp leaves. Over a simple breakfast—just dried fruit, bread, and water—they discussed their next steps.

Damrod’s voice was calm but resolved. “Hallas and I will track this corruption east,” he said, nodding toward the dark horizon. “It’s best we don’t ignore it. There may be worse things ahead.”

Hallas added, “You can come with us, if you wish, but we won’t turn aside. This might lead us far afield.”

Celenneth and Linnea shared a look. They had come so far already, but home—the Angles—called to them now more than ever. Whatever darkness lay ahead, they trusted Damrod and Hallas to face it. Celenneth shook her head gently. “No. Our road leads south.”

Linnea touched Celenneth’s arm, agreeing quietly. “We’d only slow you down. Go. Find what you must. We’ll hope for your safe return.”

There was a moment of understanding, the kind that needed no words. Damrod inclined his head, a rare softness in his eyes. Hallas offered Celenneth a brief clasp of the forearm—warrior to warrior. They then embraced, and Damrod and Linnea joined them.

The two men pulled away and turned eastward, horses stepping carefully into the hush beneath the canopy, their figures soon swallowed by the morning’s shifting light. Celenneth and Linnea mounted their own steeds and angled south, pressing toward gentler valleys and home. The forest sighed softly behind them, as their hoofbeats echoed into silence.
