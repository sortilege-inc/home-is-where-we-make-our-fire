# Chapter 6: The Festival

*Súlimë 27, 2970*

After several days of travel they came down from the hills into a sun-warmed valley where spring flowers bloomed in wild profusion and faint music drifted on the breeze — flutes and drums from a small village in a hollow, banners rippling, the folk aglow with laughter and song. They had arrived in the midst of a spring festival, and before the four could make their purpose known an older woman bustled toward them, arms flung wide. "Ah, there you are! Our beloved newlyweds — we feared you'd miss the blessing! Two fine couples, just as the tales say!"

The four traded baffled looks, but the villagers gave no chance to explain, whisking them into the heart of it — travel-stained cloaks traded for floral ceremonial robes, garlands woven into their hair, carved wooden rings slipped onto their fingers for fertility and a good harvest. In the square the elder raised a cup: "May these couples' love be strong as the earth, their passion fierce as the summer sun!" Damrod straightened to correct the misunderstanding, but Linnea slipped beside him with a mischievous twinkle. "We don't want to disappoint them," she whispered. "Let's play along." Damrod sighed, a faint smile tugging at him. "As you say. For the harvest."

So they let the village pair them as it pleased — Linnea on Damrod's arm, Celenneth dancing with Hallas — and gave themselves to the festival's chaos: spiced bread and honey-tarts pressed on them, a ring-toss Damrod won with a ranger's aim, dancing well past dusk. When the bonfire blazed at its peak the matriarch blessed "these two pairs," passing a horn of spring-mead — *may your love root strong as the oldest oak; may your bond be fierce as the storm's edge* — and the crowd cheered, and ancient songs rose to the stars. And when it wound down, the four were led to a modest lodge kept for "the newlyweds," two chambers off a small corridor.

Linnea paused there, and caught Damrod's eye, and then Hallas's. "Tonight," she said, low, "maybe something different awaits you." And she opened one door and gently sent the two men through it together, and they went — surprised, but willing — the door closing on the pair of them beneath the lamplight. Then she turned to Celenneth, her green eyes lit with promise, and drew her into the other room.[^r-festival]

The small chamber held a warm, sacred stillness, the hearth's light spilling gold over the flower-strewn robes, the air faintly sweet with lilies. Linnea brushed her fingers over Celenneth's forearm. "Let's make this ours," she said. Celenneth smiled — truly smiled, a rare thing that broke through her like sun through cloud. "Always."

Linnea drew from her satchel a long woven cord, dyed the green and deep red of the binding that had bound them years ago, and held it out, and neither spoke; none was needed. Celenneth extended her arm, and Linnea wound the cord slow up from wrist to forearm in its old deliberate pattern, the knots woven with the same reverence as that first night, and kissed the skin just above the last knot. Then Celenneth did the same for her, slower but no less sure, and pressed her lips to Linnea's forearm. "It's still perfect, isn't it?" Linnea murmured, their arms wound in a mirror of the promise they had made and remade over countless roads. "Unshaken," Celenneth said. "As strong as that first day, and stronger."

They moved to the bed, shedding the flower-strewn robes, and Celenneth kissed her deep and slow, a kiss of reverence and remembrance both. She laid Linnea back and loosened her tunic tie by tie, as though removing armor, and stilled a moment just to look at her bare in the firelight before her mouth followed her hands — the collarbone, the peak of a breast drawn between her lips until Linnea gasped and fisted a hand in her hair. They found the rhythm born of years, reverent and tender and full of need, and Celenneth mapped every scar and freckle with mouth and hands until Linnea whispered her name like a prayer and came apart beneath her, and after they lay tangled beneath the blankets, two halves of one soul.

"How do you think Hallas and Damrod are managing?" Linnea murmured, half-asleep.

Celenneth's mouth curved. "If I know Damrod, he's standing like a stone, trying not to breathe too loud. And Hallas — waiting for someone to give him leave to move." Linnea laughed, low and breathy. "They'll figure it out. Eventually." Celenneth pressed a kiss to her brow and slid from the bed, reaching for her tunic. "I'll go check on them. Help, if they need it." — "Be gentle," Linnea murmured, already drifting. Celenneth grinned. "I always am."

She knocked once, and at Damrod's low *enter* pushed the door open with the ease of someone who knew she belonged. The men's hearth burned low too; Damrod sat at the bed's edge, half-clothed, Hallas at the window as ever, watchful. Celenneth leaned back against the door, the linen sheet draped loose about her, one shoulder and one long leg bare. "A quiet night, for a pair of newlyweds," she teased.

Damrod's brow quirked, his eyes roaming her. "It does get lonely on the road. But it seems you've come to correct that." When Hallas asked, low, "And Linnea?" — her gaze softened. "Sleeping. Satisfied." She stepped forward, the sheet slipping lower. "But the village gave *us* this night. All of us. It would be a shame to waste it." Damrod's rare, dangerous heat returned to his gaze. "You're reckless, Celenneth." — "And you're too careful," she said, stopping before him, brushing her fingers along his jaw. "Let me balance the scales."

The sheet fell, and his hand caught her wrist and pulled her down into his lap, the kiss anything but tentative — demanding, claiming. She arched into him, and then broke it to turn her head toward Hallas, still by the window, his breath quickened despite his composure. "Hallas. You're not going to stand there all night." His jaw clenched, a flush at his neck betraying him, and after a long pause he crossed the room and stopped before her, and when she reached for him and murmured *this is for you, too,* he cupped her jaw with a gentleness that belied his strength and kissed her, his restraint splintering as she pressed close.

She had barely stilled before she pushed up on her elbows, breathless and wicked. "Spend yourselves within me," she said, the words landing heavy. "As many times as your bodies allow. I'll have all of it." Damrod's dark eyes narrowed, a smirk curling. "You've the appetite of a queen, Celenneth. It may break us — but you'll have it." Hallas, quiet, pressed his hands to her hips. "You're insatiable." — "Then match me."

She pulled Hallas down and kissed him deep, and behind her Damrod knelt and lifted her hips and sank into her from behind with slow, unrelenting precision that pulled a sharp gasp from her. As he set his pace, Hallas rose over her, and she opened for him and took him in her mouth, and for a long while there was only that — Damrod driving into her from behind, Hallas heavy on her tongue, her whole body strung between them — until Damrod's grip bruised at her hips and he spilled into her with a groan, and she shattered around him a breath later, muffled against Hallas.

She would not stop there. The world was soft and distant when she slipped back into Linnea's room, spent and trembling, still slick and warm with the men's shared claim. Linnea stirred as she settled in beside her, taking in the sight of her — disheveled, exhausted, utterly claimed — and in her dark eyes there was no jealousy, only something fierce and possessive, a hunger born of love and trust. Celenneth sank into the mattress and sleep took her almost at once, barely registering the press of Linnea's lips at her temple.

She woke to something warm and wet dragging slow against her inner thigh. Lifting her head, she found Linnea between her legs, dark hair spilled over her trembling thighs, her tongue working with patient, reverent care to clean away every trace the others had left. "You're a mess," Linnea murmured against her skin. "*My* mess." She licked a slow path up the length of her, tasting where the others had been, laying claim of her own. "You're mine," she whispered, low and fierce. "No matter what they gave you tonight — you belong to me." Celenneth's body arched despite its exhaustion, faint aftershocks rolling through her. "I'm yours," she gasped. "Always yours." Linnea sealed it with one last indulgent stroke and a kiss to her trembling thigh, and crawled up to cup her face. "Sleep now," she whispered against her lips. "You're safe." And Celenneth slipped back into sleep, body aching, heart full.

Morning light streamed into the inn's common room, the air thick with fresh bread and roasted meat. Celenneth emerged first, washed but still bruised faintly at neck and wrists, a rare softness of deep satisfaction on her; Linnea followed with a knowing smirk, and Damrod and Hallas last, their usual stoic masks in place but their shoulders eased. The innkeeper bustled over beaming. "Well now! Hungry, are you? After the *busy* night you all had, it's no wonder." She leaned in, loud enough for the nearest tables. "Such enthusiasm — we haven't heard such lively devotion from our guest rooms in some time. Good to see newlyweds so dedicated to their unions." A villager raised his mug: "Sounded like a festival of its own." Laughter rippled round the room.

Damrod cleared his throat, a faint color at his neck. "We take our commitments seriously," he said, perfectly dry. Celenneth met the innkeeper's amused eye. "It's important to honor traditions. After all — we were told it was expected." Hallas inclined his head gravely. "We trust expectations were met." And Linnea, biting back a laugh, murmured for the four of them alone, "We're legends now." Celenneth chuckled. "Let them talk." And for once they let themselves linger over a hearty breakfast, the spring sun on the fields outside, stitching the memory of that place into something they would carry with them.
