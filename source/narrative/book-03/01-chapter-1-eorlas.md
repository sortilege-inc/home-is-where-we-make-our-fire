# Chapter 1: Eorlas

*Úrimë 2, 2965*

They set out under a cool morning sun with the dew still bright on the grass, and the land rose gently ahead of them into rolling hills stood about with ancient trees. Nimroch went steady, his pale coat gleaming, and for a while the road was kind — birdsong, an unmarked way that asked Celenneth's sense of direction, a narrowing of the path where the slopes went to loose stone and bramble and she dismounted to lead the horse around the worst of it, losing little time. It was the sort of travelling she liked, and she had a companion now who was learning to like it too; Linnea rode easier every day, her eye going to the land the way Celenneth's did, picking up the habit of watchfulness without being taught it.

In the afternoon they crested a hill and saw a figure far off — a lone traveller in a patchwork cloak, walking with a weary, steady gait, a staff tapping out his pace. Linnea glanced at Celenneth, curious and wary both. But the traveller saw them and raised a hand in plain greeting, and when they came together he proved an old man with sharp eyes and a kind mouth, who named himself Eorlas, a wandering herbalist out after rare plants for his healing salves.

They slowed to his pace and fell to talking, and there was more to his wandering than herbs. He was bound for Dale with a message — the villages along the forest's edge had suffered raid on raid from goblins, and were desperate for supplies and allies, and he hoped the folk of Dale might answer. When they stopped by a shaded brook he unwrapped a bundle of dried herbs and showed Linnea how to know the useful ones, and his knowledge of the country ran deep: bogs in the north-east that swallowed the careless, ruins where the dead did not rest, and — this caught Celenneth's ear — a rumour of an orc camp near the south-western eaves of Mirkwood.

She pressed him on the orcs, for it fit too well with what Radagast had warned of. He had seen their smoke, he said, and heard foul chanting on his night-walks after herbs; frail as he looked, there was a sharp and steady competence in him, and Celenneth marked it. It was Linnea, taken with his herb-lore, who asked if he would travel with them a while — and then looked to Celenneth, half-afraid she had overstepped. But Celenneth only nodded, and Eorlas said, "I accept," and it was done.[^r-eorlas]

Before they went on, the old man pressed a sprig into Celenneth's hand — narrow leaves, a clean sharp scent. "Take this. Athelas — kingsfoil, some call it. No common weed. Bruised and steeped in boiling water, the very smell of it can lift the heaviness of despair from a heart the Shadow has touched." She thanked him and stowed it careful, and thought of the dreams, and of the cold the deep and the road had left in her, and was glad of it.[^t-athelas]

As they walked he went on teaching — Elvenstar Blossom and how to harvest it, a thin spiky leaf that would dull pain if handled right and sting worse than a wasp if not — and handed Linnea a small satchel to gather samples under his eye. Linnea drank it in, her face alight, and Celenneth watched her and saw what it did for her: with every leaf named, every small competence gained, she was becoming a contributor to this road and not only a passenger on it, and she knew it, and it straightened her back.

The hills gave way to denser wood, the air cooling, the smell of moss coming up around them. Eorlas crouched over broken earth. "Boar," he muttered, reading the deep prints. "We'd best keep clear — they're ill-tempered this time of year." By dusk they came to the ruined watchtower he had spoken of, crumbled and overgrown but sound enough to shelter in, its stones still holding the day's warmth, and they laid their bedrolls in its lee while Eorlas coaxed a fragrant tea from the day's gathering.

Around the fire he spoke of his hopes for Dale. "There's strength in that people. But they must see what's at stake. Let the Shadow get a foothold in the south, and it won't stop there."

Celenneth nodded into the flames. "Dale must be warned. But Radagast spoke of more than goblins. Something greater's stirring."

The camp settled, the fire fell to embers, and Eorlas sprawled snoring gently on his bedroll while the wood rustled and the stars threw their faint silver over the broken stones. And Linnea moved quietly closer to where Celenneth sat working her whetstone along the blade, and knelt, and hesitated — and then, with a shy downward glance, leaned in and stole a kiss, brief and soft, barely a brush of lips. Celenneth's surprise melted to warmth, and her hand rose to Linnea's cheek.

"I'll take first watch," Linnea whispered, quiet but resolved. "You've carried so much already. Rest. I'll wake you at the first whisper of trouble."

The old instinct pulled at Celenneth — to keep the watch herself, as she always had — but she looked at the woman's steady face and let it go, and nodded. "Wake me if you hear even that much," she said, her eyes staying on Linnea a moment longer than they needed to. Linnea smiled, sat straighter for the trust of it, and took her place at the edge of the ruins; and she watched the ranger drift down into a sleep she had not had to guard alone, and the forest hummed its quiet night around them both.

[^t-athelas]: **Athelas (kingsfoil)** — a gift from Eorlas; bruised and steeped, its scent lifts despair and the heaviness of Shadow. A ranger's remedy against the dark.
