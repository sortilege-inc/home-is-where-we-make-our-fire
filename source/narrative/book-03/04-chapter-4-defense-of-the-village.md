# Chapter 4: Defense of the Village

*Úrimë 5, 2965*

Smoke reached them before the roofs did — not the black smoke of burning but the thin blue thread of cook-fires, ordinary and unknowing. They crested the last rise and there it lay: a scatter of thatched cottages among fields going gold, folk bent to the day's work, a boy driving geese, none of them looking north to the wood that meant to come for them.

Celenneth rode straight into the middle of it, and their urgency turned every head. A stout man came forward with a hoe on his shoulder and a frown already set. "What's this about, then?"

She swung down. "You're in danger. Orcs are coming — a raiding band, out of the forest. They'll be on you before the night's old."

The murmur ran through the gathering crowd like wind through the wheat. The man's frown deepened, but under it she saw the fear he was too proud to show first. "Orcs. Here. We've had the rumors, same as everyone. They've never come so far."

"They are coming," Linnea said, and she said it from the saddle, level and certain, and the certainty in a stranger's voice did more than argument. "Barricade your homes. Arm whoever can hold a weapon. There isn't much time."

The man — Aldric, he gave his name — took it in, and to his credit he did not waste breath on more doubting. "We've a few old blades and some hunting bows. Not much. But we'll not stand and be dragged off. Tell us what to do."

There was no time to send for help; there was only the making the most of what stood in front of her. Celenneth read the village the way she read ground — the little square at its heart, the gaps between the cottages, the low hedgerow at the field's edge that a charge would have to cross. Then she began to give orders, and found the people willing to take them.

"Everything with an edge or a point," she told them. "Axes, pitchforks, scythes, knives. Anything. Pile the carts and the barrels into the gaps between the houses — close every lane but the ones we choose to leave open." She set a party of the able-bodied to the hedgerow to scrape shallow trenches behind it and seat sharpened stakes in them, angled outward. "It won't stop them. It'll break their run, and a broken run is a target."

She led another band out along the likely approaches and showed them how to hide a snare in the leaf-litter, how to bend and lash a sapling so a tripped line would whip it across a shin at the run. The work was hard and hurried and imperfect, and it was enough to give the villagers something to do with their hands besides shake.

Linnea worked among the houses — nailing shutters fast, bracing doors, and more than that: kneeling to a frightened child, steadying a woman whose hands would not stop, turning panic into a task the way Celenneth turned it into a trench. By the time Aldric came back with the village's armory — two rusted swords, a handful of hunting bows, a quiver of mismatched shafts — the place had the look of somewhere that had decided to live.

Celenneth turned the weapons over and did not pretend they were more than they were. "Not much," she said. "It'll do." Overhead the sun went down red behind the trees, and the last light drained off the fields, and the waiting began.

⁂

Linnea took a bow up onto the roof of a sturdy cottage that overlooked the square — her own choosing; the height would give her the whole approach and keep her clear of the crush. Before she climbed, Celenneth caught her arm.

"You don't have to stand this," she said, quiet, only for her. "Take Nimroch. Ride for Dale, get a start on the road."

Linnea's eyes came round to hers, and there was no give in them at all. "No. These people need every hand. I'm staying." A breath, and then, gentler: "I can do this. You showed me how."

Celenneth held her gaze a moment, and let her go. "Then use what you learned. High and steady. Loose on my word, not before."

The village stood to its makeshift barricades, and the dark thickened, and every crack of a settling branch drew a flinch down the line. Then the quiet broke — a roar out of the treeline, and another, and the crash of heavy bodies coming through brush that did not slow them. The orcs came out of the black at a run, hulking and foul, crude iron catching the torchlight, and a moan of terror went along the barricade and a few began to give back.

"*Hold!*" Celenneth's shout nailed them in place. "Wait for the word!"

She let the charge reach the hedgerow. The first rank fouled on the stakes and the trenches, the run breaking exactly as she had meant it to, bodies pitching and snarling in the tangle — and into that she flung her arm down and cried, "*Loose!*"

Arrows went into the mass, ragged but plentiful, and from the roof Linnea's shafts came down among them with a marksman's placing, seeking the ones who found the gaps. The traps took their toll; the stakes took more; the villagers fought with the wild courage of people with nothing behind them to fall back to. But the line was thin, and it began to gape.[^r-defense]

An orc broke clean through and made for the square, where a boy stood rooted with a pitchfork he had forgotten he held, watching death raise a rusted axe over him. Celenneth was too far and knew it. But a shaft took the orc through the chest and staggered it a half-step — Linnea's, loosed without time to think or aim, driven true on nothing but the refusal to watch the child die — and in that bought half-second Celenneth was there, and her sword ended it. The boy scrambled back sobbing into the dark between the houses, alive. She spared one look up at the roof; Linnea was already nocking the next, white-faced and steady, and Celenneth gave her a single hard nod and turned back to the fight.[^r-boy]

The barricades held, mostly, until the last of them came — a great orc in scavenged plate, a spiked mace in its fist, and it did not go around the carts but through them, scattering staves and men. The villagers fell back from it. Celenneth stepped into the space they left and met it there.

It was strength against speed, and she had no strength to spare it. Each fall of the mace she took not on the blade but beside it, turning aside and letting the weight carry past, and it drove craters where she had stood. The village rallied at her back, keeping the lesser orcs off her flanks so she could give the brute her whole attention. She waited out its rhythm — the heavy overhand, the recovery, the half-beat when its guard hung open — and on the third she was not where the mace fell. She stepped inside the swing and put her sword deep into its side, under the plate, and rode the blade home as it staggered. The champion went down like a felled tree, and the ground shook with it, and a ragged cheer broke from the defenders that was half disbelief.[^r-champion]

It turned the night. The orcs that were left saw their strongest dead and their raid gone to ruin, and their nerve went with it; they broke and streamed back into the trees. The villagers, spent and bloodied and short of the ones they had lost, did not chase them. They came together in the square instead, staring at the dark where the enemy had gone, not yet believing they were the ones still standing.

It was Linnea who gave them the belief. She came down off the roof and stood among them with her bow lowered, and lifted her voice — steady, warm, carrying. "You held," she told them. "You stood on your own ground and you held it, for your homes and for each other. The Shadow tested this place tonight, and this place threw it back." She swept a hand at the broken barricades, the fallen enemy. "It will think longer, next time, before it comes to your door."

The cheer that answered began small and grew until it filled the square, and the villagers were gripping one another's shoulders, laughing and weeping at once, the terror of the night burning off into something like pride. From the square's edge Celenneth watched, and let the faint smile come. She could have marshaled them and rationed them and set their watch; she could not have given them that. Linnea had — had handed them back their courage as a thing they had earned and could keep.

⁂

They were given a room for the night, modest and clean, in one of the cottages whose door they had helped brace. The aches came on once the danger let go of her, and sleep came close behind, but Celenneth lay a while in the dark with Linnea beside her, both too worn to move and neither quite gone.

"I never thought I'd stand in something like that," Linnea said against her shoulder, low. "But you made it seem a thing that could be done." A pause. "Do you think it mattered? Truly? Will they come back?"

Celenneth found her hand under the blanket and folded it in her own. "I don't know if they'll come back," she said. "I think they'll count the cost first, now, which they didn't before. But that isn't the thing that matters." She turned her head to where the pale shape of Linnea's face was just visible. "You gave those people the memory of a night they won. They'll hold to that longer than any wall we built them. That was yours. I couldn't have done it."

Linnea was quiet a moment, and then Celenneth felt her settle closer, the last of the day's fear going out of her at last, and neither of them said anything more, and the dark took them both.
