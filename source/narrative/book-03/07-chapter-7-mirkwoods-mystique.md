# Chapter 7: Mirkwood's Mystique

*Úrimë 8, 2965*

The third day opened under the same lightless canopy, and Linnea was quiet with it — keeping to Nimroch's flank, her eyes going to Celenneth more often than to the path, the night's ordeal still on her. Eorlas hummed some old thread of a tune under his breath, low, as if to hold the silence off by an arm's length. They came into a tangled stretch where roots heaved out of the ground like the fingers of something buried and the branches met so thick overhead that what light there was seemed to come up from the moss rather than down from the sky.

Celenneth stopped. Something in the stillness had changed — an unnatural held-breath quality, the wood gone quiet the way a room goes quiet when someone in it means you harm.

"Careful," she said. "The ground ahead isn't right."

Eorlas knelt and laid a hand flat to the earth. "It's been worked," he murmured. "Disturbed and covered over. Tread soft."

The proof came almost at once. The soil shifted under Linnea's boot with a faint creak, and she threw herself back as a patch of it fell away into a pit — deep, lined at the bottom with jagged roots sharpened to stakes, and cunningly hidden under a lid of turf and leaves. She landed hard on her seat at the edge of it, white, unhurt.

Celenneth's eyes narrowed at the neat cruelty of the thing. "Orcs, most like," she said. "Or Elves, hoping to catch them at it." She read the trap and the ground around it, and led them on at a slower, deliberate pace, Eorlas tapping ahead of every step with his staff. A little farther, threaded between two great trunks, she found the next of it — a tripwire strung at shin height, and above it, cradled in the branches, a crude weight of sharpened boughs waiting to fall. She crouched and worked the wire slack with steady fingers until the mechanism hung dead.[^r-traps]

"That settles it," she said, rising. "Orcs. And not far off."

By evening they had picked their way clear of the whole cunning field of it, though the tension of the day would not quite let go. They camped in a small clearing walled about with dense brush that gave at least the feel of cover. The fire had barely caught before Linnea spoke into it.

"They were ready for someone," she said. "Was it us? Or is it anyone who comes this way?"

Eorlas shook his head slowly. "Orcs don't dig pits for sport. Either they're expecting travelers on this road — or they're guarding something they'd rather no traveler found."

*Úrimë 9, 2965*

The next day's shadows pressed close from the first step. Linnea had some of her steadiness back, riding closer to alert now than to fear, and Eorlas returned to his quiet reading of the wood — a print here, a bent stem there, the small tracks of what little still lived in this part of the forest. Then the air changed. It went unnaturally still, and a faint reek of sulphur rode under the smell of rot, and the trees around them had grown into shapes that hurt to look at, their limbs wrenched toward the dim light overhead like hands clawing upward.

Celenneth reined in. Something shimmered in the air ahead — a wrongness, a distortion, as of heat over a road, where no heat was.

"Wait," she said, a hand raised. "There's something here."

Eorlas came up beside her and looked long at the path, and his face closed like a door. "There's a working here. Old, and foul." He went ahead a few careful paces and came back grim. "A ward — a snare of sorcery, laid to muddle or to harm whoever crosses it unknowing."

Celenneth reached into her pack and drew out the pendant — the old eye-graven thing she had carried since the barrow-country, its metal cool and yet holding a faint warmth against her palm, and under the warmth a malevolence she had never quite trusted. She had wondered, more than once, whether a made thing of the dark might not answer the dark. She stepped toward the ward with it, tracing the sick shimmer in the air, and the pendant woke in her hand, a low glow rising off it to show the runes cut into the ground beneath the leaf-mould.

"A ward, right enough," she said, low. "Meant to turn a mind, or break it."

She knelt at the edge of the runes, the pendant held close over them, and spoke words of warding she had learned long ago and half forgotten, the pendant taking her intent and lending it a weight it should not have had. The dark work came apart slowly, the runes flickering and going dim like coals raked out, until with a last small gesture the whole of it guttered and died, leaving only a smell of scorched earth.[^r-ward]

Eorlas stepped onto the cleared ground and studied it, then studied her. "Well done, Ranger," he said, and there was concern threaded through the respect. "But that pendant is no common trinket. It carries the strength of the one who made it, and that one stood deep in shadow. Be wary what you lean on."

Celenneth slid it back into her pack. "It earned its keep today." She looked ahead into the dark. "Whoever laid this may be near. We move."

*Úrimë 10, 2965*

On the fourth day, for the first time, the wood relented. The oppressive dark thinned as they went; the mist that had hung about them lifted; and a patch of moss shone ahead, vivid green, faintly luminous in the filtered light. The very air came lighter to the lungs, cleaner, as though the forest had drawn one honest breath.

Ahead, an ancient tree stood alone, its bark furred with a glowing lichen that cast a soft gold light over the ground, and at its foot lay a small pool of water so clear it seemed to hold light of its own. The warmth of the place reached them before they came near it — not heat, but the warmth of an old kindness, a corner the Shadow had somehow never found.

Eorlas went forward with his face open in wonder. "This is a gift," he murmured. "A blessing out of the forest's forgotten years."

The tension eased from all of them at once. Linnea knelt at the pool's rim and let her fingers trail the cool water. "It's beautiful," she whispered. "How can it be here — in all this?"

"Because not everything the Shadow reaches for does it grasp," Eorlas said. He filled his waterskin with slow, reverent movements. "Drink deep. Water from a place like this puts strength back into the body, and into the spirit besides." They drank, and the draught went through Celenneth clean and cold and left her steadier than a night's sleep had for many nights — even the cold weight the dream had lodged behind her ribs seemed, here, to lie a little lighter.[^r-pool]

Eorlas settled at the pool's edge, both hands folded on his staff, and looked into the water as though it were a window backward. "This place is a memory," he said. "A shard of what Mirkwood was, before the Shadow took root — when it was Greenwood the Great, and the light lived in every leaf." And he told them of it: of the tall trees full of song, of the Elves who had walked under them without fear, of a wood that had been all the good the lament in the clearing had mourned. His words were bright with the beauty of it, and yet his voice under them ran sorrowful the whole way, for he told it as a man tells a thing he knows will not come again in his lifetime, and perhaps not in the age of the world.

When he had finished, none of them spoke for a while. The gold light held them, and the pool lay still, and the dark of the greater forest waited beyond the ring of it. Then Celenneth rose and shouldered her pack, because the way forward was the only way through, and the others rose with her; and as they left the clearing she looked back once at the glowing tree, and carried the small light of it out with her into the shadow, the way one carries a candle from a warm room into a cold hall — knowing it will not last the passage, and glad of it all the same.
