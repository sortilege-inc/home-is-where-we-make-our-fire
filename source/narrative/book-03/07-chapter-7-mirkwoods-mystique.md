# Chapter 7: Mirkwood’s Mystique

*Book 3*

*Úrimë 8, 2965*

The third day of travel began under Mirkwood's oppressive canopy. Linnea was quieter than usual, staying close to Nimroch and glancing at Celenneth occasionally.  Eorlas hummed softly, an old tune that seemed to keep the silence at bay.

They moved through a tangled stretch of the forest, twisted roots rising from the ground like gnarled fingers, the dense canopy blocking nearly all light.

Celenneth halted abruptly. Something felt wrong—an unnatural stillness filled the air, as though the forest were holding its breath.

"Be careful," she said quietly. "The ground ahead doesn’t feel right."

Eorlas knelt, pressing a hand to the earth. "The ground has been disturbed. Tread cautiously."

The danger revealed itself almost immediately. The ground shifted slightly under Linnea’s step, and a faint creaking sound filled the air. Before anyone could react, a patch of earth collapsed, revealing a hidden pit lined with jagged roots and sharpened stakes. Linnea stumbled back just in time, narrowly avoiding the trap.

The pit was deep and cleverly hidden. Celenneth's eyes narrowed as she examined the edges. "Orcs, perhaps," she said grimly. "Or possibly elves, looking to catch them."

Nimroch snorted softly, sensing the tension in his companions, but followed Celenneth’s lead as they moved forward at a slower, deliberate pace. Eorlas tapped the ground ahead with his staff, testing for signs of instability before stepping forward.

The oppressive silence of Mirkwood deepened as they proceeded. A faint creak echoed from the ground as they navigated a narrow path between two towering trees. Celenneth halted again, motioning for Linnea and Eorlas to stop. She crouched low, spotting another subtle intrusion—a hidden tripwire stretched across the path.

She carefully disabled the trap, her movements steady and precise. Beneath the wire lay a crude mechanism, likely designed to release a cascade of sharpened branches from the overhanging trees.

"This confirms it," she said quietly, rising to her feet. "Orcs. And they’re nearby."

By evening, they managed to navigate past the series of traps, though the tension remained palpable. They found a small clearing to set up camp, surrounded by dense underbrush that offered some concealment.

As the fire crackled softly, Linnea broke the silence. "They were ready for someone," she said. "But was it us? Or are they waiting for just anybody?"

Eorlas shook his head. "Orcs rarely lay traps without reason. They may be expecting travelers—or guarding something they wish to keep hidden."

*Úrimë 9, 2965*

The morning began with the shadows pressing close under the dense canopy. Linnea had regained some of her confidence, staying close to Nimroch and scanning the forest with a sharper eye. Eorlas returned to his quiet observations, pointing out subtle signs of wildlife or faint trails left by the forest's creatures.

As they navigated a narrow section of the path, the air felt unnaturally still, and there was a faint stench of sulfur. The trees were twisted into strange shapes, their branches clawing at the dim light above.

Celenneth halted Nimroch abruptly, her sharp eyes catching a faint shimmer in the air. "Wait," she said, raising a hand. "There’s something here."

Eorlas knelt beside her and looked ahead at the path. His expression darkened. "There’s magic in this place. Old and foul." He examined the path carefully, moving ahead before returning to report. "A dark ward lies ahead—a trap of sorcery designed to ensnare the unwary."

Celenneth rooted through her bag, and pulled out the strange pendant of the eye, the cool metal radiating faint warmth in her hand. There was a malevolence to it, and she wondered if they might not be some sympathy there. She stepped cautiously toward the ward, her sharp eyes tracing the subtle distortions in the air. The pendant pulsed faintly, its soft glow illuminating intricate runes etched into the ground.

"It is a ward," she murmured, her voice steady. "Intended to disorient or harm."

Eorlas watched as she knelt, holding the pendant close to the runes. Linnea stayed back, her bow ready, her face tense with worry. Celenneth focused, murmuring words of protection she had learned long ago, the pendant amplifying her intent. Slowly, the dark magic unraveled, the runes flickering and fading like dying embers.

With a final gesture, the ward dissipated, leaving only the faint scent of scorched earth.

Eorlas stepped forward, inspecting the cleared ground. "You’ve done well, ranger," he said with quiet concern. "But that pendant is no ordinary trinket. It carries the strength of its maker, and it is shrouded in shadow."

Celenneth nodded, slipping the pendant back into her bag. "It’s proven its worth today," she replied. "But we should move quickly. Whoever set this ward may not be far."

*Úrimë 10, 2965*

Continuing on the path, the oppressive darkness lifted slightly. Mist cleared, revealing a patch of vibrant moss shimmering faintly in the filtered light. The air felt lighter, fresher, as though the forest were offering a reprieve.

Ahead, an ancient tree stood apart, its trunk covered in glowing lichen. The soft, golden light radiated warmth, filling the clearing with a sense of peace. At its base, a small pool of crystal-clear water reflected the light.

Eorlas stepped forward, his face alight with wonder. "This is a gift," he murmured. "An ancient blessing from the forest’s forgotten past."

The group paused, their tension easing as they took in the serene beauty. Linnea knelt by the pool, her hand brushing the cool water. "It’s beautiful," she whispered, awe in her voice. "How can this exist here?"

Eorlas filled his waterskin, his movements reverent. "Drink deeply," he said. "Water from such places restores strength and spirit."

Eorlas knelt by the pool, hands resting on his staff. "This place is a memory," he murmured. "A fragment of what Mirkwood was before the Shadow took hold."

He spoke with quiet reverence, sharing stories of ancient times when the Greenwood had been a place of light and life. His words were joyous but the tone was sorrowful, carrying with it the knowledge of the future.
