# Chapter 12: Dale

*Úrimë 16, 2965*

They stayed in Dale some weeks, and the city gave them a gift they had not known how badly they needed: quiet. There was time to let the road bleed out of their bones, time to sleep without a watch to keep, and — spread across the sturdy table in their lodgings, weighted at the corners with cups — time to bend their whole minds to the orc-map at last.

It became, for both of them, an obsession. Celenneth's years in the wild told in the reading of it. She traced the web of trails that ran out from Dol Guldur and named where they led: to Dale, to Lake-town, even to the doors of Erebor. Some were old and worn smooth by long use; others were fresh, and it was the fresh ones that troubled her. "These are supply lines," she murmured, following one bold stroke with a fingertip. "But this — this one runs too straight to be a supply road. They're moving something along it. Or someone."

Linnea leaned in over the map's southern edge, where a cluster of marks sat apart from the roads. "And these? They aren't trails. But they're deliberate."

"Hidden camps," Celenneth said. "Or holds."

Linnea nodded slowly, drawing her finger along the parchment. "If they mean to come against Dale, they'll gather strength in places like these first. Which means we could use them against the orcs — if we knew where to strike." Night by night the shape of it emerged, and it was a grim shape: the trails converged, and the convergence pointed north, toward Dale and the Mountain. "These aren't roads," Celenneth said at last. "They're staging points. The orcs are massing, and this city is what they're massing for." Linnea's voice was quiet and certain. "Then we stop them." Celenneth rolled the map up with care. "We will."

⁂

Linnea spent the weeks sharpening herself against the day she would need to be sharp. She walked Dale's markets and sat with its herbalists, learning to know the healing plants of this eastern country and to draw tinctures from them for the common hurts, practicing what Eorlas had taught her until his lessons were her own. It was in the market, too, that she found the woman Elenna, and pressed Eorlas's words into her hands — *Farnath is safe; Eorlas still walks; there is a light against the Shadow* — and watched a stranger's face open with relief at a message she herself only half understood.

Under Celenneth's eye she learned other things: how to think past the moment, to read ground for its advantages, to weigh timing and position and the small edges that decided a fight before it began. Bain's council chambers, which they were now free to attend, taught her how a city was held — how walls and gates and the will of ordinary people were marshaled against a siege — and she took it all in with a hunger that made Celenneth quietly proud.

Her horsemanship grew, too. She spent her free hours in the stables with Nimroch, learning the stablemaster's craft, until her seat smoothed and her hands went quiet and she moved with the horse instead of on him. And in the training yards she drew her bow against Dale's own archers and against Celenneth's exacting standard, day on day, until her aim came true even with a heart racing and no time to think — the snap shot that had saved a child on a rooftop no longer luck, but skill.[^r-advance]

⁂

The evenings were theirs. When the city had gone quiet they would find a place apart — under the open sky, or by the hearth in their shared room — and let the weight of everything set itself down for an hour. Linnea hummed the melodies she'd picked up in the markets, and Celenneth listened with the guarded set of her face going soft, and one night, lying back on a rooftop with the stars thick overhead, Linnea rested her head on Celenneth's shoulder.

"Do you ever wonder what it would be," she asked, "to live without any of this? The Shadow, the danger. To stop somewhere, and just — stay?"

Celenneth was slow to answer. Her hand found Linnea's in the dark. "I've never known that kind of life," she said. "I wouldn't know the shape of it." A breath. "But with you, I think I could imagine it. I'm only not sure how long I could hold still before the road called and my feet answered without asking me."

Linnea grew bolder, those weeks, in the showing of what she felt. She brought Celenneth small things — flowers from a market stall, a song offered up to lift her out of a dark mood — and Celenneth, who had never learned to give tenderness in kind, returned it in the quieter grammar she did know: checking Linnea's gear before practice, seeing she was rested, being simply and reliably *there* when the self-doubt crept in. On the hard days she would set a hand on Linnea's shoulder and tell her, plainly, "You've come further than you think."

And for all the shadow beyond the walls, they let a little lightness in. Linnea teased Celenneth for her gravity and worked to coax the rare laugh out of her; Celenneth answered with a dry wit that caught Linnea off guard as often as not. One evening Linnea stole the ranger's cloak while her back was turned and swept it round her own shoulders and struck a scowling, stiff-backed pose. "Well? Do I make a Ranger?"

Celenneth's mouth curved. "You'd need to scowl a great deal harder."

⁂

But the peace of Dale sat heavier on Celenneth the longer it held. She was not made to wait behind walls while a thing gathered in the dark, and at last, with Bain's blessing and a handful of his most trusted scouts, she rode out to put eyes on the rendezvous points the map had marked near Mirkwood and the reach of Dol Guldur. The small party moved quiet through forest and broken rock, Celenneth leading them unseen where they would have blundered on their own, and the king's scouts, who had come out half-doubting a stranger's skill, learned quickly to follow where she set her feet.

The first camp lay in the shadow of a craggy hill — crude tents, a cold fire-pit, the ground beaten and trampled. Celenneth knelt and read the tracks leading east. "Gone a day or two ahead of us," she murmured. At a second site near the wood's edge they found the litter of a hurried leaving: cast-off gear, the acrid ghost of old fires. One of Bain's men turned up a snapped blade in the grass and held it out, grim. "They're rallying," he said. "These aren't raiding parties any longer."

Her unease deepened as the trails drew them south, converging, every line bending back toward the ancient stronghold like water running to a drain. One night, camped in the lee of an abandoned watchtower, she caught movement in the dark — orc scouts, a dozen or more, moving east, their guttural voices carrying across the still air. She and the king's men held to the shadows, silent, unmoving, and let them pass. "They're scouting us as hard as we scout them," she breathed. "We're running short of time."[^r-scout]

After several weeks abroad the party came back to Dale with what they had found, and Celenneth laid the map out again across Bain's table and traced it for him. "They're gathering strength near Dol Guldur," she said. "If we let them, they'll move north in force, and Dale and Erebor will be the first they come for." Bain nodded, his brow heavy. "Then we'll ready our defenses," he said. "But this is larger than one city. We'll need to send word to our allies — and pray they answer."
