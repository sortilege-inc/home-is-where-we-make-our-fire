# Chapter 7: Celenneth Recalls the Shire

*Víressë 22, 2969*

The fire crackled soft, and Linnea leaned back in her chair watching Celenneth where she sat cross-legged on the rug, idly mending the fletching of an arrow. Brynja was long asleep, and the quiet of the night wrapped around them.

"You never talk much of your life before," Linnea said. "The places you've been, the things you've seen. I'd like to know you better."

Celenneth's hands stilled. She glanced up, a faint smile tugging at her. "I don't often think on those days. They feel like another lifetime. But if you want to know—" She set the arrow aside, her gaze going distant. "I spent a little time in the Shire once. A quiet, lovely place — soft hills, green fields. You'd think no trouble could find its way there. But trouble has a way of sneaking in where it isn't wanted."

"What kind of trouble?"

Her eyes glinted with amusement. "The kind with sticky fingers and too much curiosity. It began when I was passing through the Westfarthing, headed north. I didn't mean to stay. But a hobbit farmer — Harbold Took — asked my help. He'd glimpsed someone skulking his fields at night, and things had gone missing. Chickens, mostly. But odd trinkets too — an old brass lantern, a silver pipe, even his wife's best ladle."

Linnea chuckled. "An amateur thief."

"So I thought. But then I found the tracks — not hobbit-sized, nor human. Small, clawed, leading into the woods. Harbold swore it must be a fox. I wasn't so sure." Her voice took the rhythm of a storyteller. "That night I set a trap — nothing cruel, just enough to catch the mischief — and waited in the shadows with my bow ready. Near midnight I saw it. A creature no taller than my knee, with long bony fingers and glowing yellow eyes. It moved like a shadow, silent and quick, and snatched up one of Harbold's prize hens."

Linnea leaned forward. "What was it?"

"I don't know," Celenneth admitted, wonder in it. "I'd never seen its like — no orc, no goblin, nothing out of any tale I knew. When it sprang the trap it let out a screech loud enough to wake the whole Shire, I thought. Harbold came running in his nightcap, waving a pitchfork and hollering about his hens — and the thing tore free and vanished into the woods, leaving the poor man baffled."

"And you let it go?"

"I followed it," Celenneth said, quieter. "The trail led to a hollow ringed with ancient oaks, and a little burrow full of shiny things — glass, spoons, a few coins. And there in the middle of it sat the creature, cradling Harbold's hen like a treasure."

"What did you do?"

"I watched. It didn't seem evil — only odd. Lonely, perhaps. I left the hen for Harbold to find, and led the creature's trail far from the Shire before it could make more trouble." She smiled. "The Shire has a way of surprising you. Even the smallest corner of Middle-earth can hold a mystery."

Linnea studied her, a small smile playing on her lips. "You have the strangest luck."

Celenneth laughed, warm and rich. "That I do." Then her smile softened, and she reached for Linnea's hand. "But I'm grateful for the luck that brought me here. To you."
