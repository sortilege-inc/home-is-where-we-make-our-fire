# Chapter 7: Celenneth Recalls the Shire

*Book 8*

*Víressë,  2969*

The fire crackled softly, casting warm light across the room. Linnea leaned back in her chair, her gaze fixed on Celenneth as she sat cross-legged on the rug, idly mending the fletching of one of her arrows. Brynja was long asleep, and the quiet of the night wrapped around them like a comforting cloak.

“You never talk much about your life before,” Linnea said suddenly, her voice soft but curious. “The things you’ve seen, the places you’ve been. I’d like to know more. To know you better.”

Celenneth paused, her hands stilling as she considered the request. She glanced up, a faint smile tugging at her lips. “I don’t often think about those days. They feel like another lifetime. But if you want to know…” She set the arrow aside, her gaze growing distant as she began.

“I spent a little time in the Shire once. A quiet, lovely place, full of soft hills and green fields. You’d think no trouble could find its way there, but trouble has a way of sneaking in where it’s not wanted.”

Linnea tilted her head, intrigued. “What kind of trouble?”

Celenneth’s smile widened, her eyes glinting with amusement. “The kind with sticky fingers and too much curiosity. It started when I was passing through the Westfarthing, on my way north. I wasn’t planning to stay, but a hobbit farmer—Harbold Took—asked for my help. He’d caught sight of someone skulking around his fields at night, and things had been going missing. Chickens, mostly, but also odd trinkets—an old brass lantern, a silver pipe, even his wife’s best ladle.”

Linnea chuckled, her arms resting on the edge of her chair. “Sounds like an amateur thief.”

“That’s what I thought,” Celenneth admitted. “But then I found the tracks. They weren’t hobbit-sized, or even human. They were small, clawed, and led into the woods. Harbold insisted it must be a fox, but I wasn’t so sure.”

Her voice softened, taking on the rhythm of a storyteller. “That night, I set a trap—nothing cruel, just enough to catch whatever was causing the mischief. I waited in the shadows, my bow at the ready, and near midnight, I saw it. A creature, no taller than my knee, with long, bony fingers and glowing yellow eyes. It moved like a shadow, silent and quick, snatching up one of Harbold’s prize hens.”

Linnea leaned forward, her brows knitting. “What was it?”

“I don’t know,” Celenneth said, her tone tinged with wonder. “I’d never seen its like before. It wasn’t an orc or a goblin, nor anything I’d heard of in tales. When it triggered the trap, it let out the most ear-piercing screech, loud enough to wake the whole Shire, I thought.”

She laughed, shaking her head at the memory. “Harbold came running out in his nightcap, waving a pitchfork and shouting about his hens. But the creature broke free of the trap and vanished into the woods, leaving the poor man baffled.”

Linnea frowned slightly, her curiosity piqued. “And you let it go?”

“I followed it,” Celenneth said, her voice growing quieter. “The trail led me deep into a hollow surrounded by ancient oaks. There, I found a small burrow filled with shiny things—bits of glass, spoons, even a few coins. And sitting in the middle of it all was the creature, cradling Harbold’s hen like it was a treasure.”

“What did you do?” Linnea asked.

“I watched,” Celenneth admitted. “It didn’t seem evil, just… odd. Lonely, perhaps. I left the hen for Harbold to find and took the creature’s trail far from the Shire before it could cause more trouble.”

Linnea studied her for a long moment, a small smile playing on her lips. “You have the strangest luck.”

Celenneth laughed, the sound warm and rich. “That I do. But the Shire has a way of surprising you. Even the smallest corner of Middle-earth can hold mysteries.”

Linnea nodded thoughtfully, her gaze softening. “I’m glad it held you for a time. It made you the person you are.”

Celenneth’s smile turned tender as she reached for Linnea’s hand. “And I’m grateful it brought me here, to you.”
