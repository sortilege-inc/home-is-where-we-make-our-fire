# Chapter 2: The Glory of Rivenell

*Book 8*

*Súlimë 20, 2969*

Linnea stood beneath one of Rivendell’s great arches, her breath catching as she took in the breathtaking view. The soft glow of twilight bathed the valley in hues of gold and silver, the cascading waterfalls shimmering like threads of starlight. Brynja, perched on her hip, wriggled with excitement, her small hands reaching out toward the dancing lights of the lanterns hanging from the trees, their warm glow blending seamlessly with the ethereal beauty of the elven haven.

Celenneth, standing a few paces away, tilted her head back to gaze at the towering spires and intricate carvings of Rivendell’s architecture. “I love it’s  otherworldliness,” she murmured, her normally composed voice tinged with awe.

Linnea nodded, her voice hushed. “It feels like stepping into a song. So different from the Woodland Elves—so open, so full of light.”

She thought of the Woodland Realm, with its dark and majestic halls hidden beneath a canopy of trees, cloaked in mystery. Rivendell seemed to embrace the stars, its beauty not hidden but celebrated, as if inviting the heavens to share in its splendor.

An elf passed by, his robes flowing like water, his steps so light they seemed to leave no trace. He offered a gentle smile, his ageless face serene. Linnea returned the nod, suddenly aware of her own travel-worn appearance—her boots dusty, her cloak frayed at the edges.

Brynja giggled as another elf approached, carrying a small silver harp. The elf knelt gracefully, playing a soft, lilting melody that drew a delighted squeal from the child. Celenneth chuckled, her shoulders relaxing slightly. “They seem like they belong to a different age entirely,” she mused.

Linnea watched as Brynja reached for the harp, her small fingers brushing the strings and producing a faint, sweet note. “They do,” Linnea replied softly. “Like they exist outside time.”

The elf rose smoothly, bowing slightly before departing, his presence as fleeting as a breeze. Linnea sighed, shifting Brynja in her arms. “It’s beautiful here. Peaceful. But I can’t help feeling... out of place.”

Celenneth placed a steadying hand on Linnea’s shoulder. “We’re travelers here, but they welcome us.”

Linnea looked around again, the awe mingling with a quiet gratitude. “You’re right. It’s more than I expected.”

The Hall of Fire was bathed in the soft glow of Rivendell’s eternal twilight. Elrond sat at the head of a low table, his presence calm yet commanding. Celenneth stood before him, her expression composed despite the weight of the moment. The room was hushed, the crackle of the hearth the only sound save for the faint whispers of the cascading waterfalls outside.

“Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” Elrond began, his voice steady and measured, “you have come far, and for that, I thank you. But I bear tidings that will not be easy to hear.”

Celenneth inclined her head, her auburn hair falling forward slightly as her hands tightened into fists. “Speak plainly, Lord Elrond.”

Elrond’s gaze softened, though his tone carried the gravity of his words. “Balin, son of Fundin, has fallen. He perished in Moria, attempting to reclaim its halls. Darkness rose against him and his kin, and though they fought valiantly, they were overwhelmed.”

The words struck Celenneth with quiet force. She inhaled sharply, her jaw tightening as she processed the loss. “Balin was no fool,” she said, her voice low but steady. “He knew the risks. But he was also determined. He believed Moria could be restored.”

Elrond inclined his head. “He spoke of you with trust and respect. Gandalf sent you to him for good reason. He valued your wisdom and your strength.”

Celenneth’s throat tightened as memories surfaced—Balin’s laughter, his unyielding spirit, the way he spoke of his dreams for Khazad-dûm. “He was a good friend,” she said quietly. “And a better leader than most gave him credit for.”

An elf stepped forward, bearing a long, cloth-wrapped bundle. Elrond gestured toward it. “There is more. The mithral Balin entrusted to us has been crafted into armor—a gift in his memory and a token of gratitude for your service.”

The cloth was unwrapped, revealing a gleaming breastplate of mithral, its surface etched with swirling designs that caught the light like water under moonlight. Celenneth’s breath caught as she stepped closer.

“It is light yet strong,” Elrond said reverently. “A protection worthy of one who walks in shadow to guard the light.”

Celenneth reached out, her fingers brushing the cool metal. For a moment, the grief in her heart eased, replaced by a fierce determination. “I will wear it,” she said firmly. “And I will honor Balin with every step I take.”

Elrond inclined his head solemnly. “Let it serve you well, Celenneth of the Dúnedain. The road ahead will test you, but you are not alone.”

Celenneth lifted the armor, feeling its strength and the weight of her resolve. “Thank you, Lord Elrond. For this, and for remembering him.”

As she turned to leave, the mithral caught the glow of the firelight, casting faint reflections on the walls. Though her loss lingered, Celenneth felt a renewed strength, the memory of Balin now a shield for the challenges ahead.

The soft murmur of Rivendell’s streams filled the air as Linnea sat beneath an ancient elm, Brynja asleep against her chest. The child’s even breathing matched the rhythmic cadence of the waterfalls, offering a rare moment of stillness. A few steps away, Celenneth stood speaking with one of Elrond’s advisors, her posture confident, her presence magnetic.

Linnea watched her, a realization settling heavily in her chest. Celenneth was more than just a ranger. Here, among the elves who treated her with quiet reverence, Linnea could see it plainly: Celenneth was a protector, someone whose actions rippled far beyond their small family.

When Celenneth returned, Linnea hesitated before speaking. “I didn’t realize until now how much others look to you. How important you are.”

Celenneth’s brows rose slightly, though a faint smile touched her lips. “I do what needs to be done, like anyone else.”

“No,” Linnea said, her voice firm but quiet. “It’s more than that. You carry so much, Celenneth. I see that now, and I’m starting to understand... there will be times we can’t hold you back. Times we’ll have to let you go.”

Celenneth crouched beside her, her expression thoughtful. “You’ve never held me back, Linnea. You’ve given me a reason to come home. And you’ve made me realize something.” She glanced toward the valley, where the stars began to emerge above Rivendell’s spires. “We’re closer to Bree now than we’ve been in years. If we were to visit, now might be the time.”

Linnea blinked, surprised. “Bree? After everything Elrond called you here for?”

Celenneth smiled gently. “The road will always call, but Brynja won’t always be this small. And you...” Her voice softened. “You’ve given me more than a duty. You’ve given me a home. Besides, what I provided Elrond was primarily information; what he needs next, he can summon others for.”

Linnea’s gaze fell to Brynja’s small hand curled against her chest, then back to Celenneth. “If we go back, it’s not just about Brynja. It’s about us. About finding something solid.”

Celenneth nodded, her hand covering Linnea’s. “Then let’s make it solid. Together.”
