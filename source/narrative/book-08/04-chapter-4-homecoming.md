# Chapter 4: Homecoming

*Book 8*

*Tuilérë, 2969*

The wind swept low over the weathered hills surrounding Bree, carrying the sharp scent of damp leaves and distant woodsmoke. Spring had come in full force, painting the rolling fields in vibrant hues. Linnea guided her horse along the familiar, rutted path leading toward the village she hadn’t seen in years. Bree looked much the same—a cluster of stout homes with moss-covered roofs nestled beneath the protective shadow of the Chetwood. But the sight of it twisted something deep in her chest.

Memories lingered like stubborn shadows in the golden haze of early evening: her father’s booming laugh as he returned from the South Road, her cousin Miri chasing chickens through the village square, and the warm smell of her aunt Merla’s herb-laced bread wafting from the open kitchen window.

But those memories were brittle now, cracking under the weight of what had come after—her father’s murder, her capture by bandits, and the endless, brutal days that followed. Bree had remained untouched by those horrors, still peaceful and unchanged, as if the world hadn’t shattered the day she was taken.

Celenneth rode silently at her side, her sharp gaze sweeping the path ahead, ever-watchful despite the village’s apparent calm. The silver-edged embroidery on her green-and-gold Rohan cloak caught the low sun, shimmering faintly in contrast to her dark leather armor. She carried herself with the quiet authority of a ranger who had survived far worse than uneasy glances or whispered suspicions.

As they crested the final hill, Linnea drew her horse to a halt, staring down at Bree’s familiar streets winding toward her uncle’s house. Her heart pounded against her ribs, her fingers tightening around the leather reins until her knuckles ached.

Celenneth reined in her horse beside her, her sharp features softening just a fraction as she studied Linnea’s tense profile. “You’re quiet,” she said gently.

Linnea exhaled slowly, forcing her grip to loosen. “I wasn’t sure how I’d feel coming back.”

“And now?”

Linnea’s gaze lingered on the crooked stone walls marking the village’s edge. She remembered walking along those same paths as a child, her father’s hand resting on her shoulder, steady and strong. “Like I’m chasing ghosts.”

Celenneth nodded slowly, as if she understood far more than Linnea could explain. “We don’t have to stay long,” she offered, though there was no judgment in her tone—only quiet reassurance.

“No.” Linnea squared her shoulders, forcing herself to sit straighter in the saddle. “I need to do this.”

The small stone house stood just as she remembered, its moss-covered roof sagging slightly but still sturdy. Overgrown herbs spilled from the crooked garden beds—rosemary, lavender, creeping thyme—all tangled and half-feral.

The smell of baking bread drifted faintly from the chimney, mingling with the scent of autumn leaves. For a moment, Linnea felt twelve years old again, barefoot and laughing as her father lifted her high above the garden fence, teasing her about her muddy boots.

Linnea swung down from her horse in one fluid motion, landing lightly despite the weight of memory pressing on her shoulders. She hesitated at the old wooden gate, her hand resting against the weathered latch worn smooth by time and use.

Celenneth dismounted with practiced grace, hoisting Brynja down before stepping to Linnea’s side. She said nothing, simply resting her hand lightly on Linnea’s arm—a silent offer of strength.

Linnea let out a slow breath, her fingers tightening briefly over Celenneth’s. “Thank you.”

“This is your home,” Celenneth reminded her quietly.

Linnea nodded, though her throat burned with emotion. “Let’s hope they still think so.”

Before she could second-guess herself, she straightened her shoulders and strode up the worn path, boots crunching over brittle leaves. She knocked firmly on the heavy oak door, the sound echoing like the toll of a long-forgotten bell.

For a heartbeat, there was only silence, stretching taut and breathless. Then came the slow creak of hinges, followed by the scrape of worn leather boots against the wooden floor.

The door opened.

A wiry, graying man stood framed in the doorway, his weathered face etched with lines of hard labor and loss. His sharp eyes widened in disbelief, his breath catching audibly. For a moment, he looked like he might stagger back under the force of memory.

“Linnea?” His voice cracked on the name, hoarse and trembling. “Is it… is it really you?”

“It’s me, Uncle Harvin.” Her voice was steady despite the knot tightening in her chest.

Harvin’s hand braced against the doorframe, as though he feared he might fall. His mouth opened soundlessly for a moment before he turned and bellowed, “Merla! The girl’s come back!”

Footsteps thudded across the old wooden floor, followed by a sharp, disbelieving voice.

“What nonsense are you shouting now, Harvin?”

The air thickened with tension as Linnea’s aunt, Merla, emerged from the narrow hallway, her sharp-featured face lined with both age and unyielding practicality.

Her breath hitched, one hand flying to her mouth as her flour-covered fingers trembled. For a moment, her sharp, no-nonsense exterior cracked, replaced by something raw and fragile—something Linnea hadn’t expected to see.

“Linnea?” Merla whispered, her voice wavering with disbelief.

“It’s me.” Linnea’s throat tightened. “I’m… home.”

For a heartbeat, no one moved. Then Merla surged forward with a strangled cry, wrapping Linnea in a fierce, crushing embrace that smelled of rosemary and smoke and something achingly familiar.

“Stars above,” Merla whispered into her hair. “We thought… we thought you were gone.”

Linnea froze for a moment, stunned by the intensity of the embrace. Slowly, hesitantly, she lifted her arms and returned the hug, feeling the trembling in her aunt’s shoulders.

Merla pushed her back just enough to grip her face between her hands, searching every line and freckle with desperate intensity. “Let me look at you.”

Linnea stood still under the sharp scrutiny, letting her aunt take in every change—every hardened edge carved by time, survival, and war. Whatever Merla saw only deepened her frown.

“You’re too thin,” she snapped, reverting to brisk practicality like a shield. “You’ve not been eating properly.”

Before Linnea could answer, more footsteps pounded down the hall as several younger cousins tumbled into the room, drawn by the commotion. Loran, now broad-shouldered and lean, skidded to a halt, staring as though he’d seen a ghost.

“Linnea?” he whispered, his voice uncertain, disbelieving.

“It’s me.” Linnea offered a small, tentative smile.

Emily, sharper-featured and still bold as ever, shoved past Loran, her dark eyes gleaming with suspicion and something dangerously close to anger. “You’re alive? After all this time?”

“I am,” Linnea said quietly, her voice steady despite the tightness in her chest. “I came back.”

The room fell into a stunned silence. Loran and Emily exchanged a glance, equal parts relief and confusion flashing between them.

“Is that a child” one of the younger children asked, half-hidden behind Merla’s skirts, wide-eyed with curiosity.

Celenneth shifted slightly, protective but calm, her hand resting on Brynja’s back with practiced ease.

Merla’s sharp eyes darted to Brynja, narrowing in confusion. “And who’s this?”

“I’m Brynja!” Her voice was loud and clear, and she beamed up at Merla.

Before Linnea could explain, Emily folded her arms, her voice cutting like steel. “And where have you been, Linnea? Years. No word—nothing. Did you even think us?”

Linnea flinched inwardly but held her ground, her jaw tightening. “I’m sorry. It… wasn’t very feasible.”

Merla’s gaze snapped back to her, fierce and searching. “Is that the truth?”

“It is.” Linnea’s voice was firm, though the memories twisted like a knife in her chest. “This is the first opportunity I’ve had to return.”

Before anyone could press further, Harvin cleared his throat roughly, stepping forward with the same quiet authority he’d used to keep order in his household for decades.

“That’s enough for now,” he said, his voice rough but steady. “Let her breathe.”

He gestured toward the long, worn table near the hearth, where a large pot of stew simmered over the crackling fire.

“Sit,” Harvin ordered gently but firmly. “We’ll eat first. Then we’ll talk.”

Merla hesitated, clearly wanting to press further, but she finally nodded. “You’re right. Food first.”

She turned briskly toward the hearth, already reaching for the ladle.

Taking a slow, measured breath, Linnea guided Brynja from Celenneth’s arms and held her close, drawing strength from the warmth of her daughter’s tiny, steady heartbeat.

She followed her uncle toward the long wooden table, sitting carefully on the familiar, worn bench. Celenneth settled beside her with quiet, watchful grace, her eyes never ceasing their slow, protective sweep of the room.

As Merla ladled stew into cracked wooden bowls, Loran dropped onto the bench across from Linnea, his sharp-eyed gaze still locked on her.

“Where did you go?” he asked softly, though there was nothing gentle in the question.

Linnea met his gaze evenly. “Far.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“No, it’s not.” Her voice remained calm, steady. “But it’s what I have for now.”

Emily dropped into the seat beside Loran, her mouth pressed into a tight, bitter line. “You could’ve written. Something. We thought you were dead.”

Merla returned with bowls of stew, setting them down with more force than necessary. “Well,” she said briskly, her voice trembling just slightly, “you’re here now. That’s what matters.”

But Linnea could see the doubt still lingering in their wary, searching eyes—the questions that hadn’t yet been asked.

And the ones she wasn’t ready to answer.

The fire crackled in the hearth, sending flickering shadows across the worn beams of the cottage. The scent of rosemary and woodsmoke filled the cramped room, familiar yet oppressive.

Linnea sat stiff-backed at the long wooden table, Brynja nestled against her chest, her hands clutching Linnea’s cloak. Across the table, her aunt Merla paced near the hearth, her sharp gaze cutting toward Linnea with every step, like a hawk circling prey.

Harvin remained seated at the head of the table, his weathered hands resting on the worn wood, his expression tight and unreadable. He hadn’t contradicted Merla once—not since Linnea had walked through the door. Loran and Emily sat stiffly on the opposite bench, their faces tense and expectant, as though waiting for Linnea’s fragile composure to crack. The younger children had been excused, but could be heard listening at the door.

Celenneth remained utterly still at Linnea’s side, her leather armor creaking faintly as she shifted just enough to be ready—but not enough to provoke. Her sharp gaze swept the room, missing nothing. She had fought in battles less dangerous than this dinner.

Merla slapped a wooden spoon onto the hearthstone with a sharp crack, breaking the charged silence. “You could’ve sent word,” she snapped, her voice trembling with restrained fury. “All these years, and not a single letter.”

Linnea’s shoulders stiffened. “We couldn’t.”

Merla scoffed. “Couldn’t? Travelers come through Bree all the time.”

“It wasn’t safe.”

“For years?” Emily’s voice rose, sharp-edged and cutting. “You expect us to believe that?”

Linnea met her cousin’s accusing glare without flinching. “Yes.”

Merla crossed her arms, her eyes narrowing. “Where the hells were you?”

Linnea hesitated for a breath, weighing the risk of saying too much—but there was no going back now. “Rivendell. Dale. The Woodland Realm. Rohan.”

Harvin let out a sharp breath, stunned. “Rohan? That’s halfway to Gondor.”

Linnea inclined her head. “Far enough.”

Emily leaned forward, her mouth twisting with disbelief. “You expect us to believe you’ve been wandering with elves and kings?”

Celenneth’s voice cut through the rising tension, cool and measured. “She has. I was there.”

Emily’s sneer deepened. “Convenient.”

“Enough,” Harvin growled, though there was no conviction in his voice.

Merla folded her arms tighter, her mouth pressed into a thin, bloodless line. “So you left… on purpose.”

Linnea’s fingers tightened on Brynja’s blanket. “I didn’t have a choice.”

Merla’s gaze burned with sharp-edged anger. “There’s always a choice.”

The crackling fire filled the stretching silence, the weight of old wounds pressing down like a smothering blanket.

“They ambushed us on the South Road,” Linnea began quietly, her voice roughened by years of forced detachment. “Bandits. We didn’t see them coming.”

Harvin’s breath hitched audibly, his hands trembling.

“They... they killed my father first.” Linnea forced the words past the tightness in her throat. “He tried to fight, but there were too many.”

Merla staggered back a step, one hand flying to her mouth.

“They took me after he fell.” Linnea’s voice turned hollow. “Dragged me into the forest.”

Emily’s expression twisted, disbelief warring with something closer to horror.

“I escaped.” Linnea’s gaze drifted toward Celenneth. “Because of her.”

Merla’s sharp eyes snapped to Celenneth, her expression hardening into suspicion. “And how did she come into this?”

Linnea exhaled slowly. “She found me. Stole me out of their camp, right under their noses.”

“Stealth was the safer option,” Celenneth noted.

The room fell into stunned silence, broken only by the distant wind howling outside the cottage.

After a long pause, Harvin rasped, “And... after that?”

“We ran.” Linnea’s voice trembled. “Through the wilds. Always moving.”

“For years?” Merla hissed.

“We settled for a time in Edoras, in Rohan.” Linnea met her gaze, steel-edged. “But Celenneth had to travel, and Brynja was too small to.”

Merla paced sharply, her breath coming in shallow, angry bursts. “And you never thought to come back?”

Linnea’s voice cracked. “I couldn’t risk it.”

“You could’ve tried,” Emily snapped. “But you didn’t. You wanted this.”

“What I wanted was to live,” Linnea hissed, her breath shaking. “And to keep her safe.”

She held Brynja closer, her protective instincts flaring.

Emily’s lip curled. “And you think dragging a child through the wilds is safe?”

Linnea surged to her feet, her voice trembling with barely restrained fury. “I did what I had to do.”

Emily rose too, her voice venomous. “You wanted to play at being some elvish ranger. You chose this.”

Celenneth slowly stood, her posture calm but commanding. “She chose to survive.”

The fire crackled sharply, filling the sudden silence like the clash of steel on stone.

Emily flinched but didn’t back down. “You don’t belong here.”

Celenneth’s gaze darkened, though she remained still.

Merla stepped forward abruptly, her voice trembling with barely suppressed emotion. “You left. You abandoned us. You—”

Linnea’s voice cracked, raw and desperate. “I saved her.”

Merla’s breath hitched, her gaze darting toward Brynja, who stirred softly in Linnea’s arms.

The room stilled, heavy with unspoken truths.

After a long, suffocating silence, Merla spoke again, her voice softer—but still trembling with purpose.

“Let me... hold her.”

Linnea’s breath caught.

Brynja stirred, her fingers reaching toward Linnea’s cloak, seeking comfort.

“Please.” Merla’s eyes shone with desperate yearning. “She’s family.”

Linnea hesitated, her heart pounding painfully in her chest. Slowly, reluctantly, she stepped forward—keeping her gaze locked on Merla’s trembling hands.

Carefully, she placed Brynja into her aunt’s waiting arms.

Merla’s breath shuddered as she cradled the baby against her chest, her weathered fingers gently brushing Brynja’s dark curls. Tears glistened in her sharp eyes, though her expression remained conflicted—torn between grief, anger... and something far more dangerous.

“She belongs here,” Merla whispered, her voice cracking with fierce emotion. “Not... out there.”

Linnea took a sharp step forward, her breath trembling. “She belongs with me.”

Merla didn’t release her hold, her face tightening with stubborn resolve. “She could have a home, Linnea. A real home. Here.”

Linnea’s voice turned cold as ice. “She has one.”

Merla cradled Brynja against her chest, her calloused hands trembling as she stroked the Brynja’s soft curls. She held the child with fierce, protective intensity—possessive, as if trying to stake a claim.

Linnea’s breath hitched as she watched, torn between yearning and something far sharper—fear. Brynja stirred, letting out a small, curious sound, her hands tugging at Merla’s thick apron strings.

Harvin sat stiffly at the head of the table, his weathered face drawn and pale. Loran watched from the edge of the room, wide-eyed but silent, while Emily stood rigid by the fire, her mouth a tight, thin line.

Celenneth remained at Linnea’s side, calm but unyielding, her sharp eyes locked on Merla’s trembling hands. Ready—but not reckless. Her entire posture radiated defense.

Linnea’s voice broke the charged silence. “She’s our daughter.”

Merla’s gaze snapped upward, her face twisting with disbelief. “Yours?”

“Yes.” Linnea stepped forward, her breath trembling. “We’ve raised her—together. Since she was barely breathing.”

Merla’s brow furrowed sharply. “Raised her? In the wilds?”

“In Edoras.”

Merla held Brynja tighter, her lips trembling. “She belongs in a home, Linnea. Not—” she shot a sharp glare at Celenneth—“out there.”

“She has a home.” Linnea’s voice cracked. “With us.”

Merla’s mouth twisted, but before she could speak, Harvin raised a trembling hand. “How... how did she come to you?”

Linnea exhaled slowly, exchanging a brief glance with Celenneth before speaking. “We found her... near the Anduin River.”

“She was abandoned,” Celenneth added, her tone stripped of emotion. “Barely a week old.”

Merla gasped, horror flashing across her face. “Left to die?”

“Yes.” Linnea’s throat tightened. “We heard her crying... she was wrapped in nothing but an old cloth.”

“Her mother must’ve... must’ve died,” Harvin murmured, his voice rough with grief.

“We never knew.” Celenneth’s gaze turned distant, haunted. “There was no one else.”

Merla staggered back a step, clutching Brynja tighter. Her voice trembled. “And you... kept her?”

Linnea’s voice sharpened. “What should we have done? Left her there?”

“No—of course not!” Merla’s face twisted with emotion. “But you should’ve... brought her here!”

“We couldn’t.” Celenneth’s voice dropped into something colder. “The South Road wasn’t safe, and we were needed in Rohan.”

Merla’s mouth opened—but no words came.

“We fed her, carried her... kept her warm through the dead of winter,” Linnea continued, her voice rough. “We survived.”

Harvin shook his head slowly, still struggling to absorb it all. “You... raised her.”

“Yes.” Linnea’s voice softened, even as her heart ached. “She’s... ours.”

Merla’s hands clenched Brynja’s blanket. “But... she could have a better life.”

Linnea’s shoulders stiffened. “She has a life. A good one.”

Emily scoffed bitterly. “What? Dragged from town to town, hunted by who-knows-what? You call that a life?”

Linnea’s temper snapped. “She’s loved. She’s safe.”

The room crackled with charged silence.

Finally, Harvin cleared his throat roughly, his voice thick with emotion. “You’ve... done right by her, then.”

Linnea blinked, stunned—but before she could speak, Merla cut in sharply.

“She could have more,” she hissed, trembling. “Here. With us.”

Linnea’s voice rose, hoarse but fierce. “She belongs with us.”

Celenneth stepped beside her, her expression fierce with something far deeper than pride—devotion. “We swore oaths.”

Merla flinched, blinking in confusion. “Oaths?”

Linnea lifted her chin. “We swore... a life oath. To each other.”

Harvin’s breath hitched audibly. “You mean... like wedlock?”

Celenneth nodded slowly. “In every way that matters.”

Emily recoiled, stunned. “You’re married?”

“Our lives are bound.” Linnea’s voice trembled. “By choice. By vow.”

Merla stared, speechless, grappling with the realization—but unable to accept it.

Finally, Harvin spoke again, his voice rough but steady. “And now... what happens?”

Linnea hesitated, exchanging a glance with Celenneth before answering.

“We’re... heading north,” she said slowly. “To find... her people.”

Harvin frowned. “Rangers?”

Celenneth nodded quietly. “Yes.”

Merla’s face twisted with disbelief. “You’re leaving?”

Linnea’s shoulders sagged. “We can’t stay.”

Harvin’s mouth opened—but no words came.

Emily’s voice rose, sharp and incredulous. “You’re running again.”

“No.” Linnea’s voice cracked. “We’re searching. For... home.”

Merla’s expression twisted with cold fury. “She should stay here.”

Linnea took a sharp step forward, her eyes blazing. “She can’t.”

“She’s family!” Merla cried desperately. “She belongs—”

“With us!” Linnea shouted. “You don’t get to choose!”

Brynja stirred, letting out a soft whimper as the cottage fell into shocked silence.

Merla’s trembling hands loosened, her face crumpling into something shattered—something broken.

Harvin finally rose, resting a hand on Merla’s shoulder—but she jerked away, her expression still locked in bitter denial.

Linnea’s breath shook as she turned toward Celenneth—toward home.

For a long, aching moment, no one spoke.

And then... the fire crackled softly, filling the shattered silence like a fading heartbeat.

The crackling hearth dimmed as the fire burned lower, sending faint flickers of orange light dancing across the cottage’s weathered beams. Outside, the wind howled through the old apple trees.

Inside, tension coiled tight as a drawn bowstring.

Linnea stood by the worn wooden table, her breath trembling, fists clenched at her sides. Brynja stirred lightly in her arms, murmuring in half-sleep, her small face pressed against Linnea’s cloak. Celenneth remained steady beside her, silent but watchful, her sharp gaze never leaving the strained faces of Merla, Harvin, Emily, and Loran.

Merla’s hands still trembled where they’d been forced to let go of Brynja moments before, fingers curled as if clutching something already lost. Her sharp-featured face remained rigid, but her dark eyes burned with equal parts grief, anger, and yearning.

“You can’t just leave,” Merla hissed, her voice trembling with desperation. “You can’t take her.”

Emily cut in bitterly, her dark eyes gleaming with disdain. “Or at least leave Brynja. If you’re so determined to run again—”

Celenneth’s voice rang out, clear and sharp as steel drawn from its sheath.

“No.”

The cottage fell into stunned silence.

Celenneth stepped forward with quiet, commanding grace, her posture perfectly still yet radiating presence. Her voice remained calm but deadly certain.

“Brynja belongs with Linnea,” she said coldly, her piercing gaze locking onto Merla’s trembling form. “With us.”

Merla staggered back a half-step, stunned into silence.

“You think she’d be safe here?” Celenneth continued, her voice low but fierce. “Where people whisper behind your back? Where she’d grow up knowing she’s only tolerated?”

“That’s not—” Merla started, but Celenneth cut her off.

“You’d try to raise her without Linnea? Without me?” Her voice softened into something sharp and wounded. “As if she hasn’t already chosen us.”

Brynja stirred at the sound of Celenneth’s voice, her tiny hand reaching toward her without waking. Celenneth brushed a gentle hand over the girls dark curls, her expression softening just enough to show the fierce, protective love she could no longer hide.

“We are her family,” Celenneth said quietly. “We made her family. With love, with choice.”

Merla’s breath hitched audibly, her face twisting with grief. “But... you’re leaving.”

Celenneth nodded slowly. “Yes.”

Merla’s hands curled into fists, her voice rising sharply. “Then stay. At least stay until autumn!”

Harvin nodded stiffly, adding with gruff urgency, “You don’t need to rush north—not now.”

Linnea hesitated, her breath faltering. For a heartbeat, the thought of staying—of resting—burned like a cruel temptation.

But Celenneth’s steady hand brushed hers, grounding her. Reminding her of the truth.

“We can’t,” Linnea rasped. “We’re already late.”

“Late for what?” Emily demanded, her eyes flashing with anger. “For another adventure? For some quest?”

Linnea took a sharp step forward, her voice trembling with fury. “We’re not risking her life—we’re saving it!”

Emily scoffed bitterly. “From us?”

“From a world that doesn’t want us,” Celenneth answered coldly.

Merla flinched, her face pale and stricken. “We... want her.”

“No.” Linnea’s voice cracked with old pain. “You want what you think she should be.”

The room fell into stunned silence, broken only by the faint hiss of the dying fire.

Merla staggered back a step, her breath trembling. “So... you are leaving.”

Linnea’s heart broke all over again, but she nodded slowly. “We have to.”

Emily sneered bitterly. “Then... go.”

Harvin let out a ragged breath, his weathered hands trembling, but he didn’t speak. He couldn’t.

For a long, trembling moment, Linnea stared into her family’s stricken faces, aching with every shattered breath she couldn’t take back.

Then she turned toward the door.

Celenneth followed without a word, her steady hand resting gently on Linnea’s back, guiding her forward.

The cottage door closed behind them with a heavy, final thud.

The cold wind bit sharply as they reached the edge of Bree, the darkened road stretching like a scar toward the northern wilds. The stars shimmered faintly overhead, distant and unmoved by mortal grief.

Linnea staggered to a halt beneath the twisted branches of an old oak, her breath shuddering with suppressed everything.

“Linnea.” Celenneth’s voice was soft—steady.

But Linnea couldn’t hold it in anymore. Her breath broke into harsh, trembling sobs, her knees buckling beneath her. She crumpled onto the frost-covered grass, clutching Brynja against her chest as if the child were the only thing keeping her together.

Celenneth dropped beside her, pulling Linnea into her arms with fierce, protective certainty.

“I—I tried—” Linnea gasped, her words breaking into jagged, ragged cries. “I—I thought—”

“I know.” Celenneth’s voice trembled faintly. “I know.”

Linnea buried her face in Celenneth’s cloak, sobbing with all the grief, all the rage, all the loss she’d held back for years.

Celenneth held her tightly, her hands steady despite the tears shining in her own eyes. “You fought. You loved. You... tried.”

Linnea clung to her, her breath hitching on raw, gasping sobs. “They hate us—”

“They don’t understand,” Celenneth corrected gently, brushing her fingers through Linnea’s tangled hair. “They can’t.”

Linnea shuddered against her, her voice breaking. “I—I’m so tired—”

Celenneth pressed a fierce, trembling kiss to Linnea’s temple, her voice rough with emotion. “Then rest. I’ve got you.”

For a long, endless moment, they stayed there—together. Grieving, whole, unbroken.

Brynja stirred softly between them, warm and safe.

And slowly... slowly, the stars burned brighter above, distant but steadfast, promising that even in the darkest night... home was still theirs to make.

Footsteps crunched softly over frost-crusted leaves, slow and hesitant but growing steadily louder. Celenneth’s sharp gaze snapped toward the darkened path, her hand instinctively resting on the hilt of her belt-knife.

A familiar voice called out, breathless but earnest.

“Wait—please!”

Linnea stiffened, wiping her tear-streaked face with the back of her hand as she pulled Brynja closer.

Emerging from the shadows of the winding path, Loran stumbled toward them, his face pale and drawn, his breath misting in the cold. His boots were caked with mud, his coat half-buttoned as though he’d run after them in haste.

“Wait,” he panted, slowing as he approached. His hands were raised, palms open—a gesture of peace. “Please... don’t go yet.”

Celenneth relaxed slightly but remained close, her sharp eyes still watchful.

Linnea wiped her face again, trying and failing to steady her breath. “What... do you want?”

Loran’s expression crumpled with raw, unspoken regret. “To... apologize.”

Linnea blinked, stunned into silence.

He stepped closer, stopping just short of arm’s reach. “I—I’m sorry,” he rasped, his voice trembling. “For... everything.”

Linnea swallowed hard, her breath still ragged. “It... wasn’t your fault.”

Loran let out a harsh, uneven breath. “I... should’ve said something. Should’ve—done something.”

“You tried,” Celenneth interjected quietly, her voice unexpectedly soft.

His gaze darted toward her, flickering with something like surprise—respect.

“I should’ve tried harder,” he murmured, shoulders slumping. “They were... wrong. About you. About everything.”

Linnea’s breath hitched painfully, tears threatening to spill again. “It... it wouldn’t have changed anything.”

“Maybe not,” Loran admitted, his voice low. “But... I still should’ve.”

A brittle silence stretched between them, broken only by the soft rustling of wind through the frost-covered grass.

Finally, Loran straightened, forcing himself to meet Linnea’s tear-streaked gaze. “Where will you go?”

Linnea exhaled slowly, tightening her hold on Brynja. “North, mostly likely... to find her people.” She nodded toward Celenneth. “To... try.”

Loran nodded faintly, struggling to hold back his own emotions. “You’ll... make it,” he said hoarsely. “You always do.”

His gaze lingered on Brynja, who stirred softly but didn’t wake. “She... she’s lucky.”

Linnea’s voice broke. “We are.”

Loran’s mouth twisted into something between a pained smile and a grimace. “You’ll be... alright. I know you will.”

She nodded slowly, unable to speak past the lump rising in her throat.

He took a small, hesitant step back, as though unwilling to break the fragile moment—but knowing he had to.

“Safe travels,” he whispered. “Stars guide you.”

Then, with one last, lingering glance, he turned and disappeared into the darkened path—back toward the cold, unyielding world he couldn’t change.

Linnea crumpled to her knees again, tears spilling unchecked down her face. “He... he cared.”

Celenneth knelt beside her, pulling her close with fierce, protective tenderness. “Yes.”

Linnea sobbed into her shoulder, trembling with grief and relief, finally allowing herself to feel the crushing weight of everything she’d carried for so long.

Celenneth’s fingers brushed gently through her hair, her breath warm and steady against Linnea’s temple. “You’re not alone.”

“I... know,” Linnea whispered, her voice breaking. “I... know.”

And for the first time in years, she truly believed it.

Much later, after the tears had ebbed and the world stilled, they sat together on a low rise overlooking Bree. The firelight from distant homes flickered against the night, warm and familiar, yet strangely far away.

“I wanted to give you space,” Celenneth murmured, her voice quiet in the stillness. “It was... a lot tonight.”

Linnea let out a soft, almost bitter laugh. “That’s one way to put it.” She drew in a deep breath, her gaze lifting toward the glittering sky. “They’re not who I remembered. Or maybe... maybe I’m not.”

Celenneth waited, her stillness offering silent understanding.

“I always thought of Bree as home,” Linnea continued, her voice softer now. “The place I’d come back to, where people would be glad to see me. But now... now it just feels small. Like I’ve outgrown it. Like I’ve outgrown them.”

Celenneth turned toward her fully, her expression unreadable but patient. “It’s not easy—to feel like the place you called home doesn’t quite fit anymore.”

Linnea nodded, swallowing hard. “They’ll always be family... but they’ll never understand. Not really. To them, I’m still the girl who disappeared.” She gestured vaguely at herself—her travel-worn cloak, her Rohan mare tethered nearby, Brynja asleep in her lap. “Not... this.”

Celenneth rested a hand gently on her shoulder, grounding her. “It’s not a bad thing, Linnea. To grow. To change. To find a new home, even if it’s not the one you expected.”

Linnea searched her face, her voice trembling. “Do you ever feel that way? Like the road is the only place that makes sense?”

A faint, knowing smile tugged at Celenneth’s lips. “Sometimes. But I’ve learned something else, too.” She reached down, brushing her fingers gently against Brynja’s tiny back. “Home isn’t a place. It’s the people you choose. Wherever that is... it’s enough.”

Linnea’s breath hitched, her eyes shimmering with unshed tears. “I’m scared,” she admitted, her voice barely above a whisper. “That one day... I’ll stop feeling like I belong anywhere.”

Celenneth’s gaze softened with quiet certainty. “You’ll always belong. With me. With Brynja. Wherever we go, we’ll make our place.”

Linnea let out a shaky breath, leaning into Celenneth’s warmth, careful not to jostle the sleeping child. “Thank you. For standing up for me tonight. For... everything.”

“I meant every word,” Celenneth replied, her voice steady as stone. “You’ve become someone remarkable, Linnea. Don’t let them make you forget that.”

Silence settled between them, comfortable and strong. The stars stretched endlessly above, steadfast and shining, as the night whispered promises of new beginnings. Linnea felt the weight of doubt lift, replaced by something warmer, brighter.

She had outgrown Bree—but she had grown into something far greater. And with Celenneth by her side, she knew she could face whatever came next. Together, they were home.
