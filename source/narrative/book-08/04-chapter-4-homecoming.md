# Chapter 4: Homecoming

*Víressë 2, 2969*

The wind swept low over the weathered hills around Bree, carrying damp leaves and distant woodsmoke, and spring lay full on the rolling fields as Linnea guided Eorlin along the rutted path toward the village she hadn't seen in years. Bree looked much the same — stout homes with moss-covered roofs under the shadow of the Chetwood — but the sight of it twisted something deep in her chest. Memories rose in the golden evening haze: her father's booming laugh coming up the South Road, her cousin Miri chasing chickens through the square, the warm smell of Aunt Merla's herb-laced bread. But they were brittle memories now, cracking under the weight of what came after — her father murdered, herself taken by bandits, the brutal days that followed. Bree had stayed untouched by all of it, peaceful and unchanged, as though the world had not shattered the day she was dragged from that road.

Celenneth rode silent at her side, her sharp gaze sweeping the calm streets from habit, the silver-edged embroidery of her green-and-gold cloak catching the low sun. At the crest of the last hill Linnea drew rein, staring down toward her uncle's house, her heart slamming, her knuckles white on the reins.

"You're quiet," Celenneth said gently.

Linnea forced her grip to loosen. "I wasn't sure how I'd feel, coming back."

"And now?"

"Like I'm chasing ghosts."

Celenneth nodded slowly, as if she understood more than could be said. "We don't have to stay long."

"No." Linnea squared her shoulders. "I need to do this."

The small stone house stood as she remembered, its moss-covered roof sagging but sound, herbs spilling wild from the crooked garden beds — rosemary, lavender, creeping thyme, all half-feral now. Bread-smell drifted from the chimney, and for a moment Linnea was twelve again, barefoot and laughing as her father lifted her over the garden fence. She swung down, and hesitated at the old gate with her hand on the worn latch. Celenneth dismounted, set Brynja on her feet, and rested a hand lightly on Linnea's arm — a silent offering of strength.

"Thank you," Linnea breathed. "Let's hope they still think of it as home."

She strode up the path and knocked, and the sound echoed like a long-forgotten bell. For a taut heartbeat there was silence — then the creak of hinges, and the door opened, and a wiry, graying man stood framed in it, his weathered face etched with hard years. His eyes went wide, his breath catching as though he might stagger.

"Linnea?" His voice cracked. "Is it — is it really you?"

"It's me, Uncle Harvin."

He braced against the doorframe, then turned and bellowed into the house, "Merla! The girl's come back!" Footsteps thudded, and her aunt emerged, sharp-featured and lined with age and hard practicality — and then her breath hitched and her flour-dusted hand flew to her mouth, and for a moment that brisk exterior cracked into something raw. "Linnea?" she whispered.

"It's me. I'm home."

Merla surged forward with a strangled cry and wrapped her in a fierce, crushing embrace that smelled of rosemary and smoke and something achingly familiar. "Stars above. We thought you were gone." Then she pushed back to grip Linnea's face and search it, and whatever she found only deepened her frown. "You're too thin," she snapped, retreating behind briskness like a shield.

More footsteps, and cousins tumbled into the room — Loran, broad-shouldered and lean now, staring as at a ghost; and Emily, sharper still and bold as ever, shoving past him with something close to anger in her eyes. "You're alive? After all this time?"

"I am. I came back."

A younger child half-hidden behind Merla's skirts pointed. "Is that a child?"

"I'm Brynja!" the girl announced, loud and clear, beaming up at them, and Merla's sharp eyes narrowed on her. "And who's this?"

Before Linnea could answer, Emily folded her arms, her voice cutting. "Where have you been, Linnea? Years. Not a word. Did you even think of us?"

Linnea held her ground. "I'm sorry. It wasn't safe to."

"That's enough for now," Harvin said, rough but steady, with the quiet authority that had kept his household in order for decades. He gestured to the long table by the hearth, where a pot of stew simmered. "Let her breathe. We'll eat first. Then we'll talk."

⁂

But the eating did not soften it. Merla paced by the hearth, her gaze cutting to Linnea with every turn, and the questions came like blows. *You could have sent word. Travelers come through Bree all the time. Where the hells were you.*

"It wasn't safe," Linnea said, and then, because there was no going back, gave them the truth of it. "They ambushed us on the South Road. Bandits — we never saw them coming. They killed my father first. He tried to fight, but there were too many." Harvin's breath hitched; Merla staggered back a step. "They took me after he fell. Dragged me into the forest. I escaped —" her gaze went to Celenneth "— because of her. She stole me out of their camp, right under their noses."

"Stealth was the safer choice," Celenneth said quietly.

The room fell still. "And after?" Harvin rasped.

"We ran. Through the wilds, always moving. We settled a while in Edoras, in Rohan. But Celenneth's road took her away often, and Brynja was too small to travel it."

Merla's eyes snapped to the child. "And who is she to you?"

Linnea drew a breath and exchanged a glance with Celenneth. "She's our daughter. We found her in the wild north — abandoned, barely a week old, wrapped in nothing but an old cloth. We heard her crying. There was no one else. We fed her, carried her, kept her warm through the dead of winter. We raised her. She's ours."

Harvin's voice was rough with grief. "Her mother must have died."

"We never knew," Celenneth said, her gaze gone distant. "There was no one."

Merla clutched at her apron. "And you kept her. In the wilds." Then, softer, her eyes shining with a sudden desperate yearning: "Let me hold her. Please. She's family."

Linnea hesitated, her heart pounding — then stepped forward and set Brynja on her aunt's lap. Merla's breath shuddered as she gathered the girl close, brushing the dark curls, tears standing in her sharp eyes — but her face was torn, and something harder moved under the grief. "She belongs here," she whispered fiercely. "Not out there."

"She belongs with me," Linnea said.

Merla did not loosen her hold. "She could have a real home. Here."

"She has one." Linnea's voice went cold. "With us." And when Harvin, low and stunned, asked how they had come to swear themselves to one another, Celenneth answered plainly. "We swore a life-oath. In every way that matters, we are bound." Emily recoiled; Merla stared, grappling with a thing she could not accept; and Brynja, feeling the tightening arms, began to fuss and reach for Linnea.

"And now?" Harvin asked at last. "What happens?"

"We're going north," Linnea said. "To find her people. Her rangers."

"You're leaving," Merla breathed, and then her face twisted cold. "She should stay here. She's family — she belongs—"

"With us," Linnea said, taking the child back into her arms. "You don't get to choose that."

⁂

It was Celenneth who ended it, when Emily said, bitterly, that if they meant to run again they should at least leave Brynja behind.

"No." The word rang out clear as a drawn blade, and the cottage fell silent. She stepped forward, calm and commanding and terribly certain. "Brynja belongs with Linnea. With us. You'd have her grow up here, tolerated, whispered about behind her back? Raised without her mothers, as though she hadn't already chosen us?" Her voice gentled without losing its edge. "We are her family. We made her family — with love, and with choice." Brynja stirred at the sound of her, reaching without waking, and Celenneth brushed the dark curls, the fierce protectiveness plain on her face at last. "She comes with us."

Merla begged them to stay at least until autumn; Harvin gruffly allowed they needn't rush north. For a heartbeat the thought of resting burned in Linnea like a cruel temptation — and then Celenneth's steady hand brushed hers, and she found the truth again. "We can't," she rasped. "We're already late." And when Emily demanded *late for what — another adventure?* — Linnea's fury broke loose. "We're not risking her life. We're saving it. From a world that doesn't want us." — "We want her," Merla cried, pale and stricken. "No," Linnea said, and the old pain cracked her voice. "You want what you think she should be."

The fire hissed low into the silence. Merla staggered a step. "So you are leaving."

Linnea's heart broke all over again, but she nodded. "We have to."

"Then go," Emily sneered. Harvin let out a ragged breath and said nothing — could not. And Linnea looked once into her family's stricken faces, aching with every word she could not take back, and turned to the door, and Celenneth's steady hand came to rest on her back and guided her out, and the door closed behind them with a heavy, final thud.

⁂

The cold wind bit as they reached the edge of Bree, the dark road stretching north like a scar. Under an old oak Linnea's legs gave out, and the sobs came up out of her at last, harsh and shaking, and she crumpled onto the frost-cold grass clutching Brynja to her chest. Celenneth dropped down and gathered her in.

"I tried," Linnea gasped. "I thought—"

"I know." Celenneth's own voice trembled. "I know."

Linnea buried her face in her cloak and wept out all the grief and rage and loss she had held back for years. "They hate us."

"They don't understand," Celenneth said gently, brushing her tangled hair. "They can't." And when Linnea whispered that she was so tired, Celenneth pressed a fierce kiss to her temple. "Then rest. I've got you."

Footsteps came crunching over the frost then, and Celenneth's hand went to her belt-knife — but a breathless voice called out. "Wait — please." Loran stumbled out of the dark path, coat half-buttoned, hands raised open in peace. "Don't go yet." He stopped just short of them, his face crumpling. "I came to apologize. For everything. I should have said something. Should have done something."

"You tried," Celenneth said quietly, and surprise, then something like respect, flickered across his face.

"I should have tried harder," he murmured. "They were wrong. About you. About all of it." He asked where they would go, and Linnea told him north, to find Celenneth's people, to try. He nodded, holding back his own tears, and his gaze lingered on the sleeping child. "She's lucky."

"We are," Linnea said, her voice breaking.

He took a hesitant step back, unwilling to break the fragile moment, and knowing he had to. "Safe travels. Stars guide you." And with one last glance he turned and vanished back down the dark path, toward the cold world he could not change. Linnea sank against Celenneth again, weeping — but differently now. "He cared."

"Yes," Celenneth said, holding her close, her fingers steady in Linnea's hair. "You're not alone."

"I know," Linnea whispered. And for the first time in years, she truly believed it.

⁂

Much later, the tears spent, they sat on a low rise above Bree, the firelight of distant homes flickering warm and strangely far away.

"They're not who I remembered," Linnea said at last, her gaze on the stars. "Or maybe I'm not. I always thought of Bree as home — the place I'd come back to. But now it just feels small. Like I've outgrown it. Outgrown them." She swallowed. "They'll always be family. But to them I'm still the girl who disappeared. Not this." She gestured at herself — the travel-worn cloak, Eorlin tethered nearby, Brynja asleep in her lap.

Celenneth rested a hand on her shoulder. "It's no bad thing, to grow. To change. To find a new home, even if it's not the one you expected."

"Do you ever feel that way?" Linnea asked, trembling. "That the road is the only place that makes sense?"

A faint, knowing smile. "Sometimes. But I've learned something else, too." Celenneth brushed her fingers against Brynja's small back. "Home isn't a place. It's the people you choose. Wherever that is — it's enough."

Linnea's breath hitched. "I'm scared," she admitted, barely above a whisper. "That one day I'll stop feeling like I belong anywhere."

"You'll always belong," Celenneth said, with quiet certainty. "With me. With Brynja. Wherever we go, we'll make our place."

Linnea let out a shaking breath and leaned into her warmth, careful of the sleeping child. "Thank you. For standing up for me tonight. For everything."

"I meant every word," Celenneth said, steady as stone. "You've become someone remarkable, Linnea. Don't let them make you forget it."

The silence that settled between them was a strong and comfortable one, and the stars stretched steadfast overhead. She had outgrown Bree — but she had grown into something far greater, and with Celenneth at her side she knew she could face whatever came next. Together, they were home.
