# Chapter 8: Secrets of the Forest

*Book 6*

*Tuilérë, 2966*

The cold morning air gnawed at Celenneth’s skin as she stirred from her restless sleep, her broken leg throbbing like fire. She flexed her fingers to chase away the chill, forcing herself to focus. The pale light filtering through the dense canopy brought little comfort—the shadows of Fangorn seemed just as deep as the night before.

With clenched teeth, she adjusted the makeshift splint, each movement sending sharp pain through her leg. She couldn’t stay near the embankment—exposed, weak, and hunted. She needed better shelter, fresh water, and a way to set more snares before hunger overtook her.

Leaning heavily on her crude walking staff, she limped deeper into the ancient forest, her sharp eyes scanning for a defensible spot. Every sound seemed amplified in the stillness: the distant rustle of leaves, the faint calls of unseen birds, the creaking groan of wind-weary branches.

As she paused by a moss-covered rock to catch her breath, a strange sensation prickled at the back of her neck—*watching eyes.*

She whirled, weapon raised, only to find the forest standing silent and still. Yet, something felt... *wrong*. The woods were too quiet now, as if holding its breath.

Ignoring the pain, she pressed on, limping through thick underbrush until she stumbled upon a small, rocky alcove where twisted roots formed a partial shelter beneath an ancient ash tree. Relief washed over her—*this would do.*

Celenneth sank down, panting. She unpacked the last of her supplies—some dried herbs, a flint, and a knife worn from overuse. As she prepared a weak infusion from the herbs to dull her pain, her mind reeled with exhaustion, and her eyes grew heavy.

**Suddenly, a distant sound:** a soft, rhythmic tapping. It echoed faintly through the woods like hollow drumbeats on ancient bark.

Her breath hitched. *Footsteps.*

She snatched up her spear, her senses crackling with adrenaline. The tapping came again—slow, deliberate, too heavy for human steps... and far too *purposeful* to be the wind.

The shadows shifted at the edge of the clearing. For a moment, Celenneth thought she saw movement—a massive shape slipping between the trees, blending with the bark and foliage like a living part of the forest.

Then it was gone.

The tapping ceased.

Celenneth’s grip on her staff tightened as her heart pounded. Whatever had passed through the forest was watching—*waiting.*

With shaking fingers, she reinforced her shelter, deepening the root-laden hollow as best she could. The eerie silence lingered long after the sound had vanished.

The air in Fangorn felt *alive*, dense with purpose and something ancient. Celenneth strained her ears, her breathing shallow, the faint ache in her leg drowned by instinctive fear. Whatever had passed through the clearing had not left—of that, she was certain.

Hours crawled by in silence, broken only by the distant creak of ancient branches swaying with unseen force. Celenneth dared not move, keeping her makeshift spear close at hand as she remained hidden within the root-laden hollow.

*Time stretched.*

When she could bear the stillness no longer, she forced herself upright, biting down against the sharp sting in her leg. If she stayed there, she would die—either from starvation or from whatever *watched* the woods. She needed water, food... *distance.*

Celenneth limped back toward the faint murmur of the stream she had heard earlier, weaving through the maze of moss-covered roots and towering trunks. Her breath misted in the cold air, every step measured and slow.

Finally, the sound of rushing water sharpened in her ears. She pushed through a tangle of ferns and found herself standing at the edge of a narrow stream, its clear waters sparkling in the dim light. Relief surged through her—*a lifeline.*

She knelt awkwardly, cupping water in her hands and drinking deeply, savoring the icy sting against her parched throat. Her reflection rippled faintly across the water’s surface—worn, hollow-eyed, and grim.

But then—movement.

A shape *shifted* in the reflection, just beyond her shoulder—a hulking mass barely distinguishable from the bark-covered trees. Her pulse thundered as she spun around, staff raised—

**Nothing.**

Only stillness. The wind rustled softly through the canopy, stirring dead leaves.

Breathing heavily, she lowered her weapon but didn’t relax. Whatever had moved was clever—far more clever than any forest beast.

Determined not to be caught unaware, she set a few makeshift alarms near her stream-side camp: sticks wedged between stones, thin trip lines of twisted vine tied to brittle branches. They would make enough noise to warn her—*she hoped.*

As she worked, her fingers brushed against something unnatural—*cold metal.*

She unearthed a rusted dagger half-buried beneath the earth, its blade scarred and corroded by time. Faint, ancient runes were etched into its darkened steel—symbols she didn’t recognize but that stirred something dark in her memory.

Holding the weapon sent a strange, uncomfortable *thrumming* through her hand, as if the blade remembered its purpose even if she did not. The weight of forgotten history hung in the still air.

Suddenly, a low *creak* echoed from deeper in the woods—a sound like straining wood under immense pressure, unnatural and deliberate.

Celenneth clenched the dagger tightly, her spear still within reach. She stepped back slowly, scanning the forest edge, her eyes adjusting to the shifting shadows.

The sound came again, closer this time—a slow, resonant groan of something *moving.*

The forest itself seemed to breathe.

With every nerve on edge, Celenneth retreated toward her shelter, her injured leg screaming in protest. Before she reached the tangled roots, her foot struck something—*soft.*

Looking down, she saw *tracks.*

Large, deeply pressed, and not made by any creature she knew. The edges of the prints were jagged, like shattered wood—*something that walked, but was not flesh.*

The forest's *silence* returned, thick and suffocating. Whatever watched her was drawing nearer... and it was no beast.
