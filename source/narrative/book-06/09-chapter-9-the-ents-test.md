# Chapter 9: The Ent's Test

*Víressë 2, 2966*

Celenneth crouched low against the trunk of an ancient oak, her breath shallow as she scanned the twisted expanse of Fangorn. Her staff lay across her knees, the rune-etched dagger secured at her side. The deep groaning came again — that resonant creak of aged wood under strain — and this time it seemed to encircle her, moving with slow and deliberate purpose. *It knows I'm here.*

Then a sharp crack: her trip-line had sprung. Something had triggered it. She rose, favoring her good leg, staff in hand. The undergrowth rustled, and a shadow shifted between the trees — not hurried, not panicked, but measured.

"Show yourself," she commanded, steadier than she felt.

The silence held. Then, with agonizing slowness, a colossal form emerged from the foliage. Its skin was gnarled bark, ancient and scarred; thick roots twisted down its arms like veins; its legs cracked the earth with each ponderous step, and its head was crowned with jagged, leafless branches spread like antlers. Two deep-set golden eyes glimmered beneath its brow — watchful, old, and knowing. No orcish horror; no servant of Dol Guldur. An Ent.

Its great head tilted as it studied her, weighing her presence in its forest. When at last it spoke, its voice was the groan of old trees in a fierce wind. "Hoom… a wanderer. Far from where she belongs."

Celenneth lowered her staff. "I seek no harm. Only shelter."

"Shelter is not freely given, in Fangorn." Its gaze lingered on her broken leg, the raw marks of rope on her wrists, the dark blade at her belt. "But perhaps… you are not as lost as you think. Come. The forest will decide whether you remain."

With no other choice, she steadied herself on her crutch and followed, leaving the ruined traps and the strange tracks behind, the trees creaking closed around her like a will made of living wood.

She struggled to keep the Ent's slow, earth-cracking pace, her leg a torment on the uneven ground, but she pressed on. *Pain means I'm alive.* The canopy thickened until Fangorn lay in perpetual twilight, the air heavy with damp earth and moss and something older. Without warning the Ent stopped, and turned its head with glacial slowness, its golden eyes glowing faint.

"You tread on sacred earth. Few of your kind leave."

"You led me here," Celenneth said, holding its gaze. "I don't believe it was to destroy me."

The Ent regarded her in long silence. Then the trees behind her shifted, their branches curling inward like claws, moss writhing over the roots as though the wood itself had drawn breath to watch. A deep groan rolled through the soil; roots snapped like whips, carving shallow trenches around her feet.

"You bear marks," the Ent rumbled, its gaze on the scars at her wrists — the chains of Dol Guldur.

"I am no servant of Dol Guldur," she spat, fire in it despite her exhaustion. "I escaped them."

"Escape does not cleanse what clings."

The dagger at her hip pulsed cold, and she gritted her teeth, knowing she could not explain it. The branches groaned louder, judging. And then the ground heaved, and roots snapped upward like striking serpents and coiled about her boots and dragged her to her knees, her staff clattering away, the forest's ancient will bearing down on her very soul until her vision darkened.

"You carry darkness," the Ent intoned, slow and terrible. "You will face it, or be consumed."

*The world twists, and she stands in the haunted halls of Dol Guldur — stone walls slick with decay, cruel laughter ringing off cold iron gates. Chains snake around her wrists again, unbreakable. Out of the void come towering figures robed in black, their hollow eyes burning, waiting, and an insidious voice hisses through the dark: "Come… you belong here."*

*But Celenneth fights. She rips the spectral chains from her wrists with a scream, fury igniting in her — her pain, her fear, none of it will command her. "I escaped!" she roars, and the illusion shatters.*

She came back to the living world on her knees in the damp earth, her body aching, her head swimming, her leg afire. The Ent watched in heavy silence. Slowly the creeping roots loosened and withdrew into the soil, and the trees' groaning ceased, and Fangorn seemed to exhale.

The Ent inclined its massive head, just slightly. "You fight," it said, and its voice had softened, just enough to hint at approval. "Perhaps there is hope, yet." Then the grove fell still once more, and she was alone — tested by a forest that had judged her, and allowed her to remain. For now.[^s-test]
