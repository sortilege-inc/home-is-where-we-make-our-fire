# Chapter 2: The Road to Minas Tirith

*Book 6*

*Súlimë 5, 2966*

The next morning before dawn, Linnea awoke to Celenneth leaning over her, already dressed for the road. "I'm packed and I'm leaving Nimroch with you, I'll be traveling light. Hoping to be not more than a couple weeks."

She leaned down and kissed Linnea's lips, and Linnea threw her arms around Celenneth's neck and pulled her down onto the bed. "I love you, may the road be short and swift."

Celenneth eventually extricated herself, and made her way over to Brynja's crib. She kissed the infant's forehead tenderly, and left without waking her.

The day seemed touched by fortune. The weather held steady, the skies unmarred by clouds, and the road stretched invitingly ahead. Each stride felt lighter than the last, as though the boots themselves wove some spell of speed and ease into her steps. Her pace devoured the road until the sun hung low on the horizon. Along the way, she discovered a narrow game trail cutting through a copse of oaks, shorn of their winter starkness, their branches just beginning to bud. The shortcut shaved hours off her path, and by nightfall, she had made camp near a gentle brook that bubbled over mossy stones. The stars seemed brighter that night, and for once, her dreams were untroubled.

*Súlimë 6, 2966*

The next day started with a shift in the air, the wind sharp and restless as it danced through the trees. The trail led her into rockier terrain, the smooth path giving way to uneven ground that slowed her pace. By midday, the road narrowed into a gulch, its walls high and shadowed. It was there she caught sight of the orcs.

She stilled, her hand instinctively falling to the hilt of her sword. They were just ahead, a patrol of four moving with a casual arrogance that made her stomach churn. They were speaking in low, guttural tones, punctuated with sharp laughter. Celenneth crouched behind a jagged boulder, her heart pounding as she calculated her next move. The memory of her fight against Uftak flashed in her mind—a reminder of what these creatures were capable of.

She erred on the side of discretion, and fortune was with her. The orcs seemed unconcerned, their attention focused on each other rather than their surroundings. Celenneth waited, her breathing measured, until their voices faded down the trail. Only then did she move, slipping past their position with the grace of a shadow. She noted to herself how emboldened they seemed.

By the time the spires of Calenhad came into view, the day was waning, the golden light of the setting sun casting the fortress in a warm glow. Her limbs ached, her muscles taut with exhaustion, but relief flooded through her as she reached the safety of its gates. The sentries nodded as she passed, their faces friendly.

That night, Celenneth sat at the inn and mulled over the near-miss encounter.

*Súlimë 7, 2966*

The dawn broke with a radiant promise, streaks of gold and pink stretching across the sky as Celenneth prepared to move on. The air was crisp, invigorating in a way that made the weight of her pack seem lighter. She knew the day ahead would be long, but her legs were strong, and the Númenórean boots carried her as if she were gliding over the land. The road was mercifully even, and the miles seemed to pass underfoot almost without effort.

By midday, the terrain began to rise, rolling hills cresting into peaks that afforded her sweeping views of the lands ahead. Far off, she could make out the faint line of the Anduin River, its silvery sheen catching the light. Her pace quickened with the sight, and by late afternoon, she found herself standing at the base of Nardol, one of the ancient Beacon Hills of Gondor. Its summit rose like a sentinel over the land, crowned by the remnants of an old watchfire platform.

As she climbed the hill, the sound of voices reached her ears—low, steady, and unmistakably human. Cresting the ridge, she spotted a small group gathered near the platform. They wore the colors of Gondor, though their cloaks were travel-worn and their faces marked with fatigue. One of them, a man with streaks of silver in his dark hair, turned as she approached, his hand resting instinctively on the hilt of his sword.

"Peace," Celenneth said, raising her hands slightly to show she meant no harm. "I am a ranger, on my way to Minas Tirith."

The man studied her for a moment before nodding, his grip on the sword easing. "You have the look of an experienced traveler," he said, his voice steady but tinged with weariness. "Come, sit. If you bring no ill will, then you're welcome to share our fire."

Celenneth joined them, the warmth of their small fire a welcome reprieve from the chill that had settled over the hilltop. She introduced herself, and they, in turn, gave their names—Turion, the older man; Arvin, who was younger, wiry, a soldier; and Liseth, a quiet woman with the look of someone not to be challenged.

As they spoke, the conversation turned to the state of Gondor. Turion's voice grew heavy as he described the troubles that plagued the land. "The strength of Gondor wanes," he admitted, his eyes fixed on the horizon. "Our borders are stretched thin, and our forces scattered. The beacons—symbols of our might—stand cold and unused. The lands beyond the White Mountains grow wild, and our lords bicker over pride and politics while enemies gather strength in the east."

Liseth nodded, her expression somber. "Even the common folk feel it. The villages are wary, and the roads are less traveled. We hear whispers of darker things moving in the shadows."

Celenneth listened intently, her heart heavy with their words. She had seen the signs herself—the growing boldness of orc patrols, the neglected watchposts. Gondor, once a bastion of strength, seemed fragile now, its foundation cracking under the weight of neglect and division.

Arvin, however, spoke with a spark of defiance. "Yet there are still those who hold the line," he said, his gaze fierce. "We have not forgotten who we are. Gondor stands, even if it stands bloodied. The enemy will find no easy victory here."

Celenneth admired his resolve, though she could see the strain in his face, the weariness that came from fighting a battle he knew he could not win alone. She offered what comfort she could, sharing tales of her own struggles and the resilience she had found in her allies. It was a small thing, but the firelight seemed to burn brighter as they spoke, their shared stories kindling a fragile hope.

*Súlimë 8, 2966*

Celenneth packed her camp before dawn, and as the others woke up she thanked them for their hospitality and the perspectives they had shared. Turion clasped her hand, his grip firm. "You travel to Minas Tirith," he said. "Remember what you have heard here. Tell them the people still fight for Gondor, but the leaders need to muster an active defense, not merely resign themselves to passivity."

"I will," Celenneth promised, her voice steady with conviction. "Gondor will not be forgotten."

She descended Nardol as dawn began to break. The road to Minas Tirith stretched on, but her resolve had redoubled. Gondor needed encouragement, and she would bring that exhortation to the White City.

The land unfolded before her in gentle slopes and wide expanses, the weather kind, and she made excellent progress, her steady pace eating up the miles. As she moved into a glade of towering oaks, she caught sight of a pheasant, its plumage shimmering in the dappled light. She froze, her breath shallow as she observed the bird, its every movement exuding a skittish grace.

For a moment, she hesitated. Slowly, she crouched, her eyes narrowing as she studied the pheasant's behavior. It moved with caution, its head twitching at every sound, but it seemed unaware of her presence.

She reached for her bow, nocking an arrow with practiced ease. Her fingers brushed the fletching as she steadied her breathing, her body as still as the trees around her. She waited, her muscles taut, until the pheasant turned just right, presenting the perfect shot. The arrow flew true, striking the bird cleanly and swiftly. The creature fell, its death quick and without suffering—a testament to her skill.

Approaching the pheasant, Celenneth knelt and placed a hand on its side, murmuring a quiet prayer of thanks. The meat would sustain her, and the feathers could be used for fletching or ornamentation. She worked efficiently, her knife flashing in the dim light as she prepared the bird. With the application of campfire and forest herbs, the pheasant was transformed into a delicious dish.

*Súlimë 9, 2966*

On the fifth day, the terrain began to shift again, the rolling fields giving way to the craggy foothills that marked the approach to Minas Tirith. The city’s white walls and soaring spires loomed on the horizon, growing larger with every step. Celenneth quickened her pace, the anticipation of her arrival lending strength to her weary limbs.

As she climbed the winding road that led to the city’s great gates, the splendor of Minas Tirith became clear. Its towering walls gleamed in the midday sun, and the banners of Gondor fluttered proudly in the breeze. Guards stood at their posts, their silver armor catching the light as they watched her approach.

“Halt,” one of them called as she neared, his voice firm but not unkind. “What is your business in Minas Tirith?”

“I am Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” she replied, her voice steady. “I carry messages of import and seek an audience with your lords.”

The guard nodded, his sharp eyes appraising her before he signaled for the gates to open. The creak of the massive wooden doors was accompanied by the hum of life within the city—voices, footsteps, the distant clang of a smith’s hammer. Celenneth stepped through, her senses flooded by the bustling energy of the White City.

The streets of Minas Tirith were a marvel of stonework and design, their winding paths leading upward through the city’s seven levels. She followed the guards’ directions, her path taking her steadily higher, the air growing cooler with the ascent. At each level, she caught glimpses of Gondor’s people—merchants hawking their wares, children playing in narrow alleys, and craftsmen bent over their work. Yet beneath the surface, she could feel an undercurrent of tension, the shadow of Gondor’s struggles lingering even here.

Finally, she reached the Citadel, its grand gates flanked by towering statues of kings long past. The guards escorted her through, their boots echoing on the polished stone floors. She was led to a chamber just outside the great hall, where a steward waited to receive her.

“The lords of Gondor have granted you an audience,” the steward said, his tone formal. “You will be called shortly.”

Celenneth nodded, her heart steady but her mind racing with anticipation. She stepped back, her hand instinctively brushing against the hilt of her sword as she took in the grandeur of the Citadel and prepared herself.
