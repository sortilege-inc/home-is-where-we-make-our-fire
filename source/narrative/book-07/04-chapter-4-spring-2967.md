# Chapter 4: Spring 2967

*Víressë 9, 2967*

The request came on a crisp morning as Celenneth restrung her bow at the edge of the yard, the sun laying long shadows over the frosted grass, the sound of horses drifting from the stables. A rider approached, cloak snapping in the wind — Ealdric, one of the patrol captains, his face serious but not unkind.

"Celenneth. Your skill as a tracker is known to us. We're mustering a patrol for the northern borders, and your experience would be worth much. Will you ride with us?"

She glanced toward the house, where Linnea and Brynja were at the morning chores, and hesitated only a moment before she nodded. "If my skill can serve Rohan, I'll go. When?"

"First light tomorrow. The main gate. Garin leads the patrol." At the name, Celenneth's mouth pressed thoughtful, but she nodded again, and Ealdric rode off. Linnea came up wiping her hands on her apron.

"A patrol?"

"The northern borders. They need a tracker. I said yes."

Linnea studied her, then gave a small, resigned smile. "Of course you did. It's who you are." Her fingers brushed Celenneth's. "Just come back to us."

Celenneth's hand tightened over hers. "Always."

That evening Linnea slipped away to the stables, where she knew Garin would be readying for the road, and found her brushing down her bay mare.

"Linnea," Garin said, straightening, mildly surprised. "What brings you here?"

Linnea stepped closer. "I wanted a word. About the patrol. And about Celenneth."

Garin's brow furrowed. "What about her?"

Linnea met her eyes and said it plainly. "I know how you look at her. I'm not here to ask you to stop. I only want you to know — if Celenneth ends up sharing your bedroll on this ride, it's not a hurt to me. As long as she comes home to me, I'm at peace with it."

Garin's composure faltered for the first time. "You'd truly be all right with that?"

"What matters to me is that Celenneth and I are honest with each other, and that she knows where home is. She loves you as a friend, I don't doubt. If there's more to it, I don't see the harm — so long as our bond holds."

Garin was quiet a long moment, then nodded, a faint smile tugging at her. "You're remarkable, Linnea. Most wouldn't be so clear about it."

Linnea shrugged. "I know what we have. I've no cause to fear anyone, least of all you. Only promise you'll never try to come between us."

"I promise. I'll always stand behind what you two have. My word on it."

Satisfied, Linnea turned to go. "Safe travels, Garin."

The patrol left at dawn, hooves ringing over the frost. For two weeks Celenneth rode north with Garin and the others, her eyes on every horizon. Past the Snowmarch, near the shadow of the Greyfells, they cut orc-sign — a scouting party testing the border's strength, small but dangerous — and tracked it four days through the foothills before setting an ambush at Frostcrag Hollow and taking it unawares. It was over quickly, and no rider was lost. The work was hard and the land colder and harsher than Celenneth had remembered, and in the quiet hours of camp she and Garin found their way, at last, into each other's arms.[^r-patrol]

When the patrol rode home the sun was setting the sky to gold and crimson. Celenneth swung down and met Garin's eyes, and they shared a brief, tender kiss, and parted with a warmth that held no possession in it. Then she quickened her steps toward the gate, where Linnea waited with Brynja on her hip, her face lighting with relief.

That night, with Brynja long asleep, Linnea drew Celenneth to their bed. "Tell me everything," she said, resting her head on Celenneth's shoulder. "Start with what the patrol found. And then how it was with Garin — I want all of it. And hold me while you tell it."

Celenneth exhaled, and slid into the cadence of a ranger's report. "We rode north past the Snowmarch, near the Greyfells. Found tracks — orc raiders, testing the border. We ran them four days through the foothills. They were quick, and clever. Not clever enough. We set the ambush at Frostcrag Hollow, and it was quick, and we lost no one." Her gaze drifted to the fire. "But it wore at me. The land's harder than I remembered up there. Colder."

"And Garin?" Linnea prompted, quiet, curious, and unafraid.

Celenneth's lips curved at the memory. "We grew close. I half-expected it — after what you told me before I left." A pause. "I didn't expect her to know you'd spoken to her. One night after a hard ride, she pulled me aside and told me."

Linnea smiled. "She needed to hear it, too."

Celenneth shook her head, still half in wonder at it. "I didn't know what to say. Only thank you." And she told it as it had been.

The campfire had burned low, its glow carving long shadows across the snow, her breath clouding as she worked a whetstone down her blade. Garin crouched beside her. "Cold night. Mind some company?"
