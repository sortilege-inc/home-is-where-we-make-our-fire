# Chapter 2: Anniversary

*Narvinyë 12, 2967*

The midwinter cold had begun to soften as the Yule feasting wound down in Edoras, and in the household of Celenneth and Linnea a quieter celebration took its place. It was a year to the day since they had found Brynja abandoned in the winter borderlands — the day that had made them a family — and they meant to mark it.

Freowyn bustled at the hearth, sleeves rolled, a stew of rosemary and roasted roots filling the house with its smell. "Enough for a feast," she declared. "Even if it's only us, it should feel like one."

Linnea stood stringing small dried flowers onto a length of twine. "In Bree we'd hang garlands like this for a child's first birthday," she said, a faint smile on her. "For blessings in the year ahead."

"And in the North," Celenneth added, "we'd leave a ring of bread and fruit on the doorstep — a gift to the spirits, for watching over the child." She set about arranging a small offering by the door, careful and reverent.

Brynja toddled between them, wide-eyed, her small hands grasping at the garland and the bread. "For — me?" she managed, on two of her still-few words, and the wonder in it made them both laugh.

"Every bit," Linnea said, scooping her up and kissing her cheek. "Because today's the day we found you, little star."

As the sun sank and gilded the room, a few friends of the household arrived. Eadwyn, the horsewoman Linnea trained under, brought a small carved horse for Brynja, its surface polished bright. Hild, a neighbor of Freowyn's, came with a basket of honey cakes.

"A child of Rohan must have a horse of her own," Eadwyn said with a grin, pressing the carving into Brynja's hands. "Even a little one, for now."

Brynja hugged it hard against her chest. "Horsey!"

Freowyn laughed and set the stew on the table. "A fine name. Now come — eat, before the cakes vanish."

The meal was a weave of their three homes: the hearty stew beside a flatbread from Celenneth's ranger days, and a sweet berry pudding Linnea had made in the way of Bree. The table was strewn with small tokens of their travels — a polished northern stone, a Rohan weave, a wooden trinket from Bree. After, they gathered by the fire for stories: Freowyn told of a brave mare who saved her rider in battle; Celenneth followed with a ranger's fable of stars guiding the lost toward home; and Linnea, her eyes lingering on Brynja, spoke a Bree tale of the strength of unlikely families.

When the telling was done, Linnea drew a small silver pendant from her pocket, a simple star worked into its face, and fastened it gently at Brynja's neck. "This is yours, Brynja. To remember that you're our light, even on the darkest nights."

Brynja's fingers found the pendant, her small face gone serious for a moment — then split into a wide grin. "Star," she said, pleased with herself.

"Our star," Celenneth agreed, pulling her into a warm embrace.

As the night deepened the fire settled low, and Brynja dozed in Linnea's lap with the pendant clutched in one fist. Freowyn hummed as she tidied, and Eadwyn leaned back with a rare softness in her eyes as she watched the family. Celenneth reached for Linnea's hand, her thumb brushing her knuckles. "We're lucky to have her," she murmured.

Linnea nodded, her gaze on the sleeping child. "And lucky to have each other."

For a while the wide dangerous world seemed very far off, and there was only the warmth of the house and the family they had made against every odd.
