# Chapter 9: Summer 2968

*Cermië 20, 2968*

The sun hung low over the wide golden fields of the Riddermark, gilding the gathered Rohirrim in honeyed light. They stood in a broad circle — riders and their proud mounts, all turned inward — and at its center stood Linnea, straight-backed, her dark hair stirring in the breeze, while a little apart Celenneth watched with her arms folded and a faint smile on her lips.

At the circle's head stood Eadwyn, her helm cradled in the crook of her arm, and she raised her voice for all to hear. "We Eorlingas do not give our honors lightly. Blood ties us together — but so do shared purpose and earned trust. Though Linnea was not born to the Mark, she has ridden with us, learned our ways, and shown her heart to be true. She has tended our people, and proven herself in the saddle and at the bow." A murmur of assent moved through the riders, and Linnea's cheeks warmed, and she lowered her eyes a moment under the weight and the blessing of their regard. "Let all bear witness," Eadwyn went on, "that we name Linnea one of the Mark in spirit, if not in blood. May she ride under our banner, and be counted kin."

Garin stepped forward bearing a folded green cloak trimmed in gold, a white horse galloping across an emerald field stitched at its breast, and Eadwyn took it and settled it over Linnea's shoulders, where it caught the last of the day's light. A cheer rose and echoed off the far hills. Linnea lifted her head, her eyes shining. "I am honored," she said, steady despite the lump in her throat. "I'll strive to be worthy of it."

Then Eadwyn signaled, and a rider led forward a tall, sleek mare — deep chestnut, her mane and tail dark and glossy, her step proud and fluid. Eadwyn took the reins and brought the horse before Linnea. "Her name is Eorlin," she said, a quiet smile creasing her face. "Swift, strong, and loyal — and yours. You may remember her; she was a new foal when last you saw her."

Linnea drew a sharp breath. This was more than a token — it was a bond of trust laid in her hands. She reached out and stroked the mare's neck, feeling the ripple of muscle beneath the silken coat. "I'll ride her with honor," she said softly, meeting Eadwyn's eyes. "Thank you."[^r-eorlin]

Across the circle Celenneth's smile brightened, and she came through the crowd to stand at Linnea's side. "You wear their colors well," she murmured, low, for Linnea alone.

Before Linnea could answer, Eadwyn turned to Celenneth and held out a second cloak, and a profound hush fell. "Celenneth of the Dúnedain. You have fought beside us, shared our dangers, shown your mastery in the wilds. We offer you the same honor, that you might stand fully among us."

Celenneth's eyes softened, and for a heartbeat something wistful crossed her face. Then, with gentle finality, she shook her head. "I am deeply honored," she said, and the hush held each word as sacred. "But my loyalty lies with my own people. To take your cloak would be to claim a bond I could not fully keep. I cannot accept it — though I treasure the offering."

A ripple of understanding moved through the Rohirrim, and Eadwyn inclined her head, accepting it with respect. "You remain a friend of the Mark. May the road rise to meet you, wherever you wander."

As the riders raised a cheer — some for Linnea's welcome, some for Celenneth's conviction — the two women stood side by side, and Linnea reached out and brushed her fingertips to Celenneth's arm. When the celebration swelled again into song and toast, Linnea felt something new settle in her: a belonging no birthplace or bloodline could have granted. With Eorlin at her side and the green cloak of Rohan on her shoulders, she was proud of the life she had built here.

[^r-eorlin]: **Reward / Fellowship — Eorlin, and a place in the Mark.** The Rohirrim name Linnea kin in spirit, gifting her the green cloak of Rohan and **Eorlin** — the chestnut mare who was a foal during her first winter here — her own horse from this day on. Celenneth is offered the same honor but gently declines, holding to her Dúnedain loyalty; she remains a friend of the Mark.
