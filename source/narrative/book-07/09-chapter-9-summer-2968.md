# Chapter 9: Summer 2968

*Book 7*

*Summer 2968*

The sun dipped low over the wide, golden fields of the Riddermark, gilding the gathered Rohirrim in a warm, honeyed light. They formed a broad circle—riders and their proud mounts—all turned inward toward the center, where two figures stood. Linnea stood straight-backed, her dark hair whispering over her shoulders in the breeze. Not far from her, Celenneth watched quietly, arms folded, a faint smile playing at her lips.

At the forefront of the circle stood Eadwyn, helm cradled in the crook of her arm, her face lit by the sunset. She raised her voice so that all might hear:

“We Eorlingas do not give our honors lightly,” she began, her gaze sweeping over the listeners. “Blood ties us together—but so, too, does shared purpose and earned trust. Though Linnea was not born to the Riddermark, she has ridden with us, learned our ways, and shown her heart to be true. She has tended to the needs of our people and proved herself in the saddle and at the bow.”

A murmur of assent moved through the riders. Linnea’s cheeks warmed, and she lowered her eyes for a moment, feeling their regard as a weight and a blessing.

Eadwyn continued. “Let all bear witness that we name Linnea as one of the Mark in spirit, if not by blood. May she ride under our banner and be counted as kin.”

Garin stepped forward, holding a folded green cloak trimmed with golden thread. Upon its breast was stitched the emblem of Rohan: a white horse galloping across an emerald field. Eadwyn took it and draped it over Linnea’s shoulders. The fabric caught the last light of day, shining as though lit from within.

A cheer rose, echoing off distant hills. Linnea lifted her head, her dark eyes shining. “I am honored,” she said, voice steady despite the lump in her throat. “This gift... I will strive to be worthy of it.”

Just then, Eadwyn signaled, and another rider led forward a tall, sleek mare. Deep chestnut in color, her mane and tail dark and glossy, she stepped with a proud, fluid grace. Eadwyn took the reins and guided the horse before Linnea. “Her name is Eorlin,” she said, a quiet smile creasing her face. “She is swift, strong, and loyal—yours. You may remember her, although she was a new foal at the time you last saw her.”

Linnea inhaled sharply. This was more than a token; it was a bond of trust. She reached out and stroked the mare’s neck, feeling the ripple of powerful muscles beneath silken coat. “I will ride her with honor,” she said softly, meeting Eadwyn’s gaze. “Thank you.”

Across the circle, Celenneth’s smile brightened, pride evident in her eyes. She stepped closer, moving through the throng with easy grace until she stood beside Linnea. “You wear their colors well,” she said, voice low, meant for Linnea’s ears.

Before Linnea could reply, Eadwyn turned to Celenneth, extending a second cloak. The hush that fell over the crowd was profound. “Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” she said. “You have fought alongside us, shared our dangers, and shown mastery in the wilds. We offer you the same honor, that you might stand fully among us.”

Celenneth’s eyes softened, and for a heartbeat, something wistful passed over her face. Then, with gentle finality, she shook her head. “I am deeply honored,” she said, the hush holding each word as sacred. “Yet my loyalty lies with my own people. To bear your cloak would be to claim a bond I cannot fully uphold. I cannot accept, though I treasure your goodwill.”

A ripple of understanding moved through the Rohirrim. Eadwyn inclined her head, accepting her choice with respect. “You remain a friend to the Mark. May the road rise to meet you, wherever you wander.”

As the riders raised a cheer—some for Linnea’s acceptance, some for Celenneth’s conviction—the two women stood side by side. Linnea reached out, fingertips brushing Celenneth’s arm.

When the celebrations resumed—voices raised in song, mugs raised in toast—Linnea felt something new within her: a sense of belonging that no birthplace or bloodline could grant. With Eorlin at her side and the green cloak of Rohan on her shoulders, she felt a pride in the life she had built in Rohan.
