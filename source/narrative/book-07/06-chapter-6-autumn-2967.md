# Chapter 6: Autumn 2967

*Narquelië 15, 2967*

The kitchen was fragrant with dried herbs as Linnea crushed chamomile flowers in a small carved mortar, Freowyn across the table with her arms crossed and an eyebrow raised, the warm afternoon light streaming over the bundles of plants hung from the ceiling.

"This one," Linnea said, calm and sure, "is for easing pain and helping sleep. Chamomile works well in a tea or a poultice — but you can't mix it with anything too strong. Gentle, but it does its work."

Freowyn leaned in over the crushed petals. "And this grows common in Bree?"

"Very. My grandmother kept it just outside our door. She always said there wasn't a trouble that couldn't be eased with a good cup of chamomile."

Freowyn twirled a sprig of thyme between her fingers. "And this? I've only cooked with it."

"Good for cleaning a wound," Linnea replied. "A wash or a salve. Best for small cuts and scrapes — and it settles a sour stomach, brewed as a tea."

Freowyn's eyes lit. "I've seen enough scrapes in the stables to put that to use."

Their lessons became a part of the days' rhythm — Linnea guiding Freowyn through tinctures and salves and teas, sharing what she'd gleaned in Bree and on the road with Celenneth; and in turn Freowyn began to teach her the skills of a lifetime in Rohan. It began with the horses.

"A foal's no different from a soldier," Freowyn said one day in the stables, where a mare stood heavily in foal, her breathing slow and labored. "Both need care and steadiness from their first moment in the world. It isn't only what your hands do — it's being calm. They feel everything you feel." Linnea listened close as Freowyn showed her how to ready the birthing-place with clean hay and water, and when the foal came, Freowyn's steady hands and low voice guided her through it — checking the mare, clearing the foal's nose and mouth, seeing both safe. After, watching the foal find its first wobbling steps, Linnea let out a breath she hadn't known she was holding. "That was — incredible."

Freowyn grinned, wiping her hands. "You did well. A few more, and it'll come as easy as mixing one of your tinctures."

In time the lessons grew past horses. Freowyn began to teach her the rudiments of a midwife's craft for women too — the herbs to ease a hard labor, to hold off fever. "This isn't only about birth," she said one evening as they worked over a bundle of comfrey. "It's about life. About standing with someone in the hour they're most helpless, and helping them find their own strength." Linnea took in every word, her hands steady as she made up a poultice, and saw more and more how the two crafts were kin — the quiet care of the herb-garden and the hands-on mercy of the birthing-room, both asking patience, and intuition, and a steadfast will to stand by those in need.[^r-craft]
