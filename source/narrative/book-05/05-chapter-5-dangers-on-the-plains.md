# Chapter 5: Dangers on the Plains

*Narquelië 29, 2965*

The stream Ellarien had promised came into view by mid-morning, cutting a silver line through the grass. Celenneth led Nimroch down to drink and swung off, and Linnea slid from her saddle with a sigh of pure relief. "We've earned this," she said, already pulling off her boots.

The water was shallow and wide and cold enough to make them gasp, and they waded in to rinse the road from their clothes and lay them out on the rocks. Even Ellarien's stoicism softened; and when Linnea splashed Celenneth with a daring grin, Celenneth answered with a wave of water that broke over Linnea's head, and Ellarien was dragged laughing into the fray besides. For a while the three of them played like children, and the weight of the journey fell clean away.

A deep, resonant bellow stopped them. Celenneth froze, her hand going for a bow that lay on the bank. Across the stream, majestic shapes came out of the tall grass — a herd of great elk, antlers crowned with moss and small blooms, their leader a towering stag that stepped forward and regarded the three of them in the water with dark, unhurried eyes.

Ellarien lifted a hand. "Still. They're no threat unless we startle them." They stood motionless as the elk came down to drink, deliberate and serene, and the stag lifted its dripping muzzle and held Celenneth's gaze a long moment, as though some understanding passed unspoken between them, before it turned and led the herd away into the grass.

The three stood quiet in the wake of it. "I've never seen anything like that," Linnea breathed.

"Nor have I," Celenneth said, softer than her wont.

*Narquelië 30, 2965*

The day after unfolded like something out of a good dream — crisp air, endless blue, the horses light beneath them. Linnea's splinted arm no longer dragged at her, and she rode easy at Celenneth's side. And then, near midday, she slowed Mareth and tilted her head as though catching a sound too faint for the others.

"What is it?" Celenneth asked, low.

Linnea closed her eyes a moment. There was — something. Not a threat; the opposite of a threat. A presence, close and warm, brushing at the edge of the new sense the plains had woken in her. "Someone," she said. "Near. But there's no danger in it."

They crested a low rise, and a figure stood upon it, haloed by the sun at its back — tall, slender, its garments shifting between earth-tones and silver as though woven of the light itself. As they came near, warmth reached out from it to meet Linnea, not heat but a deep and settling peace, and its features clarified: a being of unearthly beauty, its skin luminous, its eyes holding a kindness old as the land, hair the color of turning leaves falling down its back, a slender staff crowned with a single glowing crystal in its hand.

Linnea reined in, her breath caught. Behind her, Celenneth's voice came tight with wariness. "Stay back. We don't know what it is." And Ellarien had dismounted slowly, reverent, one hand near her blade but making no move to draw it. "An emissary," she murmured. "Or something greater. Tread carefully."

But Linnea felt no fear, only the peace of it, and she slid down and walked forward with her good hand raised in greeting. "You're not of this world," she said, gentle, "are you?"

The being inclined its head. "Few see beyond the veil." Its voice was like the far rustle of leaves, layered with a quiet, ancient wisdom. "But you carry a light within you, Linnea — one that sees past shadow."

She startled at her own name, and yet felt no alarm, only that abiding calm. "If I carry any light," she said, "it's because others have shared theirs with me."

The spirit's mouth curved. "A rare humility. It is such that binds the broken threads of the world." It reached into the folds of its shifting garments and drew out a ring — delicate, silver, gleaming with an otherworldly sheen — and held it out to her. "A gift, for your kindness and your trust. It will help you know the hearts and minds of others, if you wear it wisely."

Linnea took it in both hands, and slipped it on, and a subtle warmth spread through her, invigorating and strange. "Thank you," she said simply.

The spirit bowed its head once more, and began to fade, its form dissolving into the golden afternoon until it was as though it had never stood there, and the air let out a held breath.

Celenneth came to her, brow furrowed. "You shouldn't have trusted it so easily. It could have been a trap."

Linnea turned the ring on her finger, and met her worry with a small, certain smile. "Maybe. But sometimes trust is worth the risk. And this —" she lifted her hand "— this feels right."

Ellarien watched them both. "You may have made a friend today," she said. "Or something greater than a friend."[^t-ring]

*Hísimë 1, 2965*

The next day the sky was clear, but the wind carried an unease, and Celenneth rode with her gaze sharper than ever. When the foreboding took shape it took it fast: dark forms moving low and quick across the plain, a dozen or more, closing with a relentless purpose. She raised a hand and stopped the others. "Orcs."

The shapes clarified — crude armor, jagged iron, and at their head a hulking brute in a spiked black helm. He flung up a hand and bellowed in the Black Speech, pointing them out. Celenneth's blood went cold. "Dol Guldur," she said. "That's who sent them."

"That's a hunting party," Ellarien said, tight. "Not a raid. Us they're after."

The open plain gave no cover, and outrunning them on tiring horses was no sure thing. But the land might serve where speed would not. Celenneth's eye caught a shallow depression to the west — a dried streambed cut into the plain, its high banks narrowing to a bottleneck. "This way!" She spurred Nimroch toward it, and the others followed, the orcs' guttural cries rising behind them.

At the streambed's edge she swung down and strung an arrow. "Ellarien — take Linnea and the horses into the tall grass. Slow, low, circle back east. I'll lead them into the cut."

Linnea's eyes went wide. "You can't take them alone—"

"I won't have to. The banks bottleneck them — they'll have to come single file, chasing one target. I climb out the far end and lose them, and meet you east." She loosed before Linnea could argue; the shaft took a lead orc in the shoulder and he howled, and the brute bellowed, "After her! She's alone!"

She darted into the cut, drawing them in, the high banks forcing them to string out behind her, and fired again — a leg this time, slowing them further. When she reached the narrowest point she vaulted up the bank, hauling on a fistful of grass and root, and loosed a last arrow that rang off the leader's helm. "Up here, fool!" she goaded, and vanished over the ridge, and the broken ground slowed their scramble after her enough that she was clear and mounted and away before they crested it. The orcs boiled out of the depression too late, their leader roaring, to see the three riders already small against the horizon.[^r-orcs]

They slowed at last, breaths ragged. Linnea turned to her, anger and awe at war in her face. "That was reckless. But it worked."

Celenneth gave a tired smile. "Sometimes the best fight is the one you don't have to finish."

Ellarien scanned the country behind. "They'll find us again if they've a mind to. But we've bought time." And they rode on into the lengthening light, the plains no longer feeling quite so open, the memory of the hunting party lingering at the edges of the day.

[^t-ring]: **Reward — the empathy ring** (gift of a plains spirit to Linnea): a silver band that aids in reading the hearts and minds of others. Given in recognition of her trust and humility; a subtle, growing gift.
[^r-orcs]: **Combat / flight** vs. a Dol Guldur hunting-party (a dozen-plus, with a captain) on the open plain. Celenneth uses a dried streambed to bottleneck the pursuit, plays decoy with her bow, and escapes over the ridge while Ellarien leads Linnea and the horses clear. No losses; the enemy shaken off but not destroyed.
