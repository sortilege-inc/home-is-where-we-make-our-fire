# Chapter 5: Dangers on the Plains

*Book 5*

*Narquelië 29, 2965*

They awoke with the scent of rain lingering in the air, though the skies were clear and blue. The group set out early, the promise of the stream ahead encouraging them.

By midmorning the stream came into view, its sparkling waters cutting through the grassy plain like a silver ribbon. The sound of the water was soothing, its gentle babble a welcome contrast to the constant hum of wind through the tall grasses. Celenneth dismounted first, leading Nimroch to the water’s edge for a drink. Linnea followed, sighing with relief as she swung down from her saddle.

“We’ve earned this,” Linnea said with a grin, already pulling off her boots. Ellarien chuckled softly, shaking her head as she began to untie the small bundle of clothes she carried.

The stream was shallow but wide, its cool waters a perfect reprieve from the heat of the day. Celenneth and Linnea waded in first, the water lapping at their knees as they rinsed their clothes and laid them out on the rocks to dry. Ellarien joined them, her usual stoicism softened by the ease of the moment.

It wasn’t long before Linnea splashed Celenneth, her mischievous grin daring a response. Celenneth’s eyes narrowed playfully as she retaliated, sending a wave of water crashing over Linnea’s head. Ellarien watched from the edge, her sharp features breaking into a rare smile before she was dragged into the fray by Linnea’s insistence.

The three played like children, their laughter ringing out across the plains. For a moment, the weight of their journey fell away, replaced by the simple joy of cool water and warm company.

The sound of a deep, resonant bellow brought them to a halt. Celenneth froze, her hand instinctively reaching for where her bow would be if it weren’t on the bank. Linnea stood waist-deep in the water, her eyes wide as she followed Celenneth’s gaze.

Across the stream, majestic creatures emerged from the tall grasses—a herd of great elk, their antlers massive and crowned with moss and small blooms. The leader, a towering stag, stepped forward, its dark eyes intelligent and calm as it regarded the humans and elf in the water. The herd moved with grace, their hooves barely making a sound as they approached the stream to drink.

Ellarien raised a hand, signaling for stillness. “Be silent,” she whispered, her voice barely audible over the rush of the water. “They are no threat unless startled.”

The group stood motionless, their earlier playfulness replaced with awe. The elk drank, their movements deliberate and serene. The stag lifted its head, water dripping from its mouth, and met Celenneth’s gaze. For a moment, it seemed as if the creature understood something unspoken, a quiet exchange passing between them before it turned and led its herd away.

The three stood in silence for a long moment, the majesty of the encounter lingering in the air. Linnea was the first to speak, her voice hushed. “I’ve never seen anything like that,” she said, her awe palpable.

Celenneth nodded, her expression softer than usual. “Nor have I.”

Ellarien, ever composed, stepped out of the water and began gathering her clothes. “A rare sight,” she said quietly.

They dried off, the sun warming their skin as they dressed and packed their now-clean clothes. The moment had left them quiet, their earlier laughter replaced with a sense of reverence. As they resumed their journey, the memory of the majestic beasts stayed with them, a fleeting glimpse of grace in the wild.

*Narquelië 30, 2965*

The following day unfolded like a dream. The air was crisp and cool, with a gentle breeze carrying the scent of wildflowers and fresh grass. The sky stretched endlessly above, a brilliant blue unmarred by even the faintest wisp of cloud. The horses moved effortlessly over the smooth terrain, their steps light and steady. Ellarien, riding at the head of the group, looked almost relaxed.

Linnea rode beside Celenneth, her splinted arm no longer a burden. Her smile was easy, her posture relaxed as the group pressed onward with an unhurried rhythm. Even Nimroch, ever the stalwart companion, seemed to enjoy the pace, his ears flicking lazily as he walked.

When the sun was nearly overhead, Linnea slowed Mareth, her gaze narrowing as she looked ahead. Her good hand tightened on the reins, and she tilted her head as if listening to a sound too faint for others to hear. Celenneth noticed the change immediately. “What is it?” she asked, her voice low but alert.

Linnea didn’t answer right away. She closed her eyes briefly, her brow furrowing, before turning to Celenneth. “I don’t know,” she said, her tone thoughtful. “It’s... something. Someone. Close, but not threatening.” She looked back toward Ellarien, who had also slowed, her sharp eyes scanning the horizon.

The group continued, the perfect day now tinged with curiosity. It wasn’t long before Linnea’s instincts were vindicated. Standing on a low hill ahead was a figure, their form partially obscured by the sunlight streaming from behind them. They were tall and slender, their posture regal but unassuming. The figure’s garments shimmered faintly, a blend of earth tones and silver that seemed to shift with the breeze.

As they approached, the figure turned toward them, and Linnea felt a warmth radiate from them—not physical, but a profound sense of peace and safety. Their features became clearer: a being of otherworldly beauty, their skin luminous and their eyes holding an ancient, knowing kindness. Their long hair, the color of autumn leaves, cascaded down their back, and in their hand, they carried a slender staff adorned with a single, glowing crystal.

Linnea stopped her mare instinctively, her breath catching in her throat. “They’re... not like anyone I’ve ever seen,” she whispered, her voice tinged with awe.

Ellarien dismounted, her movements slow and deliberate, as if the being’s presence demanded reverence. “An emissary,” she said softly. “Or perhaps more.” She glanced at Celenneth, her expression unreadable. “We should tread carefully.”

The figure raised a hand, a gesture of greeting as they waited for the group to draw closer, their serene gaze fixed upon them. The air around them seemed lighter, imbued with a subtle hum of energy that Linnea felt in her very bones.

As the group approached the figure, the air seemed to grow still, the wind itself holding its breath. Celenneth kept Nimroch’s reins tight, her gaze sharp and wary. Something about the being’s presence set her on edge. Its serene, almost ethereal demeanor felt unnatural, a perfection that bordered on unsettling.

“Stay back,” she said, her voice low but firm. “We don’t know what it is.”

Linnea glanced at her, the warmth of the figure’s presence still washing over her. “It’s not dangerous,” she said softly, her tone filled with quiet certainty. She dismounted smoothly, her steps deliberate as she walked forward. Celenneth reached out as if to stop her but hesitated, watching as Linnea moved closer.

Ellarien stood silently, her sharp eyes flicking between Linnea and the figure, her hand resting lightly on the hilt of her blade but making no move to draw it.

Linnea approached the figure, her good hand raised slightly in a gesture of peace. “You’re not of this world, are you?” she asked, her voice calm and gentle, though curiosity laced her words. The being inclined its head, its expression one of serene acknowledgment.

“Few recognize what lies beyond the veil,” the spirit said, its voice like the distant rustle of leaves, layered with a quiet, ancient wisdom. “But you carry a light within you, Linnea, one that sees beyond shadow.”

Linnea blinked, startled by the being’s knowledge of her name. But instead of fear, she felt a deep, abiding calm. “If I carry light,” she said softly, “it’s because others have shared theirs with me.”

The spirit’s lips curved into a faint smile, its crystalline eyes locking onto hers. “A rare humility. Such binds the broken threads of this world.”

Behind her, Celenneth’s hand rested on her bow, her distrust clear in her posture. “Linnea, we don’t know what this thing wants,” she called out, her voice tense. “Be careful.”

Linnea glanced back at her companion, her expression gentle but resolute. “It doesn’t want to harm us, Celenneth,” she said. Turning back to the figure, she added, “We are meeting for a reason. Why?”

The spirit reached into the folds of its shimmering garments and withdrew a small object—a ring, delicate and crafted from silver that gleamed with an otherworldly sheen. It stepped forward, holding the ring out to Linnea, its expression unreadable.

“This is a gift for you,” it said, its voice soft but clear. “A token of recognition for your kindness, your trust. It will aid you in understanding the hearts and minds of others, should you use it wisely.”

Linnea accepted the ring with both hands, her movements reverent. The metal was cool against her skin, but as she slipped it onto her finger, a warmth spread through her, subtle but invigorating. “Thank you,” she said simply, her voice filled with gratitude.

The spirit inclined its head once more, then turned and began to fade, its form dissolving into the golden light of the afternoon as if it had never been there. The air around them seemed to exhale, the tension breaking as the group stood in silence.

Celenneth approached Linnea, her brows furrowed. “You shouldn’t have trusted it so easily,” she said, her voice tinged with frustration. “It could have been a trap.”

Linnea looked down at the ring, turning it over in her fingers before meeting Celenneth’s gaze. “Maybe,” she said quietly. “But sometimes trust is worth the risk. And this...” She held up the ring with a faint smile. “This feels right.”

Ellarien watched them both, her expression unreadable. “You may have made a friend today,” she said simply, her voice carrying a note of approval. “Or something greater.”

As the group mounted their horses and resumed their journey, the ring on Linnea’s finger glinted faintly in the sunlight.

*Hísimë 1, 2965*

The air was heavy, the warmth of the sun tempered by a faint wind that seemed to whisper unease. The golden grasses of Rhovanion stretched endlessly before them, but there was no comfort in the expanse. Celenneth rode ahead, her gaze sharper than ever, scanning the horizon with an intensity that put the group on edge.

The feeling of foreboding crystallized. Celenneth spotted them first: dark shapes moving across the plain, their forms low and quick, their pace relentless. She slowed Nimroch and raised a hand, signaling the group to stop. “Orcs,” she said, her voice flat and grim.

Linnea’s eyes narrowed, following Celenneth’s line of sight. The figures became clearer as they closed the distance—at least a dozen orcs, clad in crude armor and wielding jagged weapons. At their head was a hulking figure, his blackened helm adorned with a twisted spike. His gait was purposeful, his movements betraying a cruel confidence.

“That’s no ordinary band,” Ellarien muttered, her voice tight. “That’s a hunting party.”

The lead orc raised a massive hand, his guttural voice carrying over the plains. He bellowed in the Black Speech, and gestured towards them.

Celenneth felt her blood run cold. “Dol Guldur,” she whispered, her grip tightening on Nimroch’s reins. “That’s who sent them.”

Linnea turned to her, her voice low and urgent. “What do we do?”

Celenneth glanced at Ellarien, whose face was set with grim determination. “Run. Only fight if we must,” the elf said, her hand already on her bow. “But these odds are not in our favor.”

Celenneth’s mind raced. The open plains offered no cover, no refuge. The orcs would overtake them before they could find safety. But fleeing blindly wasn’t an option either—not unless they could find a way to slow their pursuers.

The lead orc barked another command, and the band surged forward, their guttural cries tearing through the stillness of the plains. Celenneth pulled Nimroch around, her voice steady but sharp. “If we can thin their numbers, we might stand a chance.”

Linnea nodded, drawing her dagger with her good hand. “If it’s a fight they want,” she said grimly, “they’ll get one.”

“Their leader,” Ellarien said, nocking an arrow and aiming toward the advancing horde. “If we take him down, the rest may falter.”

The orcs closed in, their grotesque faces twisted with savage glee. The lead orc bared his jagged teeth, his guttural laughter echoing across the plains. “You’ll wish you’d died in Mirkwood!” he roared, his blade gleaming cruelly in the midday sun.

Celenneth stood firm, her bow drawn and ready, her heart pounding as the orcs charged. Her mind raced as they closed the distance. Their jagged weapons glinted cruelly in the sunlight, and their guttural shouts filled the air, promising bloodshed. She clenched her jaw, her hand tightening on Nimroch’s reins. A direct fight was a death sentence, and flight on the open plains seemed impossible. But the land itself might offer an edge.

Her sharp eyes swept the horizon, catching sight of a distant cluster of tall grasses and uneven terrain to the west. A shallow depression, likely a dried-up streambed, cut through the land. It wasn’t much, but it could be enough to create confusion. “This way!” she barked, spurring Nimroch forward.

Ellarien and Linnea followed without hesitation, their horses pounding across the plains as Celenneth veered sharply toward the depression. “We’ll use the land against them,” she shouted over the wind. “Force them to follow a path where they can’t see us all at once!”

As they reached the edge of the streambed, Celenneth dismounted, her boots hitting the ground with practiced ease. “Ellarien,” she said, her voice quick but steady, “take Linnea and the horses into the tall grass. Move slow, keep low, and circle back to the east.”

Ellarien hesitated for only a moment before nodding. “And you?”

“I’ll lead them into the streambed,” Celenneth replied, already stringing an arrow to her bow. “If they think I’m alone, they’ll focus on me.”

Linnea’s eyes widened. “You can’t take them on by yourself!”

“I won’t have to,” Celenneth said, glancing at Ellarien. “The streambed will bottleneck them. Once they’re in, I’ll climb out and regroup with you. They’ll be stuck chasing ghosts.”

The plan snapped into motion with desperate precision. Celenneth stood at the edge of the depression, loosing a single arrow toward the advancing orcs. It struck one in the shoulder, causing him to howl in pain and stagger back. The rest howled in fury, their leader bellowing, “After her! She’s alone!”

Celenneth darted into the streambed, her footsteps quick and deliberate as she led the orcs into the narrow corridor of earth. The high banks forced them to follow single file, their shouts and curses echoing around her. She fired another arrow, striking one of the lead orcs in the leg, slowing their advance further.

Meanwhile, Ellarien guided Linnea and the horses through the tall grass. Their movements were careful but swift, the cover of the grass obscuring them from view. Linnea clutched the reins tightly, her heart pounding as she listened to the orcs’ shouts grow distant.

At the narrowest point of the streambed, Celenneth vaulted up the bank with a burst of speed, using Nimroch’s reins for leverage. She scrambled to the top just as the orcs reached the bottleneck below. From her vantage point, she loosed another arrow, striking the leader’s helmet with a resounding clang. “Up here, you fool!” she shouted, goading them.

The orcs roared in frustration, clambering after her as she disappeared over the ridge. The uneven terrain slowed their pursuit, and Celenneth used the precious moments to regroup with Ellarien and Linnea. Together, they mounted swiftly and urged their horses into a gallop, angling away from the orcs and toward open plains.

The orcs emerged from the depression too late, their leader bellowing in fury as he saw the group disappearing into the distance. The uneven terrain and the bottleneck had cost them precious time, and their prey was now well beyond reach.

As the plains stretched out before them, the group slowed, their breaths ragged but relieved. Linnea turned to Celenneth, her face a mix of anger and awe. “That was reckless,” she said, her voice shaking. “But it worked.”

Celenneth gave a faint, tired smile, her hands steady on Nimroch’s reins. “Sometimes, the best fight is the one you don’t have to finish.”

Ellarien glanced back, her sharp eyes scanning for signs of pursuit. “They’ll find us again if they want to,” she said grimly. “But for now, we’ve bought ourselves time.”

The group pressed onward, the adrenaline of the near-escape still thrumming in their veins. The plains felt less open now, the shadows of unseen threats lingering at the edges of their thoughts. The orcs were a reminder that danger was never far, even on the sunlit grasslands.
