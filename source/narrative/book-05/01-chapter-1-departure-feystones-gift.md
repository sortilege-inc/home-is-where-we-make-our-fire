# Chapter 1: Departure / Feystone’s Gift

*Book 5*

*Narquelië 19, 2965*

The morning dawned crisp and clear, the forest still wrapped in the gentle haze of dawn. Celenneth and Linnea stood at the edge of the woodland halls, flanked by Thalion’s scouts. Eryndor and Arannis lingered with them, their farewells imbued with quiet tenderness. Arannis pressed her hands to Celenneth’s shoulders, her gaze steady and warm. “The forest will hold you in its care, even as we must let you go,” she murmured.

Eryndor stood before Linnea, his amber eyes filled with a blend of admiration and gentle sorrow. “May your path be clear and your arrows strike true,” he said, clasping her hands with a firm grip. He stepped back, and added, “We will meet again when the winds bring you.”

Thalion approached, leading a sleek chestnut mare whose coat gleamed in the morning light. “Linnea,” he said, “this is Mareth. She will carry you swiftly. When your journey leads beyond the forest, she will return to us with our scouts.” Linnea nodded, her hand brushing over the mare’s soft muzzle as she murmured her thanks. The party mounted—Celenneth astride Nimroch, Linnea on Mareth, the scouts riding ahead.

The scouts, Ellarien and Thalanar, moved with the natural grace of those born to the forest. Ellarien was lithe with her deep chestnut hair, . Her armor, woven with fine elvish craftsmanship in muted greens and browns, rendered her nearly invisible among the trees.

Thalanar, tall and rugged tied back his midnight-black hair in a loose braid, revealing a youthful face, prematurely weathered. He wore a dark gray cloak, with silver embroidery resembling flowing rivers.

They soon fell into a rhythm, Celenneth and Linnea adapting quickly to their new companions. The elves' quiet competence was a relief.

Suddenly, Celenneth pulled up on Nimroch. "Isn't it true that there is a story of a great hart in these woods?" she asked the scouts. The scouts exchanged brief glances, and Ellarien murmured, “It's true; a great hart roams this part of the forest. Its antlers span wide as a king’s crown, none have caught it.” Linnea grinned, nudging Celenneth. “A challenge?”

Celenneth’s eyes gleamed with determination. “A challenge,” she agreed, sliding from Nimroch’s back. She crouched low, studying the tracks with an experienced eye. “This way,” she said, her voice certain. The party followed as she moved with practiced ease, her senses sharp and attuned to the forest’s subtleties.

Hours passed in pursuit, the trail twisting and fading, only to reappear under Celenneth’s discerning gaze. Finally, they found it—a magnificent hart grazing in a sunlit glade. Its antlers gleamed, adorned with moss and small flowers, as if the forest itself crowned the creature. Celenneth motioned for silence, her bow already in hand. With measured breath, she nocked an arrow, aimed, and loosed. The arrow flew true, striking cleanly. The hart faltered and fell, its death swift and merciful.

The scouts stepped forward, murmuring words of respect as they knelt beside the creature. Celenneth approached, her expression a mix of triumph and reverence. “A worthy quarry,” she said softly, her hand brushing over the hart’s coat. Linnea dismounted, clasping Celenneth’s arm. “You never cease to amaze,” she said with a grin.

The hart was dressed with care, its meat divided among the party to sustain them in the days ahead. Its rack was given to Thalanar, the trophy a gift to the Woodland elves.

The night had grown quiet, the only sounds the crackle of the fire and the soft murmurs of the forest. The scouts, Ellarien and Thalanar, sat cross-legged near the flame, their silhouettes sharp against the flickering light. Ellarien, ever watchful, produced a worn leather pouch from her satchel, a faint smile tugging at the corners of her lips.

“Time to show you how the elves celebrate a good hunt,” she said, her voice low but rich with a hint of mischief. She tapped a pinch of fragrant pipeweed into a finely carved wooden pipe, its bowl stained dark from years of use. The scent of the herb was earthy, with a sharp, citrusy undertone that cut through the lingering aroma of roasted meat.

“Pipeweed?” Linnea asked, raising an eyebrow as she adjusted her cloak.

Ellarien laughed, the sound unexpectedly coarse and unguarded. “Not your Dale crop, if that’s what you’re thinking. This is feystone’s gift—burn it, breathe it deep, and it’ll take you places you’ve never been.” She struck a spark and lit the pipe, drawing in a deep pull before passing it to Thalanar, who took it without ceremony.

Thalanar exhaled a plume of smoke with a grunt, his sharp features softening momentarily. “It’s not for the faint-hearted,” he said, handing the pipe to Linnea with a smirk.

Linnea took a tentative draw, coughing at first before finding a rhythm. The smoke filled her lungs, and the world around her seemed to tilt, colors growing impossibly vibrant. She laughed—a high, giddy sound—as the fire seemed to leap and dance in impossible shapes. “The flames,” she said, wide-eyed, “they’re... alive.”

Ellarien chuckled, taking the pipe and offering it to Celenneth. “Good, isn’t it? Watch your step, though. The visions can be kind... or cruel.”

Celenneth took the pipe and inhaled deeply. At first, the effect was warm and intoxicating. The trees seemed to hum with life, their branches weaving into patterns that hinted at some unknowable language. Celenneth saw stars blooming in the sky like flowers and heard the laughter of unseen voices.

But then, the warmth twisted. The stars dimmed, replaced by swirling darkness. The laughter turned to screams, sharp and haunting. Celenneth stumbled to her feet, her breath ragged. She was no longer beside the fire. The trees loomed tall and hostile, their gnarled branches reaching for her like claws. A shadow moved between them—a figure she couldn’t quite see, but whose presence bore down on her like a weight she couldn’t escape.

“Celenneth!” Linnea’s voice broke through the vision, distant and echoing. Celenneth turned, but Linnea wasn’t there—only more shadows. A guttural whisper filled the air, repeating her name in a tone that made her skin crawl. The shadow grew, its formless shape blotting out the sky. “You cannot outrun the dark,” it said, the words a dreadful certainty.

Back at the camp, Linnea sat transfixed, giggling at a vision of dancing animals that only she could see. Ellarien and Thalanar watched her with bemusement, occasionally exchanging their own puffs of the pipeweed. Thalanar glanced at Celenneth, who now stood rigid and trembling near the fire.

“What’s wrong with her?” Thalanar asked, his voice gruff.

Ellarien’s smile faded. “Not everyone sees joy,” she said, standing and moving to Celenneth. “Sometimes the pipeweed shows truths we’d rather not face.”

Celenneth gasped as she came back to herself, the firelight harsh against her tear-streaked face. Her hand gripped Ellarien’s arm like a lifeline. “The shadow,” she whispered. “It’s coming.” Her voice was hoarse, raw with fear.

Linnea’s laughter faltered as she looked at Celenneth, her own vision fading. “What shadow?” she asked, her voice filled with concern.

Ellarien guided Celenneth to sit, her grip firm but kind. “Whatever you saw, it hasn’t happened yet,” she said quietly. “Feystone's gift doesn’t lie, but it doesn’t exactly tell the truth, either.”

Celenneth stared into the flames, her mind racing. The warmth of the pipeweed had vanished, replaced by a cold certainty: the darkness they sought to outrun was closer than she’d thought.

Linnea reached out to Celenneth, her hand tentative as it hovered just above her shoulder. “Celenneth,” she said softly, her voice carrying both concern and confusion. “Whatever you saw, it wasn’t real. It was the pipeweed. You’re safe here.”

Celenneth stiffened under Linnea’s touch, her body rigid and unyielding. She shrugged off the hand as if it burned her, her eyes locked on the flickering fire. “I don’t need comfort,” she snapped, her voice harsher than intended. “It doesn’t matter if it was real or not. What I saw—what I felt—was a warning. One I can’t ignore.”

Linnea stepped back, her lips pressed into a thin line. “Fine,” she said, her tone edged with frustration but tempered by worry. “But you don’t have to face it alone.”

Celenneth didn’t answer, her gaze unwavering as the flames danced in the darkness. The silence stretched between them, thick and uncomfortable. Linnea sighed and moved to her bedroll, lying down with her back to the fire. Sleep came fitfully, her dreams punctuated by the occasional crackle of burning wood and the faint murmur of voices.

As the hours dragged on, Celenneth remained by the fire, her thoughts churning. The shadow’s words echoed in her mind, heavy and relentless. *You cannot outrun the dark.* She poked at the fire with a stick, sending embers swirling into the night air, their brief light consumed by the oppressive blackness beyond.

Thalanar, who had been sitting quietly nearby, finally broke the silence. “You won’t find answers in the flames,” he said, his voice rough and low. He wasn’t looking at her, instead sharpening the edge of his blade with slow, deliberate strokes.

Celenneth glanced at him, her expression weary but sharp. “No, but I might find them in vigilance.”

Thalanar snorted, setting his blade aside. “You’ll find nothing but exhaustion. The shadow you saw—if it’s anything like the things I’ve seen—feeds on that. Sleep, ranger.”

Celenneth shook her head. “Not yet. I’ll take the first watch.”

Thalanar’s mouth twitched into something that might have been a smile. “Stubborn,” he muttered. “Fine, but don’t let it eat you alive.”

The night deepened, the forest around them settling into uneasy quiet. Celenneth sat alone, the fire casting sharp shadows across her face. Her mind wandered, replaying every detail of the vision in search of meaning. She didn’t look up when Thalanar approached hours later, his boots crunching softly on the forest floor.

“Your turn,” she said flatly, not waiting for him to reply before standing and stepping away from the fire. She brushed past him, her shoulders tight with unspent tension.

Celenneth found her way to her bedroll, lying down with her back to the camp. Sleep didn’t come easily, but she knew that her strength wouldn’t last forever. For now, the fire still burned, and Thalanar stood watch, his presence a quiet assurance against the shadows pressing in.
