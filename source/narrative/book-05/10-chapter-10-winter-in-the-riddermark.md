# Chapter 10: Winter in the Riddermark

*Book 5*

Winter in the Riddermark settled like a quiet blanket over the rolling plains, the golden grasses dusted with frost and the air sharp with the bite of cold mornings. Inside the warmth of Freowyn’s home, Linnea found herself dividing her days between tending to the now-thriving Brynja and pushing herself to master the equestrian arts of Rohan under the guidance of a stern but skilled horsewoman named Eadwyn.

At Freowyn's hearth, Linnea often sat with Brynja nestled against her chest, rocking her gently as she murmured lullabies learned from Freowyn’s lips. The child had grown stronger since they found her, her cries now robust, her bright eyes filled with curiosity. Linnea found herself enchanted by the tiny fingers that gripped her own and the soft coos that could make Celenneth giggle.

Yet, Linnea was not one to idle for long. After ensuring Brynja was settled with Freowyn, she would don her riding gear and stride out to the training paddocks, where Eadwyn waited. A tall, imposing woman with sharp features and an unyielding manner, Eadwyn was not one for coddling.

“Mount with purpose,” Eadwyn barked as Linnea approached one frosty morning, her steely gaze appraising. “A rider commands their horse, not the other way around.”

The lessons were grueling, the techniques complex. Linnea had always been competent in the saddle, but the Rohirrim’s methods demanded more than basic competence. Eadwyn drilled her on the finer points of balance, on controlling a steed with the lightest pressure of her knees, on shifting her weight seamlessly during a gallop.

“Your horse is an extension of you,” Eadwyn lectured as Linnea guided her mount through a tight turn. “Feel her movements as if they were your own. Trust her, and she will trust you.”

Linnea clenched her jaw, her determination burning brighter with each correction. By the end of the first month, she could ride bareback with confidence, a skill Eadwyn praised grudgingly. “You’re not hopeless,” the older woman remarked, her tone almost teasing.

Linnea responded with a smirk. “That’s high praise coming from you.”

Evenings brought Linnea back to Freowyn’s hearth, where she would collapse into a chair with an exaggerated groan. Freowyn would chuckle softly, setting a bowl of stew before her as Brynja babbled happily from her cradle.

“She missed you today,” Freowyn said one night, handing Linnea the baby. “She knows your voice, you know. She lights up whenever you’re near.”

Linnea grinned despite her weariness, cradling Brynja close. “She’s lucky she doesn’t have to deal with Eadwyn. That woman could scare the frost off the plains.”

Freowyn laughed, shaking her head. “Eadwyn is harsh, but she means well. You’ll thank her when you’re riding as well as the best of the Rohirrim.”

Linnea kissed the top of Brynja's head, her heart full despite the ache in her muscles. “Maybe,” she said softly, “but for now, this is the best part of my day.”

The sound of boots crunching on the frost outside drew Linnea’s attention, and her heart quickened as the door creaked open. Celenneth stepped inside, her frame illuminated briefly by the cold blue light of the evening before the door closed behind her. She looked winded but radiant, her cheeks flushed from the ride, and her auburn hair escaped in wild strands from beneath her cloak.

“You’re back!” Linnea exclaimed, a smile lighting up her face. She scooped up Brynja, standing to meet Celenneth. “I wasn’t expecting you for another two days.”

Celenneth smiled softly, a rare tenderness in her eyes. “I rode hard,” she said simply, her gaze flicking to Brynja. “I missed you both.”

After shedding her cloak and boots, Celenneth approached Linnea, who held Brynja securely in her arms. She placed a hand on Linnea’s shoulder and leaned down to kiss Brynja's head, her expression softening even further as the baby cooed in recognition.

Linnea chuckled, shifting Brynja to her other arm. “She missed you too, though she’s been perfectly spoiled by Freowyn.”

“I’m sure she has,” Celenneth said, her tone light. “But tonight, it’s your turn to be spoiled.”

Linnea blinked, tilting her head in curiosity. “What do you mean?”

Celenneth reached into the pouch at her belt, retrieving something carefully wrapped in cloth. She handed it to Linnea, her lips curving into a faint smile. “Open it.”

Linnea adjusted Brynja in one arm while unwrapping the parcel with the other. Her breath caught as she uncovered its contents: a delicate necklace of braided silver, its centerpiece a small, gleaming stone that caught the firelight like a fragment of a star.

“Celenneth,” Linnea murmured, her voice soft with wonder. “It’s beautiful. Where did you...?”

Celenneth’s smile widened slightly. “I had a smith in an outlying settlement make it for me, to repay me for a favor owed. I thought... you deserved something to remind you of the stars, even when the sky is hidden.”

Linnea’s cheeks flushed, and she looked up at Celenneth with a warmth that reached her eyes. “You rode for days, fought gods-know-what alongside the Rohirrim, and still thought to bring me this?”

“I did,” Celenneth said simply.

Linnea set Brynja in her cradle by the fire and stepped closer to Celenneth, her fingers brushing the necklace. “Put it on me,” she said, her voice a soft command.

Celenneth obliged, her fingers deft as she fastened the necklace around Linnea’s neck. The cool weight of the silver settled against her skin, and she turned to face Celenneth fully, her smile widening.

“Thank you,” Linnea said, her voice low and full of meaning. She reached up, her hand resting lightly against Celenneth’s cheek. “You’re full of surprises, you know that?”

Celenneth just smiled knowingly, as Linnea rose onto her toes, pressing her lips to Celenneth’s in a kiss that was slow and deep, filled with the affection and familiarity that had bonded them.

Linnea laughed softly, her forehead resting against Celenneth’s. “You’ve set the bar awfully high, you know. I’ll have to think of something remarkable for your next return.”

Celenneth smirked, her arms slipping around Linnea’s waist. “I look forward to it.”

⁂
The morning air was sharp, biting but invigorating, as the sun cast its golden light over the snow-draped fields of Rohan. The snow glittered like stars fallen to earth, a quiet blanket softening the world. Celenneth stood beside Nimroch, the stallion’s breath clouding the frosty air, as she finished tying the last of the bindings that secured Brynja snugly to her chest. The baby was swaddled warmly in layers of wool, her tiny face peeking out, pink from the cold and wide with wonder at the bright, shimmering landscape.

Linnea emerged from Freowyn’s house, bundled in a thick cloak borrowed from their hosts. She paused at the sight before her: Celenneth, standing tall and resolute, the baby nestled close, and Nimroch pawing the snowy ground impatiently. Linnea smiled, her heart warming despite the chill. “You look like a proper mother wolf with her cub,” she teased, adjusting her cloak.

Celenneth gave her a faint smile, her hand resting on Brynja's small back. “I thought today would be a good day to ride. The snow, the air—it feels... peaceful. Like the earth is embracing winter one last time before spring.”

With a practiced motion, Celenneth swung up onto Nimroch’s back, the bindings holding Brynja secure against her chest. The baby cooed softly, her tiny fingers brushing against the fabric of Celenneth’s cloak. Linnea climbed up behind them, her arms wrapping around Celenneth’s waist, her cheek pressing lightly against her shoulder.

“Ready?” Celenneth asked, her voice steady and calm.

“Always,” Linnea replied with a grin.

They rode out into the snowy plains, Nimroch’s hooves crunching softly with each step. The crisp air filled their lungs, carrying with it the faint scent of pine and distant hearth fires. Brynja's soft gurgles mixed with the quiet sounds of the journey, and Linnea occasionally leaned forward to murmur nonsense to the baby, her voice warm and light.

As they crested a small rise, the full expanse of the Riddermark spread out before them. The hills rolled like waves frozen in time, the snow unbroken except for the faint tracks of wandering deer. Above, the sky was a clear, endless blue, the sun casting long shadows that danced over the ground.

Celenneth slowed Nimroch, letting the horse breathe as she took in the sight. “This,” she said quietly, “is why I wanted to ride today. A moment to remember that the world isn’t all shadow and fear.”

Linnea rested her chin on Celenneth’s shoulder, her eyes softening. “You always find a way to surprise me,” she said. “And remind me why I followed you out here.”
