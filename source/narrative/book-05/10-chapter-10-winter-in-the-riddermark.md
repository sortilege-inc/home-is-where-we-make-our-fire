# Chapter 10: Winter in the Riddermark

*Narvinyë 20, 2966*

Winter lay over the Riddermark like a quiet blanket, the golden grass dusted white, the mornings sharp enough to sting. Linnea divided her days between the warmth of Freowyn's hearth, where Brynja grew stronger week by week, and the training paddocks, where she pushed herself to master the horse-craft of Rohan under a stern horsewoman named Eadwyn.

At Freowyn's fire she would sit with Brynja against her chest, rocking her and murmuring the lullabies she was learning from Freowyn's own lips. The child had thrived since the borderlands — her cries robust now, her dark eyes bright with curiosity, tiny fingers closing hard around Linnea's. She found herself enchanted by the small warm weight of her, by the coos that could set even Celenneth to soft laughter.

But Linnea was not made to idle, and once Brynja was settled she would pull on her riding gear and go out to where Eadwyn waited — tall, sharp-featured, and utterly without patience for coddling.

"Mount with purpose," Eadwyn barked one frost-bright morning, her steely gaze appraising. "A rider commands the horse. Not the other way round."

The lessons were grueling. Linnea had always been sound in the saddle, but the Rohirrim asked far more than soundness — balance, the guiding of a horse by the lightest press of the knee, the seamless shift of weight through a gallop. "Your horse is an extension of you," Eadwyn lectured as Linnea worked a tight turn. "Feel her as you feel your own body. Trust her, and she trusts you." Linnea set her jaw and burned brighter under each correction, and by winter's deep she could ride bareback with confidence — a thing Eadwyn allowed, grudging, was "not hopeless."

"High praise, from you," Linnea shot back, and earned the ghost of a smile. She had no horse of her own to school — her Woodland mare had gone back to Thalion with the scouts — and so she rode the Mark's, learning each new mount as Eadwyn set her to it.[^r-riding]

Evenings brought her back to Freowyn's hearth, where she'd drop into a chair with a groan and Freowyn would set a bowl of stew before her and Brynja would babble from her cradle. "She missed you today," Freowyn said one night, handing her the baby. "She knows your voice. Lights up whenever you're near." Linnea grinned through her weariness and kissed the top of Brynja's dark head. "For now," she murmured, "this is the best part of my day."

⁂

Boots crunched the frost outside, and Linnea's pulse leapt as the door swung open on the cold blue evening and Celenneth stepped through it — winded and windblown and radiant, her cheeks whipped red from the ride, her auburn hair escaping wild from beneath her hood.

"You're back!" Linnea scooped Brynja up and rose to meet her. "I didn't look for you for another two days."

"I rode hard." Celenneth's gaze went to the baby, and a rare softness came into her. "I missed you both." She shed her cloak and boots and came to press a kiss to Brynja's head, and the child cooed in recognition.

"She's been thoroughly spoiled by Freowyn," Linnea said, shifting the baby to her other arm.

"I'm sure. But tonight it's your turn." Celenneth reached into the pouch at her belt and drew out something wrapped in cloth, and pressed it into Linnea's free hand. "Open it."

Linnea unwrapped it one-handed, and her breath caught: a fine necklace of braided silver, its centerpiece a small gleaming stone that took the firelight like a fragment of a fallen star. "Celenneth — it's beautiful. Where did you—"

"A smith in one of the outlying settlements made it, for a favor I was owed." Her smile widened. "I wanted you to have something to remember the stars by. Even when the sky's hidden."

Linnea looked up, warmth flooding her. "You rode for days, and fought whatever the Rohirrim throw themselves at, and still thought of this."

"I did."

Linnea set Brynja down in her cradle and stepped close. "Put it on me." Celenneth's deft fingers fastened it at her nape, the cool silver settling against her skin, and Linnea turned to face her and rose onto her toes and kissed her, slow and deep and full of all the years they'd promised each other at Yule.[^t-pendant]

"You've set the bar high," Linnea murmured against her mouth. "I'll have to think of something remarkable for your next homecoming."

Celenneth's arms slipped around her waist. "I look forward to it."

⁂

The morning was sharp and bright, the sun laying gold across snow-draped fields that glittered like fallen stars. Linnea came out of Freowyn's house bundled in a borrowed cloak and stopped at the sight of it: Celenneth standing tall beside Nimroch, finishing the last of the bindings that held Brynja snug against her chest, the baby swaddled in wool with only her small face showing, pink and wide-eyed with wonder at the shining world.

"You look a proper mother-wolf with her cub," Linnea teased.

Celenneth gave her a faint smile, a hand resting on Brynja's small back. "I thought today was a good day to ride. The snow, the air — it feels peaceful. Like the earth holding winter close one last time before spring." She swung up onto Nimroch, the baby secure against her, and Linnea climbed up behind, arms circling Celenneth's waist, her cheek to her shoulder.

"Ready?" Celenneth asked.

"Always."

They rode out onto the white plains, Nimroch's hooves crunching soft, the cold air sweet with pine and distant hearth-smoke, Brynja's small gurgles threading through it all. And when they crested a rise and the whole Riddermark opened below them — the hills like frozen waves, the snow unbroken but for the tracks of wandering deer, the sky an endless blue — Celenneth slowed the horse and let him breathe.

"This," she said quietly, "is why I wanted to ride today. To remember the world isn't all shadow and fear."

Linnea rested her chin on her shoulder, and looked at the two of them before her — the woman she had bound her life to, and the child they had pulled from the cold — and felt, for the first time in a long road of them, something that was not quite like anything she had lost, and better than anything she had dreamed. "You always find a way to surprise me," she said. "And to remind me why I followed you out here."

[^r-riding]: **Advancement (Riddermark) — Riding.** Through the winter Linnea trains in the horse-craft of Rohan under the horsemistress Eadwyn — riding the Mark's horses, having none of her own — until she can ride bareback with true confidence.
[^t-pendant]: **Gift — the star-pendant** (braided silver, a star-bright stone): commissioned by Celenneth for Linnea in payment of a favor owed, that she might "remember the stars even when the sky is hidden." A keepsake of the winter, and of the oath they swore.
