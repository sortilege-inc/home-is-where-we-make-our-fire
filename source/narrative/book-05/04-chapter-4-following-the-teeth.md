# Chapter 4: Following the Teeth

*Book 5*

*Narquelië 26, 2965*

With dawn came a bitter chill, the wind sweeping across the plains with an edge that seemed to cut through even the thickest cloaks. The group moved swiftly despite the cold, their progress steady as the sun rose higher, bathing the grasslands in golden light. Celenneth led the way, her sharp eyes scanning the horizon for any sign of movement, while Ellarien rode at the rear, her presence a quiet assurance against unseen threats.

By midday, they paused near a shallow stream to let the horses drink and to check their supplies. It was then that Ellarien, her sharp features clouded with irritation, pulled open one of the satchels tied to Mareth.

“They’re gone,” she said flatly, holding up an empty sack that should have been filled with rations. The rest of the group froze, their expressions ranging from disbelief to concern.

“What do you mean, gone?” Thalion’s mare tossed her head nervously, sensing the rising tension.

“Exactly what I said,” Ellarien replied, her tone clipped. “The satchel’s torn. Likely happened back in that cursed marshland—we must have lost most of the rations there.”

Celenneth’s face darkened, her jaw tightening as she crouched to examine the other bags. The realization hit hard: they had only a handful of dried meats and hard biscuits left, barely enough to sustain them for another day.

Linnea exhaled sharply, her good hand brushing over her face. “We’re not turning back,” she said firmly, glancing between Ellarien and Celenneth. “We’ll make do. We have to.”

Without another word, Celenneth slung her bow across her shoulder and strode off into the tall grasses, her movements quick and purposeful. “I’ll find game,” she called over her shoulder. “Settle the horses and wait.”

Linnea glanced at the ground, her gaze sharp. “I’ll look for mushrooms,” she said, already kneeling to search the damp soil near the stream. Ellarien watched them both, her expression unreadable as she began unpacking what little food they had left, rationing it into careful portions.

Celenneth moved with practiced ease through the plains, her steps silent and her senses attuned to the land. It wasn’t long before she spotted movement—a small rabbit darting between the grasses. She nocked an arrow and drew her bow, her breath steady as she tracked the creature. The shot was clean, the rabbit falling instantly. She murmured a quiet thanks to the land as she retrieved her kill, her sharp knife already in hand.

Back at the stream, Linnea’s search proved fruitful. She knelt beside a cluster of mushrooms growing in the shade of a low bush, their caps smooth and brown. She hesitated for a moment, then picked a handful, her eyes flicking toward Ellarien. “Are these safe?”

Ellarien stepped closer, crouching to examine the mushrooms. She nodded after a moment. “Fieldcaps. Common here. They’ll keep you alive, though I wouldn’t call them delicious.”

By the time Celenneth returned with her catch, the group had rekindled their fire, its warmth a welcome reprieve from the growing chill. Linnea offered the mushrooms, while Ellarien helped Celenneth prepare the rabbit, her hands deft as she skinned and cleaned it.

The meal was modest but sustaining, the rabbit roasted over the fire and the mushrooms added to a thin soup made from their remaining supplies. Linnea sat close to Celenneth, her expression weary but determined. “We’ll be fine,” Celenneth said quietly, her gaze steady. “This is something I do know.”

The sun rose over the plains in a warm golden arc. The air carried the freshness of a world still waking, and the group set out with renewed determination. The horses moved easily over the smooth terrain, their hooves thudding softly against the packed earth. Even Linnea, her arm still splinted, managed the reins with confidence, her mare responding well to her touch.

Ellarien rode beside Celenneth at the front of the group, her sharp eyes scanning the horizon. “The weather holds,” she said quietly. “If the land is as kind as the skies, we’ll make good progress today.”

Celenneth nodded but kept her focus ahead. The openness of the plains brought its own kind of unease—a feeling of being exposed, of unseen dangers lurking just beyond the curve of the horizon. Still, the day was calm, the wind carrying only the scent of grass and distant wildflowers.

It was shortly after midday when a rise in the land revealed a peculiar formation in the distance—a jagged cluster of rocks jutting sharply from the otherwise flat plain. The rocks stood tall, weathered and cracked by centuries of wind and rain, their blackened surfaces glinting faintly in the sunlight. Celenneth pulled Nimroch to a halt, her eyes narrowing as she studied the landmark.

“I’ve seen that before,” she said, her voice tinged with uncertainty. “But I can’t remember if it’s north or south of the Riddermark.”

Ellarien reined in beside her, her gaze steady. “The Stone Teeth,” she said, her voice matter-of-fact. “We are south of them, which means the Riddermark lies ahead—eight days or less of riding, mostly south and a little west.”

Linnea guided her mare closer, shielding her eyes as she studied the landmark. “Eight days,” she said thoughtfully. “Not far, but still a stretch.”

Celenneth exhaled slowly, the tension in her shoulders easing. “Then we have our bearings,” she said. “We keep moving south.”

Ellarien nodded. “The Teeth have been a guide for centuries,” she added. “Travelers follow them like stars. We’re on the right path.”

With the landmark to orient them, the group pressed onward, the jagged rocks receding behind them as the sun dipped low, casting long shadows over the rolling plains. The terrain began to shift slightly, the grasses growing shorter and sparser, the soil harder beneath their horses’ hooves. It was a sign of change, a promise that the Riddermark was within reach.

The next morning carried the scent of distant rain. The group broke camp early, the horses restless and eager to move.

Ellarien led the group with a steady hand, her sharp eyes scanning the land for the safest route. Celenneth rode close behind, her focus unwavering. Nimroch handled the rough ground with practiced ease, his sure footing a comfort in the challenging conditions. The sun hung low in the sky by the time they made camp near a small cluster of boulders, the natural formation offering some shelter from the wind.

The fire crackled warmly, its light dancing across their tired faces as they settled in for the evening. Linnea leaned against Celenneth, her good arm draped loosely over her companion’s waist. The closeness was a comfort after the long day, their shared warmth a balm against the cool night air.

As they shifted to find a more comfortable position, Linnea wrinkled her nose and pulled back slightly. “Ugh,” she muttered, her tone half-amused and half-disgusted. “Is that us?”

Celenneth raised an eyebrow, her lips curving into a faint smirk. “What do you mean?”

Linnea sniffed pointedly, her expression exaggerated for effect. “What do I mean? I mean we smell like a barn that hasn’t seen fresh hay in weeks. Between the riding and the fire smoke, we’re ripe.”

Celenneth chuckled, her tiredness giving way to rare humor. “You’re not wrong. But unless there’s a hidden hot spring out here, we’ll have to endure a little longer.”

Ellarien, sitting across the fire, glanced up from her careful cleaning of her blade. “There’s a stream about half a day south,” she said casually, her tone teasing. “If you can bear yourselves until then, you might live to smell like grass and flowers again.”

Linnea grinned, nudging Celenneth with her elbow. “You hear that? Grass and flowers. That’s the goal.”

Celenneth shook her head, her smile lingering. “Fine. And if you behave yourself, I might change that splint bandage.”
