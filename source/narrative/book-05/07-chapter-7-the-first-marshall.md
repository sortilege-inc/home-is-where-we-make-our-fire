# Chapter 7: The First Marshal

*Hísimë 3, 2965*

They were escorted into the heart of the Riddermark, through a settlement loud with the life of the Rohirrim — the ring of smiths' hammers, the neigh of horses, the deep melodic chant of warriors at their drills — and up to the great hall of the marshal, its banners bearing Rohan's green and gold. Within, a sharp-eyed man with graying hair rose to meet them, his bearing straight and commanding despite his years: Eofred, First Marshal of the Riddermark, holding the seat while the king was abroad.

"Travelers from the north," he said, deep and steady. "You've come far. What brings you to Rhovanion?"

Celenneth stepped forward. "A warning. The Shadow of Dol Guldur stirs again."

His brow furrowed, the name landing like a curse. "Dol Guldur. That black place has slept for generations. What word do you bring of it?"

"The orcs are gathering — organized, relentless. They're sending hunting parties far from their strongholds, to fray alliances and probe defenses. We fought free of one of Dol Guldur's own after we crossed into your lands." She met his eyes. "Their numbers grow, and they answer to a hand. This is a prelude, not a raid."

Eofred studied her. "And why bring this to Rohan, ranger of the north? What do you seek here?"

"To see the Riddermark ready. Your riders are the swiftest and strongest in the North. If the orcs move against Dale or the Woodland, Rohan must not be caught flat-footed."

He nodded slowly, weighing it. "You speak truly, and Rohan cannot stand idle while shadow spreads. But I do not act in haste. The Mark has stood by striking when it is wise — not merely when it is afraid."

Linnea stepped up beside her, her voice softer but no less firm. "Marshal, we've seen what their forces can do. They won't stop at small raids. They'll come for all of us, unless we stand together."

His gaze gentled on her. "You are brave, and your words carry truth. But strength spent blindly is strength lost. React without understanding, and the advantage is already theirs." He gestured, and a captain stepped forward. "I'll send riders to Dale, and double our patrols on the northern and eastern borders. Wherever Dol Guldur's servants cross into the Mark, they'll find steel and fire. But I'll not yet commit riders to Dale or the Woodland."

Celenneth crossed her arms. "Defense alone won't stop them. If they're organizing, it's to overwhelm — not to skirmish."

"You may be right." His jaw tightened, but he did not look away. "But Rohan cannot win this alone. Gondor, Dale, the Woodland — if it grows as you fear, it must be met on many fronts. We will reassess after Yule." He rose, his frame throwing a long shadow across the firelit hall. "Rest here through the winter. Your warning will not go unheeded. By spring we'll know the truth of it — and we will act."[^r-eofred]

They were given the hospitality of the hall, and sat a while by the hearth. Linnea leaned back, tracing idle patterns on the long table. "Well. That could've gone worse."

Celenneth's eyes stayed on the flames. "It could've gone better. He's too cautious."

"Caution isn't always weakness." Linnea's tone was gentle. "Rohan's strength is its people. If he throws them away on a guess and loses them, what then? At least he's listening."

Celenneth turned to her, and the faintest smile touched her mouth. "You're right. But every day we wait, the Shadow grows."

A servant in green and gold came to lead them to a warm meal and quarters — a simple, comfortable room, fur-lined blankets over a wide bed, a basin of water, the air sweet with dried herbs, the hearth glowing against the shuttered chill. Linnea ran a hand over the soft furs and sighed. "This feels like luxury after the plains." She stretched out on the bed, her good arm across her stomach. "The kind of thing I could get used to."

Celenneth smiled from where she stood unfastening her armor, a rare softness in her tired face. "You deserve it," she said, setting the leather aside and pulling off her boots.

"You're not going to make me enjoy all this alone, I hope." Linnea patted the space beside her.

Celenneth relented with a low chuckle and settled down against her, the furs closing soft around them after so many nights of hard ground. Linnea turned to face her and brushed her fingers along Celenneth's arm. "It's nice. To have this. To feel — safe."

"It is," Celenneth admitted, quieter. "And it isn't the room, or the fire." She hesitated. "It's you."

Linnea's smile grew, color rising in her cheeks. She tucked her head against Celenneth's shoulder. "Have you thought about staying here through the winter?"

Celenneth pressed a kiss to her brow. "I've been mulling it. At least until after Yule, and the worst of the cold. I'm not even sure yet where we go from here."

Linnea hugged her closer. "Then we winter here. I want to learn how they ride — the way they are with the horses, so easy and fluid. And it feels less fraught here. We could use the rest."

"We could," Celenneth agreed, her arm tightening around her. "And we might learn more of what's stirring beyond the plains while we're at it. Travel can wait."

[^r-eofred]: **Fellowship / Courtesy** — audience with **Eofred**, First Marshal of the Riddermark (holding the seat for the absent king). He grants a guarded response: doubled border patrols and riders sent to Dale, but no committed host until Dale confirms the threat and the council reconvenes after Yule. A partial ally won; the pair are given the hall's hospitality for the winter.
