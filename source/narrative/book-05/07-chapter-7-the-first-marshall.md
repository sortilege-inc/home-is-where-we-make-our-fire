# Chapter 7: The First Marshall

*Book 5*

*Hísimë 3, 2965*

Celenneth and Linnea were escorted into the heart of the Riddermark, passing through a bustling settlement of wooden halls, smithies, and training grounds. The air was alive with the sounds of the Rohirrim: the hammering of metal, the neighing of horses, and the deep, melodic chants of warriors preparing for battle. Towering above it all was the great hall of the local marshal, its banners bearing the green and gold insignia of Rohan.

The guards led them through the great hall's entrance, where they were met by a sharp-eyed man with graying hair. His stance was straight and commanding despite his years—this was Eofred, first marshal of the Riddermark, standing in for the king while he was away. He regarded Celenneth and Linnea with a blend of curiosity and suspicion.

"Travelers from the north," he said, his voice deep and steady. "You’ve come far to reach Rohan. What brings you to Rhovania?"

Celenneth stepped forward, her presence calm but commanding. "We bring a warning," she said, her voice carrying urgency. "The shadow of Dol Guldur stirs once again."

Eofred's brow furrowed, his expression darkening. "Dol Guldur," he repeated, the name falling like a curse. "That black place has been dormant for generations. What news do you bring of it?"

"The orcs are gathering," Celenneth said. "We encountered them on the plains—organized, relentless. They’re sending hunting parties far from their strongholds, trying to disrupt alliances and weaken our defenses. Their numbers are growing, and they have leadership—we encountered a lieutenant of Dol Guldur after we entered your lands."

Eofred's eyes narrowed. "They are fools to do so, they forget themselves." He folded his arms, studying her. "And you, ranger of the north—why bring this warning to us? What do you seek in Rohan?"

"I seek to ensure that the Riddermark is prepared," Celenneth replied. "Your riders are the fastest and strongest in the realm. If the orcs move against Dale or the Woodland, Rohan must be ready. This is not an isolated threat—it is a prelude to something greater."

Eofred nodded slowly, the weight of her words settling over him. "You speak truly," he said gravely. "Rohan cannot stand idle while shadow spreads across the land. But I am not quick to act rashly. The Riddermark has stood strong by striking when it is wise—not merely when we are afraid."

Linnea stepped forward, her voice steady but softer than Celenneth’s. "Marshal Eofred, we’ve seen what their forces can do. They will not stop at small raids. They will come for all of us unless we stand together."

Eofred's gaze softened slightly as he looked at Linnea. "You are brave, and your words carry truth," he said. "But military action must be used strategically. If we react blindly, we've already lost any advantage."

Celenneth frowned. "Are you arguing for a proactive strike on Dol Guldur?"

Eofred shook his head, his tone measured. "No, I mean we should be proactive in defending Rhovania, in engaging any enemies that cross our borders—not extend beyond."

He gestured to one of his captains, who stepped forward with a respectful nod. "I will send riders to Dale affirming increased patrols and our active defense. We will ensure Dol Guldur's influence doesn't spread unchecked—but I am not yet committing riders to Dale or the Woodland Realm."

Celenneth crossed her arms, her frustration evident. "And if Dale confirms what I've told you? What then?"

Eofred met her gaze evenly. "Then we will bolster our defenses. Patrols will be doubled along the northern and eastern borders, and we will cut off the orcs' advance wherever they appear. If they probe our strength, they will find steel and fire."

"Defense alone won't stop them," Celenneth said, her voice low but firm. "If they're organizing, it's not to skirmish—it’s to overwhelm."

Eofred's jaw tightened, but he nodded slowly. "You may be right. But Rohan cannot act alone. If this threat grows, it will take more than our riders. Gondor, Dale, the Woodland Realm—this must be a fight fought on many fronts. We will reassess the situation after Yule."

Linnea stepped beside Celenneth, resting her good hand lightly on her companion's arm. "Marshal, I admire your caution," she said softly. "But don't let it blind you to the urgency."

Eofred studied her for a moment. "I will not risk Rohan's strength without understanding." He rose, his frame casting a long shadow in the firelight. "Rest here for the winter," he said, his tone final but welcoming. "Your warning will not go unheeded. By spring, we will know the truth—and we will act."

With the promise of Rohan's vigilance, Celenneth and Linnea were given the hospitality of the hall. As they sat near the hearth, Linnea leaned back in her chair, distractedly tracing patterns on the long wooden table. "Well," she said, breaking the silence, "that could have gone worse."

Celenneth didn't reply immediately, her sharp eyes fixed on the flames. The firelight cast her tense jaw in relief "It could have gone better," she said finally, her voice low. "He's too cautious."

"Caution isn't always a weakness," Linnea countered gently. "Rohan's strength is its people. If he rushes into something and loses them, what then? At least he's listening."

Celenneth turned to look at her, the faintest flicker of a smile crossing her lips. "You're right. But every day we wait is another day the shadow grows stronger."

A young servant clad in simple green and gold he stepped hesitantly towards them, bowing slightly before addressing Celenneth and Linnea. “The marshal has ordered that you be given full hospitality during your stay,” he said, his voice respectful. “A warm meal is ready, and quarters have been prepared for you both.”

Linnea gave a tired smile, rising from her seat. “Thank you. A proper meal and a real bed sound like exactly what we need.”

The servant nodded, gesturing for them to follow. Ellarien trailed behind, her quiet presence a comforting shadow. They were led down a wide corridor lined with wooden carvings of horses and battle scenes, the craftsmanship speaking to Rohan’s pride and history.

The dining hall they entered was smaller than the great hall but no less welcoming. A long table was set with earthenware bowls steaming with hearty stew, fresh loaves of bread, and a pitcher of dark ale. A handful of Rohirrim sat at the far end, their laughter and voices a low hum of camaraderie. They glanced up briefly, offering polite nods to the newcomers before returning to their conversation.

The servant pulled out chairs for Celenneth and Linnea, then poured the ale into sturdy mugs. “If there’s anything else you need, you have only to ask,” he said, before stepping aside to let them enjoy the meal.

Linnea tore off a chunk of bread, dipping it into the rich stew and sighing with satisfaction as she took a bite. “This,” she said around a mouthful, “is leagues better than hardtack and smoked rabbit.”

Celenneth took a sip of the ale, its bitterness warming her throat. “You’ve got a knack for understatement,” she said, her lips curving into a faint smile.

After the meal, the servant returned to guide them to their quarters. The room was simple but comfortable, with thick fur-lined blankets draped over a wide wooden bed. A small basin of water sat on a low table, and the scent of dried herbs hung in the air. The windows were shuttered against the chill, but the glow of the hearth gave the space a cozy warmth.

Linnea ran a hand over the soft blankets, her expression lightening. “They weren’t kidding about hospitality,” she said. “This feels like luxury after the plains.”

Linnea stretched out on one of the beds, her good arm draped over her stomach. The furs beneath her were thick and soft, the kind of luxury she hadn’t felt since leaving Dale. She sighed deeply, letting the warmth of the room seep into her bones. “This,” she murmured, “is the kind of thing I could get used to.”

Celenneth smiled faintly from where she stood near the table, unfastening the straps of her leather armor. The soft light caught the tired lines of her face, but there was a rare softness in her eyes as she glanced at Linnea. “You deserve it,” she said simply, setting her armor aside and pulling off her boots.

“You’re not going to make me enjoy all this alone, are you?” Linnea teased, her voice light but inviting. She patted the space beside her.

Celenneth relented with a quiet chuckle, crossing the room and settling down beside Linnea. The furs embraced them with their softness, a stark contrast to the rough ground and coarse blankets they’d endured during their journey. Linnea shifted, turning slightly to face her, and Celenneth found herself leaning back into the comfort of the bed, her body finally relaxing in the warmth.

Linnea reached out, her fingers lightly brushing over Celenneth’s arm. “It’s nice,” she said softly, her voice holding a trace of wonder. “To have this moment. To feel... safe.”

Celenneth turned her head, meeting Linnea’s eyes. “It is,” she admitted, her voice quieter now. “But it’s not just the room, or the fire.” She hesitated, then added, “It’s you.”

Linnea’s smile grew, her cheeks flushing slightly, though whether from the fire’s warmth or Celenneth’s words, it was hard to tell. “You’re allowed to enjoy this too, you know,” she said, her tone playful but tender.

The quiet intimacy of the room wrapped around them as they lay back together, the furs cocooning them in warmth. Linnea shifted closer, resting her head lightly on Celenneth’s shoulder. "Have you thought about staying here through winter?" she asked.

Celenneth kissed Linnea on the forehead. "I've been mulling it over," she admitted. "I think at least until after Yule and the worst of the cold. I'm also not sure where we should go, next."

Linnea hugged her closer. "We can celebrate Yule together here! And I'd like to learn how they ride; the way they are with the horses is so relaxed and fluid." She paused, her eyes meeting Celenneth’s. "And it definitely feels less fraught."

Celenneth sighed, her arm tightening around Linnea slightly. "You're right. We could use the rest. And if we stay, we might learn more about what's happening beyond the plains. Travel can wait, for now."
