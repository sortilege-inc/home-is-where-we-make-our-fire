# Chapter 8: Yule in the Riddermark

*Book 5*

*Ringarë 10, 2965*

They quickly found a rhythm, training with the Rohirrim, joining the warriors on the field and making forays to the border, either with patrols or independently. The days were filled with practice and scouting, their skills sharpened alongside the Rohirrim riders. Each night, they returned to the hall, where warmth and hospitality greeted them—comforts that were rare on the long road they had known for so long.

Winter settled slowly over the Riddermark, the crisp chill creeping in each morning as the mist clung to the fields. The traditions of this land were different, yet there was something comforting in the shared anticipation of the season. The Rohirrim would gather branches of holly and ivy, decorating their hearths with green to ward off the chill of winter. Children would carve small wooden horses as gifts. The elders told tall tales about Yules in years past.

One morning, after having returned from a patrol, Celenneth was getting ready to take Nimroch out for a ride. Linnea sat on the edge of the bed, pulling her boots on.

Linnea spoke up. “Celenneth,” she began, her voice casual but curious, “did you have any Yule traditions? Back... before all of this?”

Celenneth paused, her fingers hovering over the clasp of her cloak. She looked at Linnea, her brow furrowed slightly. “Yule?” she repeated, as if the word had surprised her. “It’s been a long time since I thought about it.”

“Well,” Linnea pressed, tying her boot laces, “it’s coming up, and while I don't mind celebrating in Rohan, I'd love for us each to bring some of our own traditions forward as well. What did Yule look like for you in the North?”

Celenneth turned back to her cloak, fastening it with a practiced motion. Her voice was quieter when she spoke again. “When I was young... Yule was simple. My family didn’t have much, but my mother always made sure we had a warm meal and a little cheer. She’d bake something sweet—honey cakes, if she could manage it. And we’d sit by the fire, telling stories until the candles burned low.”

Linnea smiled softly, her gaze warm. “That sounds... nice. Peaceful.”

“It was,” Celenneth said, her lips curving into a faint smile at the memory. “Before everything changed.”

Linnea rose, brushing her hand against her splinted arm as she moved closer. “We could do something,” she offered. “For Yule, I mean. Even if it’s just the two of us. Or... well, the three of us, if you count Nimroch.” She grinned.

Linnea’s eyes brightened. “Back in Bree, we had a strange Yule tradition,” she began, her tone a mix of nostalgia and amusement. “Everyone would wear mismatched socks—bright colors, stripes, whatever they had—as a way to bring luck for the coming year. My father swore by it, said it kept the spirits who brought bad luck confused. It was silly, but it always made me smile.”

Celenneth raised an eyebrow, a chuckle escaping her. “Mismatched socks for luck?”

Linnea nodded. “It was just one of those things. Bree folk love their eccentricities.” She patted her legs. "Well, that settles it; mismatched socks and honey cakes. If you’re telling me you don’t have a craving for honey cakes right now, I won’t believe you.”

Celenneth’s smile widened, though she tried to hide it by adjusting the strap of her quiver. “Maybe,” she admitted. “I'll see if I can arrange something with the cooks.”

“And silly stories by the fire,” Linnea added. “That’s non-negotiable.”

*Mettarë 1, Yule, 2965*

The night of Yule arrived, and the hall was filled with warmth and cheer. The scent of roasted meat, mead, and spiced ale mingled with the laughter of the Rohirrim, their voices rising in song as the evening unfolded. The long tables were laden with food, and the fire roared, casting a golden glow across the faces gathered in celebration. Celenneth and Linnea feasted, sharing in the camaraderie that filled the hall. Children darted between the tables, their laughter infectious as they played and ran, occasionally finding themselves underfoot.

A small boy, no more than five winters old, insisted on sitting in Celenneth's lap throughout the meal. She obliged, a smile touching her lips as she held the child, who giggled and smacked the table repeatedly. Linnea watched from across the table, her eyes twinkling with amusement. “You’ve been selected,” she said, her tone teasing.

Celenneth shrugged lightly. “Seems so,” she replied, glancing at the child, who was now busy playing with one of the wooden carvings that many of the children had received. Linnea’s smile widened, and she stood, excusing herself quietly.

She returned a short while later, her arms laden. “Gifts,” she said simply, her voice warm as she approached Celenneth. She handed over the first item—a small box of honey cakes, their golden crust glistening in the firelight. “The cooks helped me make them,” Linnea explained, her eyes shining. “Just like you described.”

Celenneth’s breath caught as she looked at the cakes, a wave of emotion washing over her. Linnea handed her a second bundle—mismatched socks, brightly colored and soft. “For luck,” Linnea said, grinning. “I couldn’t resist.” She pulled up her hem and showed off her own.

And lastly, she unfolded what she revealed to be a blanket, woven in shades of green and gold. “I made this for you,” Linnea said, smiling. “Some of the women here helped me... I wanted you to have something warm, something from here.”

Celenneth put the child down gently and stepped forward, wrapping her arms around Linnea, her eyes welling up. “Thank you,” she whispered, her voice thick with emotion. She wiped her tears quickly, then leaned in, pressing her lips to Linnea’s in a deep, heartfelt kiss.

When they parted, Celenneth reached into her cloak, pulling out two sets of leather bindings. She held them up, her gaze meeting Linnea’s. “There’s something I have for you,” she said, her voice quavering slightly. “This is a tradition among the Dúnedain. It’s called the Binding of Aegis. I’ve thought long and hard about what would be most fitting to commemorate our relationship, and while I know the more common tradition would be marriage... this feels truer to what we share.”

Linnea’s eyes widened, intrigued. “The Binding of Aegis?”

Celenneth nodded. “It symbolizes a commitment—life or death. A promise that we stand as each other's shield, through whatever may come. If you’d prefer something else, I’d understand, but... I wanted you to know what this means to me.”

Linnea blinked back tears, her voice barely above a whisper. “I want this, Celenneth.”

Celenneth smiled, a weight lifting from her heart. She turned, raising her voice to call for quiet. The hall slowly hushed, the attention of the Rohirrim turning to them. She cleared her throat, her gaze steady as she addressed the room. “Tonight, we celebrate Yule—an occasion for warmth, for hope, for love. And tonight, I wish to share something of my people, a tradition that is dear to my heart.”

She took Linnea’s hand, holding it gently as she continued. “The Binding of Aegis is a vow. An oath of protection and devotion, taken between those who would pledge their lives to one another. Tonight, I ask Linnea to take this oath with me.”

The hall was silent, the air thick with anticipation. Celenneth wrapped the leather strip around Linnea’s hand and arm, her fingers deftly tying it in an intricate pattern. As she worked, she spoke the words of the oath, her voice clear and unwavering. “By this binding, I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Through all trials, through all dangers, I am yours.”

When she finished, she looked into Linnea’s eyes, her heart swelling with love. She then guided Linnea’s hand, showing her how to wrap the second binding around her own arm. Linnea’s voice trembled slightly as she repeated the words, her gaze never leaving Celenneth’s. “By this binding, I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Through all trials, through all dangers, I am yours.”

When the bindings were secure, Celenneth lifted their joined hands, her voice carrying through the hall. “This is our promise—to each other, through life and death.”

A cheer rose from the gathered Rohirrim, their voices ringing out in celebration. Mead was quickly brought to them, and Celenneth and Linnea raised their cups, toasting to their future. The music swelled, and soon, the hall was filled with song and laughter once more.

Celenneth pulled Linnea close, leading her into a dance as the firelight flickered around them. Tears streamed down Linnea’s face, her smile radiant as she moved with Celenneth, their hearts and lives now bound together in the eyes of all.
