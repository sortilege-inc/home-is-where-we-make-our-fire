# Chapter 8: Yule in the Riddermark

*Ringarë 10, 2965*

They found a rhythm quickly enough among the Rohirrim — training on the field, riding out with the border patrols or alone, their skills honed against the best horsemen in the North; and each night the warmth of the hall to come back to, a comfort rare on the long road they had known so long. Winter settled slow over the Riddermark, the mornings sharp with frost and mist clinging to the fields. The season's customs here were their own — holly and ivy gathered green for the hearths against the cold, small wooden horses carved as gifts for the children, the elders spinning tall tales of Yules gone by — and there was comfort in the shared anticipation of it.

One morning, come back from a patrol, Celenneth was making ready to take Nimroch out again when Linnea, pulling on her boots at the bed's edge, spoke up.

"Did you have Yule traditions? Back — before all this?"

Celenneth's fingers stilled at her cloak-clasp. "Yule?" she said, as though the word surprised her. "It's been a long time since I thought of it."

"It's coming up," Linnea pressed, tying her laces. "And I'd like us each to carry a little of our own into it. What did Yule look like, in the North?"

Celenneth fastened the clasp, and her voice went quieter. "When I was small, it was simple. We hadn't much. But my mother always saw to a warm meal and a little cheer — she'd bake something sweet if she could manage it, honey cakes. And we'd sit by the fire and tell stories until the candles burned down."

"That sounds peaceful," Linnea said softly.

"It was. Before everything changed." A faint smile touched Celenneth's mouth at the memory, and went out.

Linnea rose and came closer. "Then we'll do something. For Yule. Even if it's only the two of us — or the three, if we count Nimroch." She grinned. "In Bree we had a strange one. Everyone wore mismatched socks — bright, striped, whatever they had — for luck in the coming year. My father swore by it, said it kept the bad-luck spirits confused. Silly. But it always made me smile."

Celenneth's brow rose, and a chuckle escaped her. "Mismatched socks for luck?"

"Bree folk love their eccentricities." Linnea patted her knees. "Well, that settles it. Mismatched socks and honey cakes. And silly stories by the fire — that part's not up for debate."

Celenneth's smile widened, and she hid it by adjusting the strap of her quiver. "I'll see what I can arrange with the cooks."

Yule came, and with it the hall filled with warmth and noise — roasted meat and mead and spiced ale on the air, the Rohirrim's voices rising in song, the long tables heavy with food, the fire roaring gold across the gathered faces. Celenneth and Linnea feasted and shared in the good cheer, and children darted shrieking between the benches. One small boy, no more than five winters, took it into his head to sit in Celenneth's lap through the whole meal, and she let him, a smile tugging at her as he giggled and smacked the table and played with the little carved horse the children had all been given. Across the way Linnea watched, eyes bright. "You've been chosen," she teased.

"So it seems," Celenneth said, glancing down at the child. Linnea's smile widened, and she slipped away without a word.

She came back with her arms laden. "Gifts," she said, warm, and set the first before Celenneth — a small box of honey cakes, their crust glinting gold in the firelight. "The cooks helped me. Just like you told it." Then a bundle of mismatched socks, bright and soft — "for luck; I couldn't resist" — and she hitched her hem to show her own. And last she unfolded a blanket, woven in green and gold. "The women here helped me make it. I wanted you to have something warm. Something from here."

Celenneth set the child gently down and stood and gathered Linnea into her arms, her eyes stinging. "Thank you," she whispered, and wiped the tears quick, and kissed her, deep and full of everything she could not say.

When they parted she reached into her cloak and drew out two strips of leather, and held them up, and her voice was not quite steady. "There's something I've carried for you. A tradition of the Dúnedain — the Binding of Aegis. I've thought long on how best to mark what we are to each other, and while the common way would be marriage, this — this feels truer." She swallowed. "It's a vow. To stand as each other's shield, life or death. If you'd want something else, I'd understand. But I wanted you to know what it means to me."

Linnea's eyes had gone wide and wet. "I want this," she said, barely above a breath. "I want this, Celenneth."

Something lifted off Celenneth's heart. She turned and raised her voice for quiet, and the hall slowly hushed and turned to them. "Tonight we keep Yule — a night for warmth, and hope, and love. And tonight I would share something of my people." She took Linnea's hand. "The Binding of Aegis is an oath of protection and devotion, sworn between those who would pledge their lives to one another. I ask Linnea to swear it with me."

The hall went still. Celenneth wound the leather strip around Linnea's hand and forearm, her fingers working it into an intricate knot, and spoke the words as she bound. "By this binding, I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Through all trials, through all dangers, I am yours." Then she guided Linnea's hands to wind the second strip about her own arm, and Linnea repeated the vow, her voice trembling and her eyes never leaving Celenneth's. "By this binding, I swear to stand as your shield, to face the darkness at your side, and to guard your life with my own. Through all trials, through all dangers, I am yours."

When the bindings were tied fast, Celenneth lifted their joined hands, her voice carrying the length of the hall. "This is our promise — to each other, through life and death."[^r-aegis]

A cheer went up from the Rohirrim, ringing to the rafters. Mead was pressed into their hands and they drank to what they had sworn, and the music swelled, and Celenneth pulled Linnea close and led her out into a dance while the firelight wheeled around them. Tears stood on Linnea's face, and her smile was radiant, and the two of them turned together in the light, bound now in the sight of all.

[^r-aegis]: **The Binding of Aegis** — the Dúnedain oath sworn between Celenneth and Linnea on Yule (Ringarë 30), 2965, in the hall of the Riddermark: a mutual vow to stand as each other's shield through life and death. The defining bond of the chronicle, witnessed by the Rohirrim. (An in-world troth-plighting, not marriage — deliberately chosen as truer to what they share.)
