# Chapter 2: The Scouts' Paths

*Narquelië 20, 2965*

Morning came with an unnatural hush, the wood slow to stir after the uneasy night. Ellarien broke camp first, quick and quiet; Thalanar grumbled at the stiffness in his back; and Celenneth rose carrying the same tension she had lain down with, her eyes shadowed but sharp as she saw to Nimroch's tack. Linnea glanced her way and thought better of speaking, and saddled Mareth in silence.

The scouts took the lead, weaving through the underbrush with practiced ease, and the air felt lighter than the day before, as though the forest had granted a small reprieve. By midday the trees thinned and the ground began to climb, and Ellarien stopped and gestured them up a narrow trail that wound between ancient boulders. When they gained the ridge, the sight took their breath: the whole vast roll of Mirkwood spread below like a dark sea, and far off beyond it the faint gleam of Dale's towers under the sun, the horizon washed in pale gold and silver. For a moment the weight of the forest felt like something they had already left behind.

Celenneth stood a little apart, her hand on Nimroch's flank, her thoughts still snarled in the vision. Linnea came to stand beside her without a word and held out a piece of dried fruit. After a long moment Celenneth took it. "Thank you," she said quietly. Linnea only nodded — an acknowledgment that asked for nothing more.

The descent was steep and slick, and the deeper they went the darker it grew again, until by the time they reached level ground the full weight of Mirkwood had settled back over them. But the night passed in a rare calm, and Celenneth lay awake a while turning over the small kindnesses of the day — Linnea's, and the scouts' — their weight unfamiliar and not unwelcome, before sleep took her at last, and for once it was dreamless.

*Narquelië 21, 2965*

Ellarien led them off at a game trail that cut west through the densest part of the wood. "The deer know the way," she said. "If we hold it, it saves us a day." The trail was narrow and root-choked, and Thalanar warned them to watch their footing — this near the heart of the forest, the ground had a way of swallowing careless feet — but the scouts moved silent and sure, and the party made good time. At midday they watered the horses at a stream, and Linnea passed Celenneth a waterskin, and the exchange was wordless and easy. The shortcut held its promise, cutting a full day from their road, and they made camp at dusk in a clearing Thalanar judged good ground.[^r-shortcut]

*Narquelië 22, 2965*

By mid-morning the game trail failed them: a wall of bramble and low branch, an hour's backtrack at least. Thalanar cursed and hauled at a thorned vine that would not give.

"Wait," Celenneth said, and dismounted. She read the ground — the subtle dips in it, the faint places where deer-tracks veered sharply left — and a slow smile came. "There's a way through. Narrow, but it saves us the hours."

Ellarien tilted her head, approving. "Lead on, ranger."

They led the horses through by hand, brambles clawing their cloaks, and came out on the far side onto the main trail with the day barely dented. "You've an eye for what the wood hides," Thalanar allowed.

"You learn to see what it wants you not to," Celenneth said, and swung back up.

They made camp that evening in a hollow ringed by old oaks, and while the others saw to the fire Celenneth came to where Linnea sat working a whetstone along her blade, and stood a moment gathering herself before she spoke.

"I owe you an apology," she said. Linnea looked up, brows drawing together. "For how I've been. Cold. Shut against you. It wasn't fair."

Linnea set the blade aside. "You've carried a lot. I understand."

"No." Celenneth held her eyes. "You've been patient. Kind. More than I've given back. And I'm grateful for it. Truly." Before Linnea could answer she stepped in and gathered her into her arms, and Linnea stiffened, and then melted into it, her hands coming to rest on Celenneth's back. They held each other with the firelight moving over them, and the shadows of the wood drew off a little.

When they parted Linnea's eyes were bright. "You've nothing to apologize for," she said. "Just — don't shut me out again."

"I won't," Celenneth promised, and meant it, and slept that night lighter than she had in days.
