# Chapter 2: The Scouts’ Paths

*Book 5*

*Narquelië 20, 2965*

The morning came with an unnatural hush, the forest seemingly reluctant to stir after the uneasy night. Ellarien was the first to break camp, her movements efficient and quiet, while Thalanar grumbled at the stiffness in his back. Celenneth rose with the same tension she’d carried the night before, her eyes shadowed but sharp as she busied herself with Nimroch’s tack. Linnea glanced her way, hesitant, but said nothing as she saddled Mareth.

The scouts took the lead as the party set off, weaving through the dense underbrush with practiced ease. The air felt lighter than it had the day before, as if the forest had granted them a rare reprieve. The trees thinned gradually, their gnarled branches giving way to smoother, younger growth. By midday, they reached the foothills of the mountains that skirted Mirkwood’s heart.

Ellarien stopped abruptly, gesturing for the others to follow her gaze. “There,” she said, her voice almost reverent. Ahead, the trees broke open to reveal a narrow path climbing steeply. The scouts exchanged a glance and led the party upward, the trail winding between ancient boulders and tufts of stubborn moss.

When they reached the summit of the ridge, the sight stole the breath from their lungs. Before them stretched the vastness of Mirkwood, its endless canopy rolling like a sea of green and black. Beyond the forest, far in the distance, faint glimpses of Dale’s towers shimmered under the sun. The horizon was painted with pale golds and silvers, and for a moment, the oppressive weight of the forest seemed a distant memory.

Celenneth stood slightly apart from the group, her hand resting on Nimroch’s flank. Her eyes drank in the vista, but her thoughts remained tangled with the shadow of her vision. Linnea approached, standing beside her without a word. She extended a piece of dried fruit, her expression calm, almost expectant.

After a long pause, Celenneth took it, the barest smile flickering across her lips. “Thank you,” she said quietly. Linnea nodded, a silent acknowledgment that needed no elaboration.

Ellarien and Thalanar exchanged hushed words, their sharp eyes scanning the treetops below. “We’ll lose the light too early if we linger,” Thalanar said, louder now, his tone gruff but lacking its usual bite. “Enjoy the view while you can. It’s all shadows from here.”

The descent was steep, the path slick with damp moss and loose soil. The deeper they went, the darker the forest became, the shadows lengthening as the sunlight above was swallowed by the canopy. The air grew thick and humid, the sounds of unseen creatures echoing faintly around them. Celenneth found herself more receptive to the occasional jest from Thalanar or the steadying hand Linnea offered when the ground became treacherous.

By the time they reached level ground again, the oppressive weight of Mirkwood had returned in full.

The night passed in a blessed calm, the forest holding its breath as if in silent truce. The campfire burned low, casting faint shadows across the bedrolls as Celenneth lay awake for a time, her thoughts swirling. The kindnesses offered by Linnea, Ellarien, and Thalanar lingered in her mind, their weight unfamiliar but not unwelcome. Eventually, the steady rhythm of the forest lulled her into a dreamless sleep.

*Narquelië 21, 2965*

Morning broke with a dull light filtering through the dense canopy, a reminder of the forest’s perpetual gloom. The group broke camp quickly, Ellarien taking the lead with an urgency that suggested she had a plan in mind. As the horses were saddled, she gestured to a barely discernible path winding to the west.

“A game trail,” she said, her voice confident. “It cuts through the denser parts of the forest and should save us a day. If we’re lucky, we won’t lose the trail.”

Thalanar snorted, slinging his bow over his shoulder. “If the deer can manage it, so can we. Just watch your footing—this close to the heart of the forest, the ground likes to swallow careless feet.”

The game trail proved narrow and uneven, twisting through dense thickets and over roots as thick as a man’s thigh. Celenneth rode Nimroch at the rear of the group, her sharp eyes scanning the surroundings for any sign of movement. Linnea followed close behind Ellarien, her borrowed horse stepping carefully over the uneven ground. Thalanar took up a middle position, his watchful gaze sweeping from side to side, ready for trouble.

Despite the trail’s challenges, the group made swift progress. The scouts moved with practiced ease, their steps silent and sure. Ellarien paused occasionally to examine faint marks on the trail, nodding to herself before pressing on. “The deer know the way,” she said once, half to herself, half to Linnea, who smiled faintly in reply.

By midday, the forest opened slightly, revealing a stream running parallel to the trail. They stopped briefly to let the horses drink, the cool water a welcome relief from the humid air. Celenneth dismounted, her hand brushing Nimroch’s flank as she stretched her legs. Linnea offered her a waterskin, which she accepted without hesitation, their exchange wordless but companionable.

The shortcut proved effective, cutting a day from their journey as promised. As the sun dipped low, painting the treetops in fleeting gold, the party emerged into a small clearing, the trail ahead winding back into the thicker part of the forest.

“We’ll camp here tonight,” Thalanar said, his tone brooking no argument. “Good ground, good visibility.” He knelt to examine the soil, nodding with satisfaction. “Nothing’s been through here in days.”

Ellarien began unpacking their supplies, her movements efficient. “It’s not often the forest gives us such a gift,” she said. “We’ll take it and rest well.”

Celenneth and Linnea exchanged a glance, the progress of the day lifting their spirits. Though the shadows pressed close around them, the air felt lighter, the camaraderie among the group holding the darkness at bay.

*Narquelië 22, 2965*

The morning began with the subdued energy of routine, the forest’s shadowy embrace ever-present but no longer suffocating. The group broke camp swiftly, their movements synchronized by now. Celenneth adjusted Nimroch’s saddle with a practiced hand, her sharp gaze scanning the forest ahead. The game trail had thinned, its winding path swallowed by creeping roots and dense underbrush.

By midmorning, the way forward seemed blocked entirely—a tangle of brambles and low-hanging branches forming an impassable wall. Thalanar cursed under his breath, tugging at a thorny vine that resisted his strength. “We’ll lose hours if we backtrack,” he muttered, his frustration evident.

“Wait,” Celenneth said, her voice cutting through the tension. She dismounted, her boots crunching softly against the forest floor. With narrowed eyes, she studied the obstruction, noting subtle dips in the ground and faint impressions where deer tracks veered sharply to the left. Her lips curved into a slight smile. “There’s a way through. It’s narrow, but it’ll save us time.”

Ellarien tilted her head, watching Celenneth with an approving glint in her eyes. “Lead the way, ranger,” she said simply.

The passage was tight, forcing them to lead the horses by hand through the winding path Celenneth had discovered. Brambles clawed at their cloaks, and roots snagged their boots, but the way remained passable. The scouts followed Celenneth’s lead without question, their movements smooth and silent. Linnea brought up the rear, her sharp eyes scanning their flanks for any sign of danger.

By mid-afternoon, the forest opened slightly, the oppressive canopy above giving way to shafts of pale sunlight. The group rejoined the main trail, their progress unhindered. “You’ve got an eye for the unseen,” Thalanar said with a gruff nod toward Celenneth. “Could’ve been hours lost, maybe more.”

Celenneth shrugged, her expression calm but pleased. “You learn to see what the forest wants to hide,” she replied, mounting Nimroch with practiced ease.

Ellarien smiled faintly. “And the forest was kind to us today. A rare gift.”

They pressed on, the shadows lengthening as the day waned. When the sun dipped low, painting the treetops in fleeting gold, they found a small hollow shielded by ancient oaks and made camp. The fire burned low, casting flickering light on the weary travelers as they settled into a comfortable silence.

As the others busied themselves with their evening routines, Celenneth approached Linnea, who was seated near the fire, sharpening her blade. Celenneth hesitated for a moment, her hand brushing against Nimroch’s saddle as she steadied herself. Then, with quiet resolve, she stepped closer, her shadow falling across Linnea.

“I owe you an apology,” Celenneth said, her voice low but steady. Linnea looked up, her brows furrowing slightly in confusion. “For the way I’ve been. Distant. Cold. It’s not fair to you.”

Linnea set her blade aside, her expression softening. “You’ve been through a lot,” she said gently. “I understand.”

“No,” Celenneth said firmly, her eyes meeting Linnea’s. “You’ve been patient. Kind. More than I deserve. And I... I’m grateful. Truly.”

Before Linnea could reply, Celenneth stepped forward and embraced her, her arms wrapping tightly around her companion. Linnea stiffened momentarily, then melted into the embrace, her hands resting lightly on Celenneth’s back. The firelight danced across their faces as they held each other, the forest’s shadows retreating in the warmth of their shared connection.

When they pulled apart, Linnea smiled, her eyes glistening. “You’ve got nothing to apologize for,” she said softly. “Just don’t shut me out again.”

Celenneth nodded, her expression lighter than it had been in days. “I won’t,” she promised. That night, as the campfire burned low and the forest settled into silence, Celenneth felt a quiet peace settle over her, the weight of the shadows easing, if only for a while.
