# Chapter 3: Rhovanian Hazards

*Book 5*

*Narquelië 23, 2965*

The fifth day began with the forest greeting them in its usual grim silence, the trail ahead winding through dense, darkened undergrowth. Ellarien led the group, her sharp gaze fixed on the path, while Thalanar brought up the rear, his gruff mood unchanged from the morning’s cold. Celenneth and Linnea rode in the middle, their mounts stepping carefully over the uneven terrain.

“We’re making excellent time,” Ellarien said, her tone even but laced with the cautious optimism of someone who knew Mirkwood well enough not to trust its favors. “If we push through until sundown, we’ll cut another half-day.”

Thalanar snorted, gesturing at the gnarled roots crisscrossing their path. “If the forest doesn’t swallow one of us first,” he muttered.

Celenneth narrowed her eyes, scanning the path ahead. The ground was becoming treacherous, the faint trail they’d been following swallowed by creeping roots and loose rocks. “Careful,” she warned. “This part of the forest doesn’t look forgiving.”

The terrain had grown harsher. They dismounted to navigate a steep incline, the horses struggling to keep their footing on the slippery moss and jagged stones. Celenneth took the lead, guiding Nimroch with a steady hand, while Linnea and Mareth followed behind.

It was halfway down the slope when disaster struck. Linnea’s mare balked at a particularly loose patch of rock, its hooves skidding. The animal let out a sharp whinny as it stumbled, its legs giving way beneath it. Linnea tried to steady the horse, but the weight of the fall threw her off balance. She hit the ground hard, rolling a few feet before coming to a stop against a jutting root.

“Linnea!” Celenneth shouted, scrambling to her side. The others rushed forward, Ellarien already scanning the area for threats while Thalanar checked the mare. Mareth, though shaken, had escaped with only a shallow scrape along its flank. Linnea, however, wasn’t as lucky.

Celenneth knelt beside her, her hands hovering over Linnea’s arm, which was bent at an unnatural angle. Linnea gritted her teeth, her face pale but resolute. “I’m fine,” she hissed, though the tremor in her voice betrayed the pain she was in.

“You’re not,” Celenneth said sharply, her voice tinged with worry. “Don’t move.”

Ellarien crouched beside them, her nimble fingers already pulling a length of sturdy fabric from her satchel. “It’s a clean break,” she said, examining the injury with a practiced eye. “Painful, but it’ll heal.” She glanced at Celenneth. “Hold her steady.”

Linnea let out a sharp breath, her eyes narrowing. “Just do what you need.”

Ellarien worked quickly, her hands deft as she set the bone and bound the arm with a makeshift splint. Linnea’s muffled groan echoed in the quiet forest, her breath ragged by the time Ellarien finished. “That should hold until we can get proper treatment,” Ellarien said, her tone calm and steady. “But you’ll need to rest that arm.”

Thalanar helped Linnea to her feet, his grip firm but not unkind. Celenneth guided Linnea back to her horse, her worry etched into her face. “I shouldn’t have let you ride so close to the edge,” she said quietly, guilt heavy in her voice.

Linnea shook her head, her uninjured hand reaching to squeeze Celenneth’s. “It’s not your fault. I’ll be more careful.”

The rest of the day passed in tense silence, Linnea riding with her arm cradled against her chest, and Ellarien taking more conservative routes.

The night passed slowly for Linnea, the firelight flickering weakly against the encroaching darkness. Though she tried to rest, the pain in her arm throbbed with each small movement, radiating up to her shoulder and leaving her breath ragged. She shifted in her bedroll, wincing as the splint pressed awkwardly against her skin.

Celenneth, ever vigilant, stirred at the slightest sound. “Do you need anything?” she asked softly, her voice carrying across the quiet camp.

Linnea shook her head, though her face twisted with discomfort. “I don't know what I need,” she said through gritted teeth. Celenneth watched her for a long moment, her expression unreadable, before settling back against her pack, though she never fully closed her eyes.

Ellarien, who had taken the first watch, crouched near the fire, her gaze steady on Linnea. “You’re stubborn, I’ll give you that,” she said, her tone not unkind. “But you’d do better to wake one of us than suffer in silence.”

Linnea managed a faint smile, but the rest of the night passed in fits and starts, each jolt of pain dragging her from uneasy sleep. By the time dawn broke, casting faint light through the thinning canopy, she looked pale and drawn but determined as ever to press on.

*Narquelië 24, 2965*

The morning greeted them with unseasonably clear skies, the sun cutting through the lingering haze of the forest. The oppressive weight of Mirkwood began to lift as the trees thinned, their towering trunks giving way to shorter, sparser growth. The air smelled fresher, carrying the faint scent of wild grass from the open plains beyond.

“Rhovanion lies just ahead,” Ellarien said, her voice carrying a note of relief. She guided her horse alongside Linnea’s, her sharp eyes flicking to the ranger’s splinted arm. “Once we reach the plains, the journey will be easier on all of us.”

Thalanar rode behind them, his expression stoic as ever. When they reached the forest’s edge, he pulled his mount to a halt, dismounting with practiced ease. “This is where we part,” he said, his gruff voice softer than usual. He glanced at Linnea, then Celenneth, lingering on the latter. “Keep her safe. Both of you.”

Celenneth nodded, her hand resting lightly on Nimroch’s mane. “Thank you, Thalanar. For everything.”

He grunted, a faint smirk tugging at his lips. “Don’t thank me yet. Rohan’s a long way, and there’s no guarantee you won’t run into more trouble before you get there.” He turned to Ellarien, his expression more serious. “Ride with them. See them safely to the Riddermark.”

Ellarien inclined her head. “I’ll see it done.”

Thalanar mounted his horse again, his gaze sweeping across the thinning trees as if searching for one last threat. With a final nod, he turned and disappeared into the shadows of Mirkwood, leaving the party to face the open plains ahead.

The transition from the dark, tangled forest to the rolling grasslands of Rhovanion was almost disorienting. The plains stretched endlessly before them, the grass shimmering golden under the sunlight. Linnea inhaled deeply, the scent of wildflowers and open air a welcome change from the damp decay of Mirkwood.

Ellarien rode close to Linnea, her presence steady and reassuring. “Mareth will stay with you until we reach Rohan,” she said, patting the mare’s neck. “Once you’ve arrived safely, she’ll know the way back with me.”

Linnea nodded, her grip on the reins firm despite her discomfort. “Thank you,” she said, her voice soft but sincere.

Celenneth rode ahead, her eyes scanning the horizon. The open land felt foreign after days in the suffocating grip of the forest, but it also brought a sense of freedom.

The first night on the open plains brought a profound quiet, broken only by the whisper of the wind through the tall grasses. The sky stretched endlessly above them, a canvas of stars more brilliant than they’d seen in days. Their campfire burned steadily, a small circle of light amidst the vast darkness of Rhovanion.

Linnea sat cross-legged near the fire, her splinted arm resting gingerly across her lap. Despite her attempts to hide it, the pain was evident in the tension around her eyes and the tightness of her jaw. She poked halfheartedly at the meal Ellarien had prepared, her appetite dulled by discomfort.

Celenneth approached, a small, well-wrapped package in hand. She knelt beside Linnea, the firelight catching the edges of her face, softening her expression. “This should help,” she said quietly, unwrapping the package to reveal a slender glass vial filled with a shimmering liquid. The faint glow of the draught seemed almost otherworldly, its golden hue reflecting the firelight.

Linnea looked up, her brows furrowing. “What is it?”

“Elrond’s draught,” Celenneth replied, her tone reverent. “He gave it to me before we left Rivendell. It’s meant to heal wounds and strengthen the body. It won’t make the pain vanish, but it will help your arm mend faster.”

Linnea hesitated, her gaze shifting between the vial and Celenneth’s face. “You’ve been holding onto this,” she said, her voice soft. “Why now?”

Celenneth’s lips pressed into a thin line. “Because you need it,” she said simply. “And because I can’t stand to see you suffer.” She met Linnea’s gaze, her voice gentling. “It’s what Elrond intended—for moments like this. I don't know why I didn't think to give it to you yesterday.”

Linnea held her gaze before nodding. “Alright,” she said. “If you’re sure.”

Celenneth uncorked the vial, the faint scent of wild herbs and honey wafting into the cool night air. She handed it to Linnea, who lifted it carefully to her lips and drank. The liquid was warm and soothing as it slid down her throat, leaving a gentle heat in its wake. Linnea exhaled slowly, the tension in her body easing as the draught began to take effect.

Ellarien watched from across the fire, her sharp eyes reflecting the flames. “Elven magic's effects sneak up on you,” she said, her voice low but approving. “You’ll feel the difference by morning.”

Linnea leaned back, her good hand pressing against her stomach as the warmth of the draught spread through her body. “It’s... strange,” she murmured, her voice tinged with wonder. “Like sunlight on my skin, even here in the dark.”

Celenneth smiled faintly, her hand brushing against Linnea’s shoulder. “Rest,” she said. “Let it do its work.”

Linnea nodded, settling back into her bedroll, her eyes already growing heavy. Celenneth remained by the fire, watching over her with a quiet intensity. As Linnea drifted into a deep, untroubled sleep, the stars above seemed to shine brighter, the vastness of the plains a little less daunting under their light.

*Narquelië 25, 2965*

The air was crisp, carrying the faint scent of wildflowers and earth, and the golden grasses of Rhovanion stretched endlessly before them. The group moved swiftly, the horses refreshed by the open plains and the clear, cool streams they encountered along the way. Celenneth rode ahead, her eyes scanning the horizon for threats, while Ellarien followed close behind Linnea, offering the occasional word of encouragement.

The mood was lighter than it had been in days. Linnea’s arm, though still splinted, had improved significantly thanks to the healing draught. She held the reins with a steadiness that spoke of growing confidence, a small smile tugging at her lips as the sun rose higher into the cloudless sky.

“You’ve got your stride back,” Ellarien observed, her voice laced with approval.

Linnea glanced back, her grin widening. “It helps not feeling like my arm’s on fire every time I move.”

Eve Mareth seemed more spirited, her steps quick and sure as the group covered mile after mile with ease. By midday, they’d passed their expected mark and were pushing farther than anticipated. The vast openness of the plains made the forest seem a distant memory, its shadows replaced by endless light.

As the sun dipped low, the landscape began to shift. The once-smooth ground turned uneven, scattered with rocky outcroppings and patches of stubborn brambles. Celenneth raised a hand and stopped Nimroch.

“What is it?” Linnea asked, her voice wary.

Ellarien rode up beside her, her expression darkening as she followed Celenneth’s gaze. “Sinkholes,” she murmured. “The ground looks solid until you step on it, then it swallows you whole.”

Linnea pulled her horse to a halt, her brow furrowing. “How do we tell where they are?”

Ellarien dismounted, handing her reins to Linnea before stepping cautiously onto the disturbed ground. She knelt, pressing her palm against the soil, then stood and tapped the earth lightly with the butt of her spear. The hollow sound that followed was enough to send a chill through the group.

“This whole area’s unstable,” she said grimly, turning back to the others. “We’ll need to pick our way through carefully.”

As the group began to skirt the sinkholes, Celenneth’s sharp eyes caught something further ahead—a faint sheen on the grass, almost like dew, though the air was dry. She raised a hand, motioning for the group to stop. “Wait,” she said, her voice low but firm.

Ellarien stepped forward, her gaze narrowing as she studied the shimmer. “Not dew,” she murmured after a moment. “Wet earth. A hidden spring.”

Celenneth knelt, pressing her hand to the soil. It gave way slightly under her touch, and the faint scent of stagnant water rose from the ground. “A marsh,” she said, her tone tense. “Usually shallow, but it could swallow a horse—or a person—if we’re unlucky.”

The group adjusted their course, skirting the edge of the marshy area with cautious steps. The horses whinnied nervously, their hooves sinking slightly into the damp ground. At one point, Linnea’s mare stumbled, her front legs sinking into the mud, but Celenneth was quick to act, grabbing the reins and pulling the animal free with a sharp tug.

By the time they reached solid ground, the sun was below the horizon, its last light painting the plains in hues of gold and orange. They stopped to make camp, weary but unscathed. Linnea sat beside the fire, her expression a mix of relief and unease. “That marsh,” she said, shaking her head. “We almost lost Mareth.”

Ellarien, seated across from them, added, “We were lucky. These plains are as dangerous as they are beautiful.”

The group fell into a quiet vigilance, their earlier lightheartedness tempered by the near-miss. The wind whispered through the grass, carrying with it a reminder that even the vast openness of Rhovanion held hidden perils.
