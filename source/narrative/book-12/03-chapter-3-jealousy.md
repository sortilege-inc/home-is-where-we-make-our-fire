# Chapter 3: Jealousy

*Cermië 18, 2970*

A low fire burned in the hearth against the cool of the summer night, filling the small cabin with its steady glow. Rain whispered against the windowpane, soft and unhurried, tracing crooked paths down the glass. Inside, the warmth wrapped around them—the furs, the quiet, the nearness—in something that felt almost outside of time.

Celenneth lay on her side, the round of her belly a gentle rise beneath the blankets, deep in sleep, her breaths slow and even. Her auburn hair spilled across the pillow like banked coals. Behind her lay Linnea, her hand resting light on Celenneth's stomach, feeling now and then the faint flutter of the life growing within.

The scene should have been perfect. For a moment it very nearly was. But something turned over uneasily in Linnea's chest, a quiet ache she had not expected and could not name. Her fingers curled inward against Celenneth's belly, uncertain of their place, and the firelight caught the tension gathering in her brow.

It was not Celenneth who had put it there, nor the child—she loved them both past any words she owned. It was the thought of the thing she could not share. The thing she would never feel.

She was happy. She told herself so, fiercely, trying to still the whirl in her chest. She was. So why did it still hurt?

The quiet of the night pressed close, but it did nothing to calm her. Her gaze kept returning to the swell beneath her hand, and the pang came again, sharp and unwelcome. She had always wanted this—to carry a child of her own, to feel it move under her heart. But her life had been too uncertain for it, too full of the road and its dangers, and now, as Celenneth carried their child, Linnea could not help the feeling that she had been left standing on the far side of a door.

The baby shifted faintly beneath her palm, and her breath caught. She swallowed and smoothed a hand through Celenneth's hair, but even that touch felt hesitant, as though she had forgotten how to give comfort she could not feel herself.

Celenneth stirred, her brow creasing, and turned to face her. Her green eyes opened, hazy with sleep, and cleared into concern the moment they found Linnea's face. "Linnea? What's wrong?"

Linnea hesitated. The words would not come loose. Her eyes strayed to the fire. "I don't know how to say it," she murmured, and her voice shook.

Celenneth's fingers brushed her arm. "Then try, love. I'm here."

Linnea closed her eyes a moment, gathering herself, and made herself meet Celenneth's gaze. "I'm happy for you. For us. Truly, I am." Her voice broke on the turn. "But I want this too. I want to carry a child. I want to feel what you're feeling. And watching you like this—as much as I love it—it hurts. I feel jealous. And I hate that I do."

The words hung heavy between them. Celenneth did not flinch from them. Her face softened instead, and she lifted a hand to cup Linnea's cheek, her thumb catching a tear before it fell.

"You don't have to hate it," she said quietly. "It's honest. And it changes nothing—not how much I love you, not what you are to me."

Linnea leaned into the touch, her eyes brimming. "But I'm not the one carrying this child. You're becoming a mother, and I feel like I'm standing outside a thing I've wanted my whole life."

Celenneth's brow drew down. Her hand slid to cover Linnea's where it lay on her belly. "You're not outside anything. Linnea—you're already a mother. To Brynja, in a thousand ways every day. This child grows in me, but they'll be yours just as surely as they're mine."

"It isn't the same," Linnea whispered. "I want to feel them move inside me. To know what it's like. I feel like I'm missing something I can never have."

Celenneth leaned forward until her brow rested against Linnea's. "I won't pretend I understand all of it, and I'm sorry that it aches. But hear me: it's because of you that this child will be born into a house full of love, and strength enough to weather anything. And I need you. Not beside this—in it."

Linnea let her eyes fall closed and let the words move through her. Slowly the ache eased—not gone, but gentled. "I only want to be enough," she said.

"You're more than enough," Celenneth answered, and there was no softness in it now, only certainty. "You're everything. You've been with me through battles and storms, through every joy and every grief I've had. We're in this together. We always have been."

Linnea's mouth trembled toward a smile, and her hand settled once more on Celenneth's belly. The faintest movement met her palm, and her heart rose to it, joy and longing braided together past untangling. "I love you," she said, and meant it to the bone.

"And I love you." Celenneth pressed a soft kiss to her lips.

They stayed like that, folded into each other while the fire ticked low and the rain whispered against the glass. The ache of Linnea's longing had not wholly gone; but it lay quiet now, warmed through by the love that held her, and by the promise of the life they were building, together.
