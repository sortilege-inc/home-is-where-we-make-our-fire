# Chapter 8: False Alarm

*Ringarë 16, 2970*

The cabin was quiet, but the quiet had a weight to it as Celenneth paced the room, one hand cradling the heavy curve of her belly. The fire had burned low, throwing its flicker against the wooden walls, and outside the winter wind moaned faintly and rattled at the windows.

She winced as another sharp pang gripped her low and deep, and her breath caught. She steadied herself against the table's edge and shut her eyes and tried to give her whole attention to breathing. The pains had begun earlier in the evening, faint at first, then stronger by slow degrees, each one clawing a little more at her composure.

From the bed Linnea watched, her dark brows drawn, her eyes following every movement. She had held her tongue at first; but when Celenneth stopped again, her hand pressing hard to her side, Linnea sat up, her voice careful. "Cel. What's happening?"

Celenneth let out a slow breath and tried to sound calm. "I think the baby might be coming. But it's too soon—isn't it?"

Linnea was beside her in a moment, her nearness steadying. "Let me see, love. Come and sit." She guided her to the edge of the bed and knelt, taking both her hands. "The baby isn't due yet, remember. This may only be your body making ready. Tell me—how does it feel?"

Celenneth shook her head, and her frustration showed through. "It's strong, Linnea. And it keeps coming. I don't know if this is it—but I've never felt the like."

Linnea nodded, and if there was concern behind her calm, she kept it there. "All right. We take it slow. Breathe with me. We'll watch it together, and see how it goes. If it's time, we'll be ready for it. And if it isn't—we'll know soon enough."

So they sat by the fire, Linnea's arm around Celenneth's shoulders, her presence a quiet reassurance. The minutes stretched into hours. The pains stayed sharp but came without pattern, and slowly, as the night wore down, they began to ease. Linnea never left her side; her low words and steady hands were a balm on Celenneth's frayed nerves.

By the time the first grey light crept through the windows, the pains had gone entirely, leaving Celenneth wrung out but weak with relief. She leaned back into the blankets, her breathing slow and even, and Linnea, sitting close, brushed a strand of auburn hair from her face, her dark eyes soft with understanding.

"It wasn't the time yet," she said gently, her thumb tracing Celenneth's cheek. "But when it is, you'll know it. And you'll be ready. We'll do this together."

Celenneth smiled faintly, her fingers curling over Linnea's. "Thank you. For staying with me. For all of it. I was so afraid."

"There's nothing to fear," Linnea whispered, pressing a kiss to her brow. "Not when we face it together."
