# Chapter 7: Entering the Frost Maw

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 30, 2970*

They slept fitfully in that makeshift camp near the old altar. The brazier’s coals glowed dimly, reflecting on the ice-slick stone and the half-faded reliefs etched long ago. Linnea dozed lightly, one hand near her bow, while Hallas, after tending his injuries, took first watch with quiet determination. Damrod sat against a fallen block, eyes half-shut but ears keen for the slightest echo. Celenneth rested close to Linnea, exhaustion tugging her into brief pockets of uneasy slumber, her mind drifting through old memories of Brandor and Orlec, and the haunting knowledge that deeper below awaited answers—or more horrors.

When they rose, it was hard to mark the passage of time. The darkness underground remained absolute except for their lantern and the faint glow of runes. Celenneth stretched, rubbing a kink out of her neck, and Linnea shared a scant meal of dried fruit and hard bread with the others. Damrod poured lukewarm water into wooden cups, and Hallas paced the perimeter, spear tapping lightly against stone.

No one spoke much at first. They understood what lay ahead: further descent into the Frost Maw’s bowels, drawn by the threads of old magic and duty. Damrod cleared his throat. “We’ve lingered long enough,” he said quietly, testing the weight of his shield. “We know now this place is tied to the Maw. We’ve seen what haunts it. If we want to end this—” he glanced at Hallas, “—we must press on.”

Hallas nodded gravely. “Aye. Much as we feared. Let’s see what fate holds below.” He turned to Celenneth and Linnea. “You lead. We stand with you.”

Celenneth’s heart squeezed at the thought of Orlec and Brandor. Somewhere ahead, their story had ended or continued in unimaginable ways. She would learn the truth. “We go together,” she said quietly, voice firm. Linnea touched her arm lightly, silent support.

They doused the fire and gathered their belongings. A single corridor led deeper into the earth, a wide passage framed by pillars crusted with ice. They stepped into that yawning darkness, lantern held high, curses and steel ready on their tongues and blades.

They traveled for a time through a corridor slanting downward. The air grew colder yet, and their breath formed thick clouds that hung longer than before. Occasionally, faint carvings or runic marks caught Celenneth’s eye, half-buried under ice. They suggested a path once well-trodden, maybe in ancient days, by those who guarded or studied this place.

As they rounded a sharp bend, Linnea raised her hand, halting them. Her keen eyes picked out disturbances in a bank of drifted snow that had settled against a collapsed pillar. Tracks again—heavy boot prints, not hound paws. Celenneth’s heart leapt painfully. “Rangers have been here,” she breathed. Damrod and Hallas exchanged looks. If rangers had passed this way, perhaps Orlec and Brandor had descended into these very halls.

They followed the trail, quiet as ghosts themselves. The prints led them through a winding series of chambers, each smaller than the last, until they reached a cramped space where ice cascaded from the ceiling like frozen curtains. There, huddled against a stone block, a figure lay, wounded and breathing raggedly. A faint torch stub flickered nearby, almost spent.

Celenneth recognized him instantly—Orlec’s gaunt face, etched with pain and weariness, older than her memory, but unmistakable. Her throat tightened. She rushed forward, Linnea right behind her. Damrod and Hallas fanned out, cautious, weapons half-raised in case something lurked unseen.

“Orlec,” Celenneth said, voice trembling. She knelt, slipping her glove off to press two fingers to his neck. He stirred, opening dull, half-focused eyes.

“Celenneth?” he rasped, voice cracked and faint. He tried to sit up, wincing. His cloak was torn, his right arm bound in a makeshift sling. Blood darkened the fabric. She offered him water, and he drank greedily, each swallow a gasp.

Linnea checked the shadows—no movement. Damrod and Hallas maintained a perimeter. The corridor beyond was silent but for Orlec’s ragged breathing.

“Orlec,” Celenneth murmured again, relief mixing with fear. “We found you. Brandor, was he—?”

Orlec’s eyes widened in sudden panic. “Brandor… still deeper,” he managed, voice hitching. “We tried to seal it… the source… he stayed behind… fighting…” He closed his eyes, pain twisting his features. “I thought… I’d never see a friendly face again.”

Tears welled in Celenneth’s eyes. She had imagined this moment countless times—finding Orlec and Brandor here or deeper in. She took his hand gently. “I’m here,” she whispered. “And there’s someone I want you to meet.” She beckoned Linnea closer. “This is my partner, my love; Linnea.”

Linnea knelt, taking Orlec’s other hand. His gaze moved to her, puzzled but curious. Celenneth’s voice trembled. “We have a daughter… Brynja. She’s safe, waiting for us at home. You would love her.”

Orlec’s eyes shone, tears forming despite the cold. “Linnea… Brynja,” he echoed softly, savoring the names. A ghost of joy passed over his features. “A daughter… such hope.” He coughed weakly, breath hitching. “You’ve grown so much, Celenneth,” he murmured. “I’m proud of you.”

Celenneth squeezed his hand, heart aching. “We’ve been searching for you and Brandor. Where is he? What happened?” Urgency crept into her voice. She had to know.

Orlec’s expression twisted, shadows passing across his face. Fear flared in his eyes, sudden and stark. He tried to speak: “He… he’s… no… you must—” His voice cut off. His body went rigid. Terror shone in his gaze as if he saw something behind them or in his mind’s last moments.

Then his breath caught, a final sharp intake that rattled in his throat. He stared at Celenneth as if trying to warn her, but no words came. A cold stillness settled over him. His heart stopped.

“Orlec!” Celenneth’s cry tore through the silence, echoing down the icy corridor. She cradled his worn face, tears freezing on her cheeks. Linnea gripped Celenneth’s shoulder, her presence a steady warmth against unimaginable grief.

Before any of them could process this loss, Damrod’s head snapped up, eyes narrowed. Hallas spun, spear raised. Linnea released Celenneth and stood. The air tensed.

Something stirred in the gloom. Dark shapes emerged from a shadowed corridor—twisted forms encased in ice-covered armor. Guardians of this place; their eyes burned with unnatural light, their armor creaking as they moved with halting steps. They bore old swords and shields crusted with frost, and their silence was more terrifying than any roar.

Celenneth, at the front, raised her sword too slowly. The nearest Guardian’s blade caught her in the side. She hissed in pain as steel and ice bit deep, warm blood seeping beneath her cloak. Damrod rushed forward to cover her, but another Guardian smashed into his shield arm, forcing him back and nearly numbing his shoulder. Hallas, bracing his spear defensively, tried to hold the third Guardian at bay. The impact drove him to one knee, spear jarring in his hands.

Linnea hung back, bow in hand, heart pounding. Three foes, all deadly and strange. She nocked an arrow, searching for an opening.

Celenneth gritted her teeth, shaking off the pain. She surged forward, sword raised, determined to strike back. But as her blade struck the first Guardian’s icy breastplate, it merely glanced off, leaving a shallow scratch that the ice seemed to smooth over instantly. She cursed under her breath. The armor almost felt alive, shifting and refreezing around each wound.

Damrod tried to drive his sword into the second Guardian’s flank. He swung wide, but the Guardian twisted awkwardly, its movements stiff yet unpredictable. The blade cut only empty air.

Hallas, grunting with effort, lunged at the third Guardian’s joints. His spear struck true beneath a shoulder plate, chipping away a chunk of ice and exposing what looked like old bone. For a moment, hope flared—then the ice crackled and re-formed, sealing the gap. Hallas’ stomach sank. They could wound these creatures, but the wounds wouldn’t last.

From the rear, Linnea released her arrow toward the second Guardian. Her aim was true and the arrow sank into a crack between the helm and collar. There was a sharp, echoing crack. The Guardian staggered, its inner cold light dimming. This one was wounded, at least. Linnea drew a sharp breath. Maybe not all strikes were futile.

The group realized they had to focus on the wounded Guardian before it recovered. Celenneth hacked again at the second Guardian’s armor, but her blow skittered harmlessly over the icy plating. Damrod tried next, sword flashing downward. This time, the ranger’s blade hit harder, and the Guardian’s chestplate fractured. Pieces of ice chipped away, revealing a hollow cavity within.

Hallas spotted an opening and drove his spear forward, trying another piercing thrust at the weakened Guardian. He aimed at the exposed core—but again, ice surged inward, sealing the gap before the spear could land a killing blow. Frustration gnawed at him. These creatures’ armor responded to every strike.

Linnea steadied herself. She decided to up the stakes. She grabbed a torch from the ground and quickly wound oil-soaked cloth around an arrowhead. A spark, a flame—and now she had a flaming arrow. She loosed it at the same wounded Guardian. The arrow hissed through the air, flames dancing. This time, it struck home. Fire licked through the icy breach Damrod had created, and the Guardian’s core shattered with a final, echoing crunch. The creature collapsed into shards, dark mist dispersing into the cold air.

One down. But Celenneth barely had time to draw breath before the remaining Guardians retaliated. The first Guardian lunged at her with renewed fury, catching her off guard. Its blade slammed into her guard, slicing painfully. She staggered, blood staining the ice at her feet. At the same moment, the third Guardian took advantage of Damrod’s focus on the fallen foe, swinging its blade in a brutal arc that tore into Damrod’s shield arm yet again. Damrod gasped at the jolt of pain.

Celenneth’s endurance flagged. Damrod, too, felt his strength wane. Hallas’ arm shook with fatigue, and Linnea’s heart pounded in her throat. They were all weary now. The fight had turned desperate. Hallas tried to hold a defensive stance, spear raised to protect his companions. Damrod positioned himself more carefully, balancing offense and defense. Linnea stepped further back, preparing to light another arrow, while Celenneth gritted her teeth and stayed on the offensive, refusing to give ground.

Celenneth struck at the first Guardian again, this time with careful precision. Her sword cut deep into its chest, forcing out chunks of ice and bone. She felt some resistance give way. Still, the creature did not fall. Damrod tried to help her but missed his mark. Hallas attempted another thrust, but the Guardian parried or twisted aside each time, leaving only scuffs on the ice plating.

Linnea, now with a lit arrow ready, aimed carefully at the first Guardian. The flaming arrow arced through the darkness and embedded itself in a crack Celenneth had made. Flame spread across the Guardian’s chest, melting and weakening the ice structure. Celenneth saw a chance and pressed harder, hammering away at the softened spot.

But the ice fought back, crackling and refreezing, resisting every blow. For a moment, Celenneth feared they would never bring it down.

Then Damrod lunged low, distracting the Guardian. Hallas used the brief opening to reposition. Linnea fired again, another flaming arrow whistling in the gloom. This time, her arrow found a gap in the Guardian’s shoulder. Fire and steel combined to overwhelm the creature’s self-repairing armor. The icy plating sizzled and split apart, revealing frail bone and old rags beneath. With a final blow, Celenneth drove her blade into the Guardian’s exposed core. Ice and bone fragments showered the floor as it collapsed, defeated at last.

Only one Guardian remained. The third had taken hits from spear and sword, but each wound had sealed quickly, leaving them no lasting advantage. Now, however, it stood alone against four determined foes. Linnea’s flaming arrows had proven effective. She lit another one and aimed for the Guardian’s center mass. The arrow struck, flame licking at the armor.

Hallas took a step forward, teeth gritted. He lunged again, and this time the combined damage—spear thrusts, sword cuts, and flaming arrows—prevented the ice from fully resealing. Damrod swung down hard with his sword, cleaving through a half-frozen arm. The Guardian tottered, struggling to maintain its form.

Linnea fired once more, the arrow’s flame dancing in the cavern’s chill. The shot punched into the Guardian’s fractured torso. With a drawn-out crack, the armor shattered, leaving the unnatural life force to sputter and fade. The final Guardian crumbled, a pile of icy shards at their feet.

The cavern fell silent, save for their ragged breathing. The floor was littered with chunks of ice and scattered arrows, stained with dark ichor and bone fragments. Celenneth clutched her wounded side, pain pulsing with each heartbeat. Damrod rotated his shield arm slowly, wincing at the ache. Hallas leaned on his spear, shoulder heaving. Linnea lowered her bow, exhaustion washing over her.

A hush fell over the corridor, broken only by the rangers’ heavy breathing. Celenneth slumped against a nearby stone, tears returning as she remembered Orlec. Linnea hurried to her side, wrapping an arm around her shoulders. Damrod and Hallas stood nearby, weapons lowered, respecting the moment of grief.

They had found Orlec, only to lose him in the same breath. His final moments, the joy at hearing of Brynja, and the terror that took him—what had he seen that would have him fear these guardians so? Celenneth closed her eyes, letting Linnea’s presence steady her. Damrod and Hallas exchanged grim looks. This place offered them no mercy.

Damrod traced the runes still visible on the shattered guardians’ armor. They were once Númenórean wardens, twisted by dark magic into eternal utensils.

After a time, Celenneth cleared her throat, voice raw. “We must press on,” she said softly. “Orlec mentioned Brandor. He must be ahead.”

Linnea nodded, reluctant but resolute. Damrod hefted his shield, and Hallas retrieved what he could of his spear’s splintered haft. They had no time for a proper burial, no means to carry Orlec’s body back now. Celenneth knelt to close Orlec’s eyes gently, whispering a promise to return if fate allowed.

They pressed deeper through the winding corridors, guided by faint bootprints and subtle signs of recent struggle. Celenneth moved quietly, her footsteps muffled by frost-covered stone, keen eyes searching the gloom. Damrod followed close behind, senses alert. Linnea and Hallas trailed them, forming a protective rear guard, weapons ready but hearts heavy from recent losses.

As they advanced, Damrod’s gaze sharpened, spotting scuffed patches of snow, partially obscured tracks leading onward. Celenneth recognized the faint trail as clearly as if etched into the tunnel, guiding the group through twisting passages with an uncanny precision.

Then, a distant flicker—a suggestion of light ahead, maybe a torch or a dying campfire. Damrod signaled caution. Celenneth slowed her pace, senses straining. She sensed movement, a weak presence lingering near that faint glow. The air tasted of desperation and old pain.

Linnea nocked an arrow, Hallas tightened his grip on his spear. Damrod edged forward silently. As they rounded a final bend, the corridor widened into a smaller chamber cluttered with ice and rubble. The faint light came from a discarded torch sputtering weakly against a stone. Slumped near it, half-buried in drifting frost, lay a wounded ranger clutching something that gleamed faintly in the half-light.

Celenneth’s breath caught in her throat. Damrod’s eyes narrowed; he recognized the worn cloak, the old ranger’s gear. Hallas and Linnea held position, scanning for threats, but none emerged.

“Cover me,” Celenneth said quietly to Damrod, her voice steady despite the sudden rush of emotion.

Before Damrod could reply, Celenneth advanced, boots skidding slightly on ice-slick rock. She reached the fallen figure and dropped to her knees, heart pounding. Even before she confirmed it, she knew: Brandor. One of her guardians. The man’s breathing was shallow, ragged clouds against the cold air. His cracked lips moved soundlessly, eyes half-lidded.

With practiced urgency, Celenneth’s hands found the worst wound beneath his cloak—a deep, frozen gash near his chest. Though faint, his pulse still flickered. She worked swiftly, using the limited supplies she carried, cleaning the wound, applying pressure, and whispering reassurances. Her efforts stabilized his breathing, though his life still hung by a thread.

Damrod knelt beside her, ready to fend off any ambush. Hallas stood guard, spear leveled at the darkness, while Linnea kept an arrow on the string. They formed a protective circle around Celenneth and Brandor, expecting danger at any moment.

Brandor stirred weakly, eyelashes fluttering. He focused on Celenneth’s face with recognition and relief. His cracked voice emerged like a whisper across decades. “Celenneth…” he managed, barely audible.

She offered him water, tilting it carefully to his lips. He swallowed with difficulty. As he steadied, his gaze drifted downward to something clenched in his bloodied grip. Celenneth followed his eyes and saw a fragment of an ancient blade. Its hilt bore Númenórean runes, faintly glimmering as if caught between worlds.

Brandor’s trembling hand lifted the hilt, offering it to her. “Take it…” he whispered, voice rough with pain and meaning. “Its... fate is yours now.”

Celenneth accepted the blade gently, feeling its weight of memory and power. Damrod watched silently, understanding that this relic mattered greatly. Linnea and Hallas remained vigilant, scanning the corridor behind them, muscles tense.

With measured steps, Celenneth and Damrod carefully lifted Brandor, supporting his weight between them. Linnea and Hallas guarded their flanks as they moved him away from the drafty intersection, seeking a more secure spot to tend him further. Brandor’s ragged breathing caught in his throat as Celenneth spoke quietly, pressing a bandage more firmly over his wound.

“We’re taking you out of here, Brandor,” she said, voice steady but layered with concern. “We’ve found Orlec’s body… he’s gone.” Her throat tightened, but she pressed on. “We’ll leave this cursed place after we finish what must be done.”

Brandor’s eyes closed at the mention of Orlec, grief twisting his features. His fingers tightened weakly on Celenneth’s arm. “Orlec… gone,” he repeated, voice splintering like brittle ice. “I feared… as much.”

He drew a trembling breath, eyes reopening, now sharper with urgency. “You… can’t leave… not yet,” he rasped, forcing each word. “There’s something… still here. We swore… to stop it. Promise me… you will.”

Celenneth’s jaw set, tears threatening her eyes. She nodded firmly. “We’ll face it,” she said. “Tell me what awaits us.”

Brandor swallowed, voice low and grave. “The altar… beneath the ruins,” he said. “Something ancient… trapped, but not fully.” He paused, struggling for breath. “We thought… we could seal it. The wards… must be reforged. If… you can’t… destroy the altar.”

He nodded toward the hilt Celenneth held. “That blade… was our key,” he murmured. “Orlec fell… buying me time.” His eyes shimmered. “You must finish it. End what we began.”

Celenneth bowed her head, resolve hardening. “We will,” she said quietly. Linnea stepped closer, placing a comforting hand on Celenneth’s shoulder. Damrod and Hallas exchanged looks, their grim determination matching Celenneth’s resolve.

Brandor’s gaze lingered on Celenneth, a mixture of pride and sorrow etched into his worn face. “Go,” he said softly, voice steady despite the pain. “I can hold on. But don’t tarry.”

Celenneth rose, the hilt of the ancient blade gripped tight. Linnea stood by her side, love and support shining in her eyes. Damrod and Hallas took up their weapons, ready to face whatever lay beyond.
