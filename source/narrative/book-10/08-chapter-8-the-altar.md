# Chapter 8: The Altar

*Book 10*

*Súlimë 1, 2970*

At last, Celenneth, Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas approached the final chamber. The passage narrowed, and a terrible cold seeped through their bones. The hum had grown into a low, resonant moan. When they emerged into a small, circular vault, they saw a pedestal of black stone at its center, cracked and weeping icy water. Runes flickered wildly over walls and ceiling, half-distorted by corruption.

Celenneth advanced slowly, the sword in hand. She recalled the patterns she had studied and the efforts she and Hallas had made above. Now she would do the same, guided by the sword’s runes. Damrod and Linnea stood guard, Hallas beside her, murmuring the curse softly to keep any lurking spirits at bay.

As Celenneth approached the pedestal, a surge of foul magic lashed out—shadows of frost and dark shapes flickered at the edges of the chamber. Hallas gripped his spear, Linnea and Damrod raised weapons, but Celenneth held the sword high, chanting the old words more firmly now, combined with fragments of what she remembered her guardians teaching her long ago.

The runes on the sword’s hilt flared bright. She traced the missing lines on the pedestal’s surface with the blade’s tip, careful and deliberate. Sparks of blue fire danced over the cracked stone, and the darkness shrank back. A keening wail rose from unseen throats.

Linnea gasped as spectral shapes tried to form—more wraiths, perhaps—and she fired an arrow at one, but the shadows danced around it. A soundless pressure built around them, and for a moment Celenneth feared a final monstrous foe would appear. But no beast or wraith emerged. Instead, the hiss of escaping magic thinned, settling into a calm hush.

She pressed the blade’s broken edge against a key rune on the dais, completing the pattern. A soft radiance emanated from the stone, and then dimmed, leaving only silence. The threat was contained.

Linnea lowered her bow, exhaling. Damrod and Hallas let out quiet sighs. Celenneth stepped back, blade still in hand, tears on her lashes. It was done. No grand explosion, no final roar—just peace returning to old bones of the earth.

Before leaving this place, they made a careful sweep of the area. Celenneth spotted a sealed alcove behind a curtain of ice. With Hallas’s help, she chipped away at the frost and discovered a Númenórean reliquary within—ornately carved, still intact after untold centuries. Inside were ceremonial tokens that once might have served as protective charms or offerings. Celenneth lifted them reverently, feeling echoes of a proud past lingering in the silent carvings.

Hallas, venturing further along the edge of the hollow, discovered a hidden chamber, its entrance half-blocked by ice. He cleared it, working steadily with the butt of his spear. Inside, he found an iron-bound chest, untouched by frost. Its hinges creaked softly as he pried it open. Ancient scrolls lay folded in oilskin, their ink barely faded, alongside old coins and a silver-bound book. Hallas ran a gloved finger over the book’s cover, marveling at the intricate script. Lore and knowledge from a distant age rested here, waiting to be carried back into the light.

With quiet efficiency, they divided the burden. Hallas took the reliquary, its silent weight pressing against him like a reminder of the past they had fought to preserve. Linnea balanced the iron-bound chest, her muscles straining but her will unyielding. They returned to Brandor and Orlec, and Damrod moved gently to lift Orlec’s body, cradling his fallen comrade as one might a sleeping friend. Celenneth supported Brandor, who leaned weakly against her but managed a stiff nod of gratitude. Together, they bore relics, memories, and their beloved guardians’ remains outward, steps careful as they retraced their path through frost-rimed corridors.

The journey back up through the Maw’s passages was grim and slow. Celenneth led, her memory guiding them surely. Damrod struggled with Orlec’s weight, shifting the body around.

At last, they emerged from the icy depths. The open sky greeted them, pale and distant, as they found a spot beneath a rocky ledge to make camp one final time before deciding their ultimate route home. They were exhausted, hollowed out by grief and hardship, yet alive and free of the malevolent whisper that had lurked below.

Celenneth gently set down the relics, eyeing the blade that had helped seal the old evil. Damrod and Hallas took up watch positions, ensuring no lurking threat followed them to the surface. Linnea coaxed a small fire into life, offering warmth and comfort. The wind here was still cold, but clean, no longer tainted by old magics.

As the night deepened and the small fire crackled softly, Brandor leaned his back against a stone, exhaustion weighing down his voice. Still, he mustered enough strength to speak.

Brandor cleared his throat, voice quiet but steady. “We never fully understood what lay below, only pieces. Orlec and I... we pushed deeper than we should have, trusting old lore and scattered runes.” He lifted his gaze to Celenneth. “We found carvings that hinted at a sealed presence, something ancient and malignant. We glimpsed frost beasts lurking in corridors of ice, wraiths that drifted without form... we tried to piece it all together, but we had no time.”

Damrod, arms folded, frowned. “So you entered knowing it was dangerous, but not how it all fit?”

Brandor managed a weary smile. “Exactly. We knew it had to be stopped. Orlec believed the wards could be restored if we found the right place and the right tools—old blades, old runes—but we only learned fragments. By the time we realized how much we were missing, it was too late.”

Celenneth listened, gripping the relics at her side. “You and Orlec gave everything to hold the line, didn’t you?”

Brandor’s eyes glimmered with grief. “We tried. I only wish we could have understood more before we ventured so far. Still, knowing you finished what we began... it eases the weight on my heart.”

Hallas tilted his head. “Did the runes offer any clue on what might have happened if it broke free?”

Brandor shuddered. “We suspected unspeakable horrors. Worse than the wraiths, the beasts, or the guardians you’ve seen. A darkness that would spill beyond these hills. That’s why we dared so much with so little.”

Celenneth closed her eyes a moment. “I’m sorry you had to face it half-blind,” she said gently. “We understand more now, thanks to your sacrifice.”

Hallas opened the reliquary, carefully revealing ceremonial tokens. Linnea unlatched the chest, unrolling a single scroll to glimpse cramped Númenórean script and delicate illuminations. Damrod marveled at the silver-bound book, wondering at its forgotten lore. Together, they pieced hints of history, power, and duty, each item a puzzle piece of ages long gone.

Linnea pulled her cloak tighter and smiled softly at Celenneth. “We never expected to become adventurers again,” she said. “After Bree, we built a life—simple, kind. Brynja’s laughter... it echoes in my mind even here. She’s so curious, always asking why the sun rises or where the birds sleep.”

Celenneth nodded, voice warm and fond. “She dances in the meadow behind our home, invents stories about foxes and giants. I miss her voice so much.”

Damrod, listening, allowed a faint grin. “Sounds like a life worth fighting for. Hard to imagine such peace from here.”

Hallas lowered his spear, leaning closer. “And you plan to return to that life once we’re free of these icy lands?”

Celenneth’s eyes shone. “Yes. Brynja deserves to know we faced these shadows and came back.”

Brandor’s voice was subdued but steady. “Celenneth,” he began, “you must know how unexpected it was for Orlec and me to raise a child. We were rangers—warriors, not caretakers. When we found you after your family was lost... we could have turned away, but Orlec said, ‘She needs us.’ That was all it took.”

Linnea reached out to squeeze Celenneth’s hand. Celenneth’s throat tightened. “You gave me a reason to survive beyond the blade and the hunt,” she said softly.

Brandor nodded. “Raising you was never easy. We knew how to track beasts, not soothe a frightened child. But you gave us purpose. You made the endless fight mean something more.” He managed a faint smile. “Now you’ve made a family of your own. Brynja... a spark of the future we never dared hope for.”

The next morning, as pale light seeped into their icy shelter, Celenneth knelt beside Brandor, changing his bandages. He caught her hand and held it gently.

“Celenneth,” he said calmly, his voice almost serene. “I’ve chosen my path. Last night, I took certain herbs—roots that will let me slip away quietly, without pain.”

Celenneth’s eyes widened, tears pricking. “Brandor... no, why—?”

He hushed her gently. “Listen to me. Seeing you alive, with Linnea, knowing Brynja waits for you... it’s more than I dreamed I’d witness. I can’t leave Orlec alone in this cold earth. My work is done, child. I fought my battles, raised you, tried to seal these horrors. You finished what we started.”

He glanced toward where Orlec lay buried. “My soul’s companion awaits. I... would never leave him behind. My life’s purpose ended with this victory and with seeing your happiness. Let me rest here, beside him.”

Celenneth’s tears slipped free, voice quavering. “I understand,” she whispered, grief and love mingling. “If that is your wish... I won’t deny it.”

Brandor smiled softly. “You’ve given me peace, Celenneth. Thank you.” He closed his eyes, breathing slowly, waiting for the quiet end he had arranged.

Celenneth wept openly, embracing Brandor. Linnea stood beside her, offering silent strength. Damrod and Hallas bowed their heads. They understood. There would be no arguing against Brandor’s choice. He was tired, his purpose fulfilled. He belonged at Orlec’s side, in death as in life.

They found a suitable place and dug the grave under the frozen sky, the earth hard but yielding eventually to their blades. They laid Orlec and Brandor side by side, hands clasped, tokens and sigils arranged with tender care. Weapons crossed over them, a final honor guard of steel. Celenneth closed her eyes as the others lowered the soil gently, snowflakes drifting down to mark the solemn rite.

Linnea whispered a prayer, her voice catching on tears. Hallas set down a small token from the reliquary. Damrod bowed stiffly, jaw clenched. Celenneth placed a woodcarving of the old blade’s runes, a memory of the sealed darkness, the foe they had bested. Together, they stepped back, hearts heavy but calmer, knowing that Orlec and Brandor slept peacefully in these wild hills.

After a long silence, Celenneth and Linnea exchanged glances, voices hushed as they stood with Damrod and Hallas near the cairn that sheltered Orlec and Brandor’s remains. Celenneth cleared her throat, voice steady despite tears drying on her cheeks. “We’ll go home,” she said, “to Bree, to Brynja. There’s nothing more we can do here.”

Linnea nodded, relief softening her features. “We’ve lost so much. A gentler path would be a mercy now.”

Damrod stepped forward, resting a hand lightly on his sword hilt. “We’ll guide you by a safe route,” he offered, sincerity plain in his tone. “We can swing east, follow valleys less observed. It’s less direct, but we’ll avoid the harshest passes.”

Hallas inclined his head. “You deserve an easier journey out of this wilderness. We know these lands—enough to steer clear of further dangers. And we would be honoured to accompany you two.”

Celenneth dipped her head gratefully. “Thank you,” she said, voice steady if sad. “Your help means more than I can say.”

With that understanding, they began to gather their gear. Hallas secured the reliquary, Damrod shouldered his pack, Linnea adjusted the chest’s weight, and Celenneth checked the straps that held the old blade. They would rest fully today, letting hearts and bodies mend a little more, and tomorrow they would start the journey home, guided by memory, loyalty, and love.

They settled in for one final night’s rest, huddled close around the fire. Sleep claimed them gradually, one by one, as the wind whispered lullabies through stunted pines. Beneath the stars, far from the horrors below, they were survivors bearing old relics and new scars. Tomorrow, they would shoulder their burdens and head for home, Brynja’s laughter and a warmer hearth waiting in the days to come.
