# Chapter 5: Leaving the Ranger’s Station

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 25, 2970*

Dawn’s gray light seeped into the station, illuminating weary faces and hollow eyes. The rangers moved about, stoking the fire, preparing weak tea. Gaidric and Aldric were stoic as ever, though they gave Eira a small, knowing nod. She returned it with a lazy wink.

Celenneth and Linnea rose quietly, shaking off stiffness. The night’s intimacies lingered in the warm glances they exchanged. Eira joined them near the hearth, where a pot of tea simmered.

For a moment, no one spoke. Outside, the wind had calmed, and the world waited.

Linnea broke the silence first, voice husky with sleep: “Well, Eira, you certainly had a... productive night.”

Eira smirked, unabashed. “I’d say I did. You two seemed inspired yourselves.” Her gaze flicked between them, teasing but not cruel.

Celenneth rolled her eyes, a faint blush coloring her cheeks. “Perhaps we all needed some warmth,” she said simply.

They shared a subdued laugh. This strange camaraderie—on the edge of a deadly mission—felt precious.

Then reality pressed in. Celenneth’s face turned serious. “Linnea and I are heading out,” she said quietly. “Thank you for all the information you’ve shared; we hope to send word to you soon.”

Eira embraced Celenneth and Linnea, and Gaidric and Aldric nodded solemnly, as Linnea and Celenneth mounted their horses and left the ranger station.

The wind off the Evendim Hills was sharp enough to steal a traveler’s breath, and as Celenneth rode beyond the ranger station’s old wooden gates, she felt its bite immediately. The station sat on the very edge of the known trails, with only broken hills and pockets of thin forest stretching northward. Ahead waited the Frost Maw—an unforgiving region of ice-choked valleys and strange ruins. Most travelers would have chosen a different path, or no path at all, but Celenneth had her reasons, and so did Linnea. Together, they pulled their cloaks tight, bracing themselves as they set out into the wild.

Celenneth was used to this sort of journey. She had the blood of the Dúnedain in her veins, and a kind of quiet confidence ran through her that helped her navigate old paths. Her companion, Linnea, was younger and less tested by the world’s cruelties, but she had instincts as sharp as a hawk’s eye. The two women moved north, through uneven ground, and the silence of the hills pressed in on them. There was no easy road here, just a jumble of stones, patches of brush, and scattered stands of winter trees rustling like dry parchment in the breeze.

They had taken up roles without argument: Celenneth guided their route, scanning ridgelines and distant passes for the best way forward, while Linnea kept watch, eyes keen for movement in the bleak landscape. They weren’t sure what they’d encounter on the way to the Frost Maw—only that it wouldn’t be kind. The weather grew harsher the deeper they pressed into the hills, and Celenneth knew it would only grow worse as winter struggled mightily to spend itself before spring.

By midday, the sky was mercifully clear, if cold. Their horses struggled through snowdrifts that nearly reached their chests, slowing their pace as they picked a careful route over windblown rises. Celenneth checked their bearings often, mindful that a slip in direction now might cost them days. Yet things went smoothly enough at first—no storms, no terrible obstacles. She led them along a faint series of old game trails, skirting around a shallow gorge where snow had drifted dangerously deep. Linnea followed silently, bow unstrung but close at hand, her senses on high alert. There was no chatter; the landscape demanded their attention.

Sensing the terrain was too rough for riding, they tethered their horses in a sheltered hollow and continued on foot, blades and bow at the ready. It was as they worked their way through a narrow pass lined with jagged stones that Linnea raised a hand, signalling Celenneth to stop. Both crouched low, hearts pounding as the wind moaned over the ridges. The younger woman had spotted something. Celenneth peered where Linnea nodded: fresh disturbances in the snow, large paw prints… signs of a lurking predator.

Celenneth knew the wild things of these hills weren’t shy about attacking travelers, especially now as winter neared its desperate end. She flattened herself against a frost-covered boulder and eased her sword in its scabbard. Linnea, calmer than one might expect for a traveler of her experience, slipped an arrow from her quiver. They listened. The wind covered small sounds, but Linnea’s eyes caught movement: a white-furred shape, low to the ground, stalking behind a ridge of ice.

A warg. Big, silent, and deadly. Its pale coat blended almost too well with the snow. Without warning, Celenneth and Linnea had stumbled into its territory, or perhaps it had found them. Now the question: fight or slip away? Celenneth glanced at the terrain. The gorge behind them was too steep to scramble back unnoticed, and turning tail would only invite a chase they likely wouldn’t outrun. Better to meet the threat head-on, and from a strong position.

They exchanged a quick look. Celenneth jerked her chin toward a rocky ledge that offered some height advantage. Linnea nodded. Under cover of scattered stones, Celenneth climbed quietly up onto the ledge, while Linnea angled around a boulder for a good line of fire. Snow crunched softly under their boots as they set their trap. They were ready.

When Celenneth finally revealed herself—stepping into a patch of weak sunlight that glinted off her sword—the warg snarled and launched itself forward. It was big, bigger than any wolf, its muzzle scarred and its fangs long. It tried to scramble up the icy rise, lunging at Celenneth, but she was already moving. From her elevated stance, she swung down hard. The beast jerked sideways, the blade skittering along its thick hide, drawing sparks on frozen rock. A miss, but it forced the warg to shift position. Down below, Linnea released her arrow. The twang of bowstring was sharp in the cold air. Her arrow flew true, slipping under the warg’s raised shoulder and biting into flesh. The beast howled, a sound that made the stones tremble.

Now furious, the warg vaulted upward, claws digging for purchase. Celenneth raised her buckler, anticipating the attack, but the creature’s weight slammed into her before she could brace. She went down hard, back scraping over ice-slick stone. She saw its teeth snap inches from her face. Pain flared across her shoulders where she’d hit the ground, but her armor saved her from a mortal wound. Gritting her teeth, she shoved the beast aside and rolled free.

Linnea’s second arrow hissed through the air. She was aiming carefully, but the warg, maddened by injury, twisted at the last second, and the shot went wide. Celenneth regained her footing, adrenaline singing through her veins. She swung again, this time a precise thrust instead of a slash. The blade plunged deep into the warg’s flank. Its howl was desperate now, breath clouding in the icy air. It stumbled, thick blood staining the snow. Linnea tried another shot, but her arrow clattered off a rock.

Wounded and raging, the warg’s teeth sought her throat, but Celenneth mustered strength and held her buckler as a bulwark. Linnea missed again, her arrow clattering off a rock. Celenneth twisted, found her footing. In that desperate second, she thrust her blade upward, into the warg’s chest. The beast hissed and howled, its blood running black on the snow. She pressed in, driving steel deeper until it collapsed, its last breath a ragged sigh.

The gorge was silent again, save for Celenneth’s rough breathing. Linnea hurried to her side, hand on her shoulder. “You hurt?”

Celenneth tested her ribs and shook her head. “Bruised, but nothing broken. Armor took most of it.” She wiped sweat from her brow, feeling the cold bite her damp skin. “Let’s move on. We can rest after we clear this gorge.”

They retrieved their horses from where they were tethered. Mounting up again, they pressed onward through the gorge, eyes and ears keen for more threats. The hills offered no welcome, just a constant reminder that they were intruders in a place meant for wolves, wargs, and spirits of old.

After hours of trudging through deepening drifts and patches of ice, the weather took a turn. A distant moan of wind through the crags warned them first. Then came the stinging snow, swirling in tight eddies around their ankles, building into a restless storm. The blizzard struck hard, forcing them to dismount and lead the frightened horses by the reins. Visibility dropped to a few arm’s lengths. Frustrated, Celenneth tried to spot any kind of shelter—a cave, a hollow, even a windbreak of thick pines—but the storm played tricks with her eyes. Every dark shape vanished when they drew close. It became clear that decent shelter would not be found easily.

They fought on, heads bowed, eyes narrowed against the biting wind. The cold was merciless, and Celenneth feared for Linnea’s strength, though her partner showed no outward sign of faltering. Once again, luck or fate took a hand. Just when the snowfall grew so thick they could barely tell up from down, Linnea spotted a narrow overhang—little more than a rocky shelf jutting out from a cliff face.

They guided the horses beneath the rocky shelf, huddling close together for warmth and protection from the wind’s direct fury. It was no place for comfort, but it could mean survival. Snow piled just a few feet away as the storm raged on. They shared a bit of dried meat and a swallow of water, saving their strength. Eventually, as dawn’s light struggled through heavy clouds, the storm relented. The world was quieter now, blanketed in fresh powder that gleamed under a pale sky.

At dawn, the storm relented, leaving a glittering landscape of ice. Celenneth and Linnea emerged stiff and sore but determined. They pressed forward and crested a small rise. On the far side, half-buried in snowdrifts and framed by jagged rocks, stood ancient ruins. Tall pillars of stone, carved with runes too faint to read, rose like broken teeth from the ice. The silence here was eerie. It was broken not by animal calls, but by a strange hum that came and went with the shifting wind. They reined in their mounts and tethered them in a wind-sheltered spot behind a low ridge. Proceeding on foot, Celenneth and Linnea moved quietly, using fallen blocks of stone for cover.

Celenneth halted. Something about this place set her nerves on edge. The Frost Maw was supposedly near here, its entrance likely hidden. Linnea rested a gloved hand on Celenneth’s shoulder, a silent question. Celenneth nodded forward, and they moved closer, carefully, keeping low and using fallen blocks of stone for cover. If anything dangerous lurked here, they would rather see it first.

As they crept near the ruins, Celenneth and Linnea spotted two figures. Men, by the look of them—tall and lean, clad in cloaks that blended into the winter gloom. Rangers, most likely. Celenneth could pick out the way they moved, controlled and cautious, studying the old carvings. Neither man had noticed the two women’s approach. Celenneth was torn: reveal herself or observe? She had no reason to distrust fellow Rangers, but neither did she know their hearts. Still, in these forsaken hills, any ally was a blessing.

With a quiet motion, Celenneth stepped from behind a pillar. Linnea remained partially hidden, one arrow at the ready. Celenneth raised a hand and called out, voice carrying cleanly in the still air, “Rangers! Be at ease. Friends approach.”

Both men spun, weapons halfway drawn. The taller one with stern eyes and dark skin let his gaze fall on Celenneth, then on the worn token she held out: a pendant with the old star. Recognition dawned in his guarded expression. He did not relax fully, but he eased his blade back into its sheath. His companion, fair haired and a few paces behind, kept a hand on his sword hilt.

The leader spoke first, voice low and measured. “Name yourself.”

“I am Celenneth of the Dúnedain,” she said. “This is Linnea, my traveling companion. We mean no harm.”

The man nodded, satisfied enough. “I am Damrod. That’s Hallas, my partner. We patrol these hills for threats—though few sane folk wander this far north in winter. What brings you here?”

Celenneth chose her words carefully. “We are searching for the entrance to  the Frost Maw. But these ruins drew our eye. I saw your cloaks and guessed you might be Rangers or friends of our kind. These lands are dangerous, and allies are scarce.”

Hallas snorted softly, rubbing a bit of frost from his beard. “Allies? Maybe. But anyone heading toward that frozen hell must have good reason. The Frost Maw’s known for swallowing travelers whole.”

Damrod raised a hand, then turned to Celenneth. “Beasts of ice have been found terrorizing people of the lone-lands, and we’ve traced their origin to here. We heard rumors of an old cache said to be hidden in these hills. We’re hoping to find the cache and address the cause of these ice monsters. This place…” He glanced around, eyes lingering on the half-buried pillars. “There’s a power stirring. If we don’t root it out, the North will continue to suffer.”

Celenneth nodded slowly. “We’ll help you if you’ll help us,” she said. “We would be glad to see the power of foul beasts weakened in these lands. In turn, I ask that you join us into the Frost Maw, in search of my mentors, Brandor and Orlec.”

Damrod’s jaw tightened. He looked to Hallas, who shrugged grimly. They did not seem eager. “The Frost Maw,” Damrod repeated, as if tasting bitter herbs. “That’s a fearsome proposition. We have old stories of that place. Not many end happily.”

Hallas looked closely at Celenneth. “Wait… you were their protege, were you not?”

“Indeed, I was,” Celenneth said, grateful for some familiarity. “Linnea is my partner. My guardians ventured into its mouth, and whether we find them, or merely their bones… I have to know, and we would appreciate the support.”

Hallas crossed his arms. “Fine. We’ll come with you—on one condition. We resolve the matter of what is cursing the  land first. A fair bargain?”

Celenneth looked at Linnea, who raised her eyebrows, then nodded slowly. Celenneth turned back. “A fair bargain. We’ll stand with you.”

After their uneasy truce and before the evening’s long shadows fully claimed the ruins, the four rangers began scouting the immediate area. Damrod and Linnea stood guard at a slight distance, scanning the broken pillars and half-buried stones for any sign of frost beasts or lurking threats. The air was thin and quiet, save for a faint, resonant hum that seemed to vibrate through the bones of the old stones.

Celenneth and Hallas had gathered near a tall, cracked pillar etched with faded runes. The markings were delicate and intricate, carved by hands long dead. Chips of ice and layers of frost obscured the designs, while centuries of weather had softened edges and marred key symbols. She held a small lamp close, its flame flickering over her gloves and the worn stone, while Hallas brushed away brittle clumps of snow.

“These runes were meant to seal or guard something beneath the ice,” Celenneth said softly, narrowing her eyes at the faint lines. “I’ve seen patterns like these in old ranger archives: binding sigils, but corrupted.”

Hallas nodded, leaning in to examine a particularly weathered segment. He tapped it lightly with the hilt of his knife, causing a few flakes of ice to fall away. “They were carefully made, once,” he said, voice lowered as if fearful to disturb whatever lay below. “But now the symmetry is broken. Look here—this entire line has been chipped away. Without it, the sequence of runes doesn’t form a proper seal.”

Celenneth traced the damaged section with a gloved fingertip. “We can attempt a restoration,” she suggested. “If the runes are stabilized, it might weaken whatever corruption is spreading these frost beasts. At least for now.”

Hallas considered her face, noting her calm determination. She was younger than he expected, but the set of her jaw and the confidence in her eyes reminded him of seasoned rangers he had known. “What tools do you have?” he asked.

From a pouch at her belt, Celenneth produced a small chisel and a thin-bladed knife with a hooked tip. She also carried a piece of charcoal wrapped in cloth. “I can re-inscribe the missing segments,” she explained, “but I must guess at some parts. We know it’s a binding ward, so I’ll follow standard patterns used by our forebears.”

Hallas stepped aside to give her room. “I’ve studied a bit of ancient lore,” he said quietly, “mostly under older Rangers who passed their knowledge on. If you need a second opinion, I can try to recall some of the old patterns.”

Celenneth gave a thin smile. “Your help would be welcome.” Together, they worked in near silence, except for soft murmurs and the faint scrape of tools on stone. Celenneth chipped away loose fragments carefully, then used the charcoal to sketch where the missing runes might have flowed. Hallas peered at another pillar, comparing runes that remained intact, calling out small corrections: “This stroke should curve slightly… I recall that the binding runes often ended in a spiral, not a sharp angle.”

Following his guidance, Celenneth etched the corrected lines. Sparks of blue light danced faintly as she connected the symbols, as though the old magic recognized her intent. At one point, a subtle tremor passed through the stone under their hands, making both rangers pause and share a startled glance. Something awakened, or at least stirred, in the deep places beneath their feet.

“Don’t stop,” Hallas said gently, voice hushed but certain. “We’ve come this far.”

Celenneth nodded, finishing the last few strokes. The runes glowed softly, then dimmed to a steady shimmer. The constant hum in the ruins mellowed, becoming less ominous. She stepped back, wiping a bead of sweat from her temple. “That should hold it for a while,” she said. “Not forever, but it might make this area less volatile.”

Hallas ran a hand over the newly inscribed runes, careful not to smudge the fine lines. “Well done,” he said simply.

Damrod and Linnea approached from the shadows, weapons lowered but eyes still wary. Linnea’s gaze flicked to the runes, then to Celenneth, a silent question. Celenneth nodded, letting a small smile show. “We’ve stabilized it,” she told them. “At least for now.”

Damrod’s stern expression eased slightly. “Good. If we can prevent these runes from fully collapsing, we might buy ourselves time to understand what we’re dealing with.”

Hallas turned to face the others. “Let’s find a place to camp,” he said, his tone firmer now. “We’ve done what we can here. Night falls fast, and we should rest before descending any deeper.”
