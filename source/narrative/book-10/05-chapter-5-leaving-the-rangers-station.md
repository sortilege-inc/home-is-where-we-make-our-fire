# Chapter 5: Leaving the Ranger's Station

*Nénimë 25, 2970*

Grey dawn seeped into the station, and the rangers moved about stoking the fire and brewing weak tea. Gaidric and Aldric gave Eira a small, knowing nod; she returned a lazy wink. Celenneth and Linnea rose stiff but warm, the night's closeness lingering in the glances they traded, and Eira joined them at the hearth.

"Well, Eira," Linnea said, husky with sleep, "you had a productive night."

Eira smirked, unabashed. "I did. You two seemed inspired yourselves." Then the levity thinned, and Celenneth's face turned serious. "Linnea and I are heading out. Thank you for what you've shared. We'll send word if we can." Eira embraced them both, and Gaidric and Aldric nodded solemn farewells, and the two women mounted and rode out into the biting wind, leaving Eira to her own road north.

The wind off the Evendim hills was sharp enough to steal breath. The station sat at the very edge of the known trails; beyond it lay only broken hills and thin forest, and somewhere ahead, the frozen ruin the rangers called the Frost Maw — a country of ice-choked valleys and strange stones. They pulled their cloaks tight and set out, Celenneth guiding their route along faint old game-trails, Linnea keen-eyed at her flank. The weather worsened the deeper they pressed, snow reaching the horses' chests, and at length the ground grew too rough for riding; they tethered the horses in a sheltered hollow and went on afoot.

In a narrow pass Linnea raised a hand: fresh disturbance in the snow, large paw-prints. A predator. They crouched, and Linnea's eye caught it — a white shape low behind a ridge of ice, near-invisible against the snow. A warg, big and silent. The gorge behind was too steep to slip back through unseen, and flight would only invite a chase, so they set to meet it from strength: Celenneth climbed a rocky ledge for the height, Linnea angling behind a boulder for a clear line.

When Celenneth stepped into a patch of weak sun the warg snarled and launched itself up the icy rise. She swung down hard, but the blade skittered off its thick hide; below, Linnea's arrow slipped under its raised shoulder and bit deep, and the beast howled. It vaulted at Celenneth and its weight bore her down onto the ice, teeth snapping inches from her face — her armor turned the bite — and she wrenched free and rolled clear. Linnea's second shot went wide as the maddened beast twisted; Celenneth found her feet and drove a precise thrust deep into its flank, and it stumbled, blood black on the snow. When its teeth sought her throat she took the lunge on her buckler and thrust upward into its chest, and drove the steel home until it collapsed with a last ragged sigh.[^r-warg]

"Hurt?" Linnea hurried to her.

Celenneth tested her ribs. "Bruised. The armor took it. Let's move."

⁂

They retrieved the horses and pressed on, and by dawn — after a blizzard that drove them to huddle a night beneath a rocky shelf — they crested a rise and saw the ruins: tall pillars of runed stone rising like broken teeth from the ice, the silence broken only by a strange hum that came and went with the wind. And among the fallen blocks, two figures moved — men, tall and lean, cloaked to blend with the winter gloom, studying the old carvings. Rangers, by the way they moved.

Celenneth stepped from behind a pillar, one hand raised, Linnea half-hidden with an arrow ready. "Rangers! Be at ease. Friends approach."

Both spun, weapons half-drawn — and the taller, dark-skinned and stern-eyed, let his gaze fall on the old ranger's star-token Celenneth held out, and eased his blade back into its sheath. "Name yourself."

"Celenneth of the Dúnedain. This is Linnea, my companion. We mean no harm."

"I am Damrod," the man said. "That's Hallas, my partner." The fair-haired ranger a pace behind kept a hand on his hilt. "We patrol these hills. Few sane folk wander this far north in winter. What brings you?"

"We seek the way into the Frost Maw," Celenneth said. "But these ruins caught our eye, and your cloaks — I hoped you might be kin, or friends of it. Allies are scarce out here."

Hallas snorted softly, brushing frost from his beard. "Anyone bound for that frozen hell has good reason. The Maw's known for swallowing travelers whole."

Damrod raised a hand. "Ice-beasts have been terrorizing the folk of the Lone-lands, and we traced them to here. There's a power stirring beneath these stones. If we don't root it out, the North keeps suffering."

Celenneth nodded slowly. "Then we'll help each other. Gladly we'd see these beasts weakened — and in turn, I ask that you come with us into the Maw, where my guardians went. Brandor and Orlec."

Damrod's jaw tightened, and Hallas looked at Celenneth more closely. "Wait — you were their protégé, were you not?"

"I was," Celenneth said, grateful for the recognition. "They ventured into its mouth. Whether we find them, or only their bones, I have to know."

Hallas crossed his arms. "We'll come — on one condition. We settle what's cursing this land first. A fair bargain?"

Celenneth looked to Linnea, who nodded slowly. "A fair bargain," she said. "We'll stand with you."[^r-damrod]

⁂

Before the shadows fully claimed the ruins, they set to work. Damrod and Linnea kept watch while Celenneth and Hallas gathered at a cracked pillar etched with faded runes — delicate, intricate work, chipped and frost-blurred. "These were made to seal or guard something beneath the ice," Celenneth murmured. "Binding sigils. But corrupted — a whole line chipped away, so the sequence no longer holds."

"Carefully made, once," Hallas said, low, as if afraid to wake what lay below. "Can it be restored?"

"We can try. If the runes are stabilized, it might weaken whatever's spreading these beasts. At least for now." From her belt she drew a small chisel, a hooked knife, a wrapped stub of charcoal, and set to re-inscribing the missing segments while Hallas — who had studied a little old lore under older rangers — compared the intact pillars and called soft corrections: *that stroke should curve; the binding runes ended in a spiral, not an angle.* Following his guidance she etched the corrected lines, and faint blue sparks danced where the symbols joined, as though the old magic knew her intent. A tremor passed through the stone under their hands, and they shared a startled glance — something below had stirred — but Hallas only said, "Don't stop. We've come this far." She finished the last strokes, and the runes glowed and then settled to a steady shimmer, and the ruins' ominous hum mellowed. "That should hold it a while," she said, wiping sweat from her temple. "Not forever. But it makes this place less volatile."[^r-runes]

Hallas ran a gloved hand over the new-cut lines. "Well done." And as night fell fast, they went to find a place to camp before descending any deeper.
