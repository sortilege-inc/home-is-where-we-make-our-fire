# Chapter 1: Heading North

*Nénimë 7, 2970*

Winter was loosening its grip on the Angle. Most of the snow had gone to slush and mud, the trees still bare but the air softening, and the scattered Dúnedain families were out mending fences and clearing ditches against the coming spring. Inside their cottage near the settlement's edge, Celenneth stood at the window while Linnea coaxed the hearth-fire to a steady glow and Brynja — four years old now, and full of questions — darted about arranging wooden figures along the sill. *Where do the birds sleep at night? Why does the snow melt when the sun comes?* They answered what they could, amused by her endless curiosity.

They had made a life here, among these quiet, resilient folk who shared their harvests and asked no questions about the past. It was good. But Celenneth had felt, all winter, an old restlessness she could not name — a habit of staring north as though listening for voices carried on the wind. She had told Linnea some of it: the rangers who had raised her, Brandor and Orlec, the men who taught her to track and hunt and read the shape of clouds, whom she had left at fifteen to walk her own road. She had not seen them in years. She did not know if they still lived.

It was Linnea who had first asked, one lamplit evening after Brynja slept: *Have you ever thought about going north? Back to your kin?* And Celenneth had turned the word over — *kin* — and admitted she had, and admitted too that she was afraid of what she'd find. Or wouldn't. *If you ever want to go,* Linnea had said, covering her hand, *I'll go with you. You won't face it alone.* And Celenneth had said, *not yet.*

The *not yet* had lingered, and ripened, until at last she could no longer bear the not-knowing. She needed the truth of them — whether the men who raised her still walked the wild roads, or whether she was chasing ghosts. It was time.

The question of Brynja settled itself. Four was too young for a hard winter trek that might end in sorrow and could stretch to weeks, so they turned to their closest friends in the Angle — the Aldions: Eadric, a steady, kind tracker; Lira, wise and gentle with her herb-lore; and their daughter Elys, Brynja's own age and inseparable friend. Eadric and Lira agreed without hesitation; they understood the pull of an old question. Brynja was uncertain why her parents would go without her, but the promise of a long stay with Elys — a "special sleepover," as Elys called it — soothed her.

They spent a few days making ready — cloaks and blankets, dried food, flint and tinder, Celenneth's blade sharpened and her bow checked — and their last quiet evenings with Brynja, explaining as best they could. *We're going to find some old friends of Mama Celenneth. We'll be gone a while, but we'll come back.* Brynja nodded, though Celenneth doubted she understood; children felt time differently.

At dawn they rose, and the Aldions came with Elys, and the two girls ran off to chase sparrows by the fence. Lira pressed a pouch of dried herbs into Linnea's hands; Eadric gripped Celenneth's shoulder in quiet solidarity. When it came time to mount, Brynja stood at the cottage door and held first Celenneth's hand, then Linnea's, serious for a child so small.

Celenneth crouched and kissed her brow. "You be good. Listen to Lira and Eadric, and we'll bring you something from our travels." And then Brynja reached up and patted her cheek, a small grave gesture that said, plain as words, *I trust you* — and Celenneth had to look away a moment to keep her composure.

They mounted Nimroch and Eorlin and set out north, and the Aldions waved until the riders were small on the horizon, the two girls already hopping into some new game. Winter's hold was loosening but not gone; snow clung in the hollows, and a chill rode the wind, but here and there a bird sang, and a few early wildflowers dared to bloom near sheltered rock. They kept off the main routes, taking the quiet scouting-paths the Dúnedain used.

"Thank you," Celenneth said after a while, the words carried on the breeze. "For coming with me."

Linnea smiled. "I promised I would."

"I remember. I just never thought I'd find the courage to actually go."

That night they camped by a stream, and over dried fruit and hard bread spoke low of where to start. "Rangers meet in certain hidden camps — places I half-remember," Celenneth said, poking the embers. "We'll try one of those old haunts, and ask in the villages between. Rangers leave a memory, if not a footprint." When Linnea asked, quietly, whether Brynja would be all right, Celenneth found she could answer it honestly: she was in good hands, and there was a home to come back to. "For a long time I never stayed anywhere long enough to call it that," she said. "And now we leave her with friends I trust. That feels like a gift."

Linnea found her hand in the firelight. "It is one. We earned it."[^r-northward]
