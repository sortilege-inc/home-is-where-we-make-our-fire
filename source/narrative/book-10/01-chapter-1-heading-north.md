# Chapter 1: Heading North

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 7, 2970*

Near the end of winter, 2970, the Angles stood in a state of quiet transition. Most of the snow had melted, leaving patches of slush and muddy paths. The trees were still bare, but the air had begun to soften. There was a sense of waiting, of leaning toward spring. Birds called early in the mornings, and the Dúnedain families who lived scattered across these gentle hills were busy repairing fences, clearing ditches, and preparing fields for the warmer seasons ahead.

Inside a small cottage near the outskirts of the Angles, Linnea knelt beside the hearth, coaxing a low fire into a steady glow. The warmth slowly spread through the main room. Celenneth stood by the window, looking out at a horizon still tinged with winter’s gray. Brynja, now four years old and full of questions, darted from one corner of the room to the next, collecting a handful of wooden figures she liked to arrange along the windowsill. Every so often, she’d ask something like, “Where do the birds sleep at night?” or “Why does the snow melt when the sun comes out?” Linnea and Celenneth answered what they could, amused by her endless curiosity.

They had made a life here in the Angles. Among these Dúnedain families—quiet, resilient folk who shared their harvests and their stories—Linnea and Celenneth had found something close to home. Brynja had friends to play with, neighbors dropped by with bread or honey, and no one asked too many questions about the past. It was a life of small comforts and daily chores: gathering herbs, helping neighbors mend a fence, trading gossip at the well. Simple, and good.

But Linnea knew that Celenneth carried a lingering restlessness. She’d seen it in the way Celenneth sometimes stared into the distance, as though listening for old voices carried by the wind. Celenneth had told her stories—of the rangers who’d raised her when she was young, of running beneath endless skies and learning to track in silent forests, of leaving them behind at fourteen or fifteen to forge her own path. They were part of Celenneth’s past, but she hadn’t seen them in years. She didn’t even know if they still lived.

Linnea recalled a quiet evening a few weeks ago, after Brynja had fallen asleep. The two of them had sat at the table, lamplight dancing on their faces, a comfortable hush between them. Linnea had asked softly, “Have you ever thought about going north? Back to your kin?”

Celenneth had paused, blade and whetstone in hand. “My kin,” she said, as if tasting unfamiliar words. She looked at the sword, then at Linnea. “I have thought about it, yes,” she admitted. “But it’s been so long. I left them when I was so young. I’m not even sure they’re alive.”

Linnea remembered how her heart had tightened at that uncertainty. “Don’t you want to know?” she’d asked gently.

Celenneth had studied her face, then looked at her empty palm as if searching for something that wasn’t there. “Sometimes I do,” she said. “But maybe I’m afraid of what I’ll find. Or won’t find. Life out there is hard. Rangers move like ghosts, leaving few traces unless they want to be found.”

Linnea had reached across the table and covered Celenneth’s hand with her own. “If you ever want to go,” she’d said, “I’ll go with you. You won’t have to face it alone.”

Celenneth’s eyes had grown distant, but grateful. “Thank you. But I’m not sure I could bear the answers. Not yet.”

That “not yet” lingered.

Now, weeks later, that moment had ripened into a decision. Celenneth had finally admitted she did need to know, or at least try. The uncertainty weighed on her too heavily. She wanted to see if the men who raised her still walked the wild roads. Even if they were gone, she needed the truth. It was time.

Linnea supported this wholeheartedly. She saw how Celenneth wrestled with her past, and she believed that facing it could give Celenneth peace—help her feel settled in their life here, or guide them toward something new. For them to truly live in the Angles, building a future with Brynja, Celenneth needed to understand her roots, or at least close that old chapter with honesty.

So they decided: they would travel north. The journey would be long, and not without risk. The winter roads were muddy, and who knew what lurked between the Angles and the far northern reaches where rangers sometimes gathered.

The question of Brynja arose immediately. The child was strong and curious, but four years old was too young for such a trek. They’d be moving through rough country, and this journey wasn’t just a holiday—it was a search that might end in sorrow or uncertainty, and it could take weeks, maybe longer.

They turned to their closest friends in the Angles, the Aldion family: Eadric, Lira, and their daughter, Elys. Eadric and Lira had welcomed Linnea and Celenneth warmly since they settled here. Eadric, a skilled tracker who often patrolled the region’s outskirts, was steady and kind. Lira was known for her herbal remedies and calm wisdom. Their daughter, Elys, was Brynja’s age, and the two girls had become inseparable, spending afternoons playing in the fields, chasing insects, and splashing in puddles.

When Linnea and Celenneth asked if they might leave Brynja with them, Eadric and Lira agreed without hesitation. They understood the pull of the past and the need to face old questions. Brynja, of course, was uncertain why her parents would go without her, but the promise of staying with Elys—her best friend—soothed her. Lira knelt before Brynja and explained softly that her parents had important errands, but would come back soon, and that in the meantime, Brynja and Elys would have many adventures together. Brynja’s worried frown eased at the mention of her friend.

The final preparations took a few days. Linnea and Celenneth sorted their gear: sturdy cloaks, warm blankets, dried foods, simple medicines. Celenneth sharpened her blade and checked her bow. Linnea packed flint and tinder, extra wool socks, and a small tin of salve for sore muscles. They gathered what coin they had, though money might not matter much if they ranged far off the beaten track.

In the evenings before their departure, they spent quiet moments with Brynja. They explained as best they could. Linnea said, “We’re going to find some old friends of Mama Celenneth. We’ll be gone a while, but we’ll come back.” Brynja nodded, eyes bright, though Linnea could see she didn’t fully understand. Children felt time differently—“a while” could mean anything.

The night before they were to leave, Linnea and Celenneth sat by the hearth after Brynja had gone to sleep. Eadric and Lira had stopped by earlier, confirming all was ready. Elys couldn’t wait for Brynja’s “special sleepover,” as she called it. Everything was in place.

Linnea studied Celenneth’s face in the firelight. “Nervous?” she asked softly.

Celenneth took a moment before answering. “A little,” she said, voice quiet. “I keep wondering what we’ll find. Maybe they’re alive and well, or maybe I’m chasing ghosts.” Her gaze dropped to the floor. “But not knowing feels worse than anything else.”

Linnea nodded, lacing their fingers together. “We’ll face it together,” she said. “And when it’s done, we’ll come home.”

Celenneth smiled at that word, “home.” It had taken time for this place—the Angles, this cottage, these neighbors—to feel like that. But it did now, thanks to Brynja’s laughter echoing in the yard, thanks to shared meals with Eadric and Lira, thanks to the sense of belonging they’d built piece by piece. If a journey north could help Celenneth settle the last unanswered questions in her heart, it was worth it.

At dawn, they rose early. Brynja was up, too, sleepy-eyed but excited because Elys and her parents would come soon. When Eadric and Lira arrived with Elys, the two girls ran off to chase a flock of sparrows near the fence. Lira handed Linnea a small pouch of dried herbs “for the road,” and Eadric gripped Celenneth’s shoulder in quiet solidarity.

Brynja returned as they saddled the horses. She stood at the cottage door, holding Celenneth’s hand tight and then Linnea’s. She looked between them, serious for a child her age. Linnea bent down, stroking Brynja’s dark hair. “We’ll be back,” Linnea promised, voice gentle. “And while we’re gone, you’ll have fun with Elys, right?”

Brynja nodded slowly. “I’ll show her how to catch beetles by the stream,” she said matter-of-factly, as if that sealed the deal.

Celenneth crouched down, pressing a kiss to Brynja’s forehead. “You be good,” she said softly. “Listen to Lira and Eadric, and we’ll bring you something special from our travels.”

Brynja’s eyes brightened at that. Then she did something that made Linnea’s throat tighten: she reached up and patted Celenneth’s cheek as if to say, “I trust you.” It was a small gesture, but so full of understanding and love that Celenneth had to look away for a moment to keep her composure.

They mounted their horses, Eorlin and Nimroch. The morning air felt crisp on their faces, and the muddy roads gleamed in the early light. They set out northward, following a path that wound through low hills and sparse woodlands. The Aldion family stood waving until the riders were small figures on the horizon. Brynja and Elys hopped and laughed, already making new games.

The landscape opened up before them. Winter’s hold was loosening, but not yet gone. Patches of snow clung in hollows, and the wind carried a faint chill. Still, birds sang here and there, and they passed a few early wildflowers daring to bloom near sheltered rocks. They avoided the main routes where possible, preferring quieter paths that the Dúnedain used for scouting and travel.

As they rode, Linnea’s thoughts drifted to their goal. They were heading toward lands where rangers moved like whispers, men and women who guarded roads and borders but seldom revealed themselves. Finding the two men who raised Celenneth might be tricky. They might need to ask quietly among scattered communities, track rumors, follow signs of ranger presence. It wouldn’t be simple. But Celenneth’s determination was solid, and Linnea’s loyalty was unwavering. They had time and patience.

Celenneth broke the silence after a few hours. “Thank you,” she said, her voice carried by the breeze. “For doing this with me.”

Linnea smiled, adjusting her cloak. “I promised you I would, remember?”

Celenneth glanced sidelong at her, warmth in her eyes. “I remember. Just didn’t think I’d ever gather the courage to actually go.”

Linnea shrugged lightly. “Sometimes you just need someone to push you a little. Besides, I’m curious, too. I’ve heard so much about these men, these ranger-guardians of your past. I want to know what shaped you into who you are.”

Celenneth’s expression grew thoughtful. “They taught me to track, to hunt, to read the weather in the shape of clouds. They were harsh at times, but kind too. I was so young, I didn’t understand half of what they said about duty and sacrifice. I wonder if I’ll understand it now.”

Linnea listened, picturing Celenneth as a wiry teenager, following two tall, weathered men through secret forest trails. She could almost see it: the stern lessons, the quiet smiles, the patient corrections. It had formed Celenneth’s character—her steady hand, her keen senses, her quiet strength. Linnea felt gratitude toward these unknown men, whoever and wherever they were, for helping make Celenneth who she became.

They rode through the remainder of the day and camped by a small stream that night. Linnea gathered tinder and built a fire while Celenneth scouted the perimeter. The world felt vast and quiet, with no sound but the flow of water and the rustle of dry reeds. Linnea missed Brynja’s chatter, but the memory of her daughter’s trust filled her with a warm resolve.

Over a simple dinner of dried fruit and hard bread, they spoke in low voices. “Any idea where to start looking?” Linnea asked.

Celenneth poked at the fire’s embers. “I know that rangers sometimes meet in certain hidden camps—places I remember vaguely. We could head toward one of those old haunts I recall, see if anyone’s there or left signs. If not, we’ll ask at small villages along the way. People might have seen them. Rangers usually leave a memory, if not a footprint.”

Linnea nodded, satisfied with this loose plan. “We’ve got time,” she said, stretching out under her blanket. Above them, stars scattered across a deep sky. She wondered if Brynja was asleep by now, curled up with Elys, or telling stories in her high-pitched voice until Lira coaxed them to rest.

“Do you think Brynja’s all right?” Celenneth asked quietly, echoing Linnea’s thoughts.

“Of course,” Linnea replied, confident. “She’s in good hands. And if there’s any trouble, Eadric can handle it. Besides, Brynja’s excited to be with Elys. I think she’ll hardly notice we’re gone once they start having fun.”

Celenneth chuckled softly. “You’re probably right.” She fell silent for a moment, then added, “I’m glad we have a home to come back to. For a long time, I never stayed in one place long enough to call it that. And now we leave Brynja with friends I trust. That... feels like a gift.”

Linnea reached out, found Celenneth’s hand in the flickering light. “It is a gift. One we earned by building a life there.”

They said no more, letting the soft crackle of the fire lull them. The journey had just begun. Ahead lay uncertainty, old memories, and perhaps difficult truths. But they were not alone. They had each other, and a place to return to. That steadied them both.
