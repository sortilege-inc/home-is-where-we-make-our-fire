# Chapter 6: The Frozen Depths

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 28, 2970*

They gathered dead branches from a small copse of twisted shrubs nearby, enough for a modest fire. Hallas scraped frost off a flat stone to form a makeshift hearth, and Linnea sparked a flame with flint and steel. Soon the warmth of a small blaze took the edge off the biting cold. Long shadows stretched across carved pillars and heaps of snow, as if ancient spirits watched in silence.

They sat close, knees nearly touching, each sipping hot water—tea leaves were scarce this far north. The silence between them felt heavy with recent events, unspoken questions, and the tension of strangers forced together by necessity.

Linnea broke the quiet first, her voice low but cordial. “We’ve fought beasts and survived storms, and yet we know little of one another beyond names and the mission at hand. Perhaps we should change that before we go below.” She offered a faint smile.

Celenneth shifted slightly, uncomfortable. “I’m Celenneth, as you know. Raised by rangers who ventured where I now must follow—Brandor and Orlec. They vanished into the Frost Maw.” She paused, glancing at Linnea. “Linnea and I traveled south and east for a time, making a life near Bree, but the North calls us back, for now.”

Linnea, resting her chin on her hand, nodded. “We built a life of sorts,” she added softly. “We have… a family waiting for us. But Celenneth’s duty and love for her lost guardians brought us here.” Her gaze flicked between Damrod and Hallas. “We owe it to them, and to ourselves, to find answers.”

Hallas’s eyes narrowed thoughtfully. “Your guardians… I believe I heard their names in passing once, some time ago. They were known for their skill and courage.” He poked at the fire with a stick. “You must be brave yourselves, to follow them into the Maw.”

Damrod took his turn. “I am Damrod, warden of these wilds, though my path has wandered close to Angmar’s old bones. Hallas and I came north tracking rumors of these frost beasts. We had hoped to nip a budding threat before it spread further south.” He looked at Hallas, who inclined his head for him to continue. “It seems the power running through these hills, through old runes and dark magic, is not easily tamed. We didn’t expect to find allies here.”

Celenneth studied Damrod’s face—lined, calm, and steady. “And you, Hallas?”

Hallas cleared his throat, choosing words carefully. “I hail from a line that served the Dúnedain cause for generations. I’ve known cold winters and hungry wolves, but these frost beasts are something else. Damrod and I hoped to end this threat quickly.” He managed a wan smile, thin but genuine. “Now it appears we must go deeper than we ever intended. Into the Maw, potentially.”

Linnea rubbed her hands over the flame’s warmth. “We understand if you wish to avoid that place. Its reputation is…” She left the sentence unfinished. Everyone knew the Maw’s dreadful legends.

A long silence followed, filled only by the crackle of burning twigs and the distant moan of the wind through broken arches. The four listened to that sound, each lost in thought. They were strangers only a few hours ago, now bound by fate’s whim.

Linnea broke the silence once more, her tone lighter. “At least we share a willingness to fight, and trust each can hold their own.”

Hallas lifted an eyebrow. “I’d say we can trust your blade and your bow. For all our sakes, I hope we prove worthy of yours.” He offered a quick nod, a ranger’s version of a handshake.

Damrod chuckled softly, though it was a humorless sound. “Worthy or not, the land doesn’t care. We have our work cut out for us come morning.”

Eventually, they banked the fire. The night grew colder, but in their small circle, a fragile trust had formed. They arranged watches—Hallas first, then Celenneth, then Damrod, and finally Linnea as the pale dawn approached. Before turning in, Celenneth met Linnea’s eyes and saw acceptance there. They were not alone.

The ruins’ strange hum lingered in the cold air as Celenneth, Linnea, Damrod, and Hallas prepared to venture below. Packed supplies rattled quietly in saddlebags left behind under guard of their tethered horses. They had found a narrow opening concealed by half-buried stones and frost-coated brush—a dark tunnel angling down into the earth. The very air near the entrance felt tighter, as if the world held its breath. The four exchanged grim looks.

“Down we go,” said Damrod quietly, adjusting the grip on his sword. Hallas nodded in agreement, though tension etched lines at the corners of his eyes. Linnea unstrung her bow to pass through the cramped entrance, then strung it again once inside. Celenneth led with a small hooded lantern, its faint light glistening off ice-slick rock walls.

The tunnel descended gradually, carved long ago by hands unknown. Runes and faint carvings—too weathered to read—lined patches of stone. The silence pressed in, broken only by the drip of meltwater and their soft footfalls. Now and then, an unsettling echo drifted through the darkness, as if something stirred ahead.

They advanced slowly. After several minutes, the passage narrowed, forcing them into single file. Celenneth held the lantern high, revealing a strange feature ahead: ice-crusted bones half buried in frost. Linnea tapped Celenneth’s shoulder and made a subtle gesture, pointing to fresh disturbances in the thin film of snow dusting the tunnel floor. Tracks. Heavy, clawed.

Hallas murmured, “Frost hounds,” lips barely moving. Damrod nodded, eyes grim. They would likely attack without warning, and this cramped space favored ambushers.

They formed a quick plan with gestures: Celenneth and Damrod would move forward slowly, drawing the hounds’ attention, while Linnea and Hallas prepared to strike from behind. Celenneth wrapped a spare cloth around the lantern’s glass, dimming its light to near darkness. If the hounds relied on sight, this might confuse them. If they hunted by scent, well, stealth would only do so much.

They crept onward, each step cautious. The tunnel curved slightly, and as Celenneth rounded the bend, she caught a glimpse of movement—low and silent shapes blending into the icy gloom. Three frost hounds crouched in the darkness. The largest one, its fur rimed with frost crystals, stood in the center, slightly ahead of its smaller pack mates. Its eyes reflected a dull, hungry blue light.

Before Celenneth could signal, the hounds attacked. The leader lunged forward with a snarl that echoed down the stone passage. Damrod rushed to meet it, sword flashing. Celenneth pivoted left, raising her buckler. Behind them, Linnea and Hallas sprang into action.

The leader struck first, colliding with Damrod’s blade. Sparks flared as steel met ice-hardened fur. The beast’s fangs snapped inches from Damrod’s face. Damrod grunted, forcing it back with a powerful shove. Celenneth took the opportunity to sidestep and slash at the hound’s flank, but its thick, frozen pelt turned the blade aside, showering them in icy chips.

From the rear, Linnea’s bowstring thrummed. Her first arrow sped between Celenneth and Damrod, finding the chest of a smaller hound creeping close to the wall. The shaft sank deep, and the creature yelped, staggering as blackish-ichor bled over white fur. Hallas pressed forward on the opposite side, spear braced low. As the wounded hound turned to snarl at Linnea, Hallas thrust upward. The point struck true beneath its jaw. A sickening crack, and that hound collapsed, limbs twitching.

Now two remained. The leader sprang at Damrod again, jaws clamping onto the ranger’s shield edge, trying to wrench it free. Damrod twisted, freeing himself, and Celenneth seized the moment. She lunged, driving her sword toward the hound’s exposed flank. The blade bit deep this time. The beast roared, kicking up shards of ice with its hind legs. Damrod followed with a downward slash at its shoulder, forcing it to release a strangled howl.

Meanwhile, the third hound tried to flank them, skittering along the tunnel’s edge. Linnea pivoted, drawing another arrow swiftly. The cramped space made it hard to aim, but she steadied her breath and loosed. The arrow nicked the hound’s hindquarters, causing it to snarl and recoil. Hallas advanced, spear lowered, but the hound dodged, claws scraping stone and ice as it tried to dart past him. Hallas swung the spear’s haft sideways, catching it across the muzzle and knocking it off balance.

Celenneth and Damrod concentrated on the leader. Wounded and furious, the big hound tore chunks of ice from the walls in its frenzy. Damrod’s shield took another heavy blow, nearly numbing his arm. Celenneth circled to the right, delivering a careful thrust to the beast’s side. With a final strangled cry, the hound stumbled. Damrod finished it with a grim strike to its neck.

The last hound realized it was alone. It backed away, hackles raised. Linnea angled for a clear shot, but the beast suddenly charged Hallas, desperate to break free. Hallas braced himself, spear forward. The hound impaled itself on the steel tip, momentum driving it closer than Hallas would have liked. The ranger gritted his teeth and heaved, twisting the spear. The hound spasmed and fell limp, its frosty breath fading into silence.

They stood panting in the gloom. The battle had been swift and brutal. Damrod wiped sweat from his brow, checking his arm and shield. Celenneth knelt, ensuring the hounds were truly dead. Linnea retrieved her arrows where possible, and Hallas caught his breath, leaning on his spear.

“These creatures,” Celenneth murmured, “they’re being guided, aren’t they?” She pointed at faint runes carved into the hounds’ hides. It looked like some cruel magic bound them. Damrod nodded. “This place grows more accursed the deeper we go.”

No one argued. They cleaned blades and tended minor scrapes. The lantern’s light, uncovered now, revealed a downward slope continuing into deeper darkness. The air grew colder still.

They pressed on, wary and tired. The tunnel walls soon widened, and after another hundred paces, they emerged into a vast underground chamber. The ceiling soared high, sparkling with ice crystals that reflected their lantern light. Carvings adorned the stone walls—ancient reliefs depicting battles between tall, cloaked figures and misshapen creatures of ice. One panel showed a line of warriors forging a great blade beneath starlight. Another depicted hooded figures inscribing runes into pillars of stone.

They spread out carefully, lantern light sweeping over worn stone floors. In the center of the chamber stood a stone altar, partially encased in ice. Strange runes, some similar to those they’d seen in the ruins above, glowed faintly. Celenneth and Damrod stepped forward, studying it. Hallas and Linnea stood guard, scanning the perimeter.

“This might be it,” Damrod said softly, voice echoing. “A place of power—perhaps what we came to find, or at least part of it.”

Hallas approached a carved relief on the left wall, running a gloved hand over the etched lines. “These warriors… they look like Númenórean rangers of old,” he said, awe creeping into his tone. “They were sealing something, binding it with runes. If this altar is linked to that magic…”

Linnea frowned. “If so, who corrupted it? We saw those beasts and their runes. Something here is twisting these old wards.”

Celenneth nodded. “We must be careful. This must be the entrance, or close to it, of the Frost Maw; it is known for devouring the unwary.”

As they examined the altar and reliefs, an uneasy hush settled. The temperature dipped sharply. Their breath hung in clouds. Suddenly, Linnea stiffened. “Do you feel that?” she whispered. A pressure in the air, a sense of eyes watching from unseen corners.

Without warning, shapes emerged from behind icy columns and fallen stones. Wraiths, pale and silent, drifting with impossible grace. Four of them, each cloaked in rime and wielding blades that shimmered with hoarfrost. Their hollow eyes glowed with a dull, hungry light.

The wraiths attacked in eerie silence. One rushed Linnea, raising its icy blade high. She leapt back, loosing an arrow that passed through its misted torso, scattering frost crystals. The creature only re-formed a pace away, twisting its head in a soundless mockery of a laugh.

Hallas met another wraith’s charge, spear poised. The spear’s tip met an odd resistance, neither flesh nor empty air. He felt a biting cold spread through the spear’s shaft into his hands. Damrod and Celenneth stood back-to-back, blades ready. A third wraith lunged at Damrod’s flank. Damrod blocked, but the spirit’s icy sword left a crust of frost along his shield rim.

Celenneth struck at the nearest phantom. Her sword passed through the wraith’s robes, finding scant purchase. It hissed, drifting aside. She realized steel alone was not enough. Memory sparked—a curse taught in old Ranger archives, a Númenórean incantation meant to unsettle spirits tied to the ancient wars.

As another wraith lunged at her, Celenneth stepped back, raised her voice, and spoke in old words that scraped like distant iron on stone. Her tone was low and forceful: **“Basannórë maitanëa, durëa hlaruvalyë!”** The ancient curse rolled through the chamber, echoing strangely. A faint tremor passed through the wraiths, as if they recognized the words.

Linnea and Hallas exchanged startled glances. Damrod, eyes narrowing, understood that Celenneth might have tapped into old lore. Another wraith advanced on him, blade raised. He repeated the words he’d just heard from Celenneth, stumbling slightly on the syllables: **“Basannórë maitanëa…”** His voice lacked her fluency, but the effect was there—he saw the wraith’s form flicker and slow, as if wading through thicker air.

Hallas caught on, muttering the curse under his breath, each repetition lending it power. Linnea’s voice joined, hesitant at first, but growing stronger. Together, they chanted this old Númenórean malediction, each voice adding weight to the ancient words. The wraiths shrank back slightly, forced to contend not just with steel, but with the heavy presence of old magic remembered.

Seizing the advantage, Linnea tried another tactic. She wrapped oil-soaked cloth around an arrowhead and sparked it aflame with quick strikes of flint. The arrow’s flame danced in the gloom. She shot at a wraith weakened by their chanting. The flame found a hold in its nebulous form, and the wraith shuddered, its silent shriek like a ripple in the air.

Hallas took the cue and struck at the same wraith. This time, his spear met something more solid, as if the curse and the flame had given the spirit just enough substance to be broken. The wraith’s form scattered into drifting motes of ice.

The remaining three grew frantic, blades chopping at the air. Damrod braced his shield as one hammered at it with furious strokes. Celenneth kept chanting, voice more certain now, her sword raised. She timed her strike as the wraith slowed under the curse’s weight. Her blade cut a faint line of frost-fire through its torso. Linnea fired another flaming arrow, and Damrod lunged, cutting horizontally. The wraith flickered and burst apart in a shower of tiny ice shards.

Two left. One hovered near the altar, trying to draw strength from the runes there. Hallas and Linnea advanced on it, chanting softly, their voices rough but insistent. The wraith tried to dart forward, but stumbled mid-lunge, trapped by old words it could not outrun. Hallas’s spear cracked into its center, and Linnea’s arrow pierced its head, scattering the phantom into formless frost.

The last wraith circled Celenneth and Damrod, wary now. They pressed it, chanting the curse in unison. Celenneth’s pronunciation was the clearest, her voice steady with remembered lore. The spirit strained against the sound, each syllable like a chain dragging it down. Damrod struck high, Celenneth low. The wraith shivered, form collapsing inward. A final gust of cold air swept over them as the wraith broke apart, leaving only empty silence.

In the battle’s aftermath, they stood panting, breath misting in the frosty gloom. Celenneth lowered her sword, heart pounding. She met Damrod’s gaze. He nodded, respect in his eyes. Linnea rubbed her stiff fingers, still holding her bow, and Hallas breathed heavily, leaning on his spear.

“That curse,” Hallas said softly, voice echoing, “you knew it would help?”

Celenneth shrugged, wiping sweat from her brow. “I remembered reading fragments of old spells used by our ancestors against spirits bound to ancient places. I wasn’t sure it would work.”

Damrod let out a short, breathy laugh. “Well, it certainly did.” He flexed his shield arm, the numbness receding now that the wraiths were gone. “Without it, I’m not sure we’d have managed.”

Linnea stepped closer, placing a hand on Celenneth’s shoulder. “Your lore saved us, love,” she said quietly, voice warm despite the chill. Celenneth offered a thin, relieved smile.

The chamber’s silence returned, but now they were alone with the altar and the old reliefs, no phantoms lurking. Tired and shaken, they decided to find a sheltered corner of the cavern to rest. Though none liked the idea of lingering in this tainted chamber, they had no choice. They were too deep to retreat easily, and too worn to push deeper without recovery.

They found a small alcove along the chamber’s perimeter, partly shielded by fallen stone blocks. Linnea treated Hallas’ wound. Damrod cleared debris as he checked for threats. Celenneth set up a makeshift camp, using spare cloaks and a folding brazier from Damrod’s pack. With careful strikes of flint, they lit a low fire. The warmth felt like a gift from a kinder world.

As they settled, Linnea boiled water for tea—just enough to fend off the biting chill. Damrod breathed easier now, gaze drifting to the carved reliefs that surrounded them. Hallas stared at the altar with troubled eyes, as if hoping to understand what they had found. Celenneth rubbed her aching shoulder, recalling how close those wraith blades had come.

In hushed voices, they conferred. It seemed this chamber was not separate from the Frost Maw, but an antechamber of sorts, leading them deeper into its icy bowels. Damrod and Hallas had hoped to address whatever cursed power plagued the region without venturing too far. But these battles with frost hounds and wraiths hinted that the corruption ran deep. Celenneth and Linnea realized that their path toward the Frost Maw and the older rangers’ guardians lay inextricably tied to solving these mysteries. Perhaps the guardians had once stood here, at this very altar, trying to hold back the dark.

For now, they would recover. Bound by a fragile alliance and tested in combat, the four rangers huddled close to the fire, tea steaming faintly in battered cups. They might not have chased away the shadows that haunted this place, but they had survived its first ordeals together. And in the quiet crackle of the small fire, amidst runes and lost histories, they prepared to face whatever lay beyond, deeper in the Maw’s frozen heart.
