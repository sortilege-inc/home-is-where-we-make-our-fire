# Chapter 3: The Broken Branch

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 20, 2970*

The days after leaving Gelden Hollow had been quiet but purposeful. Celenneth, Linnea, and Eira made steady progress north and west, edging closer to the Evendim hills. Though no great roads led there, they found narrow lanes and old tracks, following scraps of knowledge gleaned from the Silver Lily.

The journey so far had been calm enough—no sign of trouble beyond the usual wary glances of passing travelers. Eira proved a pleasant companion, quick with small bits of advice: where to find cleaner water, which shrubs hid biting insects. She could ride, handle a dagger, and had a patient way of bearing the long hours on the trail. Linnea appreciated her presence more than she’d expected. Celenneth seemed relieved to have another person who knew Brandor and Orlec, someone who understood the weight of their absence.

As they crossed into Tarnath’s outskirts, Linnea noticed the shift in atmosphere. This wasn’t a peaceful hollow like Gelden, nor a friendly hamlet. Tarnath leaned closer to the rough side of the world. Crooked buildings clustered around a muddy central lane, the smell of stale ale and smoke drifting through the air. Wagons creaked by, and weathered faces watched from shaded doorways. No one offered greetings.

Celenneth had warned them about Tarnath: it was a crossroads town with a hard reputation, a place mercenaries and cutthroats passed through. But word had it that Brandor and Orlec once stopped here too. They needed confirmation, maybe even a map or name from the tavern known as the Broken Branch.

They led their horses—Nimroch for Celenneth, Eorlin for Linnea, and Eira’s borrowed mare—toward the old tavern at the end of Tarnath’s main row. The Broken Branch sign hung crookedly, paint chipped, barely readable. Even from outside, laughter rang out, harsh and rowdy, mixed with curses and the slam of mugs on tables. The late afternoon light did little to soften the place’s rough edges.

Linnea’s shoulders tightened. She remembered the Silver Lily’s warmth and discreet comfort. This would be nothing like that. Celenneth caught her eye, giving a small nod of reassurance. Eira remained quiet, but Linnea noticed her hand hovering near her belt, where a dagger was sheathed.

“Let’s tie the horses around back,” Celenneth said softly, voice steady. “We won’t linger long.”

They found a rail behind the tavern. A few other horses stood there, thick-shouldered animals that looked well-used by hard riders. Linnea patted Eorlin, whispering a soothing word. Eira scanned the alley with a wary gaze. Satisfied, they made their way to the front door. The building’s weather-beaten planks creaked underfoot.

Inside, the Broken Branch smelled of old ale, cheap pipeweed, and sweat. Lamplight cast shadows over worn tables, and the hearth’s smoke hung in the air. Mercenaries in battered leather armor crowded around small tables, nursing drinks and grunting through broken-toothed smiles. The noise dipped slightly as the trio entered. Eyes turned their way: Linnea’s slender frame, Celenneth’s confident posture, Eira’s calm watchfulness. Three women who didn’t fit the usual mold of this place.

Linnea felt the weight of stares. These weren’t friendly travelers. The silence stretched a heartbeat too long, then broke into muttered whispers. A few low chuckles—nothing friendly there.

Eira stepped closer to Linnea, as if to say, “I’ve got your back too.” Celenneth took the lead, guiding them toward the bar at the far end of the room. They passed a table where a large man with a scarred cheek eyed Linnea with open interest. Another with a face like a weathered rock gave Eira a sidelong smirk. Linnea kept her gaze forward, heart steady. She’d seen this sort of attention before, but it never got easier.

As they reached the bar, the tavernkeeper glanced up. He was an older fellow, grizzled, with sharp eyes. He dried a mug with a rag that looked no cleaner than the floor. Linnea noticed that he, at least, seemed unimpressed by the display of interest from his patrons—perhaps he’d seen it all.

He raised a brow. “What’ll it be?” he asked, voice low and gruff.

Celenneth answered quietly. “Information, mostly. A drink wouldn’t hurt, though. Ale for three, if it’s decent.”

The barkeep gave a curt nod, poured ale into three chipped mugs, and placed them on the counter. “What kind of information?” He didn’t offer a smile, just met Celenneth’s eyes with steady patience.

Celenneth took a mug, sipped, not grimacing at the taste. “We’re looking for two rangers: Brandor and Orlec,” she said. “They passed through here some time ago, chasing rumors of old Numenorian relics. Ever hear those names?”

At that, the barkeep’s gaze sharpened. “Brandor and Orlec. Aye, I remember. Hard to forget men like them. They had a presence—rangers tend to.”

Eira slipped onto a stool beside Linnea, sipping her ale warily. Linnea took a cautious taste—bitter, but drinkable. She watched the barkeep’s face, seeing if he’d recognize Eira. He didn’t seem to, at least not openly.

The barkeep continued, voice quieter now that he knew he was dealing with serious inquiries. “They came through… oh, must have been some months back. Gathered supplies, asked questions about the hills. Thought they smelled of trouble, to be honest.”

Celenneth nodded. “They raised me, long ago. I’m trying to find out what happened to them. We’ve followed hints and rumors from Gelden Hollow and beyond.”

The barkeep tilted his head, and Linnea glimpsed a flicker of recognition at the mention of Gelden Hollow. Perhaps he understood the chain of contacts they’d tapped. “So you’ve got a trail. They headed north, didn’t they? Toward Evendim, I heard. Claimed they’d found signs of old watchtowers and inscriptions. They were in a hurry—looked over their shoulders a lot.”

Eira leaned forward slightly, voice soft. “Did they mention anyone following them, or show fear of certain people?”

The barkeep gave Eira a measured look, as if noting her short black hair and the dagger at her hip. “They didn’t name anyone, but Brandor was tense. He kept scanning the room as if expecting trouble. Orlec tried to act casual, but he couldn’t hide that edge.”

A few mercenaries at a nearby table grew quiet, listening with thinly veiled curiosity. The barkeep noticed and lowered his voice. “They asked me about the old roads that vanish in those hills, about a certain ruin that was said to hold a relic. I warned them not to trust anyone out there. Too many folk smell gold and go mad.”

Celenneth’s eyes narrowed. “They never came back here, I take it.”

He shook his head. “No. Haven’t heard a peep since. But if you’re set on following them, I can mark a rough path on a scrap of parchment. Not a proper map, mind you, but it might help. Could be they took a route that passes by Tarnath’s old logging camp—abandoned now—then north into the wilder stretches.”

Linnea felt a small surge of relief. Another lead, another piece of the puzzle. She exchanged a glance with Eira, who seemed grateful as well. Eira had insisted on joining them to find out what befell Brandor and Orlec. Any solid direction was a comfort.

The barkeep leaned under the counter, rummaging. Celenneth took another sip of ale, remaining alert. Linnea scanned the room again. The large man with the scar watched them, a predatory gleam in his eye, and now his attention shifted to Eira. He murmured something to his tablemates; one of them snorted, looking at Linnea. She stiffened. Eira placed a hand lightly on Linnea’s forearm, a silent “easy now.”

Celenneth turned slightly, noticing the exchange. She stepped closer to Linnea, body language protective. The room’s tension hummed again. The barkeep rose with a tattered scrap of parchment and a piece of charcoal. “I’ll mark what I know,” he said, oblivious or uncaring of the silent stares.

As he sketched, Eira kept a watchful eye on the mercenaries. One rose from his seat, a smirk on his lips. He swaggered over, mug in hand. Linnea’s heart thumped, but she kept her face calm. Celenneth shifted her weight, ready. Eira’s grip on her dagger tightened under the bar.

“Hey there,” the mercenary drawled, looking Eira up and down, then Linnea. “What’s a trio like you doing in a place like this? Lost your way?” His voice dripped with condescension.

Celenneth turned fully now, meeting his gaze without flinching. “We’re not lost,” she said evenly. “We’re having a conversation with the barkeep. Return to your table.”

The mercenary chuckled, glanced over his shoulder at his companions. “Feisty. Maybe we want to join your conversation. Or maybe you’d like to join us.” His eyes lingered on Eira’s blouse, and Linnea felt anger coil inside her. She remembered the Silver Lily’s discreet respect and wished for it now.

Eira’s voice came low and steady. “We’re not interested, friend. Best step back.” She didn’t raise her voice, but the steel in it was clear.

The barkeep glanced up, frowning. He caught Celenneth’s eye and sighed, as if used to breaking up such tension. “Leave them be,” he said to the mercenary, tone bored but firm. “They’re paying customers, and I’m busy.”

The mercenary’s grin faltered. He looked at the barkeep, then at the three women. Maybe he saw something in their stance—the way Celenneth stood like a coiled bowstring, the way Eira’s eyes narrowed, the quiet set of Linnea’s jaw. Perhaps he decided it wasn’t worth the trouble. He shrugged, grunted, and wandered back to his table, muttering curses under his breath.

Linnea exhaled slowly. Eira shot her a reassuring look. Celenneth remained poised, making sure the man sat down before turning back to the barkeep. The barkeep finished drawing, pushing the parchment toward Celenneth.

“Here,” he said, voice low. “This line represents a path they likely took, heading toward a broken ridge near an old logging camp. Past that, they’d veer into rough country. Not many travelers go there—hardly a road anymore. If Brandor and Orlec found that watchtower or those carvings, they’d have headed deeper into the hills.”

Celenneth nodded, eyes scanning the rough map. It was little more than a scribble of lines and landmarks, but better than nothing. “Thank you,” she said quietly. “Any last advice?”

The barkeep shrugged. “Don’t trust anyone easily. The hills draw out desperate folk. You said they were after relics, Numenorian things—those are stories that make men greedy. I’ve heard whispers that not all who search come back sane. Don’t linger in old ruins after dark.”

Eira pursed her lips, as if weighing this warning. Linnea felt a chill. Old ruins, strange relics—this was more than a rescue mission; it might be stepping into a quiet battle between those who respect the past and those who seek to profit from it.

Celenneth tucked the parchment safely in her cloak. “We appreciate it.” She placed a few coins on the bar for the ale and the barkeep’s trouble. The barkeep scooped them up with a curt nod. He didn’t offer wishes of luck; maybe he thought they’d need more than that.

They turned to leave. The room’s murmurs resumed, though a few sets of eyes still tracked their movements. As they passed the mercenaries, Linnea caught a muttered comment she didn’t bother to decipher. Eira moved close, posture protective now, as if roles had reversed and she shielded Linnea.

Outside, the fading daylight felt cleaner. The tension ebbed as they stepped away from the Broken Branch. The horses waited patiently behind the tavern. Celenneth unfurled the parchment once more to memorize the path before dusk swallowed the details.

Eira sighed softly. “That could have gone worse.”

Linnea nodded, checking Eorlin’s girth. “Yes, but we got what we needed.”

Celenneth mounted Nimroch, looking off to the north where the hills would rise unseen in the gloom. “We’ve a rough route now,” she said quietly. “Between this and what we learned from the Lily, we’re closer to tracing Brandor and Orlec’s steps.”

Eira swung into her saddle. “So we head out at dawn again?”

Celenneth considered. “We’ll find a place to camp outside Tarnath tonight. No sense staying in town. Too many curious eyes.”

Linnea agreed wholeheartedly. She’d had enough of that tavern’s atmosphere. As they led their horses out of Tarnath, the few townsfolk still about gave them sidelong glances, but made no moves to interfere. Linnea guessed the folk here knew better than to meddle in strangers’ affairs, especially ones who walked out of the Broken Branch unscathed.

They followed a narrow path out of town until it curved into a patch of low trees. Dusk settled fully, stars beginning to prick the sky. They found a sheltered spot where a fallen log created a natural windbreak. Linnea gathered kindling for a small fire, Celenneth tended the horses, Eira scouted briefly to ensure no one followed.

When Eira returned, she shook her head. “Seems quiet. No trackers I can spot.”

Linnea lit the fire with flint and tinder, a soft glow illuminating their faces. Celenneth spread the parchment on her knee, studying it in the firelight. She traced the suggested route with a fingertip. “From here,” she said softly, “we head toward that old logging camp. Then try to find the watchtower Orlec mentioned. With luck, we’ll see signs of their passing.”

Eira nodded, leaning closer to see. “If they didn’t come back, maybe they left a mark, a camp, something.”

Linnea thought of Brandor and Orlec. “Rangers are careful. They might have left a clue for someone who knew how to read it—like you, Celenneth.”

Celenneth’s lips twitched into a brief, sad smile. “I hope so. Otherwise, we may be chasing ghosts.”

They ate a simple dinner of dried fruit and hard bread. No one said much—the tension from the tavern still lingered, and the gravity of their quest weighed on them. Yet there was also a sense of accomplishment: they had another piece of the puzzle. The Silver Lily had given them purpose, and the Broken Branch had confirmed a route.

Linnea took first watch while Celenneth and Eira dozed. The wind rustled leaves overhead, and distant night birds called. As she stared into the darkness, Linnea considered how quickly their party had grown from two to three, and how their journey had led them through places so different—the quiet dignity of the Silver Lily and the grim rowdiness of the Broken Branch. Each stop added to the tapestry of this search.

At midnight, she gently woke Eira for her turn. Eira smiled sleepily, took her place by the dim embers of the fire. Linnea lay down beside Celenneth, who stirred briefly, hand brushing Linnea’s shoulder in a comforting gesture before drifting back to sleep.

Dawn came with a pale wash of color over the trees. They extinguished the fire, packed their gear, and mounted up. Tarnath lay behind them now, just another waypoint. The parchment from the barkeep would guide them deeper into the wild lands. The Evendim hills awaited.

As they rode, Linnea took heart in the quiet competence of their little band. Eira seemed resolved, Celenneth focused, and Linnea herself calmer knowing they had solid leads. The road ahead might be dangerous, but they wouldn’t face it blindly.

They followed the barkeep’s directions, turning onto an old trail that meandered through fields turning rougher and rockier as they went. The sky was clear, a crisp breeze at their backs. They would find that logging camp, then the watchtower and the forked stream. Step by step, they closed in on the mystery of Brandor and Orlec.

The Broken Branch had given them what they needed: confirmation that the rangers had indeed pressed on into dangerous territory. And though the tavern’s rough patrons had tried to rattle them, they’d emerged intact, their unity strengthened.

Linnea glanced at Eira, who caught her eye and offered a small smile. They had faced the mercenaries’ leers together, just as they’d face the unknown hills together. Celenneth guided Nimroch confidently ahead. The path bent into a small grove, green and quiet, and they vanished from Tarnath’s sphere of influence, riding onward into whatever awaited in the wild lands beyond.
