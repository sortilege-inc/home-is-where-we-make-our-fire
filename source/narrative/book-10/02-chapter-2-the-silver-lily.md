# Chapter 2: The Silver Lily

*Book 10*

*Nénimë 16, 2970*

Late afternoon light stretched across the muddy lanes of Gelden Hollow, turning puddles into pale gold mirrors. Linnea guided her mare, Eorlas, at an easy walk, scanning the shabby fronts of shops and houses. Celenneth rode beside her on Nimroch, the white horse’s coat dusty from travel. They’d arrived just as the sun dipped low, hoping to find someone Celenneth once met—someone who might know more about Brandor and Orlec’s trail.

Gelden Hollow felt tucked away from the world, neither large nor important. The buildings were simple—timber frames, thatched roofs, with crooked signs above a few doors. A blacksmith’s forge smoked in the distance, and somewhere a soft lullaby drifted from a closed window. Nothing grand, but not exactly hostile either.

They were looking for the Silver Lily, a brothel known to Celenneth from younger years. She hadn’t said much about it on the road, only that it was more respectable than one might expect, and that the people there sometimes knew things others didn’t. Linnea had raised an eyebrow at that but held her tongue. She trusted Celenneth’s judgment. If Brandor and Orlec had passed through these parts, maybe someone at the Lily could shed light on their disappearance.

They turned a corner and spotted a low building that matched Celenneth’s memory: unassuming from the outside, shutters half-drawn, no flashy signage. Just a small painted lily etched into the lintel. “There,” Celenneth said quietly.

Linnea glanced around. The street was calm—an old man shuffled by with a sack of oats, barely acknowledging them. A woman sweeping her stoop gave them a faint nod. Good. They didn’t need attention.

They dismounted near a modest stable behind the building. Linnea patted Eorlas’s neck, while Celenneth led Nimroch inside. The stableboy wasn’t around, but they found empty stalls and fresh hay. With horses settled, they returned to the Silver Lily’s front door.

Linnea studied Celenneth’s face. “You sure we’ll find something here?”

Celenneth shrugged, voice low. “If not direct answers, then at least a name or a lead. Brandor and Orlec spent time here. They trusted these people.”

Linnea nodded. Brandor and Orlec: two rangers who raised Celenneth years ago. Their trail had gone cold, and now Linnea and Celenneth were piecing together what happened. Rumor said the men had chased old Numenorian relics in the Lone-Lands. Finding their fate mattered deeply to Celenneth.

They stepped inside. The Silver Lily’s door opened onto a room lit by warm lamplight, scented with subtle incense. Linnea inhaled a blend of spices and something floral. The place felt unexpectedly cozy, not the seedy dive one might imagine. Soft drapes sectioned off lounge areas, plush seating invited patrons to relax. The atmosphere hummed with low conversation, muted laughter, and an occasional clink of glasses.

Men and women chatted softly, some leaning close over small tables, others heading upstairs arm in arm. It was calm, intimate. Linnea relaxed a bit. This was no raucous tavern but a discreet haven, a place where secrets might be kept and shared for the right reason.

A woman approached—clearly the madame, by her confident posture and assessing gaze. She wore a well-fitted dress, her eyes sharp. “Welcome,” she said smoothly, but Linnea sensed caution beneath the greeting. “Need a spot to rest? Some company?”

Celenneth offered a polite smile. “Not that kind of business,” she said softly. “We’re looking for information. About two rangers—Brandor and Orlec—who used to come here somewhat regularly. We hoped one of your staff might remember them.”

The madame’s eyes narrowed slightly, but her smile remained. Linnea watched as the woman weighed the risks. “Brandor and Orlec,” she repeated. “I remember them. Rangers leave impressions.” Her voice was cool but not unfriendly.

Celenneth nodded. “They raised me. I’m hoping to find them out in the wilds.”

A flicker of recognition crossed the madame’s face. She studied Celenneth more closely, perhaps recalling a younger version of her. “Wait here,” the madame said, gesturing to a nearby table draped in soft fabric. “I’ll check my records.”

They sat as instructed, Linnea and Celenneth settling onto a low couch. The patrons paid them little mind. This felt like a place where people respected privacy. Linnea took in the gentle glow of lanterns behind colored screens, the soft laughter from nearby alcoves. She glanced at Celenneth, who watched the entrance with focused calm.

Brandor and Orlec: their names lingered like ghosts. Linnea could sense Celenneth’s impatience and worry, even if her partner’s face remained composed.

Before long, the madame returned with Eira. Eira was tall with short black hair. Her blouse was loose and her breasts seemed to perpetually be on the verge of falling out of it. She wore a simple, elegant dress. Meeting Celenneth’s eyes, Eira’s expression shifted to surprise and something like regret.

“Celenneth,” Eira said softly, stopping a few steps away. “It’s been a long time. I never thought I’d see you again.”

Celenneth stood. “Life takes strange turns,” she said simply. “I’m sorry to show up like this, but I need your help. Brandor and Orlec were here, right? We must know what happened to them.”

Eira hesitated, glancing at Linnea briefly before focusing back on Celenneth. Finally, she nodded. “Come,” Eira said, voice quieter. “Let’s talk somewhere private.”

They followed Eira to a secluded corner draped with heavy curtains. Inside was a low table and plush couch, lamplight dim. Eira closed the curtains behind them. Linnea perched beside Celenneth, Eira sitting opposite, hands folded in her lap.

For a moment, silence. Celenneth broke it. “Eira, it seems like you might have been the last one they saw when they came through last. Did they say anything to you before they left?”

Eira’s eyes dropped. “They were excited but nervous. Orlec talked about a discovery—something old and powerful. Brandor was tense, like he sensed danger. I warned them. I said it sounded risky, but they insisted it was important. They left at night and haven’t returned. This was last summer.”

Linnea leaned forward. “Did they mention being followed, or meeting suspicious people?”

Eira frowned, thinking. “Not outright. But Orlec hinted that knowledge like theirs attracts trouble—treasure hunters, cultists, bandits. They knew the risks. But this time, I sensed desperation. They believed they had to go. Orlec mentioned a watchtower, a stream that forked, a landmark to guide them north. Something about old Numenorian script carved into stone.”

Celenneth’s jaw tightened. That was more than they’d known before. A watchtower and a stream—a place to start searching. “They never sent word after?” she asked, though the answer was obvious.

Eira shook her head. “No. I waited, hoping they’d return. They promised they would, said they’d share their findings.” Her voice grew softer. “They’re gone, aren’t they?”

Celenneth hesitated. “We don’t know. That’s why we’re going to find out.”

Eira’s gaze lifted, meeting Celenneth’s eyes. “Take me with you.”

Linnea blinked, surprised. Celenneth likewise seemed taken aback. “Eira, it’s dangerous. We’re heading into wild lands, hunting for clues in ruins. There could be bandits, beasts… worse. You don’t owe them this.”

Eira smiled sadly. “I owe them more than you think. Brandor and Orlec treated me kindly when few did. They weren’t just patrons; they respected me. If I can help find the truth, I want to. I can ride, I can handle a dagger if needed, and I know these lands. Let me help. Besides, I have business to attend to up north, and you’re safer to travel with than most.”

Linnea studied Eira’s face. She seemed sincere. Another ally could be valuable, especially one who knew the region. Celenneth weighed the risk, lips pressed thin. After a moment, she nodded. “Alright. We leave in the morning. Pack what you need. We’ll head north at first light.”

Eira let out a quiet breath, relief mixed with resolve. “Thank you. I’ll speak to the madame. She’s used to my comings and goings, though not usually for reasons like this.”

Celenneth stood and offered Eira a faint smile. “We appreciate it.”

Eira slipped out, leaving Linnea and Celenneth momentarily alone behind the curtains. Linnea reached over, touching Celenneth’s arm lightly. “Think this is a good idea?”

Celenneth shrugged. “We need all the help we can get. Eira knew them, can guide us. I trust her enough to give it a chance.”

Linnea nodded. “Agreed. Let’s find a place to sleep and leave at dawn.”

They emerged into the main room. The madame caught their eye and raised a brow. Celenneth gave a small nod of thanks. The madame inclined her head, as if to say she hoped they found what they sought.

Outside, the sky was shifting into dusk, a soft glow lingering. Linnea and Celenneth found a modest inn down the street. The inn was quiet, run by an older couple who barely asked questions. Perfect for a short stay. They took a small room with two narrow beds and simple furniture. After stabling their horses again for the night, they ate a simple meal in the inn’s common room—stew, bread, and a mug of weak ale each.

Over dinner, they spoke in low tones. “We have a direction now,” Linnea said. “Eira mentioned a watchtower and a forked stream. We’ll start there.”

Celenneth stirred her stew thoughtfully. “From there, maybe we’ll find tracks or abandoned camps. They couldn’t have vanished without leaving a trace.”

Linnea understood Celenneth’s determination. These men were important to her, a link to her past. Uncovering their fate mattered not just for closure, but to ensure Celenneth could move forward unburdened by old shadows.

They finished quietly, then went upstairs to their small room. Linnea lit a candle on the bedside table. Celenneth sat on the bed, removing her boots.

“Eira joining us changes our dynamic,” Linnea said softly, not complaining, just stating a fact.

Celenneth nodded. “It does. But she might know shortcuts, safe routes. She has a reason to help. And maybe this journey will help her too.”

Linnea trusted Celenneth’s judgment. They blew out the candle and slept. The night passed without trouble. In the early dawn, they rose and packed, ate a crust of bread, and went out to the stable. Eorlas and Nimroch were rested, nickering softly as they saddled up.

A soft step behind them: Eira appeared, cloak and pack ready, her hair braided back, a dagger at her hip. She gave them a tentative smile. “I’m ready.”

Celenneth nodded. “Good. Let’s move before the town stirs.”

They led the horses onto the quiet main street. Gelden Hollow still slumbered, only a few distant figures moving about. The smell of dew on the thatch roofs, the hush of waking birds—peaceful, yet charged with purpose.
