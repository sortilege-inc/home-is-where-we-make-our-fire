# Chapter 3: The Journey to Moria

*Book 1*

*Súlìmë 10, 2965*

The Númenórean herald’s boots had already proven their worth. With each stride, Celenneth moved swiftly and with a lightness she had never known before. She headed east from Bree, her path taking her to Weathertop, its lonely silhouette rising against the horizon. The wind swept over the ancient hill, carrying with it whispers of the past, and Celenneth paused for a moment at its summit, feeling the weight of history around her.

From Weathertop, she cut southeast towards the South Downs. The land here was untamed, filled with rolling hills and hidden valleys, and it was during this leg of her journey that she came upon a remarkable sight—a battle of stags. Two great beasts, their antlers locked, clashed in a majestic struggle for dominance. Celenneth crouched low, watching in awe as they battled, their powerful forms moving with a grace and ferocity that reminded her of the wild beauty of the world.

Once the battle was over, and the victor had claimed his territory, Celenneth moved on, her path taking her further southeast until she neared the banks of the Greyflood River. It was here that she stumbled upon something unexpected—ruins that seemed Elvish in origin, hidden amidst a copse of trees. The stonework was masterful, the carvings delicate and intricate, and though the place was too small to be a village, it was far too grand to be a mere roadside shrine. Celenneth wandered through the ruins, her fingers brushing against the cool stone as she wondered about its purpose. Perhaps it was erected in memory of some long-forgotten event? She made a note of the location, thinking that one day she might return to learn more.

Crossing the Greyflood was no easy task, but Celenneth managed, her progress slowed by the strong current. Once across, she found a secluded path that seemed to offer a shortcut, and though it did, it also revealed signs of recent use—tracks that sent a chill down her spine. Wargs. The large paw prints were unmistakable, and as she examined them more closely, she realized they were headed in the same direction she was—towards Moria.

Three days she followed their trail, her unease growing with each passing hour. Finally, she found them—four wargs resting in a valley that blocked her path. She kept her distance, her eyes narrowing as she studied the creatures. They were fierce and dangerous, and she knew better than to engage them directly.

Her mind turned to the lore she had learned over the years. She knew better than to drink the waters of Moria, for it was said that orcs had poisoned them long ago. With that knowledge in mind, she filled several bags with water from a nearby stream, ensuring she would not be left without clean water when she ventured into the dark depths of the dwarf kingdom.

The valley where the wargs rested was bordered by stone buildings—ancient structures that Celenneth recognized as the mansions of Thráin I. Rumors spoke of treasures hidden here, buried by Thráin before he journeyed east. The buildings, though crumbling, still held an air of majesty, and Celenneth couldn’t help but wonder what secrets they held.

She waited until the wind shifted, carrying her scent away from the wargs, and then she moved, her steps careful and deliberate as she tried to sneak past the resting beasts. The tension was palpable, her heart pounding in her chest as she edged along the rocky slope. But fortune favored her, and she managed to slip by unnoticed, her form blending into the shadows as she moved towards the camp of the Stair Falls.

To her astonishment, she found not an empty camp, but a small group of dwarves—four of them, their beards long and their eyes weary. They were part of Balin’s company, sent out to relay messages to their kin, but now stranded, unable to move on due to the presence of the wargs. They eyed her with suspicion as she approached, their hands resting on the hilts of their weapons.

“I come with a message for Balin,” Celenneth said, her voice calm, though her heart raced. “Sent by Gandalf the Grey.”

The dwarves exchanged glances, their expressions a mixture of surprise and wariness. One of them, a stout dwarf with a thick, braided beard, spoke up. “Gandalf, you say? It is unlikely that Balin will want to hear what Gandalf has to say.”

Celenneth frowned, sensing the tension in their words. “I can help you,” she offered. “I can lure the wargs away, give you the chance to move on. In return, I ask for safe passage into Moria.”

The dwarves shook their heads, the leader speaking again. “We will not risk Balin’s wrath. He is determined, and we cannot afford to act without his blessing. We will wait, as long as it takes.”

Seeing that they would not be swayed, Celenneth nodded, respecting their decision. She continued up the Stair Falls alone, the path steep and treacherous, her eyes scanning for any sign of danger. The grove of holly trees was dense, their dark leaves whispering in the wind as she made her way across the Sirannon. The river was swollen, but she managed to ford it, her body aching from the effort as she finally stood before the sheer mountainside that hid the gates of Moria.

For five long days and nights, Celenneth searched for the door. Each day, she grew more frustrated, her fingers tracing over the rock, her eyes scanning for any sign of an opening. On the second day, she returned to the dwarves, hoping to persuade them once more, but they were gone, vanished into the wilds. It wasn’t until the fifth day, as the sun dipped below the horizon, that she finally found it—a faint outline, a doorway hidden beneath the moonlight.

The inscription was in Ithildin, shimmering faintly under the pale light of the crescent moon. It read: “Sleeps until it is touched by one who speaks words long forgotten in Middle-earth.” Celenneth stared at the inscription, her mind racing as she tried to decipher its meaning. She deliberated, wracking her brain for the right words, until finally, she stepped forward, her hand brushing against the crescent moon.

“I come in peace and bring tidings to the dwarves,” she said, her voice echoing in the stillness.

The silence that followed was heavy, and then, with a low rumble, the great doors of Moria began to open inward, the ancient stone shifting to reveal the darkness beyond. Celenneth took a deep breath, steeling herself as she stepped forward, her torch held high. The air was cold, the scent of damp stone and decay filling her senses as she ventured into the depths of Moria.
