# Chapter 4: The Journey across Moria

*Súlimë 23, 2965*

The dark inside Moria was a weight, thick enough that her torchlight seemed to strain against it and lose. She stood a while on the threshold and let her eyes find what they could, and looked down a corridor that ran on past the reach of the flame, its arches climbing out of sight, every surface worked with a patience no living hand would ever spend again. The dwarves had made a cathedral of the mountain's bones, and then the mountain had taken it back, and left the two of them — the ruin and the dark — to keep each other company. She was one small warmth carrying one small light into all of it, and she felt the size of what she had walked into settle on her like cold water.

She went carefully, and the echo of her own boots was the loudest thing in the world. For the first hours the vastness alone was almost more than she could hold: halls whose ceilings her torch could not reach, columns cut like the trunks of stone trees marching away into black, stairs that climbed and climbed, kings standing forgotten in their niches with the dust of an age on their shoulders. There was beauty here even gutted — the ghost of forge-heat, of hall-song, of a whole people's long industry — and a grief in it too deep to name, all that had been made and mastered and then simply lost. She let herself feel both, and let neither slow her. She had one road through this place, and it was Balin.

The days ran together in the dark, for there was no sun to mark them — only the burning-down of torches and the wearing of her own strength. She crossed narrow spans over chasms her light could not sound, where a dropped stone fell and fell and never spoke of landing. She passed a gate hung with three orc skulls, a warning left to rot, and did not linger under it. She climbed stairs cut for shorter legs, and waded flooded halls where the black water threw the shadows back at her until the whole drowned space seemed to move, alive with things that were not there. She learned the trick of the deep — that it was never wholly silent, that there was always water somewhere, a drip, a distant fall, a settling of stone, so that a woman alone in it might believe herself followed for hours and be wrong, or believe herself alone and be wrong the other way.

In the upper mines she found a dead orc camp, cold fire-pits and the small gnawed bones of eaten things, and read it as she read any sign — recent enough to be wary of, old enough to have moved on — and went past it onto a straight, endless road, a great processional way that ran arrow-true into the dark. She made her camp at a wayfarers' alcove worn smooth by the resting of travellers a thousand years dead, its walls still carrying their old cut runes of shelter and hope, and ate cold, and slept little, and rose to walk again into a dark that never changed.[^r-moria]

On the seventh day it changed. She came through a broken gate into the King's Hall, and her breath caught despite herself: a vastness of pillars carved with the whole story of a people — king and forge and war and the delving of the deep — her small torchlight waking one graven scene and then the next as she passed, so that the dead history seemed to move alongside her for a moment before it fell back into the dark. She let herself look, once and fully, because some things are owed that much. Then she went on: east along the King's Road and up a molten stair to the Second Hall, a great crossroads of pillars where a dozen ways met.

And there a sound stopped her — the scuff and clash of metal on metal, close, and no echo-trick about it. She doused the torch and went toward it in the dark, feeling along the wall.

Dwarves. A knot of them, barely shapes in the gloom, falling on a small band of goblins — and the goblins, caught, were down and silenced before they could raise a cry. She waited until the last of it was still, then struck her torch alight and stepped into the glow with her hands open.

"I come as a friend," she called, and the hall carried it. "I seek Balin."

They came round on her, eyes going wide at what they saw. A broad one with a braided beard stepped out. "And who are you, to seek Balin?"

"Celenneth, of the Dúnedain. I carry a message from Gandalf the Grey — for Balin's own ear."

The look that passed between them was surprise and unease both. At last the braided dwarf gave a grudging nod. "We'll take you to him. But know this, woman: Balin has no love of Men in dwarvish halls. You'll not find him warm."

"I only mean to deliver the message," she said. "Nothing else."

That seemed to satisfy them. They motioned her on, sure of their footing even in the black where she needed the wall, and led her back through the deep — past the columns and the broken gates and down at last into the First Hall, where among the leavings of an old dwarven camp a figure looked up at their approach, his eyes sharp as struck flint: Balin, son of Fundin.
