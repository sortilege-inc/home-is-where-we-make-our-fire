# Chapter 1: The Bandits at Sarn Ford, and the Cursed Diadem.

*Book 1*

Not quite spring, in Bree.

Nénimë 6, 2965.

The heavy oak door of the Prancing Pony creaked open, letting in a gust of cold wind and a solitary figure wrapped in a travel-worn cloak. Celenneth stepped into the warmth of the inn, the door swinging shut behind her with a dull thud. She lowered her hood, revealing auburn hair tangled from the road, and took a slow breath, letting the warmth seep into her bones. The firelight flickered on her armor, still marked with the dust of long trails and faint scuffs of past battles.

The inn was bustling as always, filled with the scent of roasted meat and the tang of ale, the hum of laughter and chatter a welcome contrast to the silence of her last few days on the road. She scanned the room, her eyes resting momentarily on a familiar figure—Orlen, seated at a table near the hearth, his head thrown back in laughter at some jest. The sight of him brought a pang, a feeling that she kept buried beneath layers of practicality and caution. Celenneth never lingered, never allowed herself to entertain what-ifs. She was always coming or going, and Bree was just another stop on the journey.

She turned her eyes away before he could notice her, stepping further inside, her hand instinctively resting on the hilt of her sword as she moved through the crowded common room. The innkeeper, Butterbur, caught sight of her and waved, his round face lighting up with recognition.

"Celenneth! Back again, are ye? I swear, you're in and out like the wind itself!" Butterbur's voice boomed over the noise, drawing a few curious glances. Celenneth smiled faintly, nodding as she approached the counter.

"Aye, Barliman, it seems I can't stay away for long," she replied, her voice carrying a warmth she seldom allowed herself on the road. Butterbur filled a tankard, setting it before her with a knowing smile.

"Well, you look like you've come a long way. Sit, rest. The road can wait a while longer, eh?"

Celenneth was already distracted. Her ears caught snippets of conversation from a nearby table—rumors of brigands harassing travelers near the Baranduin. The talk was hushed, but the concern in the voices was unmistakable. She straightened, her focus shifting. As the talk continued, it seemed more sure that these were not mere speculations.

She approached the table, her presence commanding enough to make the men pause in their conversation. "Pardon me," she said, her voice even but firm. "I couldn't help but overhear. These brigands, near the Baranduin—how many, and how often?" The men exchanged glances, and then began to share what they knew—scraps of information about attacks on travelers, sightings of shadowy figures along the riverbanks. Celenneth listened intently, and made up her mind. Orlen would have to wait, not the road.

She set the tankard down with a decisive clink and nodded to Butterbur. "I'll be needing provisions again, Barliman. It seems there's no rest for me tonight."

Butterbur sighed but gave her a nod, already turning to gather what she needed. "Off again, eh? Well, you be careful, Celenneth. The roads are dark these days."

With her provisions in hand, Celenneth left the warmth of the inn behind. The chill of the night bit into her as she made her way through Bree, her steps purposeful. Celenneth set out alone, her feet carrying her swiftly along the darkened road. The sky above was clear, stars glimmering coldly as she moved with the practiced ease of one who had spent years navigating the wild.

Celenneth made excellent time coming down from Bree, first following the Greenway, then cutting across the edge of the Barrow Downs towards Sarn Ford. She moved quickly, her senses sharp and alert, the landscape shifting from rolling hills to the shadowy edges of the Downs, where the air held an eerie stillness. As she traveled, she spotted the crumbling silhouette of a tower across a ravine, its ruins standing stark against the darkening sky.

The wisp of smoke caught her eye, curling lazily above what appeared to be an abandoned highway robber camp. Curiosity piqued, she crept closer, her steps careful and silent. She caught sight of movement—a figure slipping into a nearby cave. A woman, dressed in unusual clothes that bore the look of Tharbad's exiles.

Celenneth narrowed her eyes, her instincts kicking in. This woman might have answers, or at the very least, she could be a scout for the brigands. Celenneth moved swiftly, closing the distance without a sound. She lunged, her arm wrapping around the woman's throat as she attempted to wrestle her into submission, reaching for a length of rope to tie her hands. But the woman was wiry and quick; her hands slipped free, struggling fiercely before Celenneth could secure a knot.

Thinking quickly, Celenneth drew her sword, her heart pounding. The woman twisted, her eyes wide with fear, and in that moment, Celenneth acted. She thrust the blade forward, the steel piercing the woman's chest. Celenneth clamped a hand over her mouth, muffling the cry that escaped as the woman slumped against her.

The forest fell silent once more, the only sound her own ragged breathing. Celenneth lowered the woman gently to the ground, her chest tightening as she stared at the lifeless form. She had done what she thought necessary—yet, in the cold aftermath, she knew this act of self-preservation would leave a mark on her soul. This was not a clean kill in the heat of battle; it was something darker, something that would bear its own shadow in her heart.

She wiped her blade clean, her eyes cold as she scanned the camp. There was no time for regret—not now.

After a brief survey of the camp and retrieving her rope, Celenneth headed towards the cave. She approached cautiously, her eyes scanning for any movement. As she neared the entrance, she encountered a broken old stone bridge spanning the ravine, with a makeshift rope and wooden bridge beside it. The crumbling stone structure looked unreliable, and the makeshift bridge, hastily put together, wasn't much better. She knew time was of the essence.

As she approached the bridge, she spotted a shadow—a figure on the opposite side. The man, with a frantic look in his eyes, held a knife, and with a sharp movement, began cutting the ropes holding the bridge together. Celenneth's heart raced, and without hesitation, she sprinted forward. The bridge swayed under her feet as she ran, her boots pounding the wooden planks. She leapt just as the ropes snapped, the bridge collapsing beneath her. For a breathless moment, she hung in the air before her feet hit solid ground on the other side.

The figure stumbled back, his eyes wide with surprise. He hadn't expected her to make it. Without waiting, he turned and darted into the next chamber, disappearing into the darkness. Celenneth steadied herself, her breath coming fast. She could feel the adrenaline coursing through her veins, her body trembling with the thrill of survival.

Lighting a torch, she moved into the debris-filled chamber. The air was damp, the walls lined with broken stones and shattered wood. Water trickled somewhere nearby, echoing through the cavern. Large doors stood across the room, their surface worn with age, and what looked like a small closet or cupboard door was barely visible amidst the rubble. Celenneth moved carefully, her eyes darting from shadow to shadow, her senses alert for any movement.

Suddenly, the man burst out of a pantry, wielding an axe. He swung at her with a wild cry, the blade slicing through the air. Celenneth barely dodged, the axe missing her by inches. She countered with a thrust of her sword, but the man twisted away, her blade scraping against the stone wall.

He swung again, his movements frantic but strong. Celenneth raised her buckler, the impact of the axe jarring her arm. She gritted her teeth, pushing back against him. With a swift movement, she twisted, bringing her sword up and driving it into his chest. The man's eyes went wide, his breath escaping in a ragged gasp. Celenneth yanked her sword free and kicked him back, his body crumpling against the wall before sliding to the floor.

She stood over him for a moment, her breathing heavy, her muscles tense. The echoes of the fight faded, leaving only the soft trickle of water. There was no time to search the room thoroughly; she needed to move quickly, and the body needed to be dealt with. She sheathed her sword and grabbed the man's collar, hauling him towards the edge of the ravine. With a grunt of effort, she hefted the body over the side, watching as it disappeared into the darkness below.

Celenneth took a deep breath, steadying herself. She turned back towards the chamber, her torch casting long shadows across the rubble-strewn floor.

Knowing she'd need to find another way across the ravine later, Celenneth pressed on, pushing through the larger doors. A sense of dread washed over her as she entered what appeared to be a pillared hall, now flooded. The air felt thick and stuffy, as if weighted.

She noticed inscriptions and carvings on the walls, ancient symbols and worn figures whose meanings eluded her, but whose presence felt heavy with history. Across from the entrance, a stairway beckoned, half obscured by shadows and the reflective surface of the water. She stepped further in, her boots splashing softly in the shallow flood, her eyes scanning the murky water for any sign of danger.

As she moved deeper into the hall, surveying her surroundings, a flicker of movement caught her attention. She spotted a dwarf—a brief glimpse of a small figure in dark clothing—running off into a side passage. Her heart skipped a beat, her instincts screaming a warning. Then, echoing through the chamber, she heard it: the toll of a bell, deep and resonant, reverberating through the pillars and the water.

The sound seemed to wake something, and as Celenneth's gaze snapped back to the water, she saw them—two ghoulish creatures, rising from the depths, their gaunt forms dripping and twisted. Their eyes were hollow, their mouths curled into grotesque snarls as they moved towards her, intent on attack.

Celenneth's grip tightened on her bow. She backed up, nocking an arrow and drawing it back, aiming for the closest of the creatures. She loosed the arrow, but the movement of the water made her misjudge her aim; the arrow sailed past, striking a pillar behind them. The creatures closed the gap quickly, their movements jerky and unnatural.

One lunged at her, its teeth bared, but it bit down on her leather armor, unable to pierce through. She twisted away, deflecting a clawed hand with her buckler, the impact rattling her arm. She swung her sword in retaliation, the blade slicing through the air. The first creature dodged, hissing, while the second one lunged, its bony fingers wrapping around her arm, holding her fast.

Celenneth grunted, struggling against the creature's grip. She dropped her torch, the flame sputtering as it hit the water. The creatures shied back, their eyes flickering with something that resembled fear. With a burst of effort, she wrestled against the one holding her, but its grip was iron-tight, and she failed to break free. The creature leaned in, its teeth gnashing, trying to bite through her helm.

Desperation clawed at her mind, and then she remembered the torch. Forcing herself towards it, she strained against the creature's hold, her fingers brushing the wooden handle. She grasped it, the flame flaring slightly as she lifted it, thrusting it towards the creature's face. It released her with a screech, recoiling from the fire, its companion following suit, both of them retreating hastily into the water, disappearing beneath the murky surface.

Celenneth panted, her heart pounding in her chest. She stood there, torch held high, watching the water until it was still once more. Only then did she lower the torch, her eyes shifting to the center of the chamber. There, amidst the flooded hall, were several sepulchers—ancient stone coffins, some intact, others broken open, their contents long gone or disturbed.

She moved cautiously through the water, fording across the chamber, her eyes on the sepulchers. She attempted to explore the area, searching for anything of note, but found nothing—only the cold, empty echoes of the dead. It seemed whatever secrets this place held, they were not ready to reveal themselves to her.

Celenneth took a deep breath, the sense of dread still clinging to her as she turned her gaze towards the passage ahead. There was no going back now, not with the bridge collapsed behind her. She pressed forward, her torch casting flickering light on the ancient stone walls as she moved deeper into the darkness.

Celenneth took the staircase up, her feet echoing softly on the worn stone steps. As she ascended, the air grew colder, and she emerged onto what seemed like another world—a vast expanse of ruins floating in a sea of dense mist. It was dark, and the sun hung low, a pale disc setting beyond the horizon. The mist clung to the shattered remnants of stone, the silence almost deafening. Nothing rose more than a few yards, most of the structures broken and crumbling, as though a great force had razed it all to the ground.

A tower stood amongst the devastation, its silhouette rising above the shattered remnants, the only structure left somewhat intact. Celenneth made her way towards it, her eyes scanning the ruins warily, her footsteps muffled by the mist. The walls of the tower were covered in graffiti, dark symbols scrawled hastily, marking it with a strange and unsettling presence. She approached cautiously, narrowing her eyes to make out the inscriptions.

The letters were Elvish, though written in a twisted and ancient mode. The words themselves, however, were unmistakable—the Black Speech of Mordor, used here to convey a powerful curse. Celenneth frowned, her heart heavy with unease as she deciphered the script. She could feel the oppressive weight of the words, the malevolence behind them seeping into the air around her. The curse was laid by the Witch-king of Angmar himself, his rage evident in every line. Furious at how strongly the Dúnedain had opposed him, he had cursed these ruins, binding them to his wrath as long as the torment of Lord Arveleg continued.

She did not know much of this Lord Arveleg, but it was clear enough—this place had once been a stronghold of the Dúnedain, now twisted and corrupted by dark power. The knowledge left a cold feeling in her chest, a sense of futility at the evil that had befallen this once-proud city. The weight of the curse lingered, pressing on her spirit, and she felt the Shadow grow within her. Yet, as she turned her gaze away from the curse, she took a deep breath, shaking off the heaviness. She had deciphered enough to understand, and in doing so, managed to lessen the impact on herself—though she still felt its dark tendrils wrap around her soul.

Celenneth moved away from the inscriptions, her eyes scanning the area. The air was still and silent, the ruins a stark reminder of what had been lost. As she walked further, she found an area that felt secure—a hollow beneath a broken archway, sheltered from the chill wind that swept across the ruins. She settled there, taking a short rest, her body weary from the battles and the journey. She closed her eyes, letting her guard down just for a moment, allowing herself to breathe and regain some strength.

After a time, Celenneth stood, her muscles stiff from the cold. She began to search the place, her eyes catching a glimpse of something unusual amidst the rubble—a rusty iron gate, half buried beneath stone and debris. She pulled it open, her curiosity piqued as she stepped through. Beyond lay a patch of greenery, a small oasis in the midst of the desolation—an orchard, overgrown but still bearing signs of life.

It was the remnants of an herbalist's garden, the plants thriving despite the ruin around them. Celenneth knelt, her eyes scanning the herbs, her fingers brushing over the leaves. She recognized a few—a healing herb that could prove useful. She carefully harvested some, tucking them away, and decided to use them on herself. The herbs, however, did little to ease her wounds; the fatigue and strain of the journey ran deeper than the plants could heal. She sighed, shaking her head as she gathered a bit more to take with her. It was not much, but it was something—a small glimmer of hope amidst the darkness.

Celenneth turned back towards the tower, her eyes narrowing as she considered her next move. The ruins lay quiet around her, but she knew better than to let her guard down. There were still secrets to uncover, dangers to face, and she could not afford to rest for long.

Celenneth turned away from the herb garden, her body aching from the strain and her mind heavy with the dark weight of the curse she had read. The ruins around her seemed to groan and whisper, their ancient secrets echoing in the thick mist. She knew that this place had been twisted by evil, a stronghold now shrouded in a dreadful past. And yet, there was something here—some truth or relic worth uncovering, though hidden beneath layers of dark enchantment.

Celenneth climbed the remnants of a stone stairway, each step cautious. She moved closer to the tower, its imposing silhouette still rising defiantly amidst the rubble and ruin. She hoped it might offer a vantage point, something that would give her a better sense of where to go next.

The paint-strewn walls stared back at her, the sinister letters now burned into her memory. She gritted her teeth and pushed open the heavy, splintered wooden door at the base of the tower. It groaned on rusted hinges, a deep sound that reverberated in the stillness of the ruins.

Inside, the air was thick with dust, and the fading light of her torch flickered across worn stone. The room she entered was circular, the walls lined with shelves of ancient books, their spines cracked and covers faded with age. Most of the books were beyond use, their pages crumbling to touch, but one stood out. Bound in blackened leather, it bore no title—only a sigil she vaguely recognized, a symbol of some ancient order long forgotten.

Celenneth took the book, tucking it carefully into her pack. It felt significant, though she could not say why. Something about this place seemed to be nudging her towards the past—towards history that had been twisted, lost, and buried.

Suddenly, a noise echoed from above—a faint shuffle, like someone or something moving in the upper floors. Celenneth tensed, her hand instinctively going to her sword. She took a deep breath, her eyes narrowing as she moved towards the narrow spiral staircase winding upwards along the wall. With each step, her boots made a soft scrape against the stone, the only sound breaking the oppressive silence of the place.

As she left the orchard, a figure emerged from the mist—a wraith-like woman, her flesh almost transparent, her eyes glowing with an eerie light. A hood covered most of her face, but thin strands of white hair were visible, drifting like cobwebs. The wraith spoke in a strange, lilting accent, her voice echoing unnaturally.

"Why have you come? There is nothing here. Only death. Leave this place or join me in my torment."

Celenneth studied the wraith, her mind racing. She pieced together enough fragments of old tales and the markings she had seen to believe this could be Lady Elwin—a tragic figure from the old histories of the Dúnedain.

"Are you the lady of this place? Who do you defend it against?" Celenneth asked, her voice steady, though her heart pounded.

The wraith did not answer. Instead, its face twisted in anger, and it lunged at her, its form shifting like mist. Celenneth backed away, scrambling up the stone steps to gain higher ground. She tried to draw her bow, hoping for a shot, but the wraith floated effortlessly upwards, closing the distance. Desperate, Celenneth drew her sword and slashed at the spirit. The blade passed through the wraith's head, and it let out a bone-chilling scream, its voice like a winter wind cutting through her. A cold dread gripped her, but she gritted her teeth, striking again. This time, the wraith dissipated into the air, its form scattering like fog in the morning sun.

Celenneth shivered, the cold still lingering in her bones. She took a moment to gather herself, drawing on her inner strength and the hope of her Fellowship to shake off the chill of despair. The Miserable feeling lifted, but the weight of the Shadow remained.

Celenneth looked around, her gaze returning to heights of the tower above. She moved up the stairs, her hand brushing against the rough stone walls for balance. At the top of the tower, she found a grim sight. A mummified corpse lay propped upon a rotting wooden throne, its skeletal form tied to the chair with rusted chains. A metal crown, nailed into the skull, adorned its head. The blue gem set into the crown gleamed brilliantly, its light casting eerie shadows across the room.

Celenneth stepped closer, examining the crown. She could see that the nails pierced the skull, fixing it in place. This was no symbol of honor—this was a punishment, a cruel mockery of kingship. She bowed her head in apology for what she was about to do. Dead or not, the figure before her had once been a ruler, perhaps even of the Dúnedain, and it deserved respect.

Carefully, Celenneth began to pry the crown from the corpse's head, her hands steady but her heart pounding. As the crown loosened, she felt a shift in the air, a presence behind her. She turned her head slightly and saw the wraith of Lady Elwin, her form looming but motionless. The wraith watched silently, her hollow eyes fixed on the crown.

With a final effort, Celenneth removed the crown. The mummified body collapsed into dust, the chains falling slack. For a brief moment, the wraith's form changed, her spectral features softening, her tattered robes transforming into the garments of a noblewoman. She looked at Celenneth, a silent gratitude in her eyes, before she too crumbled into nothingness, the mist that had bound her dissipating.

The blue gem's light faded, the glow that had filled the tower vanishing. Though Celenneth did not fully understand the significance of what had occurred, she felt a shift—a release. The curse that had bound this place had been broken, at least in part.

Exhausted, Celenneth decided to make camp in the tower. She barricaded one of the doors and set a simple trap on the stairs, then settled at the foot of the throne. She allowed herself a respite, her body aching from the battles and the journey.

Celenneth rested through the night, though her sleep was fitful, haunted by dreams of shadows and whispers. She woke with an unnerving feeling of being watched, her eyes darting around the room, but there was nothing there. Her trap was undisturbed. She took a deep breath, steadying herself, and prepared to move on.

Descending the tower, Celenneth noticed a change. The supernatural mist that had hung over the ruins was gone. Morning had come, and the sun shone bright, casting long shadows but illuminating the once-dreadful place. The shadows had been cast away, and with them, some of the darkness that had haunted these ruins.

She decided to explore further, her eyes catching sight of something amidst the rubble—a trapdoor, half-hidden beneath debris. She cleared the stones away, revealing an entrance to an underground chamber. The air was musty as she descended, her torchlight revealing a small hoard—treasure left behind, perhaps forgotten by those who had abandoned this place.

Among the treasure, her eyes fell upon a pair of boots. They were made of fine leather, surprisingly well-preserved. The craftsmanship was Númenórean, unmistakable in its quality, and there was a rune etched into the leather—a symbol of flight, though not in the literal sense. These were herald’s boots, blessed for travel and healing. Celenneth smiled faintly, quickly exchanging her worn boots for these. This was an auspicious find—something that would aid her in her journeys.

As she exited the chamber, Celenneth found herself face to face with a dwarf. He spoke quickly, his words in a language she did not understand, his gestures agitated. He seemed upset, perhaps angry at her presence. Celenneth, in an attempt to show goodwill, reached into her pack and withdrew a portion of the treasure she had found. She offered it to him, her gesture one of courtesy.

The dwarf eyed her, then the treasure, before nodding and stepping back. He accepted the offering, his demeanor softening slightly. He turned, gesturing for her to follow, and Celenneth did so, her curiosity piqued. The dwarf moved confidently through the ruins, leading her past a flooded chamber and into a maze of narrow corridors. There was a sense that something lay below—perhaps a hidden society of dwarves, still lingering in the depths.

The dwarf led her to a small side room, slipping through a cleverly concealed crevasse. On the other side was a staircase that ascended to a narrow ledge overlooking the ravine. Celenneth tied her rope securely, lowering herself down the ledge to cross the ravine from another side. The dwarf pointed towards the opposite edge, and Celenneth's eyes narrowed as she spotted movement. The brigands had returned, lingering near the place where the bridge had once stood. They appeared to be waiting for her, perhaps hoping to finish what they had started. But now, she had the advantage of an alternate route and a guide who knew these ruins better than anyone.

Once she reached the bottom, the dwarf untied the rope and tossed it down to her, vanishing into the shadows without a word. Celenneth gathered the rope and quickly assessed her surroundings. The terrain offered her an opportunity to sneak around the brigands, to avoid a confrontation altogether.

She moved with care, each step deliberate, the soft crunch of gravel beneath her feet the only sound. But despite her caution and the favor granted by her new boots, fate did not favor her. Her foot struck loose stones, sending them clattering down the rocky slope. The noise echoed sharply in the stillness, drawing the attention of the brigands above. Celenneth's heart pounded, her instincts screaming at her to move. She could see the figures turning, their shouts carrying across the ravine.

Without hesitation, Celenneth broke into a run, her boots barely touching the ground as she sprinted through the narrow passage. The brigands' shouts grew fainter as she put distance between them, her breath coming in quick bursts. She ran until her legs burned, her body propelled by sheer determination and the will to survive. Finally, when the sounds of pursuit faded into silence, she allowed herself to slow, her gaze scanning the surroundings for any sign of danger.

Having escaped, Celenneth took a moment to steady herself, her eyes turning towards the path that would lead her back. She knew the way well enough now, the shortcuts and the hidden routes that would take her safely to Bree. With her new boots lending her speed and stamina, she embarked on the journey, moving swiftly and with purpose. The road was long, but she felt the favor of fortune at her back, each step bringing her closer to the safety of the town.

Celenneth returned to Bree, her body weary but her spirit unbroken. The familiar sight of the village brought a sense of relief, the warmth of its lights a beacon after the cold, shadowed ruins. She made her way to the Prancing Pony, where she was able to relay a message to the other Dúnedain about the brigands, detailing their numbers and the routes they had been taking. It was important that the others knew what she had found, that they could be prepared.

After delivering the message, she allowed herself time to recuperate. She rested in Bree, letting her wounds heal and her strength return. Each day, she grew stronger, and as she did, she couldn't help but think of Orlen. She sought him out, hoping to find him at the market or perhaps at his usual place by the hearth. But Butterbur informed her that Orlen had traveled west for a market, and would not be back for a few weeks.

Celenneth hid her disappointment behind a faint smile, nodding her thanks. She knew the road would call to her again soon, but for now, she would rest, recover, and wait for the chance to see Orlen again.
