# Chapter 6: Finding the Ledge of Woe

*Súlimë 28, 2965*

They went down into the deep together, and the dark took them by degrees.

It was a different thing, travelling in company, after so long alone. The dwarves moved through the lower halls with the ease of a people who had the stone in their blood, naming what they passed — the Halls of the Roadwarden, the Doors of Durin — and Celenneth set each name against the map she was building behind her eyes, learning the shape of the deep as she had learned the wilds above, because a ranger who did not know the ground she walked was already half-lost. They went by torch and by the dwarves' surer sense of the dark, and the days below had no dawn to them, only the rhythm of walking and resting and walking again, deeper and always deeper, the weight of the mountain growing over their heads with every stair they took down.

On the first day Dagrun's pipe-smoke led them to a forge — a modest one, not of the famed mithril kind, but a proper dwarven hearth — and Regin coaxed it back to life, and they warmed themselves at a fire burning where none had burned in an age. For an hour the deep felt less like a tomb and more like a hall that had only been waiting for its folk to come home. Then they banked it and went on, and the dark closed the small warmth away behind them.

On the second they came into a hall of records carved whole from the living rock, a great chamber that had held the memory of a kingdom — and found it broken. The walls were split, the stone shelves thrown down and shattered, ages of dwarven learning strewn across the floor as though the mountain itself had shuddered and shaken it all to ruin. Regin knelt among it a long while, turning broken pieces in his ink-stained hands, grieving in a silence worse than weeping; and before they left they marked the wall by the door, a small human thing to say that they had passed, and seen, and been sorry.

By the third day the dark had a weight to it that was more than the mountain, and the air a dread that settled into all of them. The way narrowed to a ledge — sheer rock rising on one hand, on the other a chasm that dropped past all sounding — and there, as they edged along it, a gust of air came up out of the depths and killed every torch at once. They crossed it blind. Step by measured step along a hand's-breadth of stone with the black falling away beside them, each with a hand on the wall and a hand on the shoulder ahead, breath held, the only sounds their own careful feet and the far-down sigh of the wind that had no business being there. And something rose with that cold air that was not only cold, and reached into them as they crept, and clung to them long after their feet found solid ground and their trembling hands could strike a light again.[^r-chasm]

The fourth day the passages turned on themselves. They doubled back more than once, the way forking and forking and leading nowhere, and the strain of the deep began to tell — the endless dark, the endless stone, the sense of the whole mountain pressing down. Tempers frayed and were mastered. Late, at the end of a hard day's wandering, they came into an austere shining hall, its stone polished near to glass and giving their torchlight back in cold reflections — a forum once, the dwarves thought, a place of judgements — empty now but for the echo of their own steps, and none of them rested easy in it.

The fifth brought them to the flooded halls: rank standing water, foul with the years, hip-deep where it was no worse and worse where it was, so cold it stole the feeling from their legs and left them wading half-numb through the drowned dark. In one chamber they found a burned-out wine cellar, its barrels staved and charred — some old raid's leavings, a small violence done so long ago there was no one left to remember it. By then the weariness was in all of them, bone and mind both, the long descent pressing its weight down through them, the shadow of the deep grown heavy on every heart.[^s-descent] But the Ledge of Woe lay somewhere ahead, and the captives with it, and Celenneth knew as well as any of them that there was no road back — not now, not this far down, not with all that dark lying between them and the light.

[^r-chasm]: **Journey / Travel** hazard — a blind crossing of a chasm-ledge after a gust snuffs the torches. Won through; a touch of **Shadow** from the crossing lingers.
[^s-descent]: Accumulating **fatigue** and **Shadow** from the deep descent — the company presses on Weary.
