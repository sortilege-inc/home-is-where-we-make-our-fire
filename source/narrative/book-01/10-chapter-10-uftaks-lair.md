# Chapter 10: Uftak's Lair

*Lótessë 17, 2965*

Uftak was no common orc. There was troll in his blood by the look of him — a bulk and a height that dwarfed the largest of his kind — and he was Malech's enforcer, kept to make the fortress afraid. This was the thing waiting for them at the top of the stair.

They struck first, for what surprise was worth. Celenneth loosed as she came — and missed, the shaft cracking off the wall behind him — and dropped the bow and drew her sword and rushed to close before he could set himself.

He came up roaring, a great axe in his fists, and swung a stroke that would have halved her; she went under it, the wind of it stirring her hair, and drove her sword up between his ribs on the rise. He bellowed and wrenched himself off the blade. Steel rang on stone as she gave ground before his wild swings — and then his cries brought the guards, and the corridor became a battle, the dwarves throwing themselves in to keep the orcs off her back, Ida and the archers harrying Uftak while Celenneth pressed him.[^r-uftak]

She thought she had him. Her blade was set for the killing thrust when he laughed, low and guttural, and hurled the axe at her head; she ducked, and it smashed the wall where she had stood — and while she was off balance he had a spear off the wall and drove it into her side. The pain went through her white and total. She stumbled, bleeding, and made herself come on, because there was nothing to do but end it. But he fought like a cornered thing, and she was slowing, and when he lunged again the spear took her below the collarbone and put her down. The stone came up cold against her cheek. The dark crowded in at the edges of her, and she thought, with a strange flat clarity, *not like this* — and could not stop it.

She did not see the end of him. She learned it after: Hanar and Lif on him from both sides, Ida's arrows finding him one on the next, Lif's blade going deep even as Uftak drove a spear through Lif's thigh; then Regin and Tora snatching spears from the wall and running the great orc through together, until at last he shuddered out his final breath and came down like a wall falling.

She surfaced to lamplight and grim faces and the tight pull of a binding at her side. They had slowed the bleeding, but only slowed it; she was in no state to be moved far. Ginar had slipped away into the fortress after help — and come back with it, of all things: a dwarf named Imri, a smith the orcs had kept at their forge, brought round by the sight of Uftak's severed ear and Yrr's promise that they would be gone as soon as ever they could.[^r-imri] Imri hid them in a back room and stole food from the larder, and they made a cold soup and got it into her and dressed her wounds, and there they stayed two days while she mended enough to stand — not to fight, but to stand.

They could not stay longer; Uftak would be missed. And Imri, with his tormentor dead and the orcs in disarray, threw his lot in with them, and told them what he knew of the one who held this place: Malech One-Eye. A name off the Battle of Five Armies, trading on an old reputation — but weak beneath it, Imri said, and paranoid, and wholly reliant on Uftak to keep his order. Without his enforcer, he was a soft thing in a hard shell.

So they chose to push, while surprise still held. Imri showed them a hidden stair, and they went up it silencing the guards they met, quick and quiet, and broke into Malech's chambers before he knew they were in the fortress.[^r-malech]

Celenneth put an arrow into his shoulder. He roared for his guards, and no guards came, and she watched the understanding of that reach his eye. He caught up a spear and lashed out as Hanar and Lif closed — and Lif, already hurt, took the point through the gut and went down with his eyes wide, the life leaving him fast. Hanar's cry was terrible; his blade went into Malech's back. The orc reeled, wild now, and Ida and Celenneth loosed and loosed until a shaft went home and Malech One-Eye folded to the floor and did not rise.

It was done. They took his head for a trophy, and found his hoard: gold and gems out of every corner of Middle-earth — and better than gold, among it, a sword whose making she knew at a glance, a relic carried at the Battle of Five Armies; the brazen head of Dero, said to whisper secrets under the moon; and a ring of elven amethyst, blessed with the graces of courtesy and insight.[^t-loot] The dwarves stripped the best of the weapons from Uftak's chambers besides. Then they wrapped Lif's body to carry home, and Imri came away with them, hope in his face where there had been none, and left the fortress to its dead.

The road back was long, and heavier than any they had walked, for they carried Lif with them. There is a particular weight to bearing a dead friend through the dark — not the weight of the body, which they shared and spelled between them as Tora bowed under it and then Hanar and then another, but the weight of the silence that travels alongside it, the knowing that the deep had taken one of them and would not give him back. Imri led them by a feast-hall he had been marched to once under orc-guard to haul stores, and they rested there among the ghosts of some long-ago revelry, and pushed on through hard miles and failing strength, Celenneth still mending and slower than she liked to be. But at the last the new base camp opened its light to them out of the dark and took them in — relief and grief in the same breath, the joy of a homecoming and the sorrow of the one who would not share it.[^r-home] Celenneth laid the brazen head of Dero before Balin; Hanar set down Uftak's ear and Malech's head; and they laid Lif before him last, and the weight of it sat on all of them. They had not cleared the fortress, they told him — but they had broken its back, and loosened the orcs' grip on the deep.

In the weeks that followed they mended. Wounds closed; the shadow lifted by degrees as the victory settled into them. Celenneth's understanding of the deep grew with the treasures they had carried out of it, and she and the dwarves made a lay for Lif — for the friend they had lost and the fight they had won — that he might be remembered.

And yet the ending of Malech did not feel to her like an ending. Something in the aftermath sat wrong — a sense she could put no name to, of a greater eye somewhere in the dark turning slowly toward them.[^gorgol]
