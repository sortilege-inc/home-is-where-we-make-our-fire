# Chapter 5: Meeting Balin

*Súlimë 27, 2965*

"The woman seeks Balin," the dwarf said.

Balin looked up, and his gaze fixed on her and stayed. Something moved behind his eyes as he took her measure. "You've found him."

Celenneth reached into her pack and drew out Gandalf's sealed parchment, and held it out. He took it, broke the wax in his thick fingers, and read — and whatever the words were, they soured him. His brow drew down; he rolled the thing up and pushed it into his vest.

"Well?" he said, an edge on it.

She had done what she came to do, and still it sat unfinished in her. She looked around the ruined hall — the worn stone, the leavings of a kingdom.

"Well, what?" Balin pressed. "Is that the whole of it? Some other need? Or are you away, then?"

She thought of the halls she had crossed to reach him, the pillars, the drowned grandeur of the place — all that road, to turn straight round and walk it back. "I am at your service," she said, "should you have use for me."

He studied her a long moment. "By what road did you come into Moria?"

"The West Gate."

His brows went up. "And crossed the whole of it to here. Alone. Seeking me."

"Aye."

A murmur went through the dwarves at that. Balin got to his feet, and something in him eased; he clapped her on the shoulder, a blow that would have staggered a smaller woman. "Then you've earned a place in this company. We've a party going out to explore — the same you met on your way in, as it happens. We'll make our adjustments and set you among them." He did not ask so much as tell her. "The work's not gentle. We're after captives — our own, taken. We've heard them crying up out of a crevasse, and no way down to them yet."

He nodded toward the dwarves ranged nearby, and Celenneth read them the way she read anyone she might have to fight beside: quickly, and by what they showed.

The one set a little apart was Dagrun, half-wreathed in pipe-smoke, her eyes sharp above it and something behind them gone quiet and dark — the look of someone Moria had shown too much. Beside her stood Ida, a crossbow slung at her back and her fingers never far from its stock; a deadeye, the others said, and she had the stillness of it. Hanar kept his arms crossed and his beard braided tight, old scars written up both forearms — a goblin-slayer, and looked it. His brother Ginar stood off from the rest, eyes in the shadows, the kind who scouted alone and liked it. Nar was wiry and quick, forever reading the passage ahead for the safe line through it — their pathfinder. And last, out of place and unbothered by it, Regin, ink-stained fingers around a sketchbook: the one who fought when he must, but had come to set down what they found, the wonders and the ruin both.

Balin's eye came back to her, a bare edge of a smile on him. "So. The seven of you will bring our people up out of the dark."

Celenneth looked round the company, and each gave her a nod, and she gave it back. Her allies, then — for whatever the deep was keeping.
