# Chapter 11: Sensation

*Narquelië 18, 2965*

Arannis and Eryndor led them by quiet paths to a room deep in the halls — spacious but close, strewn with cushions and draped fabrics, lit gold through the leaves above, the air sweet with lavender and honeysuckle. Linnea felt the anticipation hanging between the four of them like a held note.

"We've made a gift for you," Arannis said, her eyes bright. "A game of the senses — to thank you for your courage, and to remind you there is delight to be found even with shadows near."

Eryndor drew out two lengths of silk. "Tonight we play with the senses," he said. "Trust us, and let the wood reach you a new way." He held up a blindfold, his eyes on Celenneth — a question in it.

Celenneth glanced to Linnea, and Linnea to her, and they answered together. Arannis came to Linnea with the other silk. "Only say the word, and it comes off," she murmured, and Linnea nodded, and let the world go dark.

Sight gone, everything else surged forward. She felt the cushions receive her, the warmth of the room, the slow careful hands that bound her wrists and ankles in ribbon — loose, comfortable, meant not to hold her but to give her nothing to do but feel. "A game of trust," Eryndor said close to her, and she breathed out and let herself be held by it.

Scent came first: a vial passed beneath her nose, wildflowers and green herb, and she drew it deep. Then Arannis's fingers at her lips, and honey, the sweetness lingering as the Elf's touch traced her mouth. A feather drifted along the inside of her arm and she shivered at the near-unbearable lightness of it. A bell rang somewhere near, its chime hanging in the air and blending with the rustle of leaves. Cool oil, smelling of crushed pine, worked slow into her wrist and forearm until the muscles went loose. Every sensation arrived whole and enormous, unshared with sight, and she gave herself over to each in turn.

Then hands turned her gently onto her stomach and worked warm oil into her back — jasmine and cedar, Arannis's touch firm and knowing along her spine — and she sank into the cushions and let the last tension go. She heard, distantly, Celenneth doing the same under Eryndor's hands, the small sound of her breath deepening, and it made Linnea smile behind the silk.

They were turned onto their backs, side by side. Linnea reached until she found Celenneth's hand and squeezed it, and Celenneth squeezed back. Then came the soft sound of robes falling, and warm bare skin close to hers, and the game changed its nature by slow degrees.

Weight settled over Celenneth — Linnea felt the cushions shift, heard the small catch of her lover's breath — and then the unmistakable sound of a kiss, deep and unhurried. And there, blind, Linnea built the whole of it out of sound alone: Arannis's mouth on Celenneth's, silver hair spilling over her face, the two of them close and moving; a low murmur, a shift of cushions, a gasp she knew better than her own name. She could not see it and so she saw it more vividly than sight would have allowed — every touch she could not watch drawn in bright behind her eyes — and her own body answered it, heat gathering low in her, her breath going short with wanting. Eryndor's hand traced slow up her thigh, steady, an anchor while her mind ran ahead into the dark, and she let it, aching and unhurried both.

Then she felt the silk drawn from her eyes, and the room came back gold and swimming — and there was Celenneth beside her, flushed and lost in Arannis's arms, exactly and not at all as she had pictured it. And there, kneeling between Linnea's own parted knees, was Eryndor, his gaze dark and asking, waiting on her word and not a moment before it.

She met his eyes, her breath catching, the long slow burn of watching-without-seeing still bright in her. "Yes," she said, barely a whisper. "Eryndor — yes."

He leaned down and kissed her soft, and eased against her, and pressed slowly in. She gasped, and made herself loose to take him, and the first ache gave over quickly to a deep and spreading pleasure. He moved with a careful surety, unhurried, watching her face for every flicker of it, and she rose to meet him and lost track of time entirely, until a wave broke over her and she came back to herself gasping and pushed against his chest — *more, differently* — and he understood, and rolled beneath her so that she straddled him. Hands splayed on his chest, she sank down onto him and set her own pace, slow and then not slow at all.

Then Celenneth was there. Her blindfold gone too, she caught Linnea's chin and tilted it up and kissed her — and Linnea tasted Arannis on her mouth, and the strangeness and the intimacy of it went through her like a spark. Celenneth swung a leg over Eryndor's face and settled facing Linnea, so that the two of them sat astride him together, and she framed Linnea's face in both hands and kissed her deep, and the two women moved over him joined by the kiss, breathing each other's breath.

They came in a near-tumble, one after another in quick succession — Celenneth first with a low broken sound against Linnea's mouth, then Eryndor, and Linnea holding herself down through it until he had spent himself and she collapsed back spent among the cushions. Arannis came to lie before her and gently, tenderly, cleaned the slick of him from her thighs with her tongue while Linnea shivered through the last of it, boneless and undone.

When she could move again, Linnea found Celenneth watching her with a slow, wicked warmth, already reaching for the little vial of pine oil Eryndor had used on them earlier. Celenneth warmed a few drops between her palms and set them to Arannis's back, working the fragrant oil into her shoulders and down her spine until the Elf sighed and melted under her hands — the game turned round on its makers now.

Linnea laughed, delighted, and took it up. She found the bell and rang it soft by Eryndor's ear, and watched his eyes fall shut, and traced her fingers along his chest the way he had traced hers. Celenneth took up the feather and drew it teasing down Arannis's side until the Elf gasped and squirmed and laughed; Linnea dabbed rosewater across Eryndor's chest and bent to kiss the line of it, the scent of roses winding through the pine and the honey.

So the four of them turned the whole game inside out, guest tending host, their laughter mingling low with the murmur of the wood beyond the leaves — a celebration of the senses, and of the strange kindness of this place, and of the joy that could still be found and given even with the Shadow gathering somewhere past the trees.
