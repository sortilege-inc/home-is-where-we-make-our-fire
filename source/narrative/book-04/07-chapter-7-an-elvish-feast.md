# Chapter 7: An Elvish Feast

*Narquelië 17, 2965*

The evening opened under lanterns strung through the branches, their glow warm and gold across the pavilion. Eryndor and Arannis seated Celenneth and Linnea at a long table laid with carved goblets and dishes of a delicacy that could only be Elvish, and the feast began — course upon course, brought out with an effortless grace, each one a small argument for the richness of the wood.

There was a salad of woodland herbs and flowers, bright and clean on the tongue; thin slices of river trout under a sauce of crushed berries and honey; a broth of wild mushrooms with warm crisp bread. As each dish came, so did music — flutes and strings that blended so seamlessly with the rustle of the leaves and the far calls of night-birds that it was hard to say where the wood ended and the song began.

The wine flowed golden and fragrant, and with every cup the weight of the road loosened a little more from Celenneth's shoulders. Arannis raised her goblet to the two of them. "To love," she said, "and the courage it asks." Her gaze lingered on them both as she drank.

Between courses the two Elves sang by turns. Arannis gave a song of old loves — of parting and reunion beneath the stars — her voice haunting, her eyes finding Celenneth's as though the words were meant for her alone. Then Eryndor rose, bright with mischief, and sang of revelry and laughter under the boughs, and on the swell of it he stepped forward and took Linnea's hand. "Dance with me."

Linnea hesitated, glancing to Celenneth — and at Celenneth's nod she let herself be drawn up, and Eryndor spun her light beneath the lanterns until she was laughing. Arannis watched a moment, then held out her own hand. "Will you?"

Celenneth let herself be led. The music quickened, and Arannis moved with an ease that was almost unearthly, silver hair catching the light as she turned, her laugh low and warm — and across the floor Linnea was laughing too, flushed and easy in a way Celenneth had not seen in weeks, and the sight of it lifted her more than the wine had.

As the night deepened, the courses grew stranger and more decadent — smoked venison spiced dark, tiny pastries of goat's cheese and herb, fruits that seemed to hold a light of their own, their juice so sweet it lingered on the lips. And the Elves grew bolder with it. Arannis leaned close to name each dish, her fingers grazing Celenneth's arm, her breath warm at her ear. Eryndor fed Linnea a dried fig from his own hand, his fingertips brushing her mouth, his eyes not leaving hers. "You mustn't shy from pleasure," he told her, low. "It's what makes us alive."

Celenneth was not blind to where the evening was tending. The flirtations had thinned to little more than plain invitation — a finger traced along her jaw, a hand resting warm at the small of her back, words spoken close enough to feel. And she found, wine-warm and easy under the stars, that she was not turning from it. She caught Linnea watching her across the table, cheeks high with color and something bold in her eyes, and held the look, and read in it the same openness she felt rising in herself.

"The forest has seen many lovers," Arannis murmured against her ear, "but few as radiant as the two of you. Stay with us tonight. Let us show you all the pleasures of our realm." Her lips were close, her gaze steady and unhurried, waiting on an answer and not pressing for one.

Celenneth's heart was beating high. But it was not the wine or the wood she looked to for her answer. She looked to Linnea — and Linnea, meeting her eyes down the length of the lantern-lit table, gave her the smallest nod, and a smile that had no fear in it at all.
