# Chapter 4: The Clearing

*Book 4*

*Narquelië 15, 2965*

The forest had grown more malicious as the day went on, pressing in on all sides until the path became nothing more than a tangle of thorns and roots, a wall of ancient wood that refused to let them pass. Celenneth cursed under her breath, her frustration evident in every tightening of her jaw, every hard breath she took. Linnea, struggling behind her, could only echo the sentiment with a frustrated groan as her foot caught yet again on another twisted root.

With no other options, they had turned to the river. The shallows were cold, numbing their feet as they waded upstream, the current pushing back against them as if it, too, wanted them gone. The day had been an unrelenting struggle, each step heavy with the weight of soaked clothes, each movement made cumbersome by the chill that seeped into their bones. By nightfall, they were wet, shivering, and sore, their bodies aching from the constant fight against both forest and water.

Celenneth set to work immediately, her fingers moving deftly to gather whatever dry wood she could find, her gaze flickering around the darkening forest, wary of the unseen things that watched from the shadows. Linnea led Nimroch to a patch of grass near the river, her hands moving to loosen the saddle, her fingers trembling from the cold. The horse nickered softly, leaning into her touch as she whispered soothing words, her voice trembling only slightly.

When the fire finally caught, flickering into life, Celenneth let out a long breath, the warmth an almost painful contrast to the chill that had settled deep into her limbs. She turned to Linnea, her eyes meeting hers, a small, tired smile tugging at her lips. “We need to dry off,” she said, her voice low, the exhaustion evident.

Linnea nodded, her teeth chattering slightly as she moved toward the fire, her fingers already fumbling at the ties of her soaked tunic. Together they peeled off the layers, their clothes clinging to their skin, heavy with river water. They hung them over a hastily made line, the fire’s heat already beginning to steam away the dampness. The chill of the night air touched their bare skin, but the fire crackled steadily, promising warmth.

“Go on,” Celenneth nodded towards the river, her eyes glancing at Linnea, a teasing smirk touching her lips. “We could use a proper rinse after today.”

Linnea rolled her eyes but smiled, her cheeks flushing, both from the cold and from the prospect of the river. She moved toward Nimroch, giving the horse a final pat before she nodded to Celenneth. Together, they stepped down the embankment, into the shallows of the river once more, their feet sinking into the pebbled riverbed.

The water was cold, enough to make them gasp, but it felt like it washed away the weariness of the day, the grime and exhaustion peeling away as they waded deeper, until the water reached their waists. Celenneth dipped her head under, her hair streaming behind her, and when she came up, she slicked her dark locks back, her eyes catching Linnea’s, a smile breaking across her lips.

“Come here,” she said, her voice softened by the steam rising from the water, the fire crackling behind them on the bank. Linnea moved toward her, her hands reaching up to touch Celenneth’s shoulders, her fingers brushing the skin there, lingering.

Celenneth turned her, her hands moving to Linnea’s hair, her fingers combing through the wet strands, untangling them slowly, tenderly. Linnea closed her eyes, her body relaxing into Celenneth’s touch, the chill of the water forgotten in the warmth of Celenneth’s hands.

“Did you ever hear those stories?” Linnea asked, her voice barely above a whisper, her eyes still closed. “The rumors about the woodland elves? That they would enchant travelers who wandered too deep into their woods, make them dance until they forgot who they were?”

Celenneth chuckled softly, her fingers moving to massage Linnea’s scalp, her touch firm but gentle. “I’ve heard them. My mother used to say they would make you dance until your feet bled, until you couldn’t tell the difference between your own name and the wind.”

Linnea shivered, but it wasn’t from the cold. “Do you think it’s true?” she asked, her voice distant, as though she were speaking to the river itself, her thoughts drifting with the current.

Celenneth paused, her hands stilling for a moment before she resumed, her fingers sliding down to Linnea’s shoulders, her touch gentle, tracing the curve of her collarbone. “I think... there’s truth in every story,” she said, her voice low, her breath warm against the back of Linnea’s neck. “The woods are old. Older than us. Older than the kingdoms of men. Who knows what spirits linger here, what they might want.”

Linnea turned, her eyes meeting Celenneth’s, her gaze searching, her lips parting as though she might speak, but no words came. Instead, she reached up, her fingers brushing against Celenneth’s cheek, her eyes softening.

“Then let’s not give them any reason to keep us,” she whispered, her lips curving into a small smile.

Celenneth leaned in, her forehead touching Linnea's, her arms wrapping around her waist, pulling her closer, the cold of the river forgotten, the forest forgotten, everything else forgotten but the warmth of Linnea’s body pressed against hers.

“We keep each other,” Celenneth murmured, her lips brushing against Linnea's ear, her voice a promise, a vow against the shadows that gathered around them.

The river moved around them, the water cold but the moment warm, a heartbeat of light in the dark, unyielding against the ancient forest that watched, waiting.

The river had washed away the grime and aches of the day, but it was the fire, warm and crackling on the shore, that offered them true comfort. The flames danced, their light flickering across bare skin, warming the chill left behind by the cold water. Celenneth and Linnea climbed up the embankment, their fingers entwined, their feet leaving wet prints on the stones as they moved closer to the fire.

Celenneth took the cloak, still dry, laying it out on the ground beside the fire. The air was crisp, the night biting against their damp skin, but the fire was enough, and the presence of each other more than enough to chase away the cold.

They knelt together, facing each other, the firelight casting their bodies in a golden glow, the shadows playing over the soft curves and lean lines of their forms. Linnea reached out, her fingers brushing against Celenneth’s cheek, her eyes tracing the planes of her face, the firelight reflected in her eyes. She leaned in, her lips brushing against Celenneth’s, the kiss soft at first, a whisper of warmth that deepened as their mouths found each other.

Celenneth sighed against Linnea’s lips, her hand moving to the back of Linnea’s neck, her fingers threading through her damp hair, pulling her closer. The kiss grew languid, unhurried, their bodies pressed close, their breaths mingling as the fire warmed their skin. There was no urgency here, just the slow, steady thrum of their heartbeats, the quiet crackle of the fire, the rustle of the river beside them.

Linnea’s fingers slipped down, tracing the curve of Celenneth’s shoulder, her touch light, her nails brushing against her skin. She pulled back slightly, her eyes half-lidded, a smile curving her lips as she looked at Celenneth, her other hand reaching up to tangle in the dark strands of her hair, twisting them around her fingers.

“Your hair’s a mess,” she murmured, her voice teasing, her lips brushing against Celenneth’s jaw.

Celenneth chuckled, her eyes closing as she leaned into the touch, her hands resting on Linnea’s hips, her thumbs brushing against her skin. “It’s your fault,” she whispered back, her voice a soft rasp, her lips finding Linnea’s ear, her teeth grazing the lobe gently.

Linnea shivered, a soft laugh escaping her, her fingers tightening in Celenneth’s hair as she tilted her head, her lips finding the corner of Celenneth’s mouth, kissing her softly, lazily. The world around them seemed to fade, the forest’s watchful presence forgotten, the danger beyond the firelight pushed back by the warmth of each other.

Their touches were slow, gentle, their fingers exploring familiar places, tracing old scars, feeling the steady pulse beneath each other’s skin. Linnea’s hand moved to Celenneth’s back, her nails dragging lightly down her spine, her lips pressing against her neck, tasting the salt left behind by the river.

Celenneth let out a soft sigh, her hand slipping to Linnea’s waist, pulling her closer, their bodies pressed together, their warmth shared. The fire flickered beside them, the glow casting long shadows, and they moved together, unhurried, lost in the moment, their kisses deepening, their bodies melting into each other.

When the cold began to creep back, they pulled away reluctantly, their eyes meeting, the heat of their kiss still lingering in the air between them. Celenneth reached for the cloak, wrapping it around them both, pulling Linnea close, her arms encircling her, her chin resting atop Linnea’s head as they settled down beside the fire.

Linnea curled into her, her body fitting perfectly against Celenneth’s, her arm draped across her waist. The fire crackled softly, the warmth settling over them like a blanket, and Celenneth closed her eyes, her lips pressing a soft kiss against Linnea’s hair, her fingers brushing against her arm.

“Sleep, my love,” she whispered, her voice barely audible, her breath a warm mist against Linnea’s hair.

Linnea smiled, her eyes closing, her body relaxing into Celenneth’s, the warmth of her presence wrapping around her like a second cloak. The fire burned steadily beside them, the cold kept at bay, the forest quiet for now, and together they drifted into sleep, wrapped in each other, the world beyond the fire forgotten.

Morning came reluctantly, the light filtering through the tangled canopy, the forest reluctant to yield its darkness to the day. Celenneth and Linnea awoke wrapped in each other, the fire reduced to embers beside them, its warmth nothing compared to what they found in one another’s embrace. With soft, lingering touches, they pulled themselves from sleep, dressing slowly in their now-dry clothes, shaking off the dreams that clung to them like mist.

The river, cold against their skin, rushed past them as they forded it, stepping carefully over the slick stones, the current pushing back against their legs. Nimroch crossed beside them, his ears flicking as the water splashed against his legs, his breath steaming in the cool morning air. They reached the opposite bank, shaking off the river’s chill, and began their march upriver again, the path ahead a twisting labyrinth of shadow and light.

It wasn’t long before Linnea heard it again—a faint melody, drifting on the wind. She paused, her head turning slightly, her eyes narrowing as she listened. The music was soft, lilting, the kind of tune that seemed to curl into your thoughts, wrapping itself around your mind, making itself part of your own heartbeat. It was followed by voices—whispered conversations, the words foreign, indecipherable, but filled with a strange cadence that tugged at her senses.

“Do you hear it?” Linnea asked, her voice barely above a murmur, her eyes darting to Celenneth, who walked a few paces ahead.

Celenneth stopped, her eyes narrowing, her lips pressing into a thin line. She nodded once, her gaze shifting to the dark forest around them, her hand resting on the hilt of her sword. “I hear it,” she said, her voice flat, her eyes narrowing as she scanned the woods. The music came and went, fading in and out, as though the forest itself was playing tricks on them. It felt almost mocking, a taunting sound that never stayed in one place long enough to be confronted.

The air grew warmer as they moved, an unnatural heat that seemed to rise from the earth, wrapping around them, thick and musky. It was unlike anything they’d felt before—like walking into the breath of some great beast, the air itself heavy with an unspoken presence. The warmth clung to them, seeping into their clothes, making their skin prickle, a sheen of sweat beginning to form on their brows.

“It feels wrong,” Linnea muttered, her voice barely audible, her hand tightening on the reins as she led Nimroch through the increasingly dense underbrush. The warmth was almost oppressive now, a humid heaviness that made each breath feel thick, labored.

Celenneth glanced at her, her eyes narrowing slightly as she nodded. “It does,” she said, her voice edged with unease. She could feel the wrongness in the air, the way it pressed against her chest, the way it made her skin crawl. The music and whispers continued, always just out of reach, fading and returning like a song half-remembered, like a dream that refused to let go.

As the day wore on, the sky began to darken, the sun dipping below the horizon, and the forest around them shifted, shadows growing long, the warmth of the day fading into the coolness of evening. It was then they stumbled upon it—a clearing, wide and open, the forest giving way to a grassy expanse, the river flowing lazily beside it. Above them, the sky was a deep indigo, the first stars already beginning to shine, pinpricks of light in the growing dark.

Celenneth stopped, her eyes lifting to the sky, her breath catching. The stars were brilliant, scattered across the sky in breathtaking patterns, their light casting a gentle glow across the clearing. She turned to Linnea, a smile touching her lips as she gestured to the open space. “Here,” she said, her voice soft, “this is where we rest tonight.”
