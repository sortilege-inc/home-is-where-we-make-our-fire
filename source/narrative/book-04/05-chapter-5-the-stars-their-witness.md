# Chapter 5: The Stars Their Witness

*Narquelië 16, 2965*

Linnea tipped her head back and the breath went out of her at the sight. The sky over the clearing was endless, the stars so thick and near they seemed a thing she might reach up and gather. "It's perfect," she said.

They made camp with an ease neither had felt in days. The clearing was open and it felt safe, the weight of the trees left behind them, and though the air was still warmer than a night this deep in autumn had any right to be, under the naked sky it troubled her less. Celenneth coaxed a fire to life; Linnea led Nimroch to the river's edge to drink, her ears turned toward the treeline out of habit — but here the music thinned, lost under the run of the water and the wind in the grass.

The fire took, and Celenneth looked up and found Linnea's eyes across it, and they traded a smile that needed nothing said. For tonight they had the stars, and each other, and the forest could wait.

⁂

Sleep took them under the shared cloak, and the warmth grew as the night went deeper — no longer the fire's comfort but something heavier, close, cloying. Celenneth stirred once and shoved the cloak off, her shirt damp and clinging. Beside her Linnea turned and turned, restless, and half-asleep her fingers went to the ties of her tunic and pushed the layers away, her skin sheened with sweat. And under it all, threading the dark, the music came again.

Linnea's eyes drifted open, a haze over them. The heat had left her limbs loose and heavy and strange, and the tune tugged at something under her ribs, a summons she felt more than heard — *move, sway, come.* She rose, slow, dreamlike, bare feet finding the cool grass, and she began to dance. Her arms lifted, her head fell back, her eyes half-closed, and she gave herself to the melody, turning in the firelight with her shadow thrown long behind her.

Celenneth woke to the sight of her — Linnea silhouetted against the stars, swaying, her dark hair catching the light, her body moving as though it weighed nothing at all. For a heartbeat it stopped Celenneth's breath, the beauty of it. Then the wrongness of it landed.

"Linnea?" Her voice came rough with sleep.

Linnea didn't answer. Her eyes were glassed, her lips parted, a soft breathless laugh spilling out of her as she spun, further from the fire, drawn toward the dark treeline a step at a time.

Celenneth was up and across the grass before she'd finished thinking. She caught Linnea by the shoulders, turned her, took her face in both hands. "Linnea. Look at me." She held her gaze and would not let it slide away. "You're dreaming. Come back."

For a moment longer the melody had her — and then it broke, and Linnea blinked, and came to herself with a stumble and a small, embarrassed sound. She looked around at the clearing, at Celenneth's frightened face, her breath coming quick, her skin damp. "I don't — " she started. "I was — "

"I have you." Celenneth drew her in against her, felt the racing of her heart. "It's the wood. It went looking for the loosest thread in you and pulled. You're back now."[^s-dance]

⁂

But the heat did not let go of them, and the music did not stop, and where fear had been a moment before there was now something else rising to meet it. The air lay thick and feverish on the skin, and the drums began — low in the earth, felt through the soles before they were heard, slow and building, a pulse that found the pulse in Celenneth's own chest and matched it.

Linnea's hand closed on her wrist. "Celenneth — I — " The words wouldn't come whole. The need in her was plain, and it was answered; Celenneth felt the same fever climbing in herself, and knew it for what it was — the wood's glamour, yes, but laid over a want that had been theirs and true long before the wood ever touched it. She had spent the day wading through the heat of this place. She would not fight this. She would rather burn toward the one thing here that was real.

"I need you," she breathed, and pulled Linnea's mouth to hers, and the kiss was nothing like the slow one by the fire — it was teeth and open breath and hunger, both of them gasping into it. They came down together onto the crushed grass, and there was no patience left in either of them. Celenneth's fingers found Linnea slick and ready and Linnea's found her the same, the two of them working each other in the dark with the drums driving under it, hips rocking to a rhythm that was half theirs and half the wood's. Linnea's breath sobbed against Celenneth's throat; her nails dragged down Celenneth's back and Celenneth pushed into the sting of it, wanting more of everything at once.

"Don't stop," Linnea gasped.

"I'm not going anywhere." Celenneth turned her onto her back and followed her mouth down — throat, the peak of one breast drawn between her lips, the sweat-salt hollow of her navel — while Linnea's hands fisted in her hair and her hips lifted after her. She settled between Linnea's thighs and this time she did not tease; the fever wanted more than teasing. She took her with her whole mouth, tongue flat and firm where Linnea most needed it, two fingers curling up into her in time, and Linnea came apart almost at once, arching off the ground with a cry that the drums swallowed, and Celenneth stayed and drew it out of her in wave after wave until she was wrung empty and shaking.

The drums faded then, as though they had gotten what they came for. The oppressive heat lifted all at once, a cool breeze moving across their damp skin, and overhead the stars seemed to shimmer and drift, alive, though that might only have been Linnea's spent and swimming sight.

She turned her head and found Celenneth's face gilded in the last of the firelight. Celenneth leaned in and kissed her — slow now, tender, all the fever gone out of it and only the deep thing left. "I love you," she whispered, searching Linnea's eyes as though she needed to see it answered.

Linnea's breath caught. She reached up and traced Celenneth's cheek. "And I love you," she said, unsteady with it. "More than anything."

They kissed again, gentle, and lay tangled under the drifting stars while the music of the wood thinned away to a lullaby, and slept at last with the whole clearing for their witness.
