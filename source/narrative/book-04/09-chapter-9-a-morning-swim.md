# Chapter 9: A Morning Swim

*Narquelië 18, 2965*

They woke slow, in a wide and beautiful bed, morning light falling dappled through the leaves above. The room was open to the wood on one side, so that the rustle of the branches and the murmur of a near stream came in with the air, and the two of them lay tangled and warm, the night still on their skin.

Celenneth opened her eyes to find Linnea's face on the pillow beside her, dark hair spread out around it, peaceful in a way the road rarely allowed. She brushed a strand from her cheek, and Linnea stirred, and her eyes opened to a smile. For a while they only lay there in the quiet, content.

Soft footsteps drew their attention: Arannis and Eryndor, each bearing a tray of fruit and honey and delicate pastries. "We thought you might be hungry," Arannis said, kneeling by the bed, "after such a long night." Eryndor held a piece of fruit to Linnea's lips, and she took it with a blush and a laugh, the sweet juice bright on her tongue. Arannis offered Celenneth a slice of golden peach from her own fingers. "No need to rush today," she murmured. "The forest is yours to enjoy."

When the trays were empty Arannis set them aside. "Come. There's a stream near, cold enough to wash the night away and wake you both." She rose and offered Celenneth her hand.

They were led down a winding path through a wood alive with birdsong, the air cool and sweet, and came to a stream running clear over pale stones. Arannis loosed the sash of her robe and let it fall and stepped into the water, and Eryndor after her; and Linnea, with a glance at Celenneth and a smile, let her own robe drop and followed. Celenneth watched a moment — love and simple wonder tangled together in her — then shed hers and stepped in, the cold water bracing against her skin.

They swam, and splashed, and the Elves' bright laughter proved catching, until Celenneth and Linnea were laughing too, the worries of the deep wood rinsed away. Then Arannis began to hum, and the hum became a song — old, and in Silvan, lilting over the run of the water. "It is about how the truest joy is the joy you did not see coming," she said, and taught them the words as she sang, patient and merry when their tongues stumbled over the strange sounds, until all four voices wove together over the stream. *Surprise brings delight,* she translated, her eyes on Celenneth. *The unexpected is where joy lives.*

The song ended in more laughter and a last flurry of splashing, and after a while Arannis led them back to the bank to gather their robes and return to the halls.

There Eryndor brought them to a broad room strewn with cushions and soft rugs, a carved harp standing in its center, and sat to it, and drew from the strings a slow, unspooling melody that filled the air. Arannis settled among the cushions and drew Celenneth down into her arms, fingers combing idly through her hair; Linnea lay with her head in Celenneth's lap, eyes drifting shut. The music rose and fell, unhurried, and it was a gift freely given — a display of what the Elves could do, and yet made to feel like an offering rather than a boast. Morning warmed toward afternoon, and the three of them lay lulled by the harp and by the steady rhythm of one another's breathing, at rest.
