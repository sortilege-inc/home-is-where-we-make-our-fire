# Chapter 3: Cold Nights

*Narquelië 13, 2965*

The wood took them in and closed behind them. They had left the main path long since, trading the known road for an eastward line along the Forest River, and the river ran fast and fretful at their side, hurrying past as though it were fleeing something upstream and thought them fools for walking toward it.

Celenneth led at her steady pace, but her eyes never rested — every shift of the branches read like the flick of an ear, some watcher turning to mark them. There was no birdsong here, no daytime sound at all, only the water and the vast indifferent patience of old trees. The air itself sat heavy, as if steeped in the fear of everyone who had come this way before and not come back.

Linnea kept close behind, her fingers worrying the hilt of her dagger. "It's like the wood's angry," she muttered. "Like it knows we've no business here."

"Maybe we don't." Celenneth glanced to where the river bent out of sight into deeper shadow. "But here we are. If there's something ahead, I'd sooner meet it looking at its face."

Linnea gave a thin laugh. "You always did run toward what everyone sane runs from."

"Someone has to." A flicker of a smile, gone as fast.

They had both walked Mirkwood's edges before, and both knew this was not that. This was older and more awake. The dark here was not the absence of light but a thing in its own right, a weight that leaned on the chest and thickened with every step deeper. Where the trees drew in ahead and the river narrowed, the way forward looked less like a path than a throat.

"I feel it too," Celenneth said, before Linnea had to ask. "Stay near. If we have to cut our way through something, we do it together."

"Together," Linnea echoed, and the word steadied her.

⁂

Night came down hard and cold, until every breath hung white between them. They made their camp beside the river, and the simple comfort of a fire proved beyond them — for when Celenneth first knelt to lay one, the trees answered. Their branches groaned and shifted, tightening their ring overhead with a slow, deliberate menace, and the meaning of it needed no words. *No flame.* So there was no fire, and there was cold instead.

Linnea pressed close under the shared cloak, and Celenneth wrapped an arm around her and pulled her in, and Linnea tucked her face into the warm crook of her neck and took what heat there was to take.

"This is miserable," she said, muffled against Celenneth's skin. "We could freeze out here."

"If we freeze, we'll do it in each other's arms." The humor came out flatter than Celenneth meant, but she drew the cloak tighter over them both. Around them the wood held its cold silence, and the river ran on whispering its warnings to the dark, and neither of them slept much.

⁂

*Narquelië 14, 2965*

Dawn came grudging and grey and gave no warmth. They untangled stiff and slow, breath fogging, dark circles under both their eyes from a night that had granted little rest.

"We need a fire," Linnea said, her voice cracking. "We can't do that again."

Celenneth sat up, scanning the close-standing trunks. Their bark caught the weak light like wrinkled, scornful faces. "The trees don't like it," she murmured. "Last night, when I tried — I'd swear they moved."

"Maybe they did." Linnea remembered the groan of the branches, the windless stirring of the canopy, the pressure of it. That had frightened her worse than the cold.

But another night unwarmed would leave them weak, and weak was worse than watched. Celenneth weighed it with her jaw set, then reached for the flint. "A small one. Kept low. Just enough to get us through."

The spark caught. As the little flame took, they both held their breath — and the wood seemed to hold its own. The branches creaked and the shadows swayed inward, ringing the fire with their disapproval, but no limb came down to smother it. The flames steadied, and warmth reached their frozen fingers, and for a while it was enough.

⁂

Linnea sat close to the heat with her back to the river, eyes half-shut, letting the numbness leave her hands. Then her head came up. She'd heard something under the crackle of the fire — faint, almost the wind, and not the wind. A thread of melody, impossibly far off, a tune hummed by someone who had no place being out here in the dark.

"Do you hear that?" she whispered.

Celenneth frowned. "Hear what?"

"Music. I swear it — music." Linnea turned her head, straining after it. The tune wavered, faded, and then, close behind her, came a soft breathy giggle.

She spun with the dagger already in her hand — and there was nothing there. Only the leaves and the gurgle of the water, and the air gone thick and expectant, as though it were waiting for her to lose her footing.

"There was someone," she said, pale. "Laughing. Right at my shoulder. I swear it."

Celenneth's face went flinty, her hand to her sword-hilt, her eyes on the black between the trunks. This she could not parry or outrun. She'd take orcs over it gladly — orcs she could kill. This was a thing that meant to work its way into the cracks of them and prise them open from inside. "Keep the dagger close," she said, harder than she intended. "If anything comes, we don't wait to see what it wants."

Linnea nodded and leaned into her side, and their shared warmth carried an edge of fear in it now. The fire burned small against the dark, and somewhere far off the melody went on, patient, and Celenneth did not tell her that she too had begun to catch it — the faintest curl of a song at the very edge of hearing, winding, coiling closer.[^s-glamour]

⁂

*Narquelië 15, 2965*

The fire had sunk to embers by dawn, and with the grey light the pressure of the wood drew back a little, the music and the laughter gone as though they had been a bad dream.

Celenneth woke first. Linnea lay curled against her, her face half-buried in the crook of her arm, her breathing deep and slow with a rest that had been rare in these woods. For a moment Celenneth only watched her — the tension she carried by day smoothed away, dark hair loose across her cheek, her lips parted on a soft breath. Beyond their small camp the wood was still hostile and cold; here under the cloak, warmed by each other, the world could be held off a little longer.

She leaned in without deciding to and pressed her lips to Linnea's brow. Linnea stirred, murmured, her eyes drifting open — and before she could speak, Celenneth kissed her mouth, slow and deep, her hand slipping behind her neck to draw her in.

Linnea sighed into it and woke fully under the kiss, her hand finding Celenneth's waist and pulling. There was a heat between them that owed nothing to the dead fire — the pull that had been building unspoken through every shared danger, every glance held a beat too long — and Linnea kissed her back fiercely, as if to pour all her fear into it and be rid of it.

When they parted, their breath mingling in the cold, Celenneth rested her forehead to Linnea's. "We need to move," she said, and her voice came reluctant and thick.

"I know." Linnea's fingers lingered at her waist, unwilling. "I know."

Celenneth drew back slowly, her thumb brushing Linnea's cheek as though to keep the moment before the wood pressed in again. Then she rose and went to see to Nimroch, who stamped and flicked his ears, wary of the trees, and steadied under her sure hands. Linnea watched her work — the calm efficiency of it a fixed point in a place with no others — and pulled the cloak around her shoulders, the cold biting at her again, and a warmth still lit beneath her ribs where the kiss had been. The forest watched on. But for now there was warmth, and there was each other, and whatever waited they would meet it together.

[^s-glamour]: **Shadow / enchantment** — the fey glamour of deep Mirkwood begins to work on the travelers: disembodied music and laughter at the edge of hearing, a lure that preys on the wandering mind. The trees themselves forbid fire (a Journey hazard). Both women feel it; both hold. A first, low touch of Shadow.
