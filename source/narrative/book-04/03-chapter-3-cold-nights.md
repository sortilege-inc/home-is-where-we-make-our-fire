# Chapter 3: Cold Nights

*Book 4*

*Narquelië 13, 2965*

The forest was a mouth swallowing them whole, the underbrush gnashing like teeth, each gnarled tree limb a skeletal arm reaching to pull them into Mirkwood’s suffocating maw. The main path was long behind them, the comfort of familiarity traded for an eastward journey along the twisting Forest River—a ribbon of silver that seemed to shimmer like a frightened thing, rushing past them in desperate retreat.

Celenneth led with her usual steady stride, but her eyes flickered between the shadows, every whisper of branches like the hiss of an unseen predator. The forest was not their friend. Here, the air felt heavy, as if it had been steeped in the hopelessness of forgotten travelers. Each breath felt like inhaling someone else’s fear. There was no chirping of birds, no sounds of daytime life, just the dark, indifferent watch of the ancient trees looming over them.

Linnea kept pace behind, her fingers brushing the hilt of her dagger, an anxious habit. “It’s like the forest is angry,” she muttered, voice barely rising above the sound of her own footsteps. “Like it knows we shouldn’t be here.”

“Maybe we shouldn’t,” Celenneth replied, her tone as dry as the fallen leaves they crunched beneath their boots. She paused, casting a wary glance to the side where the river flowed hurriedly, its surface dappled in shadow. “But here we are. If it’s something dangerous ahead, I'd rather meet it knowing its face.”

Linnea let out a nervous chuckle, her eyes scanning the dense underbrush as though expecting it to suddenly part and reveal whatever awaited them. “You always did have a penchant for charging toward what everyone else would run from.”

Celenneth shot her a quick, sidelong smile—one that flickered and vanished like a candle in the wind. “Someone has to.”

The river’s movements were frantic, the water rushing, splashing against rocks like panicked whispers, urging them to turn back. Celenneth could almost imagine the river personified—a creature running for its life, while they, foolhardy and stubborn, marched willingly toward whatever peril it sought to escape.

“Still,” Linnea spoke again, her voice now soft, almost lost amidst the murmuring forest, “it feels wrong. Not like the other times we were here.”

Celenneth didn’t need to ask why. The forest had always held a threat, but this was different—a primal sense that made the skin at the back of her neck crawl. It was as though the air itself was thickening, solidifying with each step, the dark of the woods not just an absence of light but a presence—a heavy blanket of something that waited, watching them.

“Trust me, I feel it too.” Celenneth’s eyes narrowed, scanning ahead. She could see where the trees closed in, the river bending out of sight, a place where visibility would be reduced to almost nothing. They’d have to cross that threshold, step into a darkness even denser than this—a plunge into the unknown.

Linnea nodded, her lips pressed into a thin line. She gripped her dagger more tightly, her eyes catching the flicker of movement in the corner of her vision—branches swaying, not with the wind, but as though disturbed by something unseen.

Celenneth moved, her stride purposeful but her eyes alert, her hand resting on the hilt of her sword. “If there’s something in these woods,” she said, her voice low, but carrying a hard edge, “we won’t let it catch us off guard.”

The river ran beside them, an anxious companion, its hurried journey east like a desperate plea. The forest thickened, shadows knitting themselves tighter around the trunks, closing over the canopy above. Celenneth could almost feel the forest drawing breath, waiting to see if they dared come further.

She glanced back at Linnea, catching the hint of worry in her eyes. “If we have to fight our way through, we’ll do it together.”

Linnea’s eyes met hers, the fear in them tempered with resolve. “Together,” she echoed, her voice steadier now, the word an anchor.

With a final breath, Celenneth led them forward, into the mouth of the deeper dark, the forest swallowing them whole.

The night crept over them like a shroud, cold and merciless, seeping into their bones until every breath clouded the air between them in fleeting plumes. They had settled beside the river, its dark surface rippling with an uneasy current, the forest pressing close around them, as though even the trees themselves sought to listen in on their every word. The idea of a fire had seemed simple at first, a comfort to stave off the chill, but the trees had spoken. Not in words, no. They had whispered their displeasure in the creaking groans of their branches, the way their skeletal limbs shifted, tightening their ring as if angered by the very idea of flame.

So there was no fire. Instead, there was cold. Linnea curled close to Celenneth, the shared cloak barely enough to keep the bite of the air from them. The ground beneath was hard, the kind of hardness that found a way to settle itself into your bones no matter how you lay. She pressed herself into Celenneth’s side, feeling her warmth, what little there was of it, radiating through her. The Ranger’s arm wrapped around her shoulders, pulling her tighter, and Linnea tucked her face into the crook of Celenneth's neck, drawing from that sliver of warmth like a starving thing.

“This is miserable,” Linnea muttered, her voice muffled against Celenneth’s skin, her lips brushing warmth into the cold night. “We could freeze out here.”

Celenneth shivered, her breath steady but sharp with each inhale, as though she were breathing daggers instead of air. “If we freeze, it’ll be in each other’s arms,” she said, the attempt at humor lacking the usual spark, but her hold on Linnea tightened. She shifted, tugging the cloak to cover them both more securely, their bodies pressed close in the only defiance they had against the creeping chill.

Linnea sighed, her breath a soft mist that vanished into the void above them. The forest loomed, branches silhouetted against the faint light of stars, skeletal fingers tracing patterns in the dark sky. The river whispered nearby, its voice low, conspiratorial, as if it were exchanging secrets with the night. Linnea hated that sound now—it was the voice of something running from a fear they didn’t yet understand.

“I hate this place,” she whispered, her teeth chattering softly. “It’s like it’s alive... like it knows we shouldn’t be here.”

Celenneth tilted her head slightly, her cheek resting against Linnea's hair. “It knows,” she agreed. The forest did seem to breathe, to watch, a thousand unseen eyes behind the bark and bramble. She could almost hear its disdain, that hiss of ancient roots disturbed, the murmur of something that did not welcome them.

Dawn came slowly, a hesitant creeping light, gray and washed out, lacking any warmth. When they stirred, untangling themselves, the cold clung to their limbs, leaving them stiff and slow. Linnea rubbed her hands together, her fingers numb, her breath fogging around her face. Her eyes found Celenneth’s, both of them marked by dark circles from a night that brought little rest.

“We need a fire,” Linnea said, her voice cracking slightly. “We can’t keep doing this.”

Celenneth sat up, her eyes scanning the forest around them, wary. “The trees don’t like it,” she murmured. Her breath misted as she spoke, and she glanced at the clustered trunks, their bark like wrinkled, scornful faces. “Last night... it was almost like they were moving when we tried.”

Linnea swallowed, the memory still vivid—the way the trees had groaned, the canopy above rustling with a sudden windless agitation, branches shifting inwards like a living, breathing thing. She had felt that pressure, that silent threat in the air, and it chilled her more than the cold ever could. “Maybe they were.”

“We’re not wanted here,” Celenneth said, her voice barely more than a whisper, her gaze lingering on the dark spaces between the trees, as if something might emerge from them at any moment. “But we can’t keep going if we’re half-frozen. We need warmth.”

Linnea nodded, her fingers flexing in an attempt to bring some life back to them. She glanced at the pile of dry wood they’d gathered last night—wood they hadn’t dared light. “We take the risk?” she asked, her eyes meeting Celenneth's.

Celenneth hesitated, her eyes narrowing as she weighed their options. The forest loomed, the sense of hostility an unspoken presence pressing in on them. The cold had sunk deep into their bones, and another night like the last would leave them weakened—vulnerable. She clenched her jaw, her gaze shifting between Linnea and the woods.

“Let’s do it,” she said, her voice steady, her resolve hard as flint. “We make a small fire. We keep it low. Just enough to get us through.”

Linnea gave a quick nod, relief mingling with fear. Together they moved, the small stack of wood gathered between them. The flint struck, the spark caught, and as the tiny flame flickered to life, both of them held their breath, waiting.

The forest seemed to hold its breath too. The flames crackled softly, the warmth reaching their frozen fingers, and Linnea sighed, leaning into the growing heat. But the trees stood watch, their branches creaking, their shadows seeming to sway, encircling them with a presence both silent and oppressive.

The fire danced in defiance of the forest's cold silence, and for a moment, it was enough.

The fire crackled, small and defiant against the cold that clung to the bones of the forest. Its warmth seeped slowly into Linnea's fingers, a fragile comfort in the face of the dark that surrounded them. Celenneth’s eyes darted between the flickering flames and the woods beyond, her expression taut as if she expected the branches to reach down at any moment and smother the fire out of existence.

Linnea sat closer to the warmth, her back to the river, her eyes half-closed as she tried to focus on the simple luxury of heat. She rubbed her hands together, feeling the numbness retreat. Then, something else came—a sound. She stopped, her head lifting, her brows drawing together. It was faint, almost a trick of the wind. A melody.

She blinked, straining to listen, her heart tightening in her chest. Music, impossibly distant, drifting like a breeze that shouldn’t exist in this place. She turned her head, her eyes scanning the shadows, her body stiffening. Her gaze flickered to Celenneth, who was watching her now, eyes narrowing in question.

“Do you hear that?” Linnea whispered, her voice barely audible over the crackle of the flames.

Celenneth frowned, her eyes searching Linnea’s face. “Hear what?”

Linnea hesitated, her lips parting, the sound teasing at the edge of her hearing—soft, lilting, like a tune hummed by someone not quite sane, a melody that had no place in the dark depths of Mirkwood. She swallowed, her breath catching. “Music. I swear I hear music...”

Celenneth’s expression darkened, her eyes shifting to the forest beyond the light of the fire. “I hear nothing,” she murmured, her voice clipped, tense. But her hand moved to rest on the hilt of her sword, fingers tightening, her gaze growing colder, more watchful.

Linnea shook her head, as if trying to rid herself of it. The melody seemed to shift, fading in and out, and then—laughter. A soft, breathy giggle, right behind her.

She jumped, her body twisting, her eyes widening as she spun to face the empty space behind her, her dagger already in her hand. The laughter vanished, the sound replaced by nothing but the rustle of leaves, the gurgling of the river. Her heart pounded, her eyes darting between the shadows. There was nothing there, but the air felt wrong, thick and alive, as though it was waiting for her to slip.

“Linnea?” Celenneth's voice came, a hard edge of concern cutting through the stillness.

Linnea turned back, her face pale, her eyes wide. “There was—someone laughing.” Her voice was a whisper, her chest tightening as she spoke. “Right behind me. I swear it.”

Celenneth’s gaze turned flinty, her eyes narrowing at the darkness. The fire between them seemed tiny, fragile, the shadows around them growing deeper, the branches of the trees above rustling, shifting closer as though reaching down with skeletal fingers. The anger of the forest was palpable, but this—this was something else, something more insidious. The sound of laughter that had no body, music that had no source.

“This is worse than orcs,” Celenneth muttered, her voice low, her eyes darting back to the forest. There was a wildness here, a madness that she could not fight with a blade, could not parry or dodge. She preferred dangers she could face, things she could kill or outrun. This—whatever this was—felt like something that would seep into the cracks of their minds, twist them until they weren’t themselves anymore.

“Keep your dagger close,” she said, her voice harsher than she intended, her gaze fixed on Linnea's frightened eyes. “If anything comes...”

Linnea nodded, swallowing hard, her fingers tightening around the hilt of her dagger. She leaned into Celenneth, their shared warmth now tinged with the electric pulse of fear, her back pressing against Celenneth’s side as though trying to ward off the encroaching dark. The fire flickered between them, its glow fighting against the cold shadows, but the melody continued, somewhere far away, distant and haunting.

Celenneth closed her eyes for a moment, steadying herself, her arm wrapping tighter around Linnea. She didn’t speak, didn’t tell Linnea that she too could now hear the faintest hint of something—just beyond the edge of what she knew was real. A song that curled at the edges of her thoughts, winding like a snake, coiling tighter.

The forest was alive, and it knew they were there. And worse—it was laughing at them.

The fire had burned low during the night, its embers glowing like the heartbeat of the earth, casting a faint warmth that held the darkness at bay. The oppressive feeling from the trees seemed to recede as sleep had taken them, the laughter fading, the melody drifting away like a nightmare giving way to the reluctant light of morning.

Celenneth stirred first, her eyes opening to the gray dawn filtering through the gnarled branches above, the first light making the world appear muted, softened at the edges. Linnea was still beside her, curled close, her face half-buried in the crook of Celenneth's arm. Her breathing was steady, her body slack with the kind of rest that had been rare in the woods of Mirkwood.

For a moment, Celenneth simply watched her, the lines of tension that usually marked her expression softening. Linnea’s face, framed by stray strands of dark hair, was peaceful in this moment, her lips parted slightly, her breath a whisper against Celenneth’s skin. The world beyond their small camp was still a shadowed place, hostile and indifferent, but here—wrapped together beneath their cloak, warmed by the shared heat of their bodies—it was almost as if the world had been pushed away, if only for a while.

Without thinking, Celenneth leaned in, brushing her lips against Linnea’s forehead, feeling the warmth of her skin beneath her lips. Linnea stirred, a soft murmur escaping her, her eyes fluttering open. Before she could speak, Celenneth captured her mouth in a kiss, deep and slow, her hand slipping behind Linnea's neck to pull her closer, her fingers threading through the tangle of Linnea's hair.

Linnea responded with a soft sigh, her body waking beneath Celenneth's touch, her hand slipping to Celenneth’s waist, pulling her closer. There was a heat between them that had nothing to do with the fading fire, an urgency that had been building unspoken—something that had grown with every shared danger, every stolen glance, every touch that lingered just a heartbeat too long. Linnea’s fingers tightened, and she kissed back fiercely, as if trying to pour every fear, every unspoken feeling into that one embrace.

When they finally parted, their breaths mingling in the cold morning air, Celenneth rested her forehead against Linnea's, her lips brushing the words, “We need to move,” but her voice was thick, reluctant.

Linnea smiled, her eyes still half-closed, her breath hitching as she gave a small nod. “I know,” she whispered back, her fingers lingering at Celenneth’s waist, unwilling to let go just yet.

Celenneth pulled away slowly, her eyes lingering on Linnea’s face, her thumb brushing the edge of her cheek, as though memorizing this moment before the world pressed in on them once more. She rose, the warmth of Linnea’s body still clinging to her as she moved to where Nimroch was tethered. The horse snorted softly, his ears flicking as Celenneth approached, his breath misting in the chill air.

“Easy, Nimroch,” she murmured, her hands moving deftly to check the straps, the bridle, her touch calm and sure. The horse seemed to sense the lingering tension from the night before, his muscles twitching, his eyes wary as he eyed the forest around them.

Linnea sat up, rubbing her eyes, watching Celenneth move with an ease that spoke of years of practice. There was something about watching Celenneth work, the way her hands moved, her focus unbroken—it was reassuring. A constant, like the sunrise or the flow of the river beside them. She pulled the cloak around her shoulders, feeling the cold biting at her exposed skin, but a warmth still lingered beneath her ribs, a flush on her lips where Celenneth had kissed her.

Linnea rose, her dagger still at her side, her gaze drifting to the forest around them. The shadows were still long, the trees still looming, but the oppressive feeling had faded, just slightly, as though the forest had drawn back, reluctant to challenge them again so soon. She moved to help Celenneth, her hand brushing against the ranger’s as she approached, their eyes meeting for a heartbeat, a flicker of a smile passing between them.

The fire had died down, and the forest still watched, but for now, there was warmth, and there was each other. They would face whatever lay ahead together.
