# Chapter 2: Guidance

*Narquelië 12, 2965*

They rode north under a clean sky, the Forest River glinting away on their right, and for a morning the going was gentle — Nimroch easy beneath them, the wind moving in the grass, and away ahead the dark wall of Mirkwood growing steadily nearer against it. Celenneth let herself feel the small mercy of open country while it lasted. It would not last long.

At a quiet bend where the water slowed, they came on a figure standing at the river's edge — cloaked, his face lost in the hood, a pack set down at his feet. He raised a hand in greeting and made no move toward them.

Celenneth signed Linnea to hold back and swung down, one hand resting easy near her sword. "What brings you here?" she called, level.

The stranger pushed his hood back a little — an older man, weathered, with sharp and careful eyes. "A wanderer, no more," he said. "The road is long, and the river's decent company. And you? Bound into the wood, by the look of you."

Linnea stayed mounted, her fingers near her bowstring. "What's it to you?"

He chuckled, his gaze moving between them. "Nothing at all. Only a word of advice: the wood is unkind to those who go in unready."

"And what would you know of Mirkwood?" Celenneth asked.

"A fair bit." He leaned on his stick. "Enough to know its paths, and which shadows to keep clear of. I'd share it — for a price."

"I carry little coin," Celenneth said plainly. "But if the offer's honest, we might trade in something else."

He tilted his head, interested. "Something useful for the road would suit me. A well-forged blade. A vial of Elvish physic. Or a tale well told."

She glanced back at Linnea, who lifted one shoulder. *We've supplies,* the look said, *and you have your songs.* Celenneth turned back. "I'll give you a thing more lasting than coin," she said. "A song. A tale to keep you company on your own long road."

Something shifted in his face — real interest now. "A song, is it? I know the old ones, all of them. You'll have to give me something I've not heard."

She did not often sing where strangers could hear. But she felt the weight of the moment, and after a pause she nodded. "Then I'll give you one worth the road."

She began low, the melody carrying grandeur and grief braided together. It was a song of Lif — the dwarf who had stood against Malech, the one-eyed orc that had made a slaughterhouse of the deep halls — of his blade bright in the dark and his spirit that would not bend even when the end was on him. Then the tune darkened, and she sang the truth of it: that courage had not been enough, that the spear had found his side all the same, and of the stillness that came after. Her voice caught once, and she carried on through it, and let the song hold the whole cost of the thing.[^r-song]

When the last note faded, Linnea was staring at her. "I've never heard that one," she said softly. "Malech, and Lif — did the dwarves teach it to you?"

Celenneth met her eyes, quieter now. "I made it. In Lif's memory — a stone to mark him, and a warning."

"It's beautiful," Linnea whispered. "And so sad."

The wanderer let out a long breath, his sharp features gone soft. "You were there," he said — not quite a question. "You saw it, with your own eyes."

"I fought beside him," Celenneth said, her gaze gone far off. "His courage earned remembering."

The man shook his head slowly, admiration plain on him. "A tale as deep as the halls it happened in," he murmured. "You've honored me, Ranger. I'll answer in kind, with good counsel." He gestured toward the dark line of the wood. "Take the left fork, and you'll keep clear of the worst of the bogs. If ever the trees creak louder than the wind can account for, put the river between you and them. And beware anything that moves in the shadows without a reason to." With a last nod he turned and went off down the trail, and was gone.

They remounted, and Linnea rode a while in thought before she spoke. "You've seen so much," she said. "Not just places — the stories in them. Do you ever wonder how much of what we know will just be lost? The way Moria was lost."

Celenneth considered it. "That's why we keep the stories," she said. "Even the sad ones. Forget them, and you lose the lesson, and the place, and the people who were there. Someone has to carry them out."

Linnea nodded slowly, and they turned toward the eaves of the wood, the lament of the dwarf-halls still moving quietly in her, and in the darkening trees ahead.

[^r-song]: **Song / Courtesy** — Celenneth trades a song for a stranger's road-lore rather than coin; her own composition (a lament for **Lif**, slain by the orc Malech in Moria) wins his respect and sound counsel on the safe paths into Mirkwood. A small Fellowship exchange.
