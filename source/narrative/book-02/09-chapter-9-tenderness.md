# Chapter 9: Tenderness

*Book 2*

*Cermië 29, 2965*

The morning dawned clear and crisp, the sunlight filtering softly through the trees. Linnea rose slowly from her bedroll, her movements unusually hesitant. She glanced toward Celenneth, who was already awake and tending to Nimroch. The ranger’s calm, deliberate manner made Linnea’s stomach flutter—she couldn’t deny it any longer. Her feelings for Celenneth had deepened beyond gratitude or admiration. They were something more, something she couldn’t keep locked inside much longer.

As she folded the shared cloak and packed her belongings, Linnea made a decision. *Tonight*, she thought. *I’ll tell her tonight*. Her hands trembled slightly as she worked, her mind racing through the words she would say. *Would Celenneth understand? Would she feel the same?* The uncertainty gnawed at her, but she pushed it aside.

“Linnea,” Celenneth’s steady voice broke through her thoughts. “Are you ready?”

Linnea startled slightly, dropping the corner of the cloak she was folding. “Yes—yes, I’m ready,” she replied quickly, her cheeks flushing.

Celenneth’s sharp gaze lingered on her for a moment before the ranger turned back to Nimroch. “Then let’s not waste daylight.”

As the morning stretched into afternoon, Linnea rode behind Celenneth on Nimroch, the steady rhythm of the horse’s gait doing little to calm her nerves. Her thoughts swirled as she silently rehearsed what she wanted to say.

“Celenneth, I need to tell you something… No, too formal.” She frowned, gripping the saddle tighter. “I care about you, more than I thought possible… Is that too much?” Her cheeks flushed as she imagined Celenneth’s calm, unwavering gaze.

The imagined responses ranged wildly in her mind. She pictured Celenneth turning, her eyes soft with understanding. “I’ve felt the same, Linnea. I just didn’t know how to tell you.” That thought filled her with a warmth she couldn’t suppress. But then, her mind conjured another possibility: Celenneth shaking her head, her expression unreadable. “It’s not possible,” the ranger might say. “This road is no place for such feelings. I have a man in the North.”

The uncertainty clawed at her, but still, she couldn’t stop imagining. As the sun climbed higher, she kept glancing at Celenneth’s back, watching the way she moved with such confidence, such purpose. How could someone like her feel the same for me? she wondered, doubt creeping in.

Celenneth, for her part, was as steady as ever. Her sharp eyes scanned the horizon, her hand resting lightly on the hilt of her sword. She seemed unaware of Linnea’s inner turmoil, her focus entirely on the road ahead.

Occasionally, she would glance back to check on Linnea. “Are you comfortable?” she asked at one point, her voice calm and measured.

“Yes,” Linnea replied quickly, though her voice wavered. Celenneth raised an eyebrow but said nothing, turning her attention back to the trail.

Each interaction only made Linnea more uncertain. Celenneth’s quiet strength was part of what drew her in, but it also made her difficult to read. Did she ever think about Linnea at all? Or was this just another journey, another person who required protection?

When they finally stopped to make camp, Linnea’s heart was pounding through her chest. She busied herself gathering firewood, hoping the motion would steady her nerves. Celenneth, as always, began tending to Nimroch and setting their supplies in order. The routine felt familiar, comforting, and yet Linnea knew that tonight would be different.

The fire crackled softly in the growing darkness, its warmth driving back the evening chill. Linnea sat cross-legged on her bedroll, her hands clasped tightly in her lap as she stared into the flames. The shadows danced across the grove, their flickering light a reflection of her restless thoughts. They had eaten in silence.

Celenneth sat nearby, fletching arrows. The rhythmic motion and calm focus only made Linnea more nervous, the words she had rehearsed all day suddenly feeling clumsy and inadequate.

Linnea took a slow breath, glancing at Celenneth from the corner of her eye. The ranger’s sharp profile was illuminated by the firelight, her expression serene but distant. It was a face Linnea had come to admire deeply—not just for its beauty, but for the strength and quiet kindness it carried.

Finally Celenneth put the arrows away and leaned back, her attention shifting to the fire. Linnea saw her chance.

“Celenneth,” she began, her voice soft but insistent. She winced at how shaky it sounded but pressed on. “Can I… talk to you about something?”

Celenneth turned her gaze to Linnea, her expression neutral but attentive. “Of course,” she said simply. “What is it?”

Linnea hesitated, her carefully rehearsed words catching in her throat. She looked down at her hands, twisting them nervously, before forcing herself to meet Celenneth’s steady gaze.

“I’ve been thinking a lot lately,” she said, her voice trembling slightly. “About everything you’ve done for me, and… about us. About how I feel.”

She paused, searching for the right words. The fire crackled softly between them, the silence stretching but not breaking.

“I care about you, Celenneth,” she said finally, her voice barely above a whisper. “More than I ever thought I could care about someone. And I just… I needed you to know that.”

For a moment, Celenneth didn’t respond. Her sharp eyes remained fixed on Linnea, her expression unreadable in the flickering firelight. Linnea felt her heart sink as the silence stretched on, her words hanging in the air like a fragile thread.

“Linnea,” Celenneth said finally, her tone calm but firm. “You’ve been through so much. It’s natural to feel gratitude, even attachment, for someone who’s helped you. But…” She shook her head slightly, looking away toward the fire. “This road, this life—it’s no place for... attachments like that.”

Linnea blinked, her chest tightening. “It’s not gratitude,” she said quietly, her voice tinged with hurt. “It's deeper than that.”

Celenneth closed her eyes briefly, as though trying to steady herself. When she looked back at Linnea, there was a vulnerability in her gaze that the younger woman hadn’t seen before. “I do know,” she admitted softly. “And that’s why this is so difficult.”

She leaned forward slightly, her hands clasped tightly together. “I’ve spent my life on this road, Linnea. I’ve learned to keep my distance because I’ve seen what happens when I don’t. Loss... heartbreak. It’s inevitable. And for someone like me…” She paused, her voice faltering for the first time. “I don’t know if I can bear it again.”

Linnea’s breath caught, her eyes searching Celenneth’s face. “So you feel the same,” she said, her voice filled with quiet hope.

Celenneth’s jaw tightened, and she looked down at her hands. “I don’t want to,” she said, her voice barely audible. “But yes.”

The fire crackled softly between them as the confession hung in the air. Celenneth glanced up, her expression conflicted but open in a way Linnea had never seen before. “There’s a man back in Bree,” she said, her voice steadier now. “Someone I thought… I hoped… I wrote him. Before I left. Confessed my own feelings, in a way. I've regretted it since. It wasn't fair to him for me to do that."

Linnea remained silent, her heart aching at the pain in Celenneth’s voice.

“And with you…” Celenneth shook her head again, her gaze hardening slightly. “When we reach Radagast, your journey with me will end. You’ll stay safe, away from this migratory life. That’s how it has to be.”

Linnea leaned forward, her hand hovering near Celenneth’s but not quite touching. “Why need it be that way?” she asked, her voice trembling. “What if we—”

“*No*.” Celenneth’s voice was sharp, cutting her off. But the edge softened almost immediately. “Linnea, I care for you more than I should. That’s the truth. But caring for you means letting you go, so you can have a life that isn’t full of danger and loss.”

Linnea lowered her head, her eyes glistening with unshed tears, emotions churning, but she didn’t press further. The firelight danced between them, and for a moment, the world felt impossibly still. When she raised her head again, her gaze was steady.

“I understand why you feel this way,” she said softly, her voice carrying a quiet strength. “I know the risks. I know what the road has taken from you. But Celenneth…” She paused, her breath catching. “We’re here now. And we don’t know what tomorrow will bring.”

She leaned forward slightly, her voice trembling but resolute. “Please. Let’s embrace what we share, even if it’s just for a few days. Even if it’s only for this moment. I am more liable to regret inaction.”

Celenneth’s sharp gaze softened, the walls she had carefully built cracking under Linnea’s words. “Linnea, I…” she began, but her voice faltered.

Linnea didn’t wait for her to finish. She moved closer, her movements slow and deliberate, giving Celenneth every chance to pull away. But the ranger remained still, her breathing even as Linnea reached out and placed a gentle hand on her cheek.

“Please,” Linnea whispered, her eyes searching Celenneth’s face. “Just this once.”

For a moment, time seemed to stop. The crackling fire, the rustling leaves, the distant call of a night bird—all faded into the background as Linnea leaned in and wrapped her arms around Celenneth. The ranger stiffened at first, her body tense, but then she exhaled softly, her resistance melting away.

Celenneth’s arms came up slowly, hesitantly, before wrapping around Linnea in return. She buried her face in Linnea’s shoulder, her grip tightening as though afraid to let go. The vulnerability she had tried so hard to hide now poured out, and for the first time in what felt like an eternity, she allowed herself to simply feel.

They stayed like that for a long time, holding each other in the quiet of the night. The fire burned low, casting faint shadows that flickered across their faces. Celenneth pulled back slightly, her forehead resting against Linnea’s as she spoke, her voice barely above a whisper. “I don’t know how to do this.”

Linnea smiled faintly, her hands still resting on Celenneth’s shoulders. “We don’t have to figure it all out now,” she said softly. “Let’s just be here. Together.”

Celenneth nodded slowly, her defenses finally lowered. For this moment, she allowed herself to let go of the burdens of the road, the past, and the uncertain future. And in Linnea’s embrace, she found a rare and fragile peace.

Linnea lingered in Celenneth’s embrace, her heart pounding but her resolve unwavering. The moment felt fragile, fleeting, but she wasn’t ready to let it slip away just yet. Gently, she leaned back, her hands still resting lightly on Celenneth’s shoulders. Celenneth’s sharp features were made prominent in the firelight, her typical stoic expression now open and vulnerable. Linnea hesitated, searching the ranger’s eyes for any sign of resistance. Instead, she saw quiet acceptance, tempered with uncertainty but no longer denial.

“Goodnight, Celenneth,” Linnea whispered, her voice trembling but filled with warmth. Then, before she could lose her courage, she leaned in and kissed her.

The kiss was gentle, almost tentative, but it carried all the emotion Linnea had been holding back. Celenneth froze for the briefest moment, then let herself sink into it, her lips responding softly. The world around them seemed to fall away, leaving only the quiet crackle of the fire and the shared warmth of their connection.

Linnea deepened the kiss, reaching around to run her fingers through Celenneth's hair. Their bodies pressed close together, and Linnea could feel the beat of her heart. When she finally pulled back, her cheeks were flushed, her heart racing. She smiled faintly, her eyes shining with a mixture of joy and gratitude. Celenneth exhaled slowly, her expression unreadable but her gaze steady on Linnea.

Without a word, Linnea pulled the cloak over them both, settling into Celenneth’s side as the night air grew cold. The fire burned down to embers, and the forest around them was still. For now, at least, they had this—a fragile but undeniable connection in the vast, uncertain world.
