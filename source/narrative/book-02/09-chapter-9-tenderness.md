# Chapter 9: Tenderness

*Cermië 29, 2965*

Morning came clear and crisp, and Linnea woke slow and reluctant, and lay a moment watching Celenneth already up and seeing to Nimroch — the calm deliberate way she moved, that put a flutter under Linnea's ribs she could no longer pretend was anything but what it was. This had gone past gratitude days ago. Past admiration. It was the other thing, the one with no safe name, and she could not keep it locked down much longer without it breaking something.

She folded the shared cloak and made her decision as she did it. *Tonight,* she thought. *I'll tell her tonight.* Her hands were not quite steady on the fabric.

"Linnea." The ranger's even voice. "Ready?"

She startled, dropped a corner of the cloak. "Yes — yes. Ready." Her face went hot.

Celenneth's sharp gaze held on her a beat, then turned back to the horse. "Then let's not waste the daylight."

She rode behind Celenneth through the morning with her arms loose at the ranger's waist and her mind anywhere but the road, rehearsing and discarding a hundred openings. *Celenneth, I need to tell you — no, too much like an ambush. I care about you more than I meant to — is that too far?* She watched the sure line of Celenneth's shoulders and thought, helplessly, *how could someone like her ever feel the same of me?* — and could not make herself stop imagining that she might.

Celenneth was her steady self, eyes on the horizon, hand near her sword, and seemed to feel nothing of the storm at her back — though once she glanced round. "Comfortable?"

"Yes," Linnea said, too fast, and the ranger raised a brow and let it go, and that easy unreadability was half of what drew Linnea in and half of what was slowly driving her mad.

By the time they made camp her heart was going like a hare's. She gathered firewood to keep her hands busy while Celenneth saw to Nimroch and the supplies; they ate in a silence that felt different tonight, weighted. And when at last Celenneth put away the arrows she had been fletching and leaned back into the firelight, her sharp face gone serene and far away, Linnea took her breath and her chance together.

"Celenneth." Her voice came out shaky and she pressed on through it. "Can I talk to you? About something."

Celenneth turned to her, neutral, attentive. "Of course. What is it?"

And now that the moment was here every careful word deserted her. She looked at her twisting hands, and made herself look up. "I've been thinking. About everything you've done for me. And about — us. About how I feel." A crackle of fire in the silence. "I care about you, Celenneth. More than I ever thought I could care for anyone. I needed you to know it."

For a long moment Celenneth said nothing, her eyes steady on Linnea's face, and Linnea felt her heart begin to sink into the quiet.

"Linnea." Calm, but careful. "You've been through so much. It's a natural thing, to feel — attachment — for the one who helped you through it. But this road, this life. It's no place for feelings like that." She looked away to the fire.

"It's not gratitude." Linnea heard the hurt in her own voice and did not hide it. "It's deeper than that."

Celenneth shut her eyes a moment, as if to steady herself, and when she opened them there was something in her face Linnea had not seen before — the guard down, and under it a plain vulnerability. "I know," she said, low. "That's why it's so hard." She leaned forward, hands clasped tight. "I've spent my whole life on this road. I keep my distance because I've learned what comes when I don't. Loss. And I don't—" her voice caught, the first time Linnea had ever heard it falter — "I don't know that I can bear it again."

Linnea's breath snagged. "Then you feel it too."

Celenneth looked at her hands. "I don't want to," she said, barely audible. "But yes."

The word landed and stayed. Then Celenneth glanced up, open in a way that cost her visibly. "There's a man in Bree. Someone I thought — hoped. I wrote to him before I left, confessed something of what I felt, in a way. I've regretted it since; it wasn't fair of me, to put that on him and ride away." She shook her head. "And you — when we reach Radagast, your road with me ends. You'll be safe, out of this wandering life. That's how it has to be."

Linnea leaned in, her hand hovering near Celenneth's, not quite touching. "Why must it?" Her voice trembled. "What if we—"

"*No.*" Sharp — then softening at once. "Linnea. I care for you more than I ought. That's the truth of it. But caring for you means letting you go, so you can have a life that isn't all danger and loss."

Linnea lowered her head, and did not press, and let the fire dance between them a while. When she raised it again her eyes were wet but her voice was level and sure. "I understand why you feel it. I know what the road has taken from you." A breath. "But we're here now, Celenneth. And neither of us knows what tomorrow brings." She leaned closer, trembling but certain. "So let's have what we have — even if it's only a few days. Even if it's only this. I'd sooner regret the having than the not."

Celenneth's guard cracked the rest of the way; Linnea watched it go. "Linnea, I—" And the ranger's voice failed her.

Linnea did not wait for the end of it. She moved closer, slow, deliberate, giving every chance to be turned away — and Celenneth did not turn away. Linnea lifted a hand to her cheek. "Please," she whispered. "Just this once."

And the world went quiet — fire, leaves, the far call of a night-bird all falling back — as she leaned in and put her arms around the ranger. Celenneth went stiff, and then let out a slow breath, and the tension ran out of her; and slowly, as if she had forgotten how, her arms came up and closed around Linnea in return, and she turned her face into Linnea's shoulder and held on like something she was afraid to lose, and all the vulnerability she had spent a lifetime hiding came quietly out of her at last. They stayed so a long time in the dark.

When Celenneth drew back it was only to rest her brow against Linnea's. "I don't know how to do this," she said, so soft it was almost nothing.

Linnea smiled, her hands still on the ranger's shoulders. "We don't have to know it all tonight. Let's just be here. Together."

Celenneth nodded, slow, all her defences finally down, and for this one moment let go of the road and the past and the uncertain way ahead — and in Linnea's arms found a fragile, unlooked-for peace.

Linnea held it a while longer, not ready to let it slip. Then she leaned back, hands light on Celenneth's shoulders, and searched the ranger's firelit face for any sign of retreat — and found none, only a quiet acceptance, wary still, but no longer denial. "Goodnight, Celenneth," she whispered, and before her courage could fail her, she leaned in and kissed her.

It was gentle, almost tentative, and it carried everything she had been holding back. Celenneth froze the briefest instant — and then let herself sink into it, her mouth answering soft, and the world fell all the way away, and there was only the small crackle of the fire and the warmth the two of them made. Linnea deepened it, her fingers finding Celenneth's hair, and felt the ranger's heart going hard against her; and when at last she drew back her cheeks were flushed and her own heart racing and her eyes bright with something between joy and gratitude. Celenneth breathed out slow, her face unreadable and her gaze steady on Linnea's.

Without a word, Linnea drew the cloak over them both and settled into Celenneth's side against the deepening cold. The fire fell to embers; the forest went still. For tonight, at least, they had this — a fragile, undeniable thing, held between them in the wide and uncertain dark.
