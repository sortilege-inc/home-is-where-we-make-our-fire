# Chapter 8: Wolves and Hearts

*Cermië 28, 2965*

The sun came up bright, throwing long shadows down the forest path, and the howl of two nights past rode with them still, a small cold reminder at the back of the mind. Nimroch went steady, his ears working back and forth as though he heard what they could not, and the morning passed without event — but the deeper into the wood they went, the heavier the air seemed to hang, until an uneasy stillness lay over everything.

Past noon she found the first sign: a fresh paw-print pressed deep in the soft earth by the trail. She swung down and crouched over it, and the size and depth of it put a chill in her. "Wolves," she said, rising fast. "Close."

Linnea's voice wavered. "What do we do?"

"Stay by me. If they come, Nimroch will hold — but we may have to fight our way clear."

They did not wait long. A low growl rolled through the trees, and five wolves came out of the shadows, eyes bright with hunger, spreading in a coordinated arc to take the pair between them. Celenneth had her bow up and drawn before the arc could close. She breathed out and loosed, and the shaft took the nearest through the flank with a wet thud; it yelped and fell back, and its pack-mates snarled and checked at the smell of its blood. "They're wary," she said, nocking again. "But they won't quit." Her second arrow put another down deeper in the pack, and while they snapped and milled around their wounded she made the only right call — not to stand and be surrounded, but to run.[^r-wolves]

"*Now!*" She put her heels to Nimroch, and the horse threw himself into a gallop, hooves hammering the forest floor, and the wolves broke into the chase behind. But no wolf of the wild could hold Nimroch's pace over open ground; she lay low along his neck with Linnea clinging hard at her back, and the pack strung out and fell away, the wounded first and then the rest, their snarls thinning into the trees until there was only the wind and the drumming of hooves. She did not slow until the last of them was gone from hearing, and then she drew rein, and the horse snorted and tossed his head but stood easy.

Linnea slid down on trembling legs. "That was — close. Too close."

"We're clear now." Celenneth's face softened a fraction. "You held on well."

They rode on, Nimroch tireless under them, the forest opening into meadows gold with the falling sun, and neither spoke of the wolves, only fixed their eyes on the miles. At dusk she found a sheltered grove by a murmuring stream, and Linnea gathered the kindling with small new efficiencies that Celenneth marked with quiet approval and did not remark on, and by the time the fire caught, some ease had come back into the camp.

After they had eaten she spread her map on a flat stone and traced their line. "Good ground covered. Hold this pace and we're within Radagast's reach in three days — four, if the weather turns."

Linnea leaned to follow her finger. "Do you think we'll meet more trouble?"

"Almost surely. The Shadow reaches far. But careful, and we can slip most of it."

"You seem sure of everything." Linnea's gaze came up. "How do you stay so steady?"

The ghost of a smile. "Years of it. And knowing that doubt slows the hand."

Linnea took that in, the firelight in her eyes. "I'm glad I'm not making this road alone," she said, soft.

The fire burned low, and Celenneth sat by it working her whetstone slow along the blade, Nimroch grazing near, the stream and his soft snorts the only sound. Linnea sat beside her, their talk faded into an easy quiet — and then exhaustion took the younger woman all at once; her head nodded, and before Celenneth could say a word she had leaned into her side and, with a small sigh, settled her head into Celenneth's lap.

Celenneth went still, the sword forgotten in her hands. Linnea's breathing had already gone deep and even, her dark hair spilling across Celenneth's knees, her face — turned up, firelit — gone soft and unguarded and trusting in a way that reached straight past every wall the ranger had spent years building.

Her first instinct was to move her, gently, to put the proper distance back between them. She did not. She sat and looked at the small turn of that sleeping mouth, the plain trust of it, and felt the warmth stir in her chest that she never let herself feel, and hated a little how much she wanted to let it.

Her hand rose toward the dark hair and stopped, and closed into a fist, and drew back. Her mind went to Bree — to Orlen, and the letter she had sent north with her heart half-open in it, and no answer come or likely to come for weeks or more. She had been a fool to wait as long as she had; but she had never once said what she felt, and he had never presumed, and what right had she to expect him to have waited on a hope she had never given him? And even were he waiting — it might be a year before the road carried her back to know it.

And Linnea. She *was* lovely, and there was something in the soft warmth of her against her side that put heat in Celenneth's face — but the woman was grieving, and far from home, and Celenneth was only the one who had happened to be there when the dark came for her. To make anything of that would be a kind of theft.

And past all of it, the plain shape of the thing: when they reached Radagast, Linnea would stay, safe in the wizard's keeping, and Celenneth would go on alone, because that was what she was — a wanderer bound to the road and the solitude it asked of her, no fit place for anyone to set their heart. *Desire is a distraction,* she told herself, jaw tightening, an old hard catechism. *Attachments only make the road heavier.*

Linnea shifted and murmured something lost and settled again, and Celenneth's resolve wavered and held, barely, and she kept the watch long into the night with her eyes on the dark and her heart at war with itself. And as the fire fell to embers she let herself, at the last, one small and fleeting smile — because this at least was hers, this quiet weight and warmth in her lap under the stars, even if it could never be a thing that lasted, even if it could never be more than this.

[^r-wolves]: **Combat / flight** — a five-strong wolf-pack. Two wounded by bow; rather than be surrounded, Celenneth breaks and runs, outpacing the pack on Nimroch.
