# Chapter 8: Wolves and Hearts

*Book 2*

*Cermië 28, 2965*

The sun rose bright and clear, casting long shadows across the forest path as Celenneth and Linnea resumed their journey. The howl they’d heard two nights ago still lingered in their minds, a faint reminder of the dangers that followed them. Nimroch moved steadily, his ears flicking back and forth as though attuned to the unseen. The morning passed uneventfully, but as they ventured deeper into the forest, the air grew heavier, and an uneasy stillness settled over the land.

As noon passed, the first sign of trouble emerged: a fresh paw print in the soft earth near the trail. Celenneth dismounted, crouching to inspect it. The size and depth of the print sent a chill through her. “Wolves,” she muttered, rising quickly. “Close.”

Linnea’s voice wavered. “What do we do?”

“Stay close to me. If they come, Nimroch will hold his ground, but we may have a fight.”

They didn’t have to wait long. Minutes later, a low growl rumbled through the trees. Five wolves emerged from the shadows, their eyes glinting with hunger and malice. Their movements were coordinated, arcing towards the pair.

Celenneth raised her bow with practiced precision. She exhaled steadily, letting the string go slack as her arrow sailed through the air. It buried itself in the side of a wolf with a sickening thud. The creature yelped in pain, staggering briefly before retreating slightly, its packmates snarling in response.

“They’re hesitating,” Celenneth said, her voice firm. “But determined.”

Celenneth nocked another arrow as Nimroch shifted beneath her, sensing the tension in the air. The second shot flew past the first wolf, striking another deeper in the pack in the flank. It howled in pain, stumbling as blood darkened its fur. The pack hesitated, snarling and snapping in response to the wounded member.

“Now!” Celenneth shouted, spurring Nimroch into a gallop. The horse surged forward, hooves pounding against the forest floor as the wolves gave chase, their hunger driving them despite their losses.

Nimroch surged forward, his powerful strides devouring the forest floor as Celenneth leaned low over his neck. The wolves gave chase, their snarls and growls echoing through the trees, but Nimroch’s speed and surefootedness were unmatched. The wounded wolves fell behind quickly, and the healthier members struggled to keep pace.

Linnea clung tightly to Celenneth, her heart pounding in her chest. She glanced back over her shoulder, her breath catching as she saw the pack growing smaller in the distance. “They’re falling behind!” she shouted over the wind.

Celenneth didn’t slow, her sharp gaze focused on the path ahead. Only when the sounds of the wolves faded completely did she pull Nimroch to a halt, the horse snorting and tossing his head but otherwise calm.

“They won’t follow us further,” Celenneth said, her voice steady but firm. She dismounted, scanning the trees for any lingering threats.

Linnea slid down after her, her legs trembling slightly as she found her footing. “That was… close,” she said, her voice shaky. “Too close.”

Celenneth turned to her, her expression softening slightly. “We’re safe now. You did well to hold on.”

After the harrowing chase, Celenneth and Linnea pressed onward. Nimroch’s pace was steady, his strength unfaltering despite the demands of the day. The forest began to thin, giving way to open meadows bathed in the golden light of the setting sun. The tension of the encounter with the wolves lingered, but neither woman spoke of it as they focused on the miles ahead.

As dusk settled over the landscape, Celenneth guided them to a sheltered grove near a murmuring stream.  Linnea helped to gather kindling for the fire, finding small efficiencies. Celenneth noted the change with quiet approval but said nothing, her focus on preparing their modest meal. By the time the fire crackled softly in the clearing, a sense of calm had returned to the camp.

After dinner, Celenneth unfolded her map, spreading it out on a flat stone. She traced their route with a finger, her sharp eyes narrowing as she calculated their progress. “We’ve covered good ground,” she said, her tone even. “If we maintain this pace, we should be well within Radagast’s reach in three days—four if the weather turns.”

Linnea leaned closer, her gaze following Celenneth’s finger on the map. “Do you think we’ll encounter more trouble?”

“Almost certainly,” Celenneth replied, glancing at her companion. “The Shadow spreads far. But if we’re cautious, we can avoid most.”

Linnea nodded, her expression thoughtful. “You seem so sure of everything. How do you stay so… steady?”

Celenneth’s lips curved into the faintest smile. “Years of practice. And remembering that doubt slows the hand.”

Linnea absorbed the words, her admiration for the ranger growing with each passing day. The firelight reflected in her eyes as she spoke softly, “I’m glad I’m not making this journey alone.”

The fire burned low, casting long, flickering shadows across the grove. Celenneth sat quietly by its warmth, maintaining her sword with slow, deliberate movements. Nimroch grazed nearby, his soft snorts and the rustle of the stream providing a soothing backdrop to the stillness of the night.

Linnea sat beside her, their conversation having faded into a companionable silence. Exhaustion finally overtook the younger woman, her head nodding slightly as she fought to stay awake. Before Celenneth could say anything, Linnea shifted, leaning against her side, and then, with a soft sigh, settled her head into Celenneth’s lap.

Celenneth froze for a moment, her hand still on the blade of her sword. She glanced down at Linnea, whose breathing had already deepened into the soft rhythm of sleep. Her dark hair fanned out over Celenneth’s lap, and her face, lit faintly by the firelight, seemed at peace.

Celenneth’s first instinct was to move, to gently shift Linnea away and put space between them. But something held her there. She watched Linnea’s face, the small turn of the mouth, the quiet trust it conveyed. A warmth stirred in her chest, a feeling she hadn’t allowed herself.

Her hand hovered over Linnea’s hair for a moment before she clenched it into a fist, pulling it back. Her mind raced, her thoughts drifting to Bree—to Orlen and her letter. She had been such a fool to wait as long as she had. But she had never intimated, and he had never presumed; what right had she to expect him to reciprocate? And even if he did; it could be weeks, or months, or years before she was back in Bree.

And Linnea? She *was* beautiful, and there was something tender and soft about her body against her that made her flush. But she was grieving, and far from home. Celenneth was just the person who happened to be there.

And then there was the reality of their journey. Once they reached Radagast, Linnea would stay, and Celenneth would leave. It was the way it had to be. She was a wanderer, a ranger, bound to the road and the solitude it required. Linnea would be safe in the wizard’s care, far from the dangers Celenneth would inevitably face.

Celenneth exhaled slowly, forcing herself to focus. *Desire is a distraction*, she reminded herself, her jaw tightening. *And attachments only make the road harder*.

Linnea shifted slightly in her sleep, murmuring something unintelligible before settling again. Celenneth’s resolve wavered, but she remained still. She stayed awake long into the night, her sharp eyes scanning the shadows while her heart wrestled with the emotions she couldn’t quite suppress.

As the fire burned down to embers, Celenneth finally allowed herself a small, fleeting smile. This was a moment she would hold onto, even if it could never be more than this.
