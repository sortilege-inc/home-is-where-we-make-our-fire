# Chapter 10: New Realities

*Book 2*

*Cermië 30, 2965*

The first light of dawn crept through the trees, painting the grove in gold and green. The fire had burned out, leaving only faint wisps of smoke curling into the cool air. Celenneth woke early, her sharp instincts drawing her from sleep at the first birdcall.

But this morning, something was different.

Linnea was still nestled against her side, the shared cloak draped loosely over them. Her breathing was soft and even, her expression peaceful. Celenneth’s gaze lingered on her, the vulnerability of the younger woman tugging at something deep within her.

Memories of the night before flooded back: the kiss, the warmth of Linnea’s embrace, the words they had shared. Celenneth’s chest tightened. She felt a strange mix of emotions—an ache of longing, a flicker of guilt, and a hesitant, fragile hope she hadn’t allowed herself to feel in years.

Carefully, so as not to wake Linnea, Celenneth shifted and sat up, wrapping the cloak back around her. She moved to tend to Nimroch, her hands steady despite the storm of thoughts within her. *This wasn’t part of the plan*.

Linnea stirred not long after, her eyes fluttering open to see Celenneth crouched by the fire pit, quietly stoking the embers to life. For a moment, she simply watched the ranger, her heart swelling with warmth and uncertainty.

“Good morning,” she said softly, sitting up and pulling the cloak around her shoulders.

Celenneth glanced over, her expression calm and unreadable. “Morning,” she replied, her voice steady. “I hope you slept well.”

Linnea smiled faintly. “I did. Better than I have in a long time.” She hesitated, her gaze searching Celenneth’s face. “And you?”

Celenneth paused, her hand hovering over the fire. “Well enough.”

Linnea bit her lip, uncertain how to bridge the gap between the closeness of the night before and the quiet distance of the morning. “About last night…” she began, her voice hesitant.

Celenneth turned fully to face her, her sharp eyes softening slightly. “You don’t need to apologize, if that’s what you’re thinking.”

Linnea shook her head quickly. “No, I wasn’t going to. I just… I don’t want you to feel like I’m asking for something you can’t give.”

Celenneth’s gaze lingered on her, the firelight flickering between them. “Linnea,” she said softly, her tone carrying more weight than before. “What happened last night… It’s not something I regret. But this road, this life—it’s unforgiving. I’ve learned that it takes more than most people can tolerate.”

Linnea moved closer, her voice firm despite the nerves tightening in her chest. “I know it’s not easy. I know you have doubts. But whatever time we have—whether it’s days or years—I want to share it with you.”

Celenneth exhaled slowly, her jaw tightening as she wrestled with her thoughts. Finally, she nodded, her voice taut. “Then let’s take it one step at a time. The road ahead will tell us what we need to know.”

They packed their camp in silence, but it wasn’t awkward. Celenneth moved with her usual efficiency, but her glances toward Linnea carried a quiet warmth that hadn’t been there before.

The day’s travel was unexpectedly smooth. The weather remained clear, and the forest paths seemed almost welcoming, with no sign of danger or shadow. Celenneth and Linnea moved at a steady pace, the tension of the previous days easing as they grew more attuned to each other’s presence.

Nimroch’s stride was confident and sure, carrying them easily over uneven terrain. Even the distant calls of birds and the rustle of leaves in the breeze felt peaceful rather than foreboding.

The night was calm, the stars shining brightly above as the campfire burned down to faint embers. Celenneth lay beneath the shared cloak, her breathing deep and steady as sleep overtook her. But the peace of the night did not extend to her dreams.

In her mind, she stood alone in a vast, dark forest. The trees loomed impossibly tall, their gnarled branches reaching out like claws against a blood-red sky. The air was thick with the stench of decay, and the distant sound of wolves howling pierced the oppressive silence.

Celenneth turned, her sword drawn, but the weight of the blade felt unnatural, as if her strength were leaving her. A figure emerged from the shadows—at first indistinct, then horrifyingly familiar. It was Linnea, her face pale and lifeless, her eyes wide with fear. She reached out a trembling hand, her voice a faint whisper. “Why didn’t you save me?”

Before Celenneth could respond, the ground beneath her feet crumbled, and she fell into an endless void. The screams of wolves echoed around her, and then a shadow darker than the abyss itself enveloped her. The last thing she saw was Linnea’s face fading into the darkness, her expression frozen in terror.

Celenneth woke with a start, her body tense and drenched in sweat. Her breathing came in ragged gasps, her hand instinctively reaching for the hilt of her sword. The faint glow of the dying fire illuminated the camp, and she realized she was safe—but the lingering dread from the dream clung to her like a shroud.

“Celenneth?” Linnea’s soft voice broke through the darkness. She was sitting up beside her, her eyes wide with concern. “What’s wrong? Did something happen?”

Celenneth hesitated, her hands trembling as she tried to push the nightmare from her mind. “It’s… nothing,” she said, her voice rough. “Just a dream.”

Linnea frowned, clearly unconvinced. “That didn’t look like nothing,” she said gently, scooting closer. “You’re shaking.”

Linnea reached out, placing a tentative hand on Celenneth’s arm. “You don’t have to face it alone,” she said softly. “Whatever it was, I’m here.”

For a moment, Celenneth wanted to dismiss her, to retreat behind the walls she had built to protect herself. But the warmth of Linnea’s touch, the quiet sincerity in her voice, broke through her defenses. She let out a shaky breath, her gaze dropping to the ground.

“It was… you,” Celenneth admitted after a moment, her voice barely above a whisper. “I dreamed I lost you. That I couldn’t save you.”

Linnea’s eyes softened, and she gently squeezed Celenneth’s arm. “You won’t lose me,” she said firmly. “We’ve made it this far together, and I trust you with my life.”

Celenneth looked at her, the vulnerability in her eyes stark against her usual stoicism. “I’ve lost people before,” she said quietly. “Too many. And the thought of losing you…”

“You won’t,” Linnea interrupted, her voice steady. “It's not allowed.”

She shifted closer, wrapping her arms around Celenneth in a protective embrace. “Whatever’s ahead, we’ll face it together,” she said softly. “But for now, you’re safe. We’re safe.”

Celenneth hesitated for a moment before leaning into Linnea’s embrace, allowing herself to take comfort in the younger woman’s warmth and steady presence. The fear from the nightmare didn’t fade entirely, but it grew quieter, less overwhelming.

Celenneth closed her eyes, her breathing evening out as sleep returned—not entirely peaceful, but less haunted. For now, at least, the darkness had been held at bay.
