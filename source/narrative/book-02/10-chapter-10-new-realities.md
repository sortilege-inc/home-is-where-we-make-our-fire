# Chapter 10: New Realities

*Cermië 30, 2965*

Dawn came gold and green through the grove, the fire down to a thread of smoke, and Celenneth woke at the first birdcall as she always did — but this morning something was different, and she knew it before she was fully awake.

Linnea was still curled against her side under the shared cloak, breathing soft and even, her face at peace. Celenneth lay and looked at her, and the night came back whole — the kiss, the warmth of her, the things they had said — and her chest drew tight around a tangle she had not felt in years: an ache of wanting, a thread of guilt, and under them both a small, frightening, fragile hope. Careful not to wake her, she eased up and tucked the cloak back around the sleeping woman and went to see to Nimroch, her hands steady though her mind was anything but. *This was not the plan,* she thought, and did not know whether she meant it as reproach or wonder.

Linnea woke to find her crouched at the fire-pit coaxing the embers back, and watched her a moment before she spoke. "Good morning."

"Morning." Celenneth glanced over, calm. "You slept well, I hope."

"Better than I have in a long while." A pause; her eyes searched the ranger's face. "And you?"

"Well enough."

Linnea worried her lip, uncertain how to cross the distance between the closeness of the dark and the daylight quiet. "About last night—"

"You've nothing to be sorry for, if that's your worry."

"No — I wasn't going to be. I only don't want you feeling I'm asking for something you can't give."

Celenneth turned fully to her, and let the guard down enough for it to show. "Linnea. What happened last night — I don't regret it. But this road is unforgiving. It asks more than most people can carry."

Linnea came closer, her voice firm under the nerves. "I know it's not easy. I know you have your doubts. But whatever we're given — days or years — I want to spend it with you."

Celenneth breathed out slow, her jaw working, and at last she nodded, taut. "Then one step at a time. The road ahead will tell us what we need to know."

They broke camp in a silence that was not awkward, and if Celenneth moved with her usual economy, her glances toward Linnea carried a warmth that had not been in them before.

The day was kind, unexpectedly — the weather held clear, the forest paths ran open and almost welcoming, no shadow on any of them, and the two of them rode easy and grew more attuned to each other with every mile, the fear of the past days loosening its grip. Nimroch went sure over the broken ground, and even the far birdcalls and the wind in the leaves seemed peaceable rather than a warning. It was, Celenneth thought, the first day in a long time she had not spent braced for the next blow. She should have known better than to trust it.

The night was calm, the stars out bright, and she lay under the shared cloak and let sleep take her deep. But the peace did not follow her down into it.

She stood alone in a grey drowned country under no sky at all, the ground sucking cold at her boots, everywhere the reek of standing water and rot. Her sword was in her hand but it weighed like an anchor, and her strength was draining out of her arm the way blood drains from a wound. A shape came toward her out of the murk — and it was Linnea. But Linnea gone grey and still, her lips blue, her eyes wide and lifeless, and she reached out one trembling hand and said, in a small voice from very far away, *Why didn't you save me?* Celenneth tried to answer and could not; the ground gave way beneath her and she was falling, and Linnea's dead face receded up and away into the dark, the terror frozen on it, and a shadow blacker than the fall closed over her and swallowed the last of the light.[^s-linnea]

She woke with a jolt, drenched and rigid, her breath coming in ragged pulls and her hand already on her sword. The dying fire, the quiet camp, Linnea safe an arm's length away — she took it in and knew herself awake, but the dread of the thing clung to her like wet cloth and would not shake loose.

"Celenneth?" Linnea's voice out of the dark; she was sitting up, eyes wide. "What is it? What happened?"

"It's — nothing." Her own voice came out rough. "A dream."

Linnea frowned, not fooled. "That wasn't nothing. You're shaking." She scooted near and set a tentative hand on Celenneth's arm. "You don't have to carry it alone. Whatever it was — I'm here."

Every old instinct told Celenneth to wave her off, to step back behind the walls that had kept her whole this long. But the warmth of that hand, and the plain sincerity in the voice, got past them. She let out a shaking breath and dropped her eyes.

"It was you," she admitted, barely a whisper. "I dreamed I lost you. That I couldn't save you."

Linnea's face softened, and she pressed Celenneth's arm. "You won't lose me. We've come this far together, and I'd trust you with my life."

Celenneth looked at her, the vulnerability stark in her eyes against all her usual stone. "I've lost people. Too many. And the thought of losing you—"

"You won't." Fierce and steady, young and certain. "I won't allow it." And she shifted close and put her arms around the ranger, protective, holding her together. "Whatever's ahead, we face it together. But for now — you're safe. We're both safe."

Celenneth hesitated, and then let herself lean into the embrace and take what comfort was in it — the younger woman's warmth, her plain unshaken presence — and the fear of the dream did not leave her, but it grew quieter, less able to fill the whole of the dark. She closed her eyes, and her breathing came back to even, and sleep took her again — not wholly at peace, but no longer hunted. For tonight, at least, the darkness was held off; and she was not, for the first time in longer than she could remember, holding it off alone.

[^s-linnea]: The **Shadow** finds Celenneth's new fear and turns it against her — the dream of Linnea dead, unsaved. Her deepening attachment is also her new vulnerability: the road's oldest lesson, come round again.
