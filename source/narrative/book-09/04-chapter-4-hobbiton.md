# Chapter 4: Hobbiton

*Cermië 9, 2969*

Morning found Linnea awake long before dawn, the night's restless silence still on her. If rest would not come, then action would; she would leave this little home at least a bit warmer than she'd found it. She crept to the small kitchen, coaxed the banked embers back to life, and rummaged the cupboards — flour, honey, oats, fresh eggs, dried herbs that smelled of sunny hillsides. Simple Hobbit fare, honest and humble, and the making of it steadied her: the crackle of the hearth, the sizzle of eggs, the scrape of a spoon in a bowl, and the scents deepening through the cottage — herbs, oatcakes, warm bread, stewed apples. Such an ordinary thing, and yet it fortified her trembling spirit.

When Marigold shuffled in and saw the table laid — herb-crusted eggs, honey-glistened oatcakes, steaming stewed apples — she pressed a hand to her mouth. "My goodness! You've been up and about! If this isn't a treat." Linnea managed a modest smile, her voice quieter and plainer than the polished courtesy of before. "It's not much. But I hope it starts your morning right. I'm grateful you let me rest here."

"Not much? Nonsense, my dear. There's a kindness in you that's hard to come by these days." Marigold patted her arm, and when Gandalf appeared, sniffing the air with a grin — "I thought I smelled hope" — the old wizard's keen eyes softened at the sight of her, quiet and cooking breakfast for a Hobbit family, as if it were a sign that not all paths led to darkness.

They ate, and Linnea helped tidy, and Marigold wrapped bread and cheese for the road and dabbed at a tear. "Your hospitality meant a great deal," Linnea told her, standing straighter. "I'll always remember this welcome."

⁂

They rode out under a pale morning sky, and for a while let the Shire's gentle scenery unfold — tidy farms, hedgerows of summer flowers, soft lanes winding toward distant meadows. But the charm nagged at Linnea, a creeping worry that would not settle, and on a quiet stretch of the East Road she spoke. "Gandalf — that charm I took off the intruder. Something's wrong with it. I can feel it even when I'm not holding it. Like it's alive, somehow. Or at least that it remembers."

Gandalf slowed his horse and put out a hand. "Show me." Linnea worked the charm free of her pack, still wrapped in cloth; her fingers trembled, as if the thing resisted her even now. He turned it over — the twisted, vine-clawed hand, the ash-dark wood — and muttered words in an ancient tongue, tracing the air with his staff. A shimmer of silver-white light flickered round him, and the carved lines of the charm flared faint and red, like a creature caught off guard and thrashing in its captor's hold. With a final decisive word the glow vanished, and the charm went dull and cold in his hand.

He wrapped it again and returned it. "This is no idle trinket. It was made with foul intent, bound to old memories that linger. A thing like this doesn't only compel the flesh — it tries to reach into the bearer's mind. To influence. To twist. Keep it covered. Keep it at a distance. And whatever you do, don't sleep with it near you."

Linnea swallowed. "Celenneth found something like it," she said quietly. "The cursed blade, in Fangorn. If that's so — could this be calling to hers, too? Could it reach her?"

Gandalf's face grew troubled, and his silence hung heavy. "If Celenneth carries a similar relic, then yes — there may be a connection. Dark objects call to one another; they carry echoes of their pasts. If you feel this one's pull, it may reach for hers as well. You must be vigilant, Linnea. For both your sakes."

Her heart sank. "How do I keep it from sinking in? How do I shield myself — and her?"

He guided his horse closer, lowering his voice. "There's no simple cure. But there are ways to weaken its hold. Keep it wrapped. Don't dwell on it — the shadow loves attention. Keep your mind grounded: remember who you are, and who you love. Surround yourself with what gives you strength and light — friends, purpose, laughter. And if Celenneth is struggling too, find her, and stand together. You'll need each other's hearts to stay whole."

Linnea drew a steadying breath. "I'll not handle it carelessly. I'll find Celenneth, and we'll stand against whatever this is."

"Good," Gandalf said. "Even in dark times, resolve and kindness matter. They can't break every curse — but they can keep you from breaking yourself."[^r-charm-lore]

⁂

They rode on through the summer morning, and Gandalf hummed an old tune, wistful and hopeful at once; shy at first, Linnea tried to hum along, and then to sing a few verses of it — travelers passing through twilight, never losing hope. When they finished she found herself calmer, the tension in her chest eased, thinking of Brynja, who would love a tune like this.

But approaching Hobbiton, the air shifted. The hobbit-holes still looked cozy, gardens tidy, smoke rising — yet the Hobbits themselves seemed restless and afraid, peeking from windows and vanishing. An older woman stacking firewood bolted indoors when Linnea asked if something was wrong. "They're scared of something," Linnea told Gandalf. "The Shire shouldn't feel like this."

"The Shire is changing," he said, frowning. "Or something is pressing in on it. Let's not linger. We've business with Bilbo Baggins — he may know more."

In the narrow lanes there were small signs of trouble — an overturned cart, bruised fruit spilled and abandoned. A Hobbit named Tom Brockhouse, eased by her gentle manner, told her low: "Folk have gone missing along the road. Goods vanish. Strangers lurking at night. Nobody feels safe." He nodded toward Bag End. "If you're here for Mr. Baggins, best get going. Rumor is he's been expecting someone."

As they neared Bag End's hill, Linnea dismounted and read the ground: footprints too large for Hobbits near the garden beds, thin scratches on the gate latch as though tested by a blade. A glint in the grass caught her eye, and she knelt and drew out a small iron clasp engraved with a wolf's head — no Hobbit trinket. She slipped it into her pouch, unsettled.[^t-clasp]

A rustle from beyond the hedgerow set her heart racing — but no one came, only the creak of the round green door as it opened. "Gandalf? Is that you?"

"Bilbo, old friend," Gandalf called, leaning his staff by the step. "It's me."

Bilbo Baggins stood in the doorway, older and more pensive than Linnea had imagined, a wary smile on him. His eyes flicked to her. "And you've brought a friend, I see."

"This is Linnea," Gandalf said, calm and warm. "We're traveling through, and hoped for counsel — and a cup of tea, if you're hospitable."

Bilbo puffed his pipe, weighing them. "Come in, then. But mind — if you've brought trouble, I'll have the kettle singing and my doors locked by sundown."

A dry laugh escaped Linnea. "We're not here to cause problems," she said, plainer now. "Only questions, and a road that won't be easier without some honest talk."

"All right." Bilbo stepped aside. "Come in. We'll see if we can't shed light on whatever darkness you're dragging behind you." And Linnea followed Gandalf into the lantern-warm hush of Bag End, the scent of old books and pipeweed and polished wood closing round her, and let out a quiet breath. Inside these round walls, perhaps, they would find some answers.

[^r-charm-lore]: **Lore** (Gandalf) — Gandalf examines Linnea's cursed **charm** and confirms its nature: a made thing that reaches into the bearer's mind to influence and enslave.
