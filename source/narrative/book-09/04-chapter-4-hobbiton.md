# Chapter 4: Hobbiton

*Book 9*

*Cermië 9, 2969*

The morning found Linnea awake long before dawn, the night’s restless silence still fresh in her mind. She rose slowly, wrapping a shawl around her shoulders against the lingering chill. The dim interior of the Bramblefoot cottage felt safe, if fragile, like a bubble of calm in a world that had grown uncertain. She hadn’t managed much sleep—her dreams had been too tangled, her heart too heavy. Still, she’d decided something while lying awake: if rest would not come, then action would. She would leave this little home at least a bit warmer than she’d found it.

A few soft footsteps carried her from the guest nook down the short hall to the small, cozy kitchen. The hearth held the last red glow of yesterday’s fire. Bending low, she coaxed the embers back to life, gently feeding them kindling, then a log. Sparks danced upward, and soon the glow deepened, radiating comfort through the snug kitchen.

Linnea rummaged quietly through the cupboards and small cellar. She found flour, honey, oats, a clutch of fresh eggs, and dried herbs that smelled of sunny hillsides. Her fingers lingered on each item. Simple Hobbit fare—humble and honest. It made her think of better days, days when she hadn’t worried about cursed blades or old shadows. She separated the eggs into a bowl, whisking them lightly with a pinch of fresh herbs, imagining how the flavors would mingle. She ground oats and folded them into a batter, adding a drizzle of honey that caught the torchlight in sweet amber ribbons. Sliced apples, a dusting of spice—everything came together easily, each step a small balm on her uneasy mind.

Soon, gentle sounds filled the kitchen: the crackle of the hearth, the sizzle of eggs in the pan, the quiet scrape of a wooden spoon against a mixing bowl. The scents deepened and expanded, drifting through the cottage: fragrant herbs, baking oatcakes, warm bread, and stewed fruit. Such a normal, homey thing—preparing breakfast—yet it steadied her trembling spirit. She closed her eyes, just for a moment, and let that warmth fortify her.

As the first hints of dawn’s light filtered through the windows, Linnea arranged everything on the small table. She didn’t do it as a grand gesture, just a simple thank-you to the Bramblefoots for their kindness. They had let her into their world, so it felt only right to leave it brightened.

Soon enough, gentle footsteps sounded in the hallway. Marigold Bramblefoot shuffled in, rubbing the sleep from her eyes. When she noticed the feast laid out—herb-crusted eggs frying crisp around the edges, tender oatcakes glistening with honey, stewed apples steaming in a bowl—she pressed a hand to her mouth in surprise.

“My goodness!” Marigold gasped, cheeks flushing pink. “You’ve been up and about, haven’t you? Dear me, if this isn’t a treat!” The Hobbit matron looked truly pleased, and Linnea felt a small spark of satisfaction. This was what she’d hoped for: to leave something good behind.

Linnea managed a modest smile. “It’s not much,” she said, her voice quieter now, less polished than before. She’d grown weary of formality—perhaps the road and the weight of her quest had chipped away at that. “But I hope it starts your morning off right. I’m grateful you let me rest here.”

Marigold patted her arm. “Not much? Nonsense, my dear. This is a blessing—so much warmth and care. Travelers passing through usually keep to themselves, but you’ve brought more than breakfast. There’s a kindness in you that’s hard to come by these days.”

Linnea just nodded, letting Marigold’s words wash over her. Her throat tightened, but she tried not to show it. She poured hot tea, arranged cups on the table. She needed this moment of peace. Outside, the world was wide and uncertain, but inside the Bramblefoot cottage, it was safe and bright.

It wasn’t long before Gandalf appeared, tall and stooped slightly beneath the low Hobbit ceiling. He sniffed the air, grinning as if he’d caught the scent of a long-lost memory. “I thought I smelled hope,” he said lightly, settling beside the hearth. He gave Linnea a thoughtful glance, and for a moment, his keen eyes softened. Seeing her like this—quiet, cooking breakfast for a Hobbit family—felt like a sign that maybe not all paths led to darkness.

Marigold fussed over him, bringing extra cushions, and Linnea took a seat, finally tasting her own handiwork. The bread was warm, the honey sweet, the eggs perfectly spiced. It was simple fare that somehow tasted like a promise that life could still be good. She caught herself exhaling deeply, loosening a knot in her chest.

After they ate, Linnea rose to help tidy up. She didn’t feel the need to say much—her smile and presence were enough. Marigold wrapped some bread and cheese for their journey, insisting they take provisions with them. Linnea thanked her with genuine warmth this time, standing a bit straighter. “Your hospitality meant a lot,” she said, voice softer, more natural. “I’ll always remember this welcome.”

“Then we’ll call it even,” Marigold replied. She dabbed at a tear in her eye, as if Linnea’s kindness reminded her that good hearts still passed along these roads.

Soon enough, Linnea and Gandalf stepped out into the pale morning. The sunlight stretched lazily over the rolling green hills, and the Shire looked as innocent as a painting. Linnea adjusted the straps of Eorlin’s bridle, noting that her mare seemed rested and eager to move on. Gandalf swung up onto his horse with practiced ease. A quiet peace settled over them as they rode away from the Bramblefoot home. Linnea cast one last glance over her shoulder at Marigold, who waved from the doorstep, face lit with hope.

They rode at a comfortable pace for a while, letting the Shire’s gentle scenery unfold: tidy farms, hedgerows brimming with spring flowers, soft dirt lanes winding toward distant meadows. But Linnea’s mind drifted. She couldn’t stop thinking about the dagger she’d found. Something about it pulled at her, a subtle, creeping worry that kept tugging at the back of her mind. She decided to say something—if not now, then when?

Waiting until they were on a quiet stretch of the East Road, she finally spoke up. “Gandalf,” she said, “that dagger I found... something’s off about it.” She spoke simply, less like a formal student and more like someone who just needed to get the words out. “I don’t know how to describe it. It’s like I can feel it... even when I’m not holding it. I can’t shake this feeling that it’s alive, in some way—or at least that it remembers.”

Gandalf’s horse slowed, and he reached out a hand. “Show me.”

Linnea wrestled the dagger free from her pack, careful to keep it wrapped in cloth. She felt a slight tremor in her fingers, as if the blade resisted her even now. Handing it to Gandalf, she watched him narrow his eyes, mutter quiet words under his breath, and trace the air with his staff. A shimmer of silver-white light flickered around him as he examined it. The dagger’s dark metal glinted, runes flaring faint red in protest.

It reminded her of a creature caught off guard, thrashing silently in its captor’s hold. Gandalf’s voice rose in a firm, ancient tongue. With a final decisive word, the glow vanished, and the dagger went dull and cold.

Gandalf handed it back, wrapping it carefully. “This is no ordinary knife,” he said. “It was crafted with foul intent, bound to old memories that linger. Such a weapon doesn’t just cut flesh—it tries to cut into the bearer’s mind. It wants to influence, to twist. You must keep it covered, keep it at a distance. And whatever you do, don’t sleep with it near you.”

Linnea swallowed hard. “Celenneth found something similar,” she said quietly. “If that’s the case, could it be calling to her, too? Could it hurt her if she still carries something like it?”

Gandalf’s face grew troubled, and his silence felt heavy. “If Celenneth found a similar relic, then yes—there might be a connection,” he said slowly. “Dark objects call out to each other. They carry echoes of their pasts. If you feel its influence, it might also reach for her. You must be vigilant, Linnea. For both your sakes.”

Linnea’s heart sank. Celenneth was dear to her, and the thought of both of them tangled in this darkness weighed heavily. “How do I keep it from... from sinking in?” she asked. “How do I shield myself and her?”

Gandalf guided his horse closer, lowering his voice. “There’s no simple cure. But there are ways to weaken its hold. Keep it wrapped. Don’t speak its name or dwell on it if you can help it—the shadow loves attention. Keep your mind grounded: remember who you are, and who you care about. Surround yourself with what gives you strength and light. Friends, purpose, laughter—these things help. If Celenneth is struggling too, find her and stand together. You’ll need each other’s hearts to stay whole.”

Linnea nodded. She took a steadying breath. “I’ll do my best,” she said. “I won’t handle it carelessly. I’ll find Celenneth, and we’ll stand against whatever this is.”

Gandalf nodded, satisfied for now. “Good. Remember, even in dark times, resolve and kindness matter. They can’t break every curse, but they can keep you from breaking yourself.”

They continued riding, the morning sun climbing higher, spreading pale gold light over the fields. Eventually, Gandalf began humming an old tune. He didn’t name it, but Linnea let the melody wash over her. It sounded at once wistful and hopeful, reminding her of old stories, old paths. Soon, curiosity got the better of her, and she tried to hum along. She stumbled at first, feeling shy. Her voice, less formal now, was still clear and earnest. Gandalf seemed pleased by her effort. Encouraged, Linnea sang a few verses as he listened, smiling slightly.

The song told of travelers passing through twilight, never losing hope, holding on through long roads and fading stars. She pictured Celenneth, Brynja, old forests, wide rivers, and distant horizons. When they finished, Linnea found herself calmer. The tension in her chest had eased. She thought of Brynja, the child she knew would love a tune like this. Maybe one day Brynja would sing it too. Gandalf seemed to understand her thoughts without her needing to say more.

They rode onward through a gentle afternoon, letting silence and birdsong be their companions. The East Road spread out ahead, and Linnea marveled at how peaceful it seemed. But the Shire’s quiet fields could hide many secrets. As the day stretched on, the green hills and distant hedgerows began to look different—familiar but somehow watchful.

Approaching Hobbiton, Linnea felt a subtle shift in the air. Something was off. It wasn’t obvious; the hobbit-holes still looked cozy, their gardens tidy, smoke rising from chimneys. But the Hobbits themselves—those she could glimpse—seemed restless or afraid. A few peeked out of windows, then quickly disappeared. One older Hobbit woman stacking firewood avoided Linnea’s eyes entirely, bolting indoors as soon as Linnea asked if something was wrong.

Linnea returned to Gandalf, tension gathering between her shoulder blades. “They’re scared of something,” she said quietly. “They won’t say what. It’s not right. The Shire shouldn’t feel like this.”

Gandalf frowned. “The Shire is changing. Or perhaps something is pressing in on it,” he said. “Let’s not linger. We have business with Bilbo Baggins—he might know more.”

They guided their mounts through the narrow lanes. Linnea noticed small signs of trouble: an overturned cart in the market square, bruised fruit spilling onto the ground as if abandoned in a hurry. When she approached a group of Hobbits near the cart, she used a gentler, friendlier tone than before. “I’m sorry to bother you,” she said, careful not to sound too stiff. “My name’s Linnea. We’re just passing through and mean no harm. Do you know what’s going on?”

One Hobbit, Tom Brockhouse, stepped forward, eyeing her suspiciously at first. Her courtesy seemed to ease him. “Can’t say exactly,” he replied, voice low. “Folk have gone missing along the road. Goods vanish, too. Strangers lurking at night. Folks don’t feel safe, you see?”

Linnea nodded, lips pressed thin. “I’m sorry. We’ll be on our way soon.”

Tom glanced toward Bag End. “If you’re here for Mr. Baggins, best get going. Rumor is he’s been expecting someone.”

With a quiet thank you, Linnea returned to Gandalf, relaying what she’d heard. “They’re nervous,” she said. “I don’t like the sound of strangers lurking about. Doesn’t feel random.”

Gandalf urged them onward. “Then let’s keep moving.” His voice had a tense edge. The idea of hidden watchers, old shadows encroaching on the Shire—none of this sat well with him.

The late afternoon sun dipped low. Hobbiton’s neat gardens and round doors took on warm hues, yet the silence remained uneasy. As they neared Bag End’s familiar hill, Linnea dismounted, studying the ground. Soft earth near the garden beds bore footprints too large for Hobbits. The gate latch had thin scratches, as though tested by someone’s blade. A faint glint in the grass caught her eye, and she knelt, retrieving a small iron clasp engraved with a wolf’s head. Definitely not a Hobbit’s trinket. She slipped it into her pouch, unsettled.

A rustling sound from beyond the hedgerow set her heart racing. She straightened, ready for trouble, but no one emerged. There was just the quiet creaking of the round green door as it opened. She heard a familiar voice, lined with age and curiosity: “Gandalf? Is that you?”

Gandalf stepped up, leaning his staff against the step. “Bilbo, old friend,” he called lightly. “It’s me, yes.” Linnea stayed close, scanning the shadows one last time. Whoever had been watching was either gone or hiding well.

Bilbo Baggins stood in the doorway. He looked older and more pensive than Linnea had imagined from Gandalf’s stories. He wore a wary smile, one that said he wasn’t entirely sure if these visitors brought good tidings or trouble. His eyes flicked to Linnea. “And you’ve brought a friend, I see. Care to explain?”

Before Linnea could say anything, Gandalf stepped in. “This is Linnea,” he said, tone calm but warm. “We’re traveling through and hoped to find a bit of counsel. Perhaps a quiet place to think—and maybe a cup of tea, if you’re feeling hospitable.”

Bilbo puffed his pipe, observing them both. “Well, come in then,” he said gruffly, though not unkindly. “But mind you, if you’ve brought trouble with you, I’ll have the kettle singing in no time—and lock my doors by sundown!”

A dry laugh escaped Linnea. She stepped forward, adjusting her cloak. “I promise, we’re not here to cause problems,” she said, voice simpler, more grounded than her earlier politeness. “We’ve just got questions and a road that won’t be any easier without some honest talk.”

Bilbo’s eyes narrowed thoughtfully. “All right, come in,” he repeated, stepping aside. “And bring that wizard of yours. We’ll see if we can shed some light on whatever darkness you’re dragging behind you.”

Linnea followed Gandalf inside, feeling the warmth of Bag End’s lantern light greet her. A scent of old books, pipeweed, and polished wood drifted through the entrance hall. As the door clicked shut behind them, she let out a quiet breath. Inside these round walls, they might find answers—or at least more pieces of the puzzle.

Outside, the evening sky deepened. Shadows stretched across Hobbiton’s gentle hills, hiding secrets. The world beyond Bag End’s threshold remained uncertain, but Linnea carried a resolve inside her now—firm, human, and quietly fierce. She had left one Hobbit home warmer than she’d found it, and perhaps here at Bilbo’s, they could rekindle some light that would help guide her and Gandalf forward on their long road ahead.
