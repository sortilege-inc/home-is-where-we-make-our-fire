# Chapter 8: Orlen and Maren in Bree

*Cermië 14, 2969*

Linnea left the Marish with a determined heart and a wary eye, the memory of the willow-shadowed men and the strange relic she now carried heavy in her thoughts. But she fixed on the road ahead, guiding Eorlin south-east toward the Angle near Bree, where Celenneth and Brynja waited — and that thought alone urged her on.

The Shire's farmland faded into open country, the summer air dusty and sweet with distant flowers. With the Wyrmscale Sigil hidden in her pack and the whispers of the marsh still fresh, she kept alert; if the men had followed, she saw no sign. Still she pressed on at a swift pace, wanting the Shire's borderlands behind her before dark. The sigil, wrapped in cloth, gave off a faint old warmth, like a distant ember, and Gandalf's counsel to be wary echoed in her — but worry would not help her now, so she rode.

Somewhere before midnight her weariness grew too great, and though reason told her to stop she was stubborn and pressed on until first grey light, when she led Eorlin off the road to a scrubby stand of trees, tied her, and slept at once against a trunk. She woke to late-morning warmth, stiff but clear-headed; no one had troubled her. After a check of her gear — and the relic — she rode on.

The land grew familiar as the day wore on: a leaning oak by a stream, a cluster of white boundary-stones. This close to Bree the roads felt known, and memories of her childhood tugged at her — market smells, distant laughter, the comfort of a place where people knew your name. By late afternoon she reached Bree's outskirts and passed under the barely-curious eye of the gatekeeper, just another tired traveler come home from far places.

There was someone in Bree she meant to see. Orlen and Maren — an old thread. She remembered Maren from childhood, a kind woman who had known her parents in passing; and Orlen was someone Celenneth had once admired, perhaps even loved from a distance, though nothing had come of it. Celenneth had written him once, and he had replied, and there it had rested. Linnea knocked at the modest house on the quiet street, and after a pause Orlen opened the door — older than she'd imagined, lines at the corners of his eyes, a seriousness that spoke of old regrets. He did not know her.

"Can I help you?"

"I'm Linnea," she said quietly. "Celenneth's partner."

He went still, struggling to place the name, and then surprise and something older crossed his face, and he stepped back to let her in. The house was warm with stew and baking bread. Maren emerged from a back room, puzzled until Linnea named herself again. "Linnea — I remember you as a child," she said, warmer than Orlen's cautious quiet. "It's been years."

They sat in a small room, and Linnea saw how Orlen's eyes drifted to her now and then, searching her face for signs of Celenneth. "She's well, as far as I know," Linnea said gently. "We've built a life together, full of traveling. I know she wrote to you once."

Orlen cleared his throat. "I wrote back. Never heard anything after. I wondered if she got it." His eyes dropped to his hands. "I'm glad she found someone who sees her worth."

"She did," Linnea said. "We have a daughter, Brynja. We're a family. It took her time to trust in what we had — but she's happier now. She never forgot the people who mattered. She doesn't say it often, but the past is still with her."

Orlen looked wistful, but not bitter. "I'm glad she's happy. That's all I ever wanted." Maren reached for his hand, and Linnea gave them both a respectful nod, understanding this was a moment of quiet acceptance. After a little more talk of Bree and old acquaintances, Orlen retired early, and Maren led Linnea to a guest room and invited her to stay the night.[^r-orlen]

Later, by the hearth, Maren's curiosity grew bolder, and at last she lowered her voice, fiddling with her apron. "I hope you don't think me rude — but I never expected Celenneth to find a woman to share her life with. How does it… work? I don't mean to offend. I've just never known anyone like that."

Linnea smiled gently; the ignorance came from inexperience, not malice. "It's all right. It's not so different from any other love. We care for each other, look out for each other, share our lives and our burdens and our joys."

Maren leaned in, blushing. "But physically… how do you… well, satisfy each other? I'm sorry — I just don't understand."

Linnea kept her tone calm and kind. "There are many ways to be close," she said. "Love and intimacy aren't limited to one idea. It's about knowing each other's bodies, listening, being patient and gentle. We find ways to make each other happy, as any couple would — man and woman or otherwise. The details differ; the point is that we share love, and trust, and pleasure. We've learned what works for us. That's what matters."

Maren nodded slowly. "I'd never thought of it like that. Thank you. I suppose love is love, no matter who it's between."

"Exactly," Linnea said, and her eyes softened.

They spoke of lighter things then — Maren's garden, the season, Bree gossip — until Linnea's eyes grew heavy, and she excused herself to a bed that felt heavenly after so many nights on the road, and slept deep, dreaming of Celenneth and Brynja.

Morning came bright. Orlen had gone into town early — needing space, she sensed, to sit with all he'd learned — but he'd left word that she was welcome any time. She left a note of thanks with Maren and rode out into the sun, rested and more at peace.

The Angle was not far now, a day's ride at most, and the land grew more familiar with every mile — memories of building their home warming her thoughts. By late afternoon she saw the signs: a bent birch, a rocky outcrop, and then, over a gentle rise, the low building they called home, smoke curling from the chimney. She urged Eorlin to a canter, her heart quickening, and a small figure came racing out to meet her — Brynja, her laughter reaching Linnea before her face did. Celenneth appeared in the doorway, a hand raised against the sun.

Linnea swung down and Brynja barreled into her legs, and she lifted her daughter up and breathed in the sun-and-grass scent of her hair. Celenneth came near, a slow smile forming, relief and a little concern in it.

"I'm home," Linnea said softly. "Miss me?"

"Always," Celenneth murmured, touching her arm. "Long trip?"

"Long and strange. There's a great deal to tell you. But I'm here now." And Celenneth did not press — she knew Linnea would speak when ready — and guided her inside, Brynja chattering of butterflies and sparrows, the warmth of home closing round her, smelling of cooking herbs and clean linen. There would be time enough to tell of the men in the marsh, the Wyrmscale Sigil hidden in her pack, the cursed charm, the meeting with Orlen and Maren. For now she let herself have the simple, priceless comfort of home, grateful for every step that had led her back to where she belonged.[^r-home]

Home at last, with Celenneth and Brynja.
