# Chapter 2: Traveling Westward

*Cermië 1, 2969*

The wind over the Angle carried the warmth of high summer, the land here lying south and a little east of Bree, a quiet reach of Eriador seldom spoken of in grand tales. From that unassuming place Linnea set out westward at Gandalf's side. She had known him only briefly, but the unshakable wisdom in his keen eyes convinced her that wherever he led would be worth the following.

Her horse, Eorlin — the proud chestnut mare of Rohan — stamped the ground as if sensing the road. Gandalf sat astride his own mount, an old but steady creature, and inclined his head to mark the day's start. "Well," he said, voice low yet carrying, "the road ahead is not short, but neither so long that we cannot endure it. Nine days of steady pace, if Eorlin is sure-footed and we meet no ill luck, should bring us to the borders of the Shire. From there, Hobbiton is a quiet ride."

They set off. The Angle's low hills gave way to mild slopes and quiet vales, and the East Road ran westward to guide them; for a while the only sound was the rhythm of hooves and Gandalf's occasional humming of old traveling songs. By afternoon Linnea found herself easing into the saddle, the knots of worry loosening under Eorlin's steady gait — though she kept her eyes moving, as Gandalf had counseled.

They rode until the shadows lengthened, and then a rustling in a stand of thorn drew her gaze. Gandalf slowed, raising his staff. With sudden ferocity two ragged figures leapt out, spears raised — lean, desperate men in stained cloaks and patchwork leather. Brigands.

"Stay your hands!" Gandalf's voice rang clear, but desperation seldom heeds wisdom, and the men took a hesitant step. They had looked for easy prey — and found instead a wizard, and a woman on a horse that could outrun any chase, who did not cower but studied them.

Linnea knew the worth of a kind word over a drawn blade. She raised an empty hand and eased Eorlin forward. "Ho there," she called, quiet but clear. "Strong men like yourselves have no need to waylay weary riders. There's little profit in spilled blood. Far wiser to earn your coin without risk of your lives. Let us pass — and perhaps we come to some small arrangement that spares everyone's skin." She spoke as though greeting guests at a distant homestead, and let them believe themselves reasonable men rather than cornered rats. The brigands traded uncertain glances, their knuckles whitening on the shafts — and then the taller spat to the side. "We ain't fools," he muttered, as if to convince himself. "Coin's better than steel." They took a few silver pennies to see her and Gandalf safe past the next rough stretch, and melted back into the scrub.[^r-parley]

"Well played," Gandalf murmured. "Your courtesy saved us trouble — and them as well."

"Desperation makes men foolish," Linnea said. "But I'm glad we parted without blood. Let's go on."

The next days unfolded in a comforting pattern — the East Road west, once more through the outskirts of Bree-land, the summer air clear and the road quieter beyond. On the fourth day they crossed the Brandywine Bridge, and even as they passed into the gentle land beyond, Linnea felt a change: the grass greener, the breeze sweet with turned earth and warm hay, sheep on the distant hillsides, and here and there a round door set into a hill with smoke curling from a half-buried chimney. Hobbit-holes.

On the evening of the sixth day, near the rolling rises of the Green Hill Country, a stillness settled — and then, drifting on the wind, came a faint singing, clear and lovely and shadowed with sorrow. She and Gandalf halted. "No Hobbit tune," he whispered. Linnea called out, "Fair voice among the trees, we mean no harm! If you are friend, come and speak plainly." The singing ended, and a slender figure stepped from the wooded gloom — an Elf, wearing sorrow like a faded mantle. Fëarnor of Lindon, he named himself, and Linnea dismounted and bowed, and her courtesy softened his guard.

"The barrows to the south have grown restless," he murmured, gentle as distant bells. "The Old Forest stirs, and not kindly. Dark lights flicker near Needlehole, west in the Shire's Westfarthing. Few know what it means. Be cautious. Not all lights are meant to be followed." When Linnea offered him company he declined — "My path leads east; yours west. May the stars guide you" — and slipped away, leaving only a thread of Elvish song fading into the wind.

They rode on, subdued. "Strange lights on the moors — that's no idle fancy," Linnea said. "I've heard tales of will-o'-the-wisps, lights that lead travelers astray. Usually just stories to frighten children in Bree's common-room."

Gandalf nodded. "Keep your wits about you. Shadows linger in Middle-earth, even after great evils are diminished — in hollows and forgotten places, waiting for the unwary."

On the seventh day, watering the horses at a slender stream, Linnea asked the thing that had been on her mind. "Gandalf — you speak with ease of distant lands and old alliances, and I've heard you name Celenneth. I know who she is to me: my beloved, my partner, the mother of my child, a Ranger of the North. But what is she to you?"

He lit his pipe and let the smoke drift. "Celenneth. Yes — we've crossed paths a few times. Not long ago I asked her to carry a message to Balin in Moria. Before that we met near Fornost, when the Dúnedain brought me troubling reports of strange doings in the North, and I sought keen-eyed help. She joined me in studying the old stones and weathered runes. She had only just begun the Ranger's path, and yet she impressed me — her courage, her swift understanding, her steadfast heart. We were not long together, but it was enough for me to trust her judgment. In uncertain times, that trust is precious."

Linnea absorbed every word — parts of Celenneth's story she had never heard, not kept from her but simply never spoken. She pictured her beloved as a young Ranger, striding beside this grey pilgrim, poring over inscriptions older than kingdoms. "It seems you've found friends in every corner of this land," she said. "Elves, Dwarves, Rangers."

Gandalf smiled gently. "All who walk under these skies have their stories, Linnea. I don't claim them as friends lightly — but I'm grateful for each honest heart that crosses my path. Including Celenneth's. And yours, if I may say so."

They talked of many things as they rode, and made camp that night in a sheltered hollow, and in the morning a soft mist draped the hills. Linnea's mood was calm and resolved. They carried warnings, yes — but also hope. The Shire was no land of horrors; perhaps its peaceful folk knew nothing of any disturbance, and these flickers of unease would stay distant flickers only.

[^r-parley]: **Persuade / Courtesy** — waylaid by desperate brigands on the road west, Linnea talks them down with flattery and reason, buying safe passage for a few silver pennies rather than a fight. No blood shed.
