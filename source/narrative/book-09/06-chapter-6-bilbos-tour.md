# Chapter 6: Bilbo’s Tour

*Book 9*

*Cermië 10, 2969*

Linnea woke to the gentle sound of birdsong. At first, she couldn’t place the warmth she felt—both within and without—until she remembered where she was. Her eyes opened to the soft light filtering through the round window at Bag End. The linens smelled clean, and the faint scent of baking bread and fresh herbs drifted into her room. She stretched slowly, savoring the feeling of waking up in comfort. After weeks of restless nights under open skies, the quiet security of Bilbo’s home was a welcome change.

For a while, she just lay there, absorbing the morning stillness. Outside, Hobbiton moved at its own gentle pace. She could imagine the Hobbits tending their gardens, chatting softly over fences. The tension that usually clung to her had eased—maybe it was something about the Shire’s slow rhythm, or maybe just a good night’s sleep.

Eventually, Linnea pushed back the covers and rose from the bed. She crossed to the small writing desk near the window and pulled out a sheet of parchment. The nib of her pen was slightly worn, and the ink had thickened a bit, but it would do. She felt the weight of unspoken words in her chest, words she meant for Celenneth.

Sitting down, she began to write:

*My Dearest Celenneth,The world feels quieter here, as if the troubles I carry are muffled by hills of green and flowers in bloom. I’m in the Shire—it’s as peaceful as the old stories say. Hobbits are kind but private. They take their comfort seriously, and it shows in their warm kitchens and tidy paths.*

*I wish you could see it. Brynja would laugh at the little ones running about, chasing butterflies without a care. She deserves that kind of peace, and so do you. There’s more to say—about the journey so far, about the secrets I’ve learned—but I want to tell you in person. Until then, stay safe. I hold you both in my heart.*

She signed her name, folded the letter carefully, and tied it with a small ribbon. For now, it would remain tucked away in her pack until she could deliver it herself. Just putting those words on paper made her feel closer to home.

Rising from the desk, Linnea stepped into the hall. She smelled fresh bread, honey, and something savory—maybe mushrooms—and heard the light clink of teacups. Bilbo was up and about. Her boots made almost no sound on the polished floors as she followed the warm scents to the kitchen.

Bilbo turned from the stove, smiling broadly. “Ah, good morning! You look better rested,” he said, wiping his hands on a cloth. “Slept well, I hope?”

Linnea returned his smile. “Like a log. I’m not sure I’ve slept that soundly since... well, I can’t remember when.” She moved closer, eyeing a basket of fresh berries and a loaf of brown bread. “Can I help with anything?”

“Help? By all means!” Bilbo gestured to the berries. “Sort these out, would you? The ripe ones go in this bowl for breakfast; the others, we can stew later. And if you know your way around a pan, I wouldn’t mind a hand with these eggs and mushrooms.”

Linnea rolled up her sleeves, content to settle into the simple, homely tasks. Soon she was rinsing berries, discarding a few overripe ones, and slicing mushrooms into neat pieces. It felt good to focus on something ordinary. Not cursed daggers, not eerie spirits—just breakfast.

As she stirred the eggs over a gentle flame, Bilbo watched her, leaning against the table. “You know, not many travelers come through here, and fewer still stay a while. Most are passing through on their way to grander places. What about you, Linnea? Where are you headed next?”

She gave the pan a careful tilt. “I’m thinking of going north,” she said. “Celenneth—my partner—was raised by Rangers up that way. We’ve talked about visiting them, learning more. But nothing’s set. We haven’t made firm plans yet.”

Bilbo nodded thoughtfully, his expression a bit more serious than before. “The North can be tricky ground. Old stories linger there, and not all of them friendly.” He paused, adding quietly, “Speaking of roads and journeys, Gandalf left at dawn. He didn’t wake you, but he left a note.”

Linnea turned off the flame, heart giving a small twist. She wiped her hands and accepted the plain folded note Bilbo offered. Gandalf’s seal—simple, practical—was pressed into the wax. She opened it, scanning the familiar bold script.

*Linnea,I must leave sooner than I intended. Dark whispers move in places we cannot ignore, and I’ve got to see to them. Do not fear; you’re stronger than you know. Keep to the road and trust yourself. Seek knowledge where it endures, and light where it hides. I’ll find you again when the time is right.*

*—Gandalf*

Below that, scrawled in a hurried hand, he’d added:

*“The clasp was no accident. Be wary.”*

Her fingers tightened slightly on the parchment. The clasp—the one she’d found near Bag End’s hedgerow. Gandalf’s warning confirmed her suspicions. Something was off in this quiet land.

“Bad news?” Bilbo asked gently, noting her change in posture.

Linnea shook her head, folding the note carefully. “Not bad, just... a reminder that I need to keep my eyes open.” She tucked the note into her pack. “Thanks for the heads-up.”

They finished preparing breakfast and carried it to the table: fresh bread, scrambled eggs with mushrooms and herbs, berries, and honeyed tea. Linnea tasted each bite as if it might be her last, savoring the comfort of well-made food.

“I’ve been thinking,” she said after a while, “I might stay another day—if you don’t mind. I’ve never really seen Hobbiton except in passing, and it’d be nice to know it better before I move on.” She kept her tone easy. She didn’t mention Gandalf’s caution. No need to worry Bilbo further, not yet.

Bilbo beamed. “Stay as long as you like. The weather’s fine, and there’s more to the Shire than meets the eye. We can stroll down to the Green Dragon Inn later, maybe visit the marketplace. Nothing like good ale and friendly chatter to settle the mind.”

After breakfast, they washed dishes together, the companionable silence punctuated by Bilbo’s humming. Linnea felt strangely at home. Maybe some part of her craved this ordinary life, far from bandits and curses. But reality waited. Gandalf’s note weighed on her mind, reminding her that shadows had a way of creeping in where least expected.

She decided to look into the matter of that clasp. Later, after a pleasant morning helping Bilbo with small chores and chatting about nothing in particular, Linnea asked if he’d show her around. He obliged eagerly, and soon they were walking down Hobbiton’s lanes, passing bright gardens and tidy hedgerows.

They wandered through the local market first. Vendors called out as they passed—fresh radishes, honeycombs, wheels of cheese, clay mugs painted with bright patterns. Bilbo greeted many by name, and everyone smiled at Linnea politely, though she caught a few curious glances. A tall, traveling woman in Bree-style garb wasn’t the usual market guest.

Bilbo insisted she try some cider: “Best apple cider in the Westfarthing!” He pressed a cup into her hands. It tasted crisp and sweet, with just a hint of spice. She could get used to this.

By midday, they headed toward the Green Dragon Inn, a warm-looking establishment near the water. The inn had a homey feel—low beams, wooden tables, and a friendly hum of conversation. Bilbo ordered two pints of ale, and they found a quiet corner.

It was here, with the soft afternoon sun glowing through the windows and the comforting rumble of Hobbit voices around them, that Linnea decided to bring up the clasp. She reached into her pouch and laid it on the table. The iron piece, engraved with a wolf’s head, looked oddly menacing amid such gentle surroundings.

Bilbo’s eyes narrowed, his usual good cheer fading into something more serious. He picked it up, turning it carefully in his hands. “Where did you find this?” he asked quietly.

“Near Bag End,” Linnea said, keeping her voice low. “It felt... like it was left there on purpose.”

Bilbo frowned, studying the engraved wolf’s head. “Not Hobbit-work. Wolves don’t figure much in our decorations. This... I recall something like this from stories. Men of the North—some tribes or groups that come down from wild places—carried charms like these. Hunters, wanderers.” He looked up. “Or worse.”

A chill brushed the back of Linnea’s neck. She had hoped for a simple explanation, but deep down, she knew better. “So this might be a warning?”

Bilbo tapped a finger on the table, thinking. “The Shire’s not fond of strangers lurking about, especially not ones who leave calling cards. If you’ve picked up unwanted attention, this might be someone’s way of letting you know they’re watching.”

Linnea exhaled slowly, weighing the possibilities. That meant whoever left it knew she was at Bag End and might know more about her plans than she was comfortable with. She glanced around the cozy inn, suddenly more aware of the open door and passing faces.

“Has there been other trouble?” she asked quietly.

Bilbo nodded, leaning in closer. “A few odd travelers seen on the West Road. Not merchants—folks said they stayed near the Marish, keeping to the shadows. Sometimes livestock vanish, carts turn up empty. Small things, mostly—but small things add up.” He tapped the clasp. “And then this. If someone’s sniffing around Bag End, it can’t be good.”

Linnea thought about Gandalf’s words, about being wary. If these strangers were in the Marish, maybe she needed to see for herself what was going on. She had no intention of leading trouble back to Celenneth and Brynja. Better to face it head-on, gather what information she could, and handle it quietly.

“I should check the Marish,” she said finally. “Before I leave. Find out who’s skulking around and why.”

Bilbo gave her a long, measured look. His earlier good humor was replaced by something more protective. “Be careful,” he said at last. “This isn’t Bree or Rohan. The Shire’s not used to rough company. Don’t stir up more trouble if you can help it.”

She managed a half-smile. “Trouble and I have a way of finding each other, but I’ll be careful. I promise.”

They finished their ale in relative silence, both lost in thought. Eventually, Bilbo sighed, as if shaking off a heavy cloak. “Come on, let’s enjoy what’s left of the day,” he said, pushing the clasp back toward Linnea. “No sense fretting ourselves sick. The Shire has its share of shadows, but this is still a place worth seeing.”

Linnea pocketed the clasp and followed him out. The afternoon had a warm glow, and she let Bilbo show her more of Hobbiton’s gentle corners—quiet meadows, a small orchard buzzing with bees, a footbridge over a lazy creek. Children’s laughter drifted from somewhere beyond a hedge. For a moment, it was easy to pretend all was well, that the outside world and its dangers were far away.

They ended the day back at Bag End, where Bilbo offered her a taste of pipe-weed. Linnea had never tried it, but she was curious. She took a mild puff and coughed lightly at the spicy-sweet smoke. Bilbo only grinned, amused, and settled back in his armchair, telling her little stories about the local families—harmless gossip, memories of old festivals, jokes about how greedy some Hobbits got during harvest feasts.

She let the stories wash over her, absorbing the calm atmosphere. Outside, dusk fell gently, and the scent of chamomile drifted in from the window. Eventually, when the lamp-light grew soft and her eyes heavy, Linnea excused herself and slipped back to her guest room.

Before sleeping, she placed Gandalf’s note and the clasp side by side on the desk for a moment, studying them. Gandalf’s warning and the iron piece—two puzzle pieces hinting at something lurking just beyond the Shire’s peaceful edges. She didn’t have all the answers yet, but she would do her best to find them. Then she tucked the note into her pack, slipped the clasp into a safe pocket, and climbed into bed.

The night passed quietly, and once again, sleep came easily. She dreamt of green hills rolling on forever, and distant laughter echoing over sunlight fields. No shadows pressed at the edge of her vision, no weight sank on her chest. Just softness and rest.

Morning dawned fresh and clear, a new day all laid out before her. Linnea joined Bilbo in the kitchen, helping slice bread and ladle out honeyed oatmeal. A simple breakfast, but hearty enough to set her on her way. She thanked him warmly for everything—for the bed, the food, the conversation, even the pipe-weed experiment.

“You’re welcome anytime,” he said, smiling. “You might find the Shire quiet, but I think it suited you. You look more at ease.”

Linnea laughed softly. “It did me good, no doubt. I feel... steadier.” She hoisted her pack onto her shoulder. “Time I got back on the road, though. Too much world out there to see.”

Bilbo nodded, but his eyes flicked to her pack as if remembering the clasp and what it implied. “Safe travels,” he said quietly, “and remember what I said. Be careful.”

She met his gaze and nodded firmly. “I will.” Then, more softly, “Thank you, Bilbo.”

Outside, the morning air was cool and fresh, the sky a bright, pale blue. She turned away from Bag End’s round green door, stepping onto the lane that wound down toward the distant fields. Linnea wouldn’t march straight home yet, though that was her eventual goal. She had the Marish to consider—quietly, cautiously. If someone was watching, it’d be best to understand why before heading north. She owed it to Celenneth and Brynja, to ensure no threat followed her steps.

But first, she let herself enjoy one last lingering moment in the Shire. The wind brushed through the hedgerows, carrying the scent of dew and distant baking bread. Hobbiton was still waking up: a farmer called out to his neighbor, a dog barked lazily, children’s voices drifted like bells. Linnea stored these impressions away, knowing she might need to recall this peace when darker hours came.

With that, she set off, boots softly tapping along the well-worn road. Her bag felt lighter than usual, despite its contents. Maybe the Shire’s comfort had worked its magic, or maybe she was simply more confident now. Either way, she walked with steady purpose, the letter for Celenneth tucked safely inside, and Gandalf’s warning echoing in her mind.

Soon, Bag End and Bilbo’s kind smile were out of sight behind a bend in the lane, but their memory lingered—like a small light in her heart. Linnea would carry it forward, into whatever lay ahead.
