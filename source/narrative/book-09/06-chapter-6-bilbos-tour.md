# Chapter 6: Bilbo's Tour

*Cermië 10, 2969*

Linnea woke to birdsong and the soft light of the round window, and for a while simply lay there, absorbing the morning stillness — after weeks of restless nights under open skies, the quiet security of Bag End was a rare gift. The tension that usually clung to her had eased.

At length she rose and crossed to the little writing desk, and drew out a sheet of parchment. She had words in her chest meant for Celenneth, and she began to write:

*My dearest Celenneth,*

*The world feels quieter here, as if the troubles I carry are muffled by hills of green and flowers in bloom. I'm in the Shire — as peaceful as the old stories say. Hobbits are kind but private, and take their comfort seriously. I wish you could see it. Brynja would laugh at the little ones running about, chasing butterflies without a care. She deserves that kind of peace, and so do you. There is more to say — about the road, about what I've learned — but I'll tell you in person. Until then, stay safe. I hold you both in my heart.*

She signed it, folded it, and tied it with a ribbon to carry home.

The smell of fresh bread and mushrooms drew her to the kitchen, where Bilbo turned from the stove with a broad smile. "Good morning! You look better rested. Slept well?"

"Like a log. I don't remember the last time I slept so soundly." She eyed a basket of berries. "Can I help?"

"By all means!" He set her to sorting berries and slicing mushrooms, and she settled gladly into the homely tasks — no eerie whispers, no curse thrumming in her pack, just breakfast. As she stirred the eggs, Bilbo leaned against the table. "Not many travelers stay a while. Where are you headed next?"

"North, I'm thinking. Celenneth was raised by Rangers up that way. We've talked of finding them." She kept it light.

Bilbo nodded, more serious now. "The North's tricky ground. Old stories linger there, and not all friendly." He paused. "Speaking of roads — Gandalf left at dawn. He didn't wake you, but he left a note."

Linnea's heart gave a small twist as she took the folded paper, Gandalf's plain seal pressed in the wax.

*Linnea — I must leave sooner than I intended. Dark whispers move in places we cannot ignore. Do not fear; you're stronger than you know. Keep to the road, trust yourself, and seek light where it hides. I'll find you again when the time is right. — Gandalf.*

And below, in a hurried hand: *The clasp was no accident. Be wary.*

Her fingers tightened. The wolf's-head clasp from Bag End's hedgerow. Gandalf's warning confirmed her unease. "Bad news?" Bilbo asked gently.

"Not bad. Just a reminder to keep my eyes open." She tucked the note away.

They ate together, and washed the dishes to Bilbo's humming, and Linnea felt strangely at home — some part of her craved this ordinary life, far from bandits and curses. But Gandalf's caution weighed on her, and after a pleasant morning of small chores she asked Bilbo to show her about, meaning to learn what she could of the clasp.

They wandered Hobbiton's lanes and its bright market — radishes and honeycombs, wheels of cheese, painted clay mugs — Bilbo greeting half the vendors by name, pressing on her the best apple cider in the Westfarthing. By midday they took a quiet corner of the Green Dragon Inn with two pints of ale, and there Linnea laid the iron clasp on the table, its wolf's head looking oddly menacing amid such gentle surroundings.

Bilbo's good cheer faded as he turned it in his hands. "Where did you find this?"

"Near Bag End. It felt left there on purpose."

He frowned. "Not Hobbit-work. Wolves don't figure in our decorations. But I recall something like it from old stories — Men of the North, some wild tribes and wanderers, carried charms like these. Hunters." He looked up. "Or worse. The Shire's not fond of strangers lurking about, especially ones who leave calling cards. If you've picked up unwanted attention, this may be someone's way of saying they're watching."[^r-clasp]

A chill brushed her neck. "Has there been other trouble?"

Bilbo leaned in. "A few odd travelers on the West Road — not merchants. Folk said they kept near the Marish, in the shadows. Livestock vanish, carts turn up empty. Small things, mostly. But small things add up. And then this."

Linnea weighed it. If these strangers were in the Marish, she needed to see for herself. She would not lead trouble back to Celenneth and Brynja. "I should look into the Marish," she said, "before I go. Find out who's skulking about, and why."

Bilbo gave her a long, protective look. "Be careful. This isn't Bree or Rohan. The Shire's not used to rough company. Don't stir up more than you must."

She managed a half-smile. "Trouble and I have a way of finding each other. But I'll be careful. I promise."

They finished the day back at Bag End, where Bilbo offered her a taste of pipe-weed — she coughed at the spicy-sweet smoke, and he grinned — and told her harmless stories of the local families as dusk fell soft and the scent of chamomile drifted through the window. Before sleeping she set Gandalf's note and the clasp side by side on the desk, two pieces of a puzzle hinting at something lurking just beyond the Shire's peaceful edges. She would do her best to find the rest.

The night passed quietly, and sleep came easy — green hills rolling on forever, distant laughter over sunlit fields, no shadow pressing at the edge of her vision. In the morning she helped Bilbo with breakfast and thanked him warmly for everything. "You're welcome anytime," he said. "You might find the Shire quiet, but I think it suited you. You look more at ease."

She laughed softly. "It did me good. I feel steadier." She shouldered her pack. "Time I got back on the road, though. But first — the Marish. Quietly. If someone's watching, best I understand why before I head north. I owe Celenneth and Brynja that." Bilbo's eyes flicked to her pack, remembering the clasp, and he only said, "Safe travels. And be careful."

Outside, the morning was cool and bright. She let herself linger one last moment in the Shire — the wind through the hedgerows, the scent of dew and baking bread, a farmer calling to his neighbor, children's voices like distant bells — and stored the peace away, knowing she might need to recall it when darker hours came. Then she set off down the lane, the letter for Celenneth tucked safe inside, and Gandalf's warning echoing in her mind.

[^r-clasp]: **Insight** (with Bilbo) — the wolf's-head clasp is no Hobbit trinket but the mark of Men of the North — hunters, wanderers, "or worse" — and, left deliberately at Bag End, a watcher's calling-card. It points Linnea toward the strangers said to lurk near the Marish.
