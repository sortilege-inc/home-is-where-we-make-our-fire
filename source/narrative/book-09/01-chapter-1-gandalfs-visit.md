# Chapter 1: Gandalf's Visit

*Nárië 30, 2969*

The summer wind came warm off the hills, carrying the smell of sun-dried grass and cold river water. Where the Hoarwell met the Loudwater their waters sang in a ceaseless murmur, cradling the small cabin nestled in the shelter of a pine grove; smoke curled from the chimney against the gathering dusk. Inside, the hearth crackled, and Linnea stirred a pot of venison stew, its rich scent filling the room. Brynja sat cross-legged on the worn rug, arranging her wooden animals in an elaborate story only she understood, while Celenneth sat on a low stool by the window, sharpening her hunting knife with slow, practiced strokes, her eyes flicking now and then to the darkening forest.

A knock at the door broke the quiet — sharp, deliberate.

Celenneth was on her feet in an instant, her hand at the worn hilt of her knife. Linnea traded a glance with her, alert but composed; visitors were rare this far north, and uninvited ones rarer still. At Linnea's nod, Celenneth eased the door open just enough to see the figure outside — tall and weathered, leaning on a twisted staff, his long grey cloak damp with river mist, his wide-brimmed hat pulled low.

"Good evening," he said, his deep voice warm despite the distance of strangers, his eyes sharp and knowing beneath the brim.

Celenneth blinked, then her expression softened into something like respect. She stepped back and pulled the door wide. "You'd better come in. The wind's turning."

The old man ducked to fit through the doorway, his boots thudding softly on the worn floor, his gaze sweeping the modest room — the hearth, the plain table, the child watching him from behind Celenneth's legs.

"Linnea," Celenneth said, closing the door. "This is Gandalf."

The name tugged at something half-remembered — campfire tales of a wandering wizard, known across the wildlands for deeds both wondrous and dangerous. Linnea had not expected to find him standing rain-dappled in her home. "Gandalf," she repeated, wiping her hands. "You've come far."

"I often do," he said, inclining his head. "And rarely without reason." His gaze went to Celenneth, and softened. "You've built something strong here. A good home."

"What brings you this far?" Celenneth asked.

"Ah — we'll come to that. But if it's no trouble, a cup of tea would be most welcome after such a road."

Linnea fetched a sturdy clay mug and set him by the fire, and he lowered himself into a chair with a murmur of thanks, stretching his long legs to the warmth. Brynja peeked out, and his face gentled. "And who might this young lady be?"

"Brynja," Linnea said, placing the steaming mug in his hands. "Our daughter."

He sipped, and his brows rose in appreciation. "Fine work. Good tea is harder to come by than you'd think, in these parts." Then he set his staff against the chair, and his tone turned serious. "I need someone to undertake a journey." His gaze settled on Linnea, weighing her.

"What sort of journey?"

"The ring you carry," he said, low and grave. "The one given you by the spirit near the Anduin." Celenneth tensed; Linnea met his eyes and did not let her unease show. "I still have it," she said carefully.

"Good. Its nature remains uncertain — but I fear its history may be older, and darker, than even you know. There is someone in the West, in the Shire, who understands such things. He can examine it."

"You want me to take it there."

"To him. Quietly."

Celenneth stepped forward, her jaw tightening. "Linnea's capable. But I'm the faster traveler. Let me take it."

Gandalf inclined his head. "You are all those things, Celenneth. But this task belongs to Linnea. The burden was placed into her keeping. It is hers alone to carry."

Linnea exhaled slowly, already turning over routes and dangers. "When do I leave?"

"At first light."

Brynja's small fingers slipped into Celenneth's hand, and Celenneth squeezed them, her face unreadable but her eyes fierce. Linnea looked to her. "You know him far better than I. What do you think?"

Celenneth sighed, her shoulders slumping a little. "If he says you need to go, you need to go. Brynja and I will keep things here."

Linnea stepped close and wound her fingers into Celenneth's. "I'll be back soon. Always."

"I'll be waiting," Celenneth said, her grip tightening.

And as the wind rose again outside and the rivers whispered their endless song, shadows deeper than the summer dusk gathered, unseen, in the farthest reaches of the West.[^r-charge]

[^r-charge]: **Undertaking** — Gandalf charges Linnea (and Linnea alone — the burden is hers to bear) to carry her spirit-given ring west to the Shire, to one who can read its nature. She sets out at first light.
