# Chapter 5: Bilbo & The Ring

*Book 9*

*Cermië 9, 2969*

When Linnea steps into Bag End, the warmth of the place drifts over her like a welcoming embrace. The hall is lit by a soft lamp, its copper shade glinting dully, and the scent of pipe-weed and dried herbs lingers in the air. Somewhere ahead, a hearth crackles, making the world outside feel even farther away. She lets that warmth settle into her bones, grateful to be off the road at last.

Bilbo Baggins appears from around a corner, his face lighting up at the sight of Gandalf. He’s smaller than Linnea imagined from stories, but there’s a brightness in his eyes and a confident friendliness in his stance. Arms open wide, he greets the wizard as one old friend to another.

“There you are, Gandalf!” he exclaims. “I thought I heard your voice.”

Gandalf smiles warmly, inclining his head. “It’s been too long, Bilbo.”

Bilbo’s gaze slides over to Linnea, taking her in with curious interest. He was clearly expecting Gandalf, but the presence of another traveler piques his attention. “And who might this be?” he asks, voice friendly but inquisitive.

Linnea steps forward, meeting his eyes. “My name is Linnea,” she says simply, her tone calm but open. “Thank you for letting me in. I’m grateful.”

Bilbo waves off the thanks. “Any friend of Gandalf’s is welcome in Bag End,” he says, though there’s a note of question in his voice. He leads them into a sitting room with low, comfortable chairs and shelves crammed with books. A kettle sits ready on a trivet near the hearth. “Come in, come in. Take a seat. It’s late, and you two look like you’ve seen a long road.”

Linnea settles into a chair that feels surprisingly plush. Gandalf ducks a rafter and rests his staff against the wall, while Bilbo pours tea into small cups—carefully, like it’s a treasured ritual.

“Now,” the Hobbit says, handing them each a cup, “I know Gandalf, of course, but I know nothing about you.” He regards Linnea with open curiosity, eyes bright with a mixture of caution and welcoming. “You’re not a Hobbit, obviously, and you’ve come a fair distance I’d guess. If I may ask—where are you from, and what brings you to the Shire?”

Linnea takes a sip of tea. It’s warm and floral, easing the dryness in her throat. “I’m from Bree,” she begins, choosing her words easily. “My father was a merchant there. I never planned to leave home for very long, but... life leads you places you never expected.”

Bilbo nods thoughtfully. “Bree, eh? I’ve passed through once or twice. Friendly enough folk, but not many from Bree wander too far off. How did you end up traveling west all the way here?”

Linnea thinks back. It’s not a painful question, just a heavy one. “I was heading east first,” she says, with a small, wry smile. “This is the second half of a journey that looped through more places than I ever meant to see. Bree feels like another life sometimes.”

Bilbo’s eyebrows lift at that. “East, then west. That’s a long circuit. I take it you’ve seen more than just Bree and the Shire?”

Linnea nods, resting her tea cup on her knee. “I have. I’ve traveled as far as Dale, where the buildings rise strong and proud after hardships past. I passed through Mirkwood, careful as one can be around the Woodland Elves. And I spent time in Rivendell—a place so calm it’s as if the world’s troubles sleep at its borders.”

“Dale! Rivendell!” Bilbo exclaims quietly, delight creeping into his voice. “I’ve been to both places as well. Dale’s a city of effort and resilience, and Rivendell... well, there’s nowhere like it.”

Linnea shares a knowing smile. “I’ve never forgotten Rivendell,” she agrees. “It felt like stepping into a living story.”

Gandalf remains silent, letting them talk. Bilbo leans forward, elbows on his knees, curiosity deepening. “So you’ve seen the Elven realms and the lands of Men. What about Rohan? Have you ever ventured that far south, to those windy plains?”

Linnea’s expression softens. “I have. Rohan’s a proud land. The people there treated me well enough, once they knew I meant no harm. I learned a lot from them—about honesty, courage, and how the wind never stops blowing.”

Bilbo seems delighted, as if Linnea’s experiences are gifts he’s unwrapping. “You’ve wandered a fair piece of the map, then.” He tilts his head. “I can’t help but wonder: did you travel alone all that time?”

Her gaze drifts briefly to the hearth, where the fire pops softly. “Not always. I met someone on the road... a Ranger named Celenneth. She saved my life when bandits took me near the Anduin. Without her, I might not be here now.”

Bilbo’s tone turns gentle. “A Ranger saved you. They’re mysterious folk, but I’ve known a few good ones. Did you stay with her?”

Linnea’s smile widens. “Yes. We did more than stay together. We built a family. We have a daughter, Brynja. She’s safe, waiting for me far from here. Everything I do, I keep her in mind.”

Bilbo’s eyes grow bright. “A daughter! How wonderful. I imagine that gives you a reason to keep traveling—and to know when to head home.”

“It does,” Linnea admits softly. “She gives me hope.”

For a moment, the three of them lapse into quiet reflection. The crackle of the hearth and the scent of tea fill the silence comfortably. Bilbo finally clears his throat, voice still kind but practical. “Well, I don’t mean to pry overmuch. I’m just curious. You must have a reason for coming to Hobbiton. You didn’t stumble in by pure chance, did you?”

Before Linnea can reply, Gandalf gently sets down his tea and gives Bilbo a meaningful look. “We have matters to discuss, old friend,” the wizard says, voice even but firm. “Matters that need your particular perspective.”

Bilbo straightens, looking between the wizard and Linnea. “My perspective? I’m flattered—though I can’t imagine what I know that a wizard and a world-traveler would need.”

Gandalf only smiles faintly and touches the pipe in his vest. “Let’s say you’ve had certain experiences that might shed light on a question or two.”

Linnea glances at Gandalf, then decides to trust him. She takes off her glove and reveals the silver ring on her finger. The runes catch the firelight in a subtle way. Bilbo’s curiosity sharpens once more, and he sets his cup aside.

“Well, now,” he says quietly, “that looks like something old, and perhaps not ordinary.”

Linnea nods, steadying herself. “It came to me under strange circumstances. I’d like to know more about it—what it might mean.”

“Magic rings, eh?” He tries for a light tone. “There are tales... old ones. Rings that do strange things, forged in older ages by Elves, Dwarves, and others.” He looks sharply at Gandalf. “Why do you ask?”

Linnea hesitates, then slides off her glove. She doesn’t want to hide this any longer. She extends her hand, revealing the simple silver band she’s worn for a while now. Its faint runes catch the lamplight, hinting at old craft.

“This is why,” she says. “I’ve carried it for some time. I got it... well, from a spirit, I think. A wandering presence near the Anduin. I didn’t know what to make of it, so I kept it. It never felt dangerous, just... sad.”

Bilbo’s posture relaxes the moment he sees it. “Ah. Not mine,” he says, relief washing over his face. The tension leaves his shoulders, and he even smiles apologetically. “For a second there, I thought—well, never mind what I thought.”

Gandalf shifts, lighting his pipe again. He seems to be watching Bilbo closely, but the Hobbit focuses on the ring in Linnea’s hand. He fetches a small magnifying lens from a cluttered shelf, then holds the ring gently between thumb and forefinger.

“Hmm,” he murmurs, studying it in silence. “Old. Possibly Second Age. Elven craft... but not the common kind. These runes... they’re about holding on. A memory ring, maybe.” He looks up at Linnea. “Such a ring might be made by someone who refused to let something vanish with time.”

Linnea leans closer. “You think it’s preserving a memory?”

Bilbo nods slowly. “Not a memory like yours or mine, but more like a piece of someone’s essence. Could be a promise. Could be a farewell. Hard to say without more knowledge. But I sense no darkness. Just a longing that never settled.”

Gandalf releases a gentle stream of smoke. “And no resonance?” he asks quietly, voice measured.

Bilbo shakes his head. “No. It doesn’t respond to... others I know of.” He smiles wryly, meeting Gandalf’s gaze. “Whatever else it is, it’s its own thing. Not connected to...” He taps his waistcoat lightly, leaving the rest unsaid.

Linnea picks up on the undercurrent, but doesn’t pry. Gandalf asked about resonance, and Bilbo seems relieved that her ring isn’t connected to whatever he’s hiding. That’s enough for now.

“So what do I do with it?” Linnea asks, voice subdued. “I’ve worn it without trouble. Should I take it off? Keep it wrapped up?”

Bilbo returns the ring to her, looking thoughtful. “It chose you. Seems it wanted you to have it. I doubt hiding it will help you understand it. Keep it close, listen to your instincts. Maybe one day it’ll make sense.”

Gandalf nods. “Sometimes these old relics guide rather than harm. You said it came from a spirit. That spirit might have needed you to carry something forward—some memory they couldn’t let die.”

Linnea runs a thumb over the runes. “I haven’t heard voices or seen visions. Just a feeling now and then, like being watched, but never threatened.” She slips the ring back on her finger, deciding for now to trust herself.

“Best leave it at that,” Gandalf says. “We have enough mystery to fill a dozen nights. No need to unravel it all this evening.”

Bilbo sets the lens aside. “Agreed. If the ring is patient, we can be too. Now, you both look like you could use proper rest.” He stands, back straightening, and leads them down a hall lit by a single lantern. “Bag End has a guestroom just made for weary travelers. Probably needs a dusting off, but it’s yours.”

As they pass down the corridor, Linnea notices the thick wooden walls, the polished floors, the homely scent of baking lingering in the wood. She wonders if Brynja would like it here—most likely, the child would race up and down these halls, poking at books and nibbling seedcakes. The thought warms Linnea’s heart.

Bilbo opens a door to a snug room with a small bed under a round window. Moonlight drifts in, painting the floor silver. A quilt folded neatly at the foot of the bed, a little writing desk, and a washbasin—everything she needs for comfort. After weeks on the road, it’s perfect.

“Thank you,” she says, voice quiet but earnest. “This means a lot.”

Bilbo nods, smiling gently. “No trouble at all. Get some sleep, my dear. Morning’s a safer time for questions.”

He leaves, closing the door softly. Linnea stands there a moment, listening to the hush. She removes her belt pouch and pulls out the iron clasp she found near Bilbo’s garden gate. The wolf’s head etched into it seems significant. She turns it over, wondering who dropped it and why. There’s a story here too, lurking just outside the warm light of Bag End’s walls. She decides not to dwell too long on it now. Gandalf will have thoughts, but that can wait until sunrise.

She tucks the clasp away and sets her pack down. The bed is calling, and her body aches for it. It’s been too long since she slept without one eye open. Maybe here, behind Bilbo’s friendly walls, she can let go for a night.

Kneeling by the desk, she tugs off her boots, stretches her toes. She yawns, the day’s tension seeping out of her muscles. The pillows are soft, the quilt heavier than she’s used to, but in a comforting way. Linnea slides under it, sinking into a mattress that doesn’t poke or shift like the forest floor.

Outside, the Shire slumbers. She imagines hobbit-holes tucked under the earth, warm and safe. The thought steadies her breathing. No rushing wind, no creak of distant trees, no threat lurking at the edge of campfire light. Just muffled silence and the memory of kind words.

Her eyes drift closed. For once, she doesn’t fight it. Sleep comes gently, drawing her into quiet darkness. No troubled dreams rush forward. No ghosts of old fears reach out. She only hears the faint whisper of leaves outside the window and the steady rhythm of her own heartbeat.

In Bag End, with Gandalf and Bilbo near at hand, Linnea rests. She’s carried stories from Bree to the far corners of Middle-earth, braved bandits and strange forests, trusted a Ranger who saved her life, and gained a family she never expected. She holds a ring that might carry a memory of ages past, a clasp that might hint at some lurking presence outside these friendly walls. But for tonight, that can all wait.

Morning will come soon enough, and with it more questions. But tonight, within the quiet comfort of Bag End, Linnea sleeps deeply, breathing easily, letting the world’s troubles spin silently beyond the round door.
