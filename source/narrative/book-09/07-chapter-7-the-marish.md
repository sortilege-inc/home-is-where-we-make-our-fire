# Chapter 7: The Marish

*Book 9*

*Cermië 11, 2969*

Linnea left Bag End early, heading southwest with a quiet, steady resolve. The comforts of the Shire lay behind her now—the tidy hobbit-holes, the cheerful markets, Bilbo’s welcoming hearth. She didn’t regret taking the time to rest there; it had given her strength. But she had business in the Marish now, and her mood reflected the land’s shifting tone.

The Marish lay at the edges of Hobbiton’s farmland, in low, swampy ground that stretched out like a secret. The gentle rolling hills gave way to wetlands and tangled copses of willow and alder. Fog sometimes clung to the marsh grass in the early mornings, and by afternoon, the land smelled of stagnant water and decaying leaves. Hobbits rarely ventured there, except a few hardy souls who trapped or fished. It was the sort of place that discouraged visitors, offering no clear paths, only half-submerged trails and thick reeds swaying in muddy ground.

Linnea rode Eorlin along the outskirts, keeping to the less-traveled fringes. She’d learned enough in the last day to know something was wrong, something that didn’t belong in the Shire’s quiet heart. Bilbo’s warning and the clasp she’d found near Bag End suggested trouble had followed her here or was already stirring on its own.

She tried to keep a low profile. Linnea knew she wasn’t the world’s stealthiest scout—travelers from Bree didn’t often practice skulking in marshes—but she made do. She dismounted at times, leading Eorlin behind stands of reeds and small hills. The horse seemed content to follow her lead, trust in her steady hand and soft words of reassurance.

By midday, the sun hung pale and distant, filtered through a haze that rose from the wetlands. Linnea paused on a low mound that offered a bit of dry footing. The quiet pressed in around her, and for a moment, she wondered if she was on a fool’s errand. Perhaps nothing was here after all—just her nerves and the memory of that iron clasp. But even as doubt crept in, she spotted movement at the far edge of her vision.

Shadows flickered among old willow trees, not far from a patch of land that looked firmer than the rest. She reached for Eorlin’s reins and guided the mare behind a thicket of brush, tying her securely. Then Linnea crept forward on foot, heart thumping. Voices drifted on the sluggish air, too low to understand, but the tone was unmistakable: hushed, urgent, and purposeful.

Keeping low, she moved closer, using the uneven ground and tall reeds as cover. The ground sucked at her boots, and she took care with every step to avoid a splash or crackle of dried reeds. When she finally caught sight of the figures, she saw at least two men—maybe more. They were human, that much was clear, dressed in dark cloaks and simple leathers that seemed well-worn. Not farmers, and not friendly traders. They moved with a certain deliberation, as if searching for something or setting a trap.

The men drifted deeper into the marsh, heading toward a small clearing hidden behind drooping willow branches. Linnea followed at a distance, her mind racing. Who were they? She remembered Bilbo’s words about strangers lurking on the edge of the Shire. Maybe these were the same folk. If so, what was their goal?

As she crept closer, she caught fragments of their conversation:

“They’ll come soon,” said one, voice gruff, as he crouched near a hummock of damp earth. “We’ve made enough noise already.”

“Good,” replied another, tone quieter but tense. “We need to be gone before the next shift arrives.”

Shift? Linnea frowned. That sounded organized. These weren’t random thieves or lost travelers. They were expecting someone—or something—and had a schedule to keep.

She pressed herself closer, nearly holding her breath. The men seemed impatient. She watched them check their gear, glance around warily, and mutter about not being seen. Everything about their body language screamed that they were up to no good.

Fascinated, Linnea waited. The men moved on, and after a careful pause, she followed. Soon, they led her to a deeper part of the marsh, where willows clustered and the ground rose slightly, forming a hidden clearing of firmer earth. There, a few more figures waited. Linnea counted four now. They conversed in low voices, and from her vantage point behind some tall reeds, she struggled to catch more than a few words.

What she did pick up worried her. They spoke of “the others coming by nightfall” and “needing to move soon.” This sounded like a hand-off or a rendezvous. With who? For what?

Linnea edged closer, step by careful step. She couldn’t get too near. If they spotted her, she’d be in serious trouble—four against one was bad odds, even if she was armed. Better to learn what she could and slip away. So she stayed hidden, watching them lay out small packs and bundles. One of them handled a particular bundle with great care, setting it down on a flat stone, almost like an altar.

Her curiosity sharpened. What was in that cloth-wrapped bundle? The way they hovered around it suggested something precious—or dangerous. They were too far to let her hear details, but she could see their worried glances and the tension in their shoulders.

A gust of wind rustled the willows, and Linnea took the chance. She moved closer, using the noise as cover, creeping along the edge of the clearing. When she was near enough to see the bundle clearly, she noticed faint gleams of metal beneath the cloth. Something valuable. Something old.

Linnea waited until the men were distracted again. One stepped away, scanning the treeline as if searching for a signal. Another bent over a small campfire pit, busy with something on the ground. The other two turned to each other, whispering. Their focus wasn’t on the bundle for a precious few moments.

Now or never. Linnea slid forward, easing behind the boulder where the bundle lay. Her heart pounded in her ears. She reached out carefully, lifting a corner of the cloth just enough to see what lay beneath.

At first, she only saw metal, etched with unfamiliar runes. She squinted, pushing the cloth a bit further aside. A small, circular sigil reflected a muted light. The etchings reminded her of dragon scales, or perhaps some old heraldic emblem. An uneasy feeling prickled at the back of her neck—this thing wasn’t just valuable, it felt... alive in some subtle way. Not literally breathing, but humming with a faint magic that prickled along her fingertips.

She recognized the style: old stories mentioned items tied to ancient dragons, granting strange powers. This must be one of those wondrous relics, something that could produce fire or enhance weapons. She recalled tales from merchants who’d traveled farther than she ever had—stories of relics lost in wars long ago. Owning such a thing would give someone dangerous advantages.

A twig snapped. Linnea’s breath caught, and she lowered the cloth. One of the men shifted, looking her way. She froze, hardly daring to breathe. After a tense moment, he turned back, apparently satisfied. But this was too close. She had to move.

Her gut told her that letting these men keep this artifact was a terrible idea. Given their secrecy, their careful planning, and their midnight rendezvous, nothing good would come of it. She made a split-second decision. With a quiet, deft motion, she lifted the entire artifact, cloth and all, and slipped back into the reeds.

It was risky and reckless, but she figured taking it out of their hands would at least slow them down. If they were waiting for someone, that someone would be disappointed. It might buy her time to figure out what to do with it—maybe show Gandalf, if she could find him again, or stash it somewhere safe.

Linnea moved as silently as she could, each step measured. She could almost feel the men’s presence behind her, their tension thick in the air. But they had no reason to suspect someone would dare steal their prize right from under their noses. Just as she cleared the clearing, though, she heard a sudden curse and a flurry of whispers. They must have noticed the artifact was missing.

Crouching low, she inched away, heart hammering. She’d have to reach Eorlin and ride hard. She knew the land better than these strangers—at least she hoped so. If she could vanish into the Shire’s gentle farmland, they might not dare follow her openly. At worst, she’d face pursuit, but at best, they’d waste hours searching the marsh.

The men’s voices rose, urgent and angry now:

“Check the perimeter!” one barked.

“Gone? It can’t just vanish!”

Linnea swallowed hard and moved faster, careful not to crash through the brush. Within minutes, she found her mare waiting faithfully where she’d left her. She swung into the saddle and steered Eorlin away from the clearing, taking a wide loop to avoid detection. The horse’s ears twitched nervously, as if sensing Linnea’s tension, but otherwise stayed quiet.

After a good while, when the sound of angry voices and rushing footsteps had faded to nothing, Linnea allowed herself a shaky breath. She still had no idea who those men were or what they intended, but now she carried their mysterious artifact. That thought was both comforting and worrying. It made her a target, most likely.

Once on drier ground, she urged Eorlin into a steady canter. If the men came looking, she wanted to be long gone. The farmland around the Shire would soon roll into view, and she decided to pass through a small village nearby. She remembered Bilbo mentioning scattered farms and tiny hamlets between the Marish and the main roads. It wouldn’t hurt to warn them to keep an eye out for suspicious folk.

Eventually, she reached a small settlement—just a handful of cottages around a common yard, where a few sheep grazed lazily. An older woman paused in her task of hanging laundry, eyeing Linnea’s approach warily. Linnea offered a friendly wave, trying to appear nonthreatening.

“Morning,” she called softly. “Pardon me. Have you seen any strangers around here lately?”

The woman frowned. “Travelers aren’t common these days, miss. Why do you ask?”

Linnea dismounted, speaking quietly but firmly. “I saw some odd men in the marsh. They didn’t look friendly. I think they’re up to no good, maybe stirring trouble. Just watch yourselves, keep your doors locked after dark. They might move around at night.”

Several other villagers wandered over, concerned. Linnea repeated her warning, describing the general direction of the Marish and the kind of behavior she’d seen. She didn’t mention the artifact—no need to alarm them with talk of magical relics. Simpler to say: “Strangers lurking about, might be thieves.”

One of the men, a wiry fellow with a dirt-stained apron, nodded grimly. “We’ve found odd tracks near our fields lately. We’ll spread the word and stay on our guard.”

Linnea thanked them, relieved they took her seriously. Not everyone would trust a stranger’s warning, but these folk seemed sensible. She didn’t linger long, unwilling to draw more attention than necessary.

Back on Eorlin, she set off at a careful pace. The villagers would watch their corners, and that might at least make things harder for the suspicious group. Linnea wondered what Gandalf would make of the wyrm-scale sigil. If it truly allowed one to breathe fire or enchant weapons, that could tip the balance of any conflict. In the wrong hands, it spelled disaster.

For now, Linnea decided she’d keep it secret. She’d learn more about it on her own if she had to. Perhaps she’d come across a lore-master or a Ranger with knowledge of ancient relics. Celenneth had connections to the Rangers of the North; maybe they could shed some light. This wasn’t the sort of artifact one wanted to show off in a tavern.

The day stretched on as she guided Eorlin through familiar tracks, aiming roughly for the roads that would take her back toward Bree and beyond, toward wherever the next step lay. Her pack felt heavier now—physically no, but spiritually, as if this newfound burden carried a piece of old stories and old fires within it.

Still, she wasn’t frightened. She’d dealt with dangers before. If these men wanted their relic back, they’d have to find her first, and Linnea wouldn’t make that easy. She’d keep her eyes open for suspicious tails on the road. Maybe they’d lose interest. Or maybe she’d force a confrontation on her own terms.

As the sun dipped lower, turning the sky the color of old honey, Linnea looked back only once, scanning the horizon behind her. The wetlands were far out of sight, hidden by distance and gentle hills. No figures on horseback or foot seemed to follow. The village she’d warned would soon spread the news to neighboring farms, putting everyone on alert. The men would have a hard time sneaking around unnoticed now.

Linnea pressed on, each steady hoof-fall of Eorlin’s reminding her that she was moving forward. The Shire faded behind her, giving way to more open country. She tightened her cloak around the artifact, feeling the faint warmth and odd hum it seemed to carry.
