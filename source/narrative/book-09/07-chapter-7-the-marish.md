# Chapter 7: The Marish

*Cermië 11, 2969*

Linnea left Bag End early, riding southwest with a quiet resolve. She did not regret the days of rest — they had given her strength — but she had business in the Marish now, and her mood matched the land's shifting tone. The gentle hills gave way to low, swampy ground, tangled copses of willow and alder, air that smelled of stagnant water and warm decaying reeds. Hobbits rarely went there but for a few hardy trappers; it was the sort of place that offered no clear paths, only half-submerged trails and thick reeds swaying in the muddy ground.

She rode Eorlin along the outskirts, keeping to the fringes. She was no master of stealth — travelers from Bree did not often practice skulking in marshes — but she made do, dismounting at times to lead the mare behind stands of reed. By midday, on a low mound of dry footing, she caught movement at the edge of her sight: shadows among old willows, not far from a patch of firmer land. She tied Eorlin behind a thicket and crept forward on foot, and voices drifted on the sluggish air — hushed, urgent, purposeful.

Keeping low, using the reeds and uneven ground for cover, she moved closer. At least two men, human, in dark cloaks and worn leathers — no farmers, no friendly traders. They drifted deeper toward a hidden clearing, and she followed at a distance, catching fragments. "They'll come soon," one muttered, crouched by a hummock. "We've made enough noise." — "Good," said another, tenser. "We need to be gone before the next shift arrives." *Shift.* That sounded organized. These were no random thieves; they were expecting someone, and kept a schedule.

In the deeper clearing four figures waited now, conversing low. She caught only scraps — "the others coming by nightfall," "needing to move soon" — a hand-off, a rendezvous. And she saw one of them handle a cloth-wrapped bundle with great care, setting it on a flat stone almost like an altar. Her curiosity sharpened; the way they hovered spoke of something precious, or dangerous.

A gust rustled the willows, and she used the noise to creep to the edge of the clearing. When the men's focus drifted from the bundle — one scanning the treeline, another bent over a fire-pit, two whispering together — she eased behind the stone and lifted a corner of the cloth. Beneath lay metal, etched with unfamiliar runes: a small circular sigil that caught a muted light, its etchings like dragon-scales, or some old heraldic emblem. An uneasy prickle ran up the back of her neck — the thing was not merely valuable; it hummed with a faint magic that thrummed along her fingertips. She knew the style from old merchants' tales: relics tied to ancient dragons, granting strange powers — fire, or the enchanting of weapons. In the wrong hands, disaster.

A twig snapped, and she froze, lowering the cloth; one of the men shifted, looked her way, and after a breathless moment turned back. Too close. Her gut told her that letting these men keep this thing was a terrible idea — and so, with a quiet, deft motion, she lifted the whole bundle, cloth and all, and slipped back into the reeds. It was reckless. But taking it from their hands would at least slow them, and whoever they waited on would be disappointed.

She had barely cleared the clearing when a curse rang out and the men's voices rose — "Check the perimeter!" "Gone? It can't just vanish!" Crouching low, she reached Eorlin and swung up and steered wide of the clearing, the mare's ears twitching but her feet quiet. When the angry voices had faded to nothing she let out a shaky breath. She still had no idea who those men were — but now she carried their prize, which surely made her a target.[^r-sigil]

On drier ground she urged Eorlin to a canter, and passing a small settlement — a handful of cottages round a common yard — she stopped to warn the folk. "I saw odd men in the marsh," she told the wary villagers. "Up to no good. Watch yourselves. Keep your doors locked after dark." She said nothing of the relic — simpler to speak of thieves. A wiry man in a dirt-stained apron nodded grimly. "We've found odd tracks near our fields. We'll spread the word and stay on guard." They took her seriously, and she did not linger.

Back on the road she wondered what Gandalf would make of the wyrm-scale sigil. If it truly let one breathe fire or enchant a blade, it could tip the balance of any conflict — and in the wrong hands, spell ruin. For now she would keep it secret, and learn what she could on her own; perhaps a lore-master, or one of Celenneth's Rangers, would know of such old relics. This was not a thing to show off in a tavern.

The day stretched on as she guided Eorlin toward the roads that would carry her back toward Bree, her pack heavier now — not in weight, but as though it held a piece of old stories and old fires. She was not frightened; she had faced danger before, and if the men wanted their relic back, they would have to find her first. As the sun sank and turned the sky the color of old honey, she looked back only once. No riders followed; the wetlands were far behind, hidden by distance and gentle hills. She pressed on, each steady hoof-fall carrying her forward, and drew her cloak close around the bundle, feeling the faint warmth and odd hum it seemed to carry.
