# Chapter 3: Hospitality and Threat

*Cermië 8, 2969*

The sun was sinking low, painting the sky rose and amber, when Linnea drew her cloak against the evening cool and breathed in turned earth and distant chimney-smoke. Gandalf rode beside her sprinkling small memories into the ride like breadcrumbs of comfort — an oak that once sang with nightingales, a hollow where Dwarves had camped decades past — reminders that even in uncertain times, old paths held kind things.

"You know," Linnea said with a wry smile, "I've heard rumors of your fireworks. The Hobbits seem to think you a traveling wonder-maker."

Gandalf laughed, deep and genuine. "They do love a good story in the Shire. And they're not entirely wrong. I've set off a few in my time — bright ones, colorful ones. They remember them fondly, as they remember any magic that doesn't trouble their garden walls." His voice dropped. "The Shire is small, Linnea. That's what makes it worth protecting. The larger world may thrash and burn, but here things stay gentle — at least for now." He turned thoughtful. "In the end it isn't always grand armies that save us. Sometimes it's a soft word, a helping hand, a small kindness that turns the tide. Don't forget that."

The day's last light slipped behind the hills. Soon a glow on the western slope revealed a cluster of lights — Whitfurrows, a small Hobbit village. "We'll try there for the night," Gandalf said. A stout, rosy-cheeked Hobbit closing his market stall called them toward the home of Marigold Bramblefoot, a warm-eyed matron who beamed at their request. "I've stew on and bread from the oven — I'll not turn you away. Come in!" She lowered her voice a notch. "Strange lights have folk uneasy these days. No trouble, I hope?"

"Just travelers in need of comfort," Gandalf said smoothly.

Linnea let him handle the easy chatter, but she marked the tension in some of the Hobbits' eyes; rumors of odd lights in the Westfarthing had reached even here. When it seemed all was settled, she slipped outside to tend the horses and steady her thoughts. In the stable she brushed down Eorlin and Gandalf's mount — and then, stepping back, felt the air shift. A faint rustle whispered through the hedgerow, too quiet to be an animal. Eorlin tossed her head, ears flicking.

Linnea trusted her instincts. Silent, she circled to the stable's side and kept low in the shadows. A hooded shape moved past the back gate, slow and careful, and the faint scrape of metal on wood told of a drawn blade. She crept to the house and tapped the windowsill; Gandalf came out at once, staff in hand, his old eyes gone grave. "Someone's out there," she whispered. "Casing the stable. Armed." He studied the bent grass at the gate. "Fresh tracks. Two-legged, and no Hobbit. We're not alone tonight." He offered to ward the house while she kept the watch, and she agreed, heart pounding but steady — for this she knew: silent vigilance, the weight of a drawn bow, the patience to wait.

She crouched by a low hedgerow with an arrow nocked. Minutes crept by. Then she saw him again, a shape slipping along the stable's edge, blade dull in the moonlight, moving to get inside where he might kill the horses or reach the house. She whispered a silent apology to the night and loosed. The arrow took his shoulder; he staggered, hissing, his hood falling to show a gaunt face — and then he ran. Linnea vaulted the fence and gave chase, tracking him by moonlight and a thin blood-trail across a field, catching him at a small clearing where he stopped, gasping, a hand to his wound.

She drew again. "Throw down the blade, or the next goes through your heart."

His eyes darted like a trapped animal's, and the knife slipped from his fingers. And the moonlight showed her more than fear: a blackened brand on his forearm, a twisted mark carved and burned into the flesh. "Gandalf — over here." The wizard came, his staff-light cutting the dark, and studied the brand, his frown deepening. "You bear a sign of enslavement," he said sternly. "Some darkness made you its servant. Who commands you?"

The man shook, sweat beading. "You don't understand. If I talk, they'll come. They'll find you. It's too late."

Linnea softened her tone, though her bow held steady. "I don't want to kill you. But you leave these Hobbits alone. Swear it, and go. If there's anything human left in you, don't harm them."

He looked at her like a man handed a scrap of hope in a wasteland. "If I stay, worse comes. Fine. I'll go. I swear it — I'll do no harm here." Gandalf gave a small nod, his staff glowing brighter as if to seal the bargain. "Then go. But know this: if we meet again and you bring darkness with you, mercy ends." The man fled, stumbling, into the trees.[^r-intruder]

Linnea lowered her bow, and her eye caught something on the ground where he'd dropped his knife: a small wooden charm, carved into the shape of a twisted hand with vines clawing round it, its surface darkened with ash. When she picked it up a faint, unsettling pulse thrummed beneath her fingertips, and she shuddered and slipped it into her pouch as Gandalf stepped close.

"This is worse than I feared," he said quietly, peering at the pouch as if he could see through it. "A charm of enslavement and summoning. It binds its bearer to old powers, long dormant."

"So he really was driven here?"

"Likely. Or at least driven by something beyond ordinary greed." He drew a warding sign in the air.[^t-charm]

They returned to the Bramblefoot home, and Gandalf smoothed it over with a genial word about foxes near the stable, and Marigold ladled out hot stew and fresh tea. Linnea forced her shoulders down and ate, acutely aware of the charm hidden in her pouch, and after, in the tiny guest room, she lay listening to the household settle and wished Celenneth were here — Celenneth would know what to say, would crack a quiet joke about nosy foxes or hum an old tune until the tremor left her. Instead she had a wooden charm thrumming with old malice, and the memory of a branded man no healing herb could cure.

Sleep, when it came, brought no comfort.

*She stands in a warped forest, tangled roots and twisted trunks stretching into endless gloom, the air old and damp and suffocating. Whispers call her name and hiss promises she does not want. A flicker of blue-white flame bobs in the distance, and though she knows it a mistake she follows, the whispers pulling her on, branches scratching her arms, vines twisting about her ankles. And in the shifting dark she glimpses a blade — the strange rune-scarred dagger Celenneth once found in Fangorn — and she tries to cry out, to ask why these things haunt her now, but no sound comes. A figure looms ahead, faceless but for two embers glowing like eyes, and the vines drag her down into damp earth. "Why?" she gasps. "What do you want from me?" And the whispers answer in layered voices, some trembling with sorrow, some laughing with cruelty: "You carry what is ours. You will return… or be consumed."*

The dream shattered, and she woke in the Hobbit guest room, clammy with sweat, the hearth's embers burned low. She recognized in that threat a hint of Celenneth's own long struggle, as if a single curse reached across their dreams, and it brought tears of helpless anger to her eyes. She lay a long while unable to sleep, listening to Marigold's soft snore through the wall, remembering how Celenneth had once described her nightmares — like old roots digging into the soul. Now Linnea understood too well. She did not close her eyes again, and waited for the first grey hint of morning to ease the weight of the night.[^s-charm-dream]
