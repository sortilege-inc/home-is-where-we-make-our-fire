# Chapter 2: A Stop in Bree

*Súlimë 24, 2971*

Bree's market hummed with spring—merchants hawking early greens, children weaving between the stalls, the smell of warm bread and damp earth. Linnea tried to move through it unremarked, and knew it was hopeless. Beside her Celenneth carried Hallas; Eryndil trailed close in her serene composure; and Brynja clung to Linnea's hand, eyes darting at every curious stare.

It did not take long. Evanor—one of her cousins—saw them first and nearly spilled her basket of apples. "Linnea? You've come back." Her gaze traveled over Celenneth, the Elf, the baby, and her face said plainly it was a great deal to take in. Her younger siblings Pelle and Mareth gawked openly. Then Tharen and Jocelyn arrived, drawn by the buzz—Tharen amused, Jocelyn's eyes sharp and cold.

The questions came fast and pointed. Where had she been. Who was the father. *Don't tell me it's the elf.* Did she truly think she could stroll in, say nothing, and have them all be fine with it.

Brynja's fingers tightened. "Mama," she whispered, "why are they so angry?" The small voice cut straight through Linnea's chest. This was meant to be home. It did not feel like home.

Celenneth stepped forward, her voice level and carrying over the noise. "The child bears his father's name," she said. "Hallas. That is all you need to know." The quiet firmness of it shut Tharen up for a moment—but Jocelyn's eyes only narrowed.

"Hallas," she repeated, dripping insinuation. "As in that Ranger? So you've been playing house with rangers now."

Then Merla and Harvin pushed through—older kin who had never troubled to hide their disapproval. "What's the meaning of this?" Merla demanded. "Strolling into Bree with strangers and a baby, making a scene in the market?"

Linnea had meant to be civil. She had wanted to show them she'd grown, that she was happy and safe. She tried, her voice measured. "Merla, Harvin—please. I know how this looks. But I came to tell you that I'm alright. Yes, I have a child, and yes, Celenneth and Eryndil travel with me. That doesn't change who I am—"

"You think waltzing in after months of silence, with an elf and a ranger's baby, is meant to make us feel better?" Merla cut across her. "We're the ones left with the questions and the rumors. You've embarrassed us before. Now this? What will folk think?"

And there it was. Something in Linnea's chest, held so carefully, let go.

"What they *think*?" Her voice rose, fury and hurt at war. "Since when is that worth more than your own blood? Is your pride worth more than my happiness?" She flung a hand toward Hallas, safe in Celenneth's arms. "This is my son. He's named for a good man—a better one than you'd credit. And Celenneth is more family to me than you lot have ever been."

Jocelyn's lip curled, and she lunged—an open hand swinging for Linnea's face. The crowd gasped. But the blow never landed: Celenneth caught the wrist mid-arc, her grip firm and controlled.

"No one touches my wife," Celenneth said quietly, and every word rang clear. Jocelyn froze. The market went utterly still.

Linnea laid a hand on Celenneth's arm. "Let her go," she said. "She's not worth it." Celenneth opened her hand, and Jocelyn stumbled back, glaring but cowed.

Linnea gave them all one last look. "Bree isn't home. Not anymore. Home is where my family is—Hallas, Brynja, Celenneth. We're leaving, west to the Grey Havens. If I never set foot here again, that's too soon." And she turned her back on them and walked.

⁂

They took a little respite at the Silver Lily before quitting the town. Lantern-light and the low hum of talk soothed her frayed nerves, and Eira found them at their corner table with a curious grin. "Look who's the talk of Bree," she teased. "Haven't had this much excitement in years." She dropped into the bench with the ease of an old friend, and for a while the barbs of the market eased. A merchant raised his mug in salute; a shy young woman asked Eryndil about the Havens and near-swooned at the soft-spoken answer.

Then a timid man hovered near, fiddling with his cloak-hem, and finally spoke barely above a whisper. "I only wanted to say—I admire what you're doing. Being open about who you are. It's not something I've managed yet." He glanced about him. "What you said out there—it means a great deal. Maybe one day I'll find that courage too."

Linnea's heart tugged. "I hope you do," she said gently. "I believe it's in you."

He nodded quickly, eyes bright, and slipped back into the crowd. And she thought that perhaps that alone had been worth the coming.

⁂

Come morning they made for the western gate. They had nearly cleared the town when footsteps hurried up behind—Pelle and Mareth, red-faced from running, contrite rather than angry.

"We're sorry about yesterday," Mareth said. "We didn't handle it right. We should've spoken up for you." Pelle held out a small cloth bundle. "For Hallas. A traveling blanket—Mum's charm for luck." Mareth produced a carved wooden horse for Brynja. "So you'd not leave without something good from home."

Brynja's whole face lit as she took the horse, and Linnea felt tears prick at her eyes. "Thank you," she managed. "It means more than you know that you came." She watched them go, and the weight she had carried into Bree lifted a little from her chest. Bree would stay a memory—some of it bitter, some of it, to her surprise, sweet. But she was done letting it name her. Her home rode west with her.[^m-bree]

[^m-bree]: **Fellowship — the return to Bree.** Linnea's estranged kin (Evanor, Tharen, Jocelyn, Merla, Harvin) meet her with scorn; Celenneth stays a slap meant for her. But not all of Bree is against her: **Eira** welcomes them at the Silver Lily, a stranger takes quiet courage from Linnea's openness, and the younger cousins **Pelle** and **Mareth** catch them at the gate to make amends with parting gifts.
