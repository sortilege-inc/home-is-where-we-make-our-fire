# Chapter 6: Arrival in the Grey Havens

*Book 13*

The wind that brushed the canopy overhead carried a hint of salt. Celenneth could taste it on her lips, faint yet unmistakable, as if a secret messenger whispering that the sea—and the Grey Havens—lay not far off. Hallas, snug in his sling, fussed now and then at the unfamiliar tang, but Celenneth soothed him with soft words, her own heart fluttering with anticipation.

They had traveled many leagues since fleeing the site of their last battle. The day’s early brightness was mellowed now by hours of riding through tangled forest paths. Brynja was perched before Linnea on Eorlin, humming a little tune, though the child’s voice sometimes caught with tiredness. Eryndil rode behind on her dappled mare Silme, ever watchful, scanning the shadows beneath the trees for any sign that the danger lurking behind them might still be near.

It was late morning when Brynja’s quiet humming faded. She turned her head and asked in her small, serious voice: “Mama Celenneth... what happened to the bad people?”

Celenneth glanced back from atop Nimroch, meeting the girl’s wide, curious eyes. For a moment, she hesitated. How could one explain the grim necessity of violence to a child so young? “They won’t harm us anymore,” she answered gently. “We had to fight them to keep you safe.”

Brynja’s small hands tightened on Eorlin’s mane. “Did you hurt them?”

“Yes,” Celenneth replied, no avoidance in her tone. “Sometimes, to protect the ones we love, we must stop those who mean us harm. But it’s never an easy choice.”

Linnea placed a comforting arm around Brynja’s middle, her voice as soft as a lullaby. “We do it because we want you and Hallas to grow up safe and free.”

Brynja nodded slowly, her brow furrowed with a child’s earnest worry. “I’m glad you’re here,” she said at last, leaning back against Linnea’s chest. “And that Hallas is safe, too.”

Celenneth and Linnea exchanged a tender look. The exhaustion of the road lifted, just a fraction, at the reminder of why they traveled—together, as a family, forging ahead despite fear and uncertainty.

The forest opened onto a gently sloping ridge, and for a while, the group found some ease in the wider path. Yet as midday stretched into afternoon, the terrain grew unexpectedly difficult again—thorny undergrowth snared the horses’ hooves, and low-hanging branches forced them to detour.

Linnea, acting as scout, tried to find the clearest route, but luck was not with her. She led them down a track that seemed passable at first but quickly turned treacherous. The trail all but vanished into a dense thicket, obliging them to circle back a good half-league. By the time they regained the main path, the sky’s light had begun to slant, an orange cast heralding evening.

Brynja, normally full of questions, was uncharacteristically silent; the day’s detours had sapped her energy. Even Eorlin, docile as she was, tossed her head in weary frustration. Linnea’s shoulders drooped with unspoken apology as she and Celenneth took stock of their slow progress.

Eryndil guided Silme up alongside them. “We could press on, but we’ll wear the horses to the bone,” she offered quietly. “Better to camp now and hope for a smoother day tomorrow.”

Celenneth ran a hand over Nimroch’s perspiring neck, feeling the horse’s trembling muscles. “Agreed,” she said. “Let’s stop before full dark.”

And so they found a small clearing near a trickling stream. Linnea dismounted heavily, lips pressed together in a mixture of fatigue and regret. “I’m sorry,” she murmured, mostly to Celenneth. “We lost so much time.”

Celenneth touched Linnea’s cheek gently. “It happens,” she said. “We’ll manage. We have to trust that tomorrow will be kinder.”

They set to making camp: Eryndil collected fallen branches for a fire, while Celenneth scouted the perimeter for signs of predators or unfriendly eyes. Brynja helped Linnea spread bedrolls on soft pine needles. The mood was subdued, but the crackle of the fire brought a little cheer.

Later in the evening, Hallas fed and dozing contentedly, Celenneth rejoined the others by the fire. Brynja had drifted off in Linnea’s lap, curls spilling over Linnea’s arm. Eryndil sat nearby, methodically checking the fletching on her arrows. The hush of the forest lay over them, punctuated by chirping crickets.

Linnea spoke softly, trying not to wake Brynja. “I keep thinking about how close we are,” she said. “If the stories are true, we’re only a few days from the Havens. It almost doesn’t feel real.”

Celenneth nodded, gazing at Hallas’s sleeping face. “We’ve come so far—further than I ever imagined I would, in so short a time.”

Eryndil lifted her gaze from the arrows. In the firelight, her sea-grey eyes reflected flickers of gold. “You have something rare,” she remarked, voice gentle. “Such devotion to each other... it is a gift to see.”

Linnea pressed a kiss to the crown of Brynja’s head. “We haven’t always been so fortunate,” she murmured. “We’ve fought and lost and struggled. But still we stand together.”

A moment of companionable silence fell. The stars blinked into view between high branches, and for all the day’s missteps, the quiet promise of family and friendship made the long road seem worthwhile.

Dawn came glimmering and mild. Determined to make up for lost time, the company packed up camp in brisk efficiency. Linnea’s mouth was set in a firm line—she was clearly bent on redeeming herself after the previous day’s misdirections. They started well, following a clear path that wound along the base of a rugged slope. Overhead, the sun lent the leaf canopy a bright glow, and for a time, it seemed the day would finally smile upon them.

Then, without warning, a distant rumble disturbed the morning peace.

“What’s that?” Brynja asked nervously, clinging to Linnea’s belt from the saddle.

Celenneth felt the hairs on her arms rise. Nimroch quivered beneath her as the rumble deepened into a low thunder that seemed to come from within the earth. “Something’s moving... up there!”

She had barely spoken when a thunderous roar overtook them. Rocks and dirt began to cascade down the steep hillside, dislodging in a chain reaction that sent boulders crashing. An entire section of the slope seemed to shift, the ground trembling underfoot.

“Rockfall!” Eryndil cried, swinging off Silme in one fluid motion. Her voice rang with urgency. “This way—move!”

The horses spooked, whinnying in terror as clods of earth rained down. A torrent of stones battered the trail, raising clouds of dust that choked the air. Linnea clutched Brynja, forcing Eorlin forward despite the mare’s attempts to shy away.

Celenneth’s heart hammered. With Hallas pressed tight against her, she urged Nimroch after Linnea. The path threatened to vanish under sliding gravel. A deadly hail of smaller rocks pummeled the ground, and it was clear that only swift action would save them from being trapped—or crushed.

“Overhang!” Linnea shouted, voice cutting through the chaos as she spotted a protruding shelf of rock ahead. The shelf jutted out from the base of the slope, forming a shallow alcove. If they could reach it, they might avoid the worst of the avalanche.

Eryndil, on foot, grabbed Silme’s reins with one hand and half-dragged, half-led the panicked mare in that direction. Celenneth spurred Nimroch, the horse’s eyes wild with fear. Stones smacked the ground mere yards behind them. Dust choked Celenneth’s lungs, forcing her to cough. She held Hallas to her chest, shielding him from the swirling debris.

Linnea reached the overhang first. She dismounted in a flurry, yanking Brynja down just as a chunk of hillside tumbled across the path behind them. Brynja gave a frightened wail, pressing her face into Linnea’s cloak. Eorlin skittered sideways, neighing in blind panic.

“Easy, girl!” Linnea gasped, pulling the reins down firmly. Beneath the overhang, the air felt marginally safer, though the pounding above them was deafening.

Eryndil appeared seconds later, practically wrestling Silme beneath the shelter. One final boulder crashed onto the trail where they had been standing mere heartbeats ago, sending up a spray of earth and cracked stone. Celenneth brought Nimroch in last, forcing the terrified stallion to squeeze into the small space.

Dust billowed everywhere, making it hard to see or breathe. Rocks continued to tumble for a while—though blessedly smaller in size—until at last the thunderous roar faded to a grinding rumble, then to echoes, then to silence.

For several long breaths, no one spoke. Brynja clung to Linnea, tears streaking through the dust on her cheeks. Hallas squirmed in Celenneth’s arms, letting out a shrill cry, no doubt picking up on the raw panic that still gripped them.

Linnea pressed a kiss to Brynja’s temple, murmuring reassurances: “You were brave, love. We’re safe. We’re safe now.” Then she turned to Celenneth, voice trembling, though relief shone in her eyes. “Are you hurt? Hallas?”

“We’re fine,” Celenneth managed between coughs. “Just shaken. You saved us with that overhang... If we’d been a moment slower—”

Eryndil ran a hand over Silme’s heaving flanks, calming the mare with elven whispers. Dust coated her usually pristine hair, but her posture was as steady as ever. “It’s passed,” she said softly, peering out from the alcove. “Though the path behind is ruined.”

Celenneth dismounted stiffly, her legs wobbling. Nimroch snorted, foam flecking his mouth. “Steady,” she soothed, rubbing the horse’s muzzle. Her gaze swept the scene beyond the overhang. The trail was littered with piles of debris, chunks of rock as large as barrels. In some places, earth had slid away, leaving the hillside naked and treacherous.

Eventually, they led the horses out from under the overhang, picking their way around smaller mounds of rubble. The slope above still looked unstable in places, so they moved cautiously, hearts lurching at every minor shift of gravel.

“Any more, and we’ll be pinned,” Celenneth muttered, scanning the hillside with wary eyes.

Eryndil pointed to a safer rise of ground. “Let’s make camp beyond that ridge. We need to recover—everyone is rattled, the horses especially.”

No one argued. The short distance to the ridge took the better part of an hour, thanks to the precarious footing. At last, they found a sheltered hollow dotted with sturdy trees, well away from the broken slope. The sun, once shining warmly, now felt oppressive in their fatigue, and though it was still afternoon, they decided to stop for the night. The day’s misfortune had sapped their strength beyond measure.

Linnea slipped off Eorlin, turning a worried gaze to Brynja, who had kept silent since the rockfall. The girl’s eyes were distant, as if replaying the moment the hillside thundered down. Linnea scooped her daughter close. “I’ve got you,” she whispered.

Brynja simply buried her face in Linnea’s tunic.

Celenneth gently unwrapped Hallas. He wailed louder than usual, perhaps hungry, perhaps unsettled by the tension in the air. She rocked him, heart twisting at the memory of those thunderous seconds. She could still feel the dust in her throat and the echo of panic in her bones.

Eryndil remained outwardly composed, but when she set about gathering firewood, her hands trembled slightly on the axe’s handle. Once the campfire was lit, they sank onto bedrolls and fallen logs, trying to muster enough will to prepare a proper meal.

Between them, they cobbled together a stew from dried vegetables and leftover broth. Brynja mostly picked at it, though Hallas seemed content once he was fed. Celenneth and Linnea exchanged worried glances, uncertain how to soothe Brynja’s shaken spirit—other than to offer presence and quiet reassurances.

As night fell, tension began to ease, replaced by a shared gratitude for survival. Brynja eventually pressed her head against Linnea’s lap, eyelids drooping at last. Eryndil paused in her tasks to look upon them all, dust-smudged and exhausted, her voice quiet: “Truly, each day with loved ones is a gift. This day’s terror reminds us how fleeting such gifts can be.”

When morning broke, the company rose stiffly. Their muscles ached, and the horses still showed signs of nervousness, but the path ahead looked mercifully open. They pressed onward, pushing through the final leg of their journey. By midday, Brynja’s spirits began to revive; she asked a few hesitant questions about how the Havens might look, or whether the sea would be bigger than the rivers she’d known. The adults did their best to answer, weaving hope into their replies.

A little luck finally found them. They discovered a faint side trail that allowed them to skirt around more hazardous slopes. It cut some corners and saved them a day’s worth of detours—an unexpected boon. Eryndil recognized subtle Elven markings along the way, as if ancient travelers had once left hints for safe passage.

Celenneth offered a small smile. “A shortcut indeed. Let’s hope it leads us all the way to the Grey Havens without more surprises.”

And, as twilight neared, the final bend in the path revealed a sight that chased away lingering fears. The land dipped into a vast, open vista where the sea glimmered under the dying sun. The **Grey Havens**—or Mithlond, as the Elves called it—sprawled along the shore, a shimmering cluster of spires, domes, and graceful quays. Lights twinkled like stars descending to earth, reflecting in the quiet harbor.

It was near sunset by the time they actually reached the city’s gate. The air smelled tangibly of salt and kelp, mingling with the perfume of flowers draping from stone arches. Brynja let out a hushed “Oh!” at the first true glimpse of open water. Hallas, half-asleep in Celenneth’s sling, stirred at the distant cry of gulls. Linnea’s mouth parted in wonder at the tall towers shining gold and silver in the twilight.

Eryndil guided them through the gates, where Elven sentinels nodded in serene welcome. The thoroughfares within were crafted of smooth, pale stone that gleamed under softly lit lanterns. Everything felt at once ancient and ageless, as though the city had weathered millennia without losing its youthful grace.

Brynja’s eyes flitted from one marvel to the next: an intricately carved pillar topped with a lantern that glowed like starlight; a fountain shimmering with faint bluish luminescence; distant spires that seemed to touch the sky. “Mama Linnea,” she whispered, “it’s so beautiful.”

Linnea embraced her gently. “It is. A place like no other.”

Celenneth, exhausted yet enraptured, gazed around, her mind spinning with the surreal reality that they were finally here. Eryndil pointed out a tall tower crowned with silver mosaic. “The Tower of the Stars,” she said. “From its top, one can see all the way to the wide sea beyond.”

Celenneth clutched Hallas closer, her heart full. The travails of the forest, the battle, the rockfall—everything—they had survived it all to stand in this city of wonders.

Following a winding side street, they reached a quieter district of the Havens. Smaller homes lined the lane, each built with Elven elegance but scaled for comfort rather than grandeur. Eryndil dismounted near a tidy wooden door framed by ivy.

“This is mine,” she said, a note of apology coloring her voice. “I travel often, so it’s modest compared to most. Yet it’s a safe place.”

She led them inside. The main room was cozy, with carved shelves holding a few books, scrolls, and shells. A subtle fragrance of cedar drifted in the air. Brynja blinked at the nautical maps pinned to one wall, each annotated in a flowing Elvish script.

Eryndil turned to them. “Please, make yourselves at home. The horses can stay in the small courtyard outside. I only wish I had more space, but you’ll have my bed. I’ll manage on the sofa.”

Linnea, arms aching from the long ride, gently set Brynja on her feet. “Thank you,” she said, voice earnest. “Truly, we can’t repay your kindness.”

Eryndil smiled faintly. “You needn’t repay anything. I’m glad to have your company tonight.”

With dusk settling into darkness, they stripped off travel-stained cloaks and boots. Brynja was nearly asleep on her feet, so Linnea guided her to the foot of the bed. The child murmured something incoherent before drifting off, face still etched with lingering wonder from the day’s discoveries. Hallas, likewise exhausted, nursed briefly in Celenneth’s arms, then let out a soft coo and fell asleep.

Eryndil offered a few simple refreshments—bread, fruit, and cool water from a pitcher. Despite their weariness, Celenneth and Linnea found themselves nibbling, relief and awe warring in their chests. Outside, Elven lanterns cast a soft glow across the lane, and distant singing drifted through open windows: a gentle, timeless melody that mingled with the hush of waves lapping the harbor.

“We’ll stay here tonight,” Eryndil explained, tidying a small stack of charts on a side table. “In the morning, I’ll bring you to meet Círdan, the Shipwright. He is... father to this place, in a sense. He has guided these Havens for countless years.”

Celenneth slipped onto a small stool, cradling sleeping Hallas. “We owe him no small debt for maintaining this haven of peace,” she murmured. “Though perhaps tomorrow we’ll learn how to unlock the secrets Eryndil carries.” She glanced at the elf. “And how my Númenórean blood might help.”

Eryndil inclined her head, her silver-streaked hair falling about her shoulders. “I suspect so,” she said, her tone thoughtful. “But first—rest. All of us.”

They settled for the night in Eryndil’s dwelling. Linnea and Celenneth lay side by side on the narrow bed, Brynja curled up at their feet, and Hallas snug between them. Though the bed was hardly spacious, an air of warmth and closeness wreathed the family. Eryndil, for her part, took the sofa with graceful acceptance.

Outside, the city of the Grey Havens seemed never to truly sleep. Soft Elven songs rose and fell in the distance, weaving through the hush of the waterfront. Sometimes, a gull’s cry punctuated the melody, reminding them all of the nearness of the sea. In that peaceful lullaby, the nightmares of rockfall and flight gave way to a gentle, dreamlike calm.

Celenneth found herself reflecting on how far they had come: from the quiet cabin in the Angles, to Bree’s hostility, to storms and shadows on the road, culminating in that terrifying moment when the hillside nearly buried them. And now, here they were, in a place so far removed from fear it felt almost unreal. A small tear pricked the corner of her eye—relief, or gratitude, or both.

She closed her eyes, breathing in the faint cedar scent and the salt-tinged breeze drifting through the window. Tomorrow promised new revelations. For now, she would rest with her beloved family, content to let the city’s soft magic cradle them.
