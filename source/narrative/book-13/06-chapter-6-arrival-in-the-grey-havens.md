# Chapter 6: Arrival in the Grey Havens

*Lótessë 4, 2971*

The wind through the canopy carried a hint of salt now; Celenneth could taste it on her lips, faint but sure—a whisper that the sea, and the Grey Havens, lay not far ahead. They had put many leagues behind them since the ambush, and the miles told on all of them.

Late one morning Brynja's humming faded, and she asked in her small, serious voice, "Mama Celenneth—what happened to the bad people?"

Celenneth hesitated. How did one explain the grim arithmetic of violence to a child of five? "They won't harm us anymore," she said gently. "We had to fight them to keep you safe."

"Did you hurt them?"

"Yes." She would not lie to her. "Sometimes, to protect the ones we love, we have to stop those who mean us harm. But it's never an easy choice."

"We do it," Linnea added softly, an arm around Brynja's middle, "so that you and Hallas grow up safe and free." Brynja nodded slowly, her brow furrowed, and leaned back against Linnea's chest. "I'm glad you're here," she murmured. "And that Hallas is safe too."

The forest gave onto a ridge, but by afternoon the going grew hard again—thorny undergrowth, low branches—and Linnea, scouting ahead, led them down a track that dwindled into a thicket and cost them a half-league doubling back. She dismounted at their camp that evening heavy with apology. "I'm sorry. We lost so much time."

Celenneth touched her cheek. "It happens. We'll manage. We have to trust tomorrow will be kinder."

Dawn came mild, and they set out briskly to redeem the lost day—until, without warning, a distant rumble disturbed the morning. Celenneth felt the hairs rise on her arms; Nimroch quivered beneath her. Then the rumble deepened to a thunder that seemed to rise from within the earth, and the slope above them gave way.

"Rockfall!" Eryndil cried, swinging off Silmë. "This way—move!"

The horses screamed as clods of earth and then boulders came crashing down. "Overhang!" Linnea shouted, spotting a shelf of rock jutting from the base of the slope. They drove for it—Eryndil half-dragging the panicked Silmë, Celenneth spurring Nimroch with Hallas crushed to her chest, stones smacking the ground mere yards behind. Linnea reached the shelter first and yanked Brynja down just as a chunk of hillside tumbled across the path they'd stood on. One last boulder crashed where they had been a heartbeat before. Then the roar faded to a grinding rumble, and at last to silence.

For several breaths no one spoke. Brynja clung to Linnea, tears cutting tracks through the dust on her cheeks; Hallas shrieked in Celenneth's arms. "You were brave, love. We're safe now," Linnea murmured, and turned, trembling, to Celenneth. "Are you hurt? Hallas?"

"We're fine. Shaken." Celenneth coughed the dust from her throat. "You saved us with that overhang. A moment slower—" She did not finish.

The trail behind lay buried under rock, the slope above still treacherous. They picked their way clear and made an early camp beyond the next ridge, everyone rattled, the horses worst of all. Only that night, with Brynja finally asleep against Linnea's lap, did the tension ease into a shared gratitude for survival. "Truly," Eryndil said quietly, looking on them all, dust-smudged and weary, "each day with those we love is a gift. This day's terror only reminds us how fleeting such gifts can be."[^r-rockfall]

A little luck found them the next day: a faint side-trail, marked with subtle Elven signs, that skirted the worst of the broken slopes and saved a day's detour. And as twilight neared, the last bend of the path opened onto a wide vista—the land dipping to a sea that glimmered under the dying sun, and the **Grey Havens**, Mithlond as the Elves named it, sprawled along the shore: spires and domes and graceful quays, lights twinkling like stars come down to earth.

They reached the gate near sunset, the air rich with salt and kelp and flowering vine. "Oh!" Brynja breathed at her first true sight of open water. Hallas stirred at the cry of gulls; Linnea's lips parted at the towers shining gold and silver in the dusk. Elven sentinels nodded them through in serene welcome, and the streets within were pale, smooth stone, gleaming under soft lanterns—at once ancient and ageless. Eryndil pointed out a tower crowned with silver mosaic. "The Tower of the Stars. From its top one can see all the way to the wide sea beyond."

She led them by a winding side-street to a modest ivy-framed door. "This is mine," she said, a note of apology in it. "I travel often, so it's small. But it's safe." Inside, the room was cozy—carved shelves of books and shells, nautical charts pinned to one wall in flowing Elvish, a faint scent of cedar. "Make yourselves at home. You'll have my bed; I'll manage on the sofa." She would hear no argument.

That night they lay close in the narrow bed—Brynja curled at their feet, Hallas snug between them—while outside the city seemed never quite to sleep: soft Elven song rising and falling through the hush of waves, a gull's cry now and then to mark the nearness of the sea. Celenneth found herself turning over how far they had come—from the quiet cabin in the Angle, through Bree's scorn and the road's storms and shadows, to the moment the hillside had nearly buried them—and now to this, a place so far removed from fear it felt half a dream. A small tear pricked the corner of her eye. In the morning, Eryndil had said, they would go to meet Círdan the Shipwright. For now she breathed the cedar and the salt and let the city's soft magic cradle her family to sleep.
