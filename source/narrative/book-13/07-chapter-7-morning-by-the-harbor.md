# Chapter 7. Morning by the Harbor

*Book 13*

Dawn broke over the Grey Havens in a gentle wash of gold. Winding lanes and silvery spires took on a soft glow that seemed almost otherworldly. Inside Eryndil’s modest house, the family stirred awake one by one, rousing slowly from the warmth of blankets and the lingering comfort of their last night on solid ground.

Linnea was the first on her feet, stretching out the fatigue of the past days. She caught sight of Brynja, sitting on a low stool and peering at the window as if expecting something miraculous. The child had been brimming with excitement watching the tall sails in the harbor.

Celenneth rose soon after, carefully lifting Hallas to her breast. The baby fussed briefly, then accepted the morning’s first feed with a few soft coos. Watching her wife nurse Hallas, Linnea felt a wave of contentment mingled with anticipation. They had come so far—through storms, battles, even a rockfall—and now they would depart on a vast new undertaking.

Eryndil entered from the adjacent kitchen alcove, bearing a simple tray of bread, cheese, and tea. “Best to keep our strength,” she said, giving them a subdued smile. “We’ll be busy once we reach the docks.”

Brynja hopped to her feet, heading straight for the table. “Is today the day?” she asked breathlessly, eyes flicking between Eryndil and her mothers. “Do we go on the ship now?”

Linnea ruffled her hair, laughing softly. “Soon, love. We have to meet someone, first.”

Brynja’s face lit up, her impatience momentarily checked by the promise. Hallas gave a tiny grunt of protest, as though aware of the bustle around him. Celenneth murmured reassurance, shifting him to a more comfortable position. In that moment, the small group felt an electric buzz of anticipation—a realization that once they left this house, their lives might change forever.

Some time later, with cloaks and packs readied, they ventured back into the city’s winding lanes. Dawn had blossomed into a bright morning, casting delicate shadows on the ivy-draped walls. The Grey Havens was vibrant with Elven activity: merchants carried parcels from nearby farmland, and mariners prepared for voyages of their own. Even so, the harbor retained its innate serenity, a hush born of centuries of tending to the sea and its mysteries.

Eryndil led Celenneth, Linnea, Brynja, and baby Hallas up a winding path along rugged cliffs, where seabirds cried overhead and the wind carried a briny tang. At the path’s summit, they arrived at a stone terrace overlooking the Gulf of Lune. There, the waves shone beneath the midday sun, and Círdan the Shipwright stood gazing out as though he could read fortunes in the distant horizon.

He turned at their approach, his silver beard catching the light, expression calm but curious. Eryndil bowed her head. “My lord Círdan,” she began clearly, “allow me to present Celenneth, a ranger of the North—and her wife Linnea, with their children, Brynja and Hallas. They have come through many trials: a rockfall that nearly claimed them on the road, a confrontation with hostile forces, mistrust in Bree. And”—Eryndil’s tone grew more reverent—“they survived the Frost Maw.”

Círdan’s thoughtful gaze swept from Eryndil to the small family. Brynja peered back with open curiosity, while Linnea kept a protective hand on her shoulder. “I see you stand together,” Círdan observed, voice resonating with mild approval. “Elrond suggested there was one among the Dúnedain who might serve a particular need—yet he named Celenneth, not the more renowned rangers. I appreciate your acceptance of this undertaking.”

Círdan beckoned them closer, stepping away from the terrace’s edge. “I would speak with you, Celenneth,” he said, his voice kind yet clearly expectant. “Elrond’s counsel is rarely misplaced. But I admit—I know little of you. Why should you, above others of the Dúnedain, be chosen for this task?”

Celenneth released a soft breath, exchanging a glance with Linnea. Then she stepped forward. “My lord, I’ve been a ranger most of my life. Brandor and Orlec—renowned mentors among our people—took me in at nine years old, adopting me when I was orphaned. By fourteen, I was already roaming the wilds, aiding travelers against orcs, trolls, and worse. It’s been nearly two decades of such work.”

Círdan arched an eyebrow. “You began your patrols so young. That speaks of skill—or perhaps necessity.”

Celenneth’s lips curved in a faint, wry smile. “A little of both. I’ve patrolled the Greenway, the Weather Hills, and the outskirts of Fornost’s ruins. I’ve seen glimpses of what lurks beyond quiet homesteads. But it was in the northern mountains—the Frost Maw—where everything changed. We discovered an ancient site, rumored to hold a bound evil from an elder age. Its chains were weakening. My wife Linnea and I helped strengthen those wards, if only for a time, and in doing so, I stumbled upon fragments of Númenórean lore long buried beneath ice.”

At this, Eryndil nodded in support. “She risked her life there—both of them did. What they found went beyond rumor: ward and relics tied to the faithful of Númenor.”

Celenneth inclined her head. “I never expected word of my actions to travel far. Then Brandor entrusted me with a blade—a Nurhael’s Warden Blade, though it required reforging.”

“Fascinating,” Círdan murmured, stepping closer, his sharp eyes flicking to the hilt at Celenneth’s side. “Tell me more of your grasp on Númenor’s history. Can you name the founding pillars of Arnor, or the lineage of Elendil?”

Celenneth answered steadily, “Elendil established Arnor after the downfall of Númenor. He arrived in Middle-earth and made Annúminas his seat. For generations, Arnor’s heirs maintained a line unbroken, though the kingdom split into Arthedain, Cardolan, and Rhudaur. Many records were lost in those troubled days, but the Dúnedain survived—Orlec insisted I memorize aspects of our history.”

Círdan nodded, satisfaction deepening the faint lines at his eyes. “And Númenor itself? What can you tell me of its legacy, particularly as it relates to the faithless kings who led to its downfall?”

Her voice lowered. “Tar-Ancalimon’s era seeded the division between the Faithful and the King’s Men, culminating in Ar-Pharazôn’s pride. Númenor fell beneath the waves, save for those led by Elendil who refused to forsake the old ways. My family’s oath has ever been to remember that doom and attempt to learn from it..”

Linnea gently hushed Hallas, who stirred with a fussy cry, while Brynja watched Celenneth with shining admiration—rarely had her mother spoken so completely of her ranger’s heritage.

Círdan, for his part, let a slow exhale escape. “You are well-versed indeed. Many among your folk know these tales in fragments, but you speak them with coherence and conviction.” He paused. “Tell me, then—what is your desire, Celenneth, daughter of the Dúnedain? Why have you come to the Havens?”

Celenneth’s gaze flicked to Linnea, to Brynja, then returned to meet Círdan’s. “Elrond told Eryndil I might be of use to you. If my blood or my knowledge can preserve what remains of Númenor’s wisdom—or if I can help hold back the darkness still bound in hidden places—then I will stand ready. My family and I want a future free from creeping shadows, not just for ourselves, but for all free folk.”

A breeze whispered across the terrace, carrying the tang of salt and distant gull cries. Something warm flickered in Círdan’s ancient eyes. “Your feats speak louder than mere rumour, Celenneth. The Frost Maw alone would test any ranger, yet you emerged with deeper insight—and the skill to re-bind an evil best left dormant.”

He glanced at Eryndil. “You have done well, seeking this heir at Elrond’s counsel.”

Eryndil bowed her head. “She has surpassed my every expectation, my lord.”

Círdan folded his hands before him, regarding the whole family—Linnea’s quiet determination, Brynja’s bright innocence, Hallas’s small coos. “Truly, you have come a long way, Celenneth of Arnor’s line. And in you, I glimpse a spark of Númenor’s old light. This is more than I had dared hope when I sought Elrond’s advice. In the tasks that lie ahead, you shall have the support of these Havens.”

He offered a gracious nod, then allowed a gentle smile. “Let us speak further of your next steps. There is a journey—by sea—should you choose to undertake it.”

Círdan guided them into a spacious chamber facing the sea, where a broad map lay spread upon a polished table. He rested his fingertips along its edges, voice calm and assured. “There is a place,” he began, scanning the faces of those gathered, “far to the west and north, some three weeks by ship. At first glance, it’s a lonely rock in the ocean—barren, windswept—but my people discovered a remarkable ruin within its depths.”

He traced a path across the parchment to a small, unmarked spot in the western waters. “This ancient site bears symbols and architecture unlike anything else we’ve seen. It was clearly Númenórean in origin—yet we soon learned it was warded. The very stones recognize only certain bloodlines, turning away all who are not heirs of Arnor.”

Celenneth’s breath caught, her hand brushing against the hilt of her Warden Blade. “So… you believe I can pass into it?” she asked, her tone steady, if tinged with curiosity.

Círdan gave a small nod. “Indeed. Your heritage runs deep. Only one of Númenórean descent—strong in that lineage—might pass those wards. Within this ruin, there may be knowledge or artifacts that could illuminate much we’ve lost over time. Until now, no one has been able to open its doors.”

Eryndil tilted her head thoughtfully. “So you propose a voyage,” she said.

A hint of reverence touched Círdan’s expression. “Yes. It is no small task: the seas can be perilous, but we have the best of ships and sailors. If we prepare well and proceed with resolve, the journey can be accomplished.”

Celenneth glanced at Linnea, who met her eyes with quiet resolve. “You want me to join the expedition,” Celenneth said, though there was little doubt in her voice.

“Yes,” Círdan replied, turning his full attention on her. “Your presence is the key. What dwells behind those wards could reshape certain histories—or at least preserve them from fading away forever.”

A hush fell. Brynja tugged at Linnea’s sleeve, voice hushed. “Mama… are we going?” she asked.

Linnea crouched beside her, smoothing the girl’s hair with a gentle hand. “We’ll discuss it, my love,” she said, her tone warm but serious. “And we’ll decide as a family.”

That evening, as the sunset painted the sky in strokes of gold and violet, Celenneth followed Eryndil out to the shore. Over the rhythmic hush of waves, they spoke in hushed tones about the voyage that lay ahead.

“The sea is no tame road,” Celenneth murmured, arms folded against the gathering chill. “Storms, sudden squalls… an island no one frequents. Are we truly prepared?”

Eryndil offered a careful nod. “Such journeys are never without risk. But Círdan will see that the ship is strong and the crew well-chosen. I trust his judgment; the Sea-Elves do not take voyages lightly.”

Celenneth glanced out at the broad waters, a frown creasing her brow. “And what of the island itself? If it’s warded for my bloodline, there could be other dangers. My family—”

Eryndil’s voice softened. “I understand your worry. Yet from what we know, the ruin has stood for ages, silent and sealed. Whatever challenges wait inside won’t likely stir until it’s opened. In other words, if anyone has the best chance of success—and safety—it’s you.”

Celenneth allowed the words to settle, the hush of surf washing through her thoughts. Finally, she let out a slow breath. “All right,” she said quietly. “Let’s trust Círdan. I’ll speak with Linnea.”

They returned to Eryndil’s modest home, where the interior glowed in firelight. Linnea stood by the hearth, Hallas dozing in a cradle at her side and Brynja already curled up on a floor cushion. She looked up when Celenneth entered, tension flickering in her eyes—an unspoken fear that she might be told to stay behind.

Celenneth crossed the room with calm purpose. “I’ve weighed the risks,” she began softly, “and I believe this voyage will be in good hands. I want you with me, Linnea. All of us—together.”

Linnea’s eyes widened, and for a moment she simply stared. “I thought…” She swallowed, glancing at the sleeping children. “I assumed you’d want me to remain here.”

Celenneth shook her head, reaching to take Linnea’s hand. “We’ve always faced life’s trials side by side. I can’t imagine stepping aboard a ship without you and the children.”

Relief washed over Linnea’s features, followed by a hesitant smile. “I’ve never sailed,” she admitted, a laugh tinged with uncertainty escaping her lips. “But if you think we should, then yes—I’ll come. We’ll face the sea and whatever follows.”

Celenneth squeezed her fingers gently, the flickering fire casting dancing shadows on the walls. “I’m just as uncertain, but I want to explore the world with my family at my side.”

Early sunlight streamed through the windows, rousing the family to new purpose. Brynja perched on a stool, her legs swinging idly while Celenneth and Linnea knelt beside her. Outside, Hallas gave a brief, cheerful cry, as though sensing the stir of adventure in the air.

“We have big news,” Linnea began, drawing Brynja’s attention. “We’re going on a journey.”

Brynja leaned forward, eyes bright. “Where?” she breathed.

Celenneth gave her daughter a reassuring smile. “Across the sea—there’s an island we need to visit. Círdan will help us sail there.”

The girl’s face lit up with a delighted gasp. “A ship with sails? I’ll get to see it up close?”

Linnea chuckled, smoothing Brynja’s hair. “Yes, a real ship. It might be bigger than any boat you’ve ever imagined. But the sea can be unpredictable, so you’ll need to be our bravest explorer.”

Brynja folded her small arms, resolve gleaming in her eyes. “I’m not scared,” she declared. “I want to see everything.”

Celenneth exchanged a look with Linnea, heart buoyed by Brynja’s enthusiasm. “Then it’s settled—we sail together. And Hallas, too,” she said, nodding at the baby nestled against a cushion.

Brynja giggled softly, sliding off the stool. “I want to help! Can I pack my things now?”

Linnea laughed, giving Brynja’s shoulder a playful pat. “Go ahead, little scout. We have a busy day ahead.”

In that moment, the once-distant idea of a sea voyage suddenly felt real and immediate. As Brynja dashed off to gather her belongings, and Hallas cooed in the morning’s light, Celenneth and Linnea locked eyes—each silently acknowledging the step they were about to take, unified by hope and a touch of daring.

In the stillness of early afternoon, Celenneth and Linnea made their way through the winding lanes of the Grey Havens, Brynja skipping a few paces ahead and Hallas snug in Celenneth’s sling. Eryndil walked quietly at their side, offering a reassuring nod whenever Linnea cast a glance her way—this meeting felt momentous, and there was no telling how Círdan would respond.

Eventually, they arrived at a tall, arched entrance opening onto a broad courtyard lined with slender birch trees. A cluster of elves—likely attendants and scribes—were gathered near a pillared veranda. Upon seeing the newcomers, they bowed in greeting and guided them inside.

They found Círdan in a modest, elegant receiving room, its windows overlooking the harbor beyond. Seabirds circled overhead outside, the sound of their distant calls drifting in on the breeze. As Celenneth, Linnea, Eryndil, and the children stepped forward, Círdan turned from a low table spread with charts, his silver hair catching a sunbeam filtering through the high windows.

“Welcome,” he said in a measured tone, inclining his head with quiet respect. “I trust you have given thought to my proposal.”

Linnea placed a hand on Brynja’s shoulder to keep her from wandering around the unfamiliar room, then nodded at Celenneth. Celenneth cleared her throat and met the shipwright’s kind but discerning gaze. “We have, my lord. After much discussion, we wish to undertake the voyage. Our children and horses will join us as well.”

Círdan’s keen eyes flicked between them—Linnea’s steady resolve, Celenneth’s calm determination, Brynja’s bright anticipation, and Hallas’s tiny curious glance from his sling. A subtle curve of approval touched his lips. “Then let us speak of the vessel that shall carry you.”

He led them out onto a side terrace overlooking the harbor, then through a smaller gate that opened onto the quays. The crisp salt wind teased Brynja’s hair as they rounded a corner. Linnea kept a protective arm around her daughter’s shoulders, while Celenneth hitched Hallas higher against her chest, eyes scanning the bustling piers. Eryndil followed in thoughtful silence, her gaze flicking occasionally to Círdan, noting his assured pace.

At length, Círdan paused near a sleek, moored vessel. Its bow bore a graceful, flowing script that caught the sunlight:“Here is the ship awaiting you,” Círdan said, letting them take in the sight. “We have gathered supplies, charts, and crew. She is nearly ready.”

Celenneth and Linnea approached the gangplank, Brynja trailing behind with unabashed wonder in her eyes. The hull of silvery-white wood glinted faintly, tall masts rising toward the sky with sails folded tight. Hallas stirred, letting out a small, intrigued coo as if responding to the hushed marvel around him.

“This is Eärlindë,” Círdan continued, gesturing for them to step aboard. “Her timbers are strong, woven with subtle enchantments to steady her in rough waters. She carries three main masts, each rigged with sails of sturdy Elven fabric. You’ll find both speed and grace under a skilled hand.”

The ship was manned by Captain Calion—a tall, composed Elf with a dignified bearing—and Ithildor, his first mate and navigator, whose star-etched instruments lay carefully on a table near the helm. The rest of the dozen-strong crew were equally poised, each fulfilling roles with an efficiency that spoke of centuries of shared seafaring traditions.

Brynja pressed herself against Linnea’s side, eyes round as she glimpsed a small cluster of crates lashed in neat rows. “It’s so big,” she whispered, voice tinged with awe.

They walked along the deck, where coiled ropes and gracefully looped rigging showed the ship’s readiness for travel. “Elven craftsmanship ensures rigging so fine yet unyielding,” Círdan said softly, pointing to the slender lines and knots. “We can catch the faintest of winds, or weather a sudden gale.”

Celenneth nodded, fingertips skimming a coil of rope. “Impressive,” she murmured.

Círdan then guided them to a narrow passage below deck. Here the air felt slightly cooler, the soft glow of lanterns illuminating wooden walls. “This corridor leads to the sailors’ bunks,” he explained, nodding toward evenly spaced doors. “It’s modest, but every berth is well-kept.”

Brynja peered curiously down the hall, then up at Círdan. “So they all sleep here, like one big family?”

A slight smile crossed his ancient features. “In a sense, yes. Each has duties, but they share in the ship’s life—much as you will, though from different quarters.”

They followed him to a hatch near the stern, revealing two small but well-appointed cabins. Gently glowing stones were set into the walls, casting the space in a soft, warm light. A narrow bunk, tidy storage chests, and even a compact writing desk gave the cabin a cozy feel. Hallas wriggled in Celenneth’s arms, as though testing the atmosphere himself. Mounted securely to one wall was a cradle—crafted of smooth wood and hung on a gentle pivot, so the motion of the ship would rock Hallas without jarring him.

“A quiet place to rest,” Círdan said, “though I’d warn you the sea can be restless at times. You’ll need to secure your belongings carefully.”

Linnea reached out to run a hand over the polished wall, relief and excitement mingling on her face. “This will suit us well enough. Brynja, we can decorate a corner just for you—and Hallas, of course,” she added with a smile at the dozing infant.

At last, Círdan led them to the deeper recesses of the ship, where a the hold awaited. The sweet scent of clean straw and faint brine filled the air. Wide beams arced overhead, carved in swirling wave patterns reminiscent of the Havens’ architecture.

“Here is where your horses will remain,” Círdan said, pausing by a row of enclosed stalls. “We have designed it to ease their journey: ample straw, secured feed troughs, and ventilation shafts to ensure fresh air. An unsettled horse can be calmed with gentle words—and frequent visits.”

Worry flickered in Linnea’s eyes. “We’ll be allowed down here often, then? Eorlin will need reassurance.”

“Certainly,” Círdan replied, his voice gentle. “They will know you are near. And I suspect they’ll adapt more swiftly if you tend them regularly.”

Brynja leaned over, trying to peek into a stall. “Nimroch and Eorlin will be right here?” she asked, half-whispering as though the silent, empty stalls already had a presence of their own.

Celenneth rested a comforting hand on Brynja’s shoulder. “Yes, little one. We won’t leave them behind.”

Círdan gestured toward the far corner of the hold, where a smaller stall had been similarly prepared. “I believe you’ll find space for Silme as well,” he remarked casually.

Eryndil glanced over, her brow furrowing. “My lord?” she asked, confused.

Círdan offered a faint smile. “Yes—your mare, Silme. I’ve arranged for her to join this voyage, naturally, while you sail with Celenneth and Linnea. I cannot lead this expedition myself, so you will need to be my eyes and ears.”

Eryndil stood speechless for a moment, then gave a slow nod. “I… yes, of course, my lord. It only seems right.”

Círdan turned, beckoning them back toward the corridor. “Then it’s settled. The Eärlindë sails under Calion’s command in a few days, and you, Eryndil, shall sail in my stead.” He paused, meeting Eryndil’s eye. “We’ve prepared a bunk in the crew quarters for you.”

Eryndil gave him a respectful bow.

“Eryndil,” Linnea said softly, “why not stay in the passenger quarters with us? We can make room, and…” She trailed off, giving a small, inviting smile. “We’ve faced many roads together. It would be strange to keep you below deck.”

Celenneth, at Linnea’s side, gave a confirming nod, her tone warm. “Consider yourself part of the family. Brynja adores you, and Hallas practically falls asleep in your arms.”

At that, a rare flicker of vulnerability crossed Eryndil’s demeanor. She inclined her head slightly, gratitude in her eyes. “Thank you,” she said, voice barely above a whisper. “I will.”

Círdan watched the exchange with that gentle, knowing smile. “So be it,” he said, offering a mild nod. “The Eärlindë can accommodate all your needs. I leave the details of sleeping arrangements to you.”

Círdan turned, facing the family in the lantern-lit hold. “In the days remaining, we’ll finalize all supplies and fit the rigging for the route. Once you step aboard for the voyage, rest assured we sail with skill and determination.” His silver-bearded visage softened. “I am glad you have chosen to embark together. A family’s unity is a powerful thing.”

In the days leading up to departure, Celenneth, Linnea, Brynja, and Hallas found themselves immersed in the quiet splendor of the Grey Havens. While ship preparations took place—ropes inspected, supplies catalogued, sailcloth checked for every seam—the family used each moment to explore the ancient coastal city they might never see again.

At first, Eryndil served as their attentive guide, leading them down winding streets flanked by low stone arches and delicate balconies covered in sea-worn ivy. She showed them hidden corners where elven craftsmen plied their trades, the gentle echo of hammers against metal or chisels on wood blending with the ever-present hiss of the sea. Brynja skipped along, enthralled by every new sight: a statue of a seafarer carved into a fountain’s edge, the salt-glazed ornamentation of a smith’s workshop, or the soft-glowing lanterns that lined the streets in the evenings.

Some mornings, Celenneth and Linnea ventured out alone with the children, giving Eryndil a chance to coordinate details with the ship’s crew. They visited the market district, a gathering of stalls and small shops tucked beneath the spires. Brynja dashed from stall to stall, giggling at the array of silver trinkets, polished seashells, and embroidered linens, while Hallas dozed in Celenneth’s sling.

They found a quiet herbal shop, its shelves lined with dried seaweeds and mountain herbs. Linnea—a competent healer in her own right—conversed with the soft-spoken elven apothecary about salves useful for seasickness or minor injuries at sea. In exchange for a small pouch of herbs, Linnea shared a recipe of her own for a calming tea made from northern highland plants. Brynja proudly clutched the herb pouch all the way home, eager to see if it might be tested aboard the ship.

On afternoons when Eryndil joined them, she led them to vantage points overlooking the harbor—places where the wind carried the scent of brine and gulls circled overhead. One such perch was a sea-worn parapet at the edge of the city, beyond which stretched a boundless horizon. Brynja leaned precariously over the parapet’s edge until Linnea caught her cloak with a gentle scold. Hallas, cradled close to Celenneth’s chest, gurgled as though in conversation with the crashing surf below.

Their last  evening, as twilight deepened and lamplight shimmered on the water, they gathered at a low stone wall near the main quay. The Eärlindë was visible in the distance, her silhouette dignified against the glimmering sea. Brynja, sleep heavy in her eyes, let out a hushed whisper: “She’s so pretty... I can’t wait.” Linnea kissed the top of her head, while Celenneth exchanged a reassuring glance with Eryndil, who stood near enough to catch every soft word. They felt the weight of the coming voyage—and the wonder of it—like the hush before a new dawn.

The crew busied themselves stowing crates of dried foods, barrels of fresh water, and medical supplies for the journey. Meanwhile, Celenneth and Linnea secured personal items—extra blankets for the children, traveling clothes suited for briny winds, a small stash of herbs for healing. They also arranged for Nimroch, Eorlin and Silme to be carefully walked aboard, calming them with soft words once they were tethered in the lower hold.

The Eärlindë’s deck bristled with anticipation. Bright sunlight sparkled on the harbor, turning the waters into a shimmering tapestry of blues and whites. Captain Calion stood near the helm, posture steady, while Ithildor and other crewmembers busied themselves with rigging. Celenneth and Linnea guided Brynja up from the hold, and Eryndil followed with quiet poise.

The crew assembled to greet them all formally. “Welcome aboard, travelers,” Calion said, voice carrying over the soft lap of waves. “By Círdan’s command, we sail under clear purpose: to bring you safely to your destination and home again. The sea is vast and unpredictable, but you’ll be in good hands.”

From behind, an Elf named **Laerion**, the ship’s cook, offered Brynja a small sweet biscuit. “A taste to calm your first sail,” he said cheerfully, and the child’s eyes lit up with delight.

Meanwhile, Ithildor nodded to Celenneth, gesturing at a star chart pinned near the wheel. “We’ll chart our course using these,” he explained. “The island is some weeks’ travel, depending on winds. We navigate by stars and currents—Elven methods refined over many ages.”

Celenneth nodded in acknowledgement, a rush of gratitude filling her chest. Everything was new and daunting, yet the presence of these calm, capable Elves lifted some of her worry. She glanced at Linnea, who wore a similar expression of hopeful awe. Hallas stirred in her arms, letting out a small whimper at the unusual bustle. Eryndil stepped closer, gently rocking him for a moment until he quieted.

At midday, a horn sounded from the quay—one long, low note. The final moorings were released, and a hush rippled through the gathered Elves. With a soft groan, the Eärlindë eased away from the dock, gliding into open water. Ropes hissed as they slid against wood, sails unfurled in a whisper of fabric, and soon the wind caught them, propelling the ship into the harbor’s expanse.

Brynja gripped the railing, balancing on tiptoes to see the docks receding. “We’re really moving!” she cried, excitement overriding any lingering trepidation.

Linnea rested a hand on Brynja’s shoulder, her own heart pounding. “Yes, love,” she murmured. “This is it.”

Círdan himself stood on a high vantage in the Havens, raising one hand in a stately farewell. Eryndil returned the salute, while Celenneth, cradling Hallas, dipped her head in a silent thanks. They carried the blessings of the ancient Shipwright now, and that knowledge gave them courage.

As the Eärlindë ventured beyond the protective arms of the harbor, the sea’s presence grew more tangible. The deck rose and fell gently with each swell. Linnea clung to Brynja, eyeing the waves with a mixture of awe and apprehension. Eryndil took a moment to demonstrate how to keep one’s balance, guiding them to bend their knees slightly.

“It helps to flow with the ship’s motion,” Eryndil explained in her calm, measured tone. “Rather than resist it.”

Brynja nodded intently, trying to mimic Eryndil’s stance. “Like dancing,” she said, giggling as the deck tilted again.

Celenneth watched them, a flicker of a smile on her lips. She could feel Hallas’s weight shift in her arms every time the ship rocked—her maternal reflexes promising she would not let him be jostled. Nimroch and Eorlin were out of sight below, but a faint whinny rose through the boards, reminding her she would soon need to go down and reassure them. For now, the deck bustled with last-minute adjustments, the crew double-checking lines, sails, and cargo.

It took about an hour before the Eärlindë cleared the wide bay of the Grey Havens and entered the open sea. The once-sheltered waters gave way to a broader expanse of waves rolling in from the west. Brynja stared, captivated, at the seemingly endless horizon. Linnea linked arms with Celenneth, bracing herself for the subtle changes in the ship’s motion.

A gentle but persistent breeze filled the sails. Ithildor kept an eye on the wind direction, periodically calling out instructions to the crew. The low hum of Elven songs rose from the sailors as they moved, giving each task a steady rhythm. Occasionally, sunlight glinted off the *song stones* embedded in the railings, sending faint prisms across the deck.

“We’re free of the harbor now,” Captain Calion announced, stepping up beside them. “With luck and fair winds, we’ll maintain our heading steadily.”

Eryndil looked toward Celenneth. “How are you faring?” she asked softly, noting the baby in her arms and the way Linnea gripped the railing.

Celenneth exhaled, a half-laugh escaping. “It’s… surreal,” she admitted. “I’ve never been on a ship of this size. We are truly at the mercy of the sea, aren’t we?”

Eryndil nodded. “Yes, but the Eärlindë is well-crafted, and the crew is skilled. Have faith. And for the horses, a calm hand will keep them settled.”

Linnea added quietly, “We’ll need to check on Nimroch and Eorlin soon. Brynja, want to help?”

Brynja bounced on her heels. “Yes!”

But Calion held up a hand with a kind smile. “Give them another hour or two,” he suggested. “The initial swells can unsettle any creature not used to it. Let them adjust before you go below.”

Linnea conceded with a nod. She and Brynja ventured to the starboard rail, scanning the horizon where sea and sky merged in a haze of blue. Hallas stirred, frowning, as if unsure of this constant rocking beneath him. Celenneth gently swayed him, murmuring soothing words.

As the day went on, Celenneth’s family began to find a rhythm aboard the Eärlindë. The gentle rocking that at first felt disorienting soon became background music to their steps. Brynja discovered she could dart from bow to stern—under the watchful eye of Linnea or Eryndil, of course—and find a vantage point for every new wonder: a flock of sea birds diving for fish, the shifting color of water as the sun rose higher, the crew’s quiet, efficient dance around the masts and rigging.

Linnea, uncertain about seafaring at first, found solace in tasks. She helped secure crates, learned how to coil ropes properly, and even assisted the cook, Laerion, in gathering water from the barrels for dinner. In all these small acts, she built up her comfort with life aboard a ship, discovering an unspoken synergy with Celenneth, who balanced caring for Hallas with checking on the horses below deck.

Eryndil moved easily among the crew, her history as a ranger and traveler blending well with Elven seafaring traditions. She took shifts on watch, ensuring the horizon remained free of threats. Whenever she could, she made time to guide Brynja in simple tasks—tying knots or reading the wind in the sails—a form of “hands-on” teaching that enthralled the child.

By early evening, the ship was well at sea. The coastline was a distant memory, a faint line behind them. Linnea, Celenneth, and Brynja regrouped in their shared cabin, Hallas stirring in his cradle.

“I’ll check on Nimroch and Eorlin,” Celenneth offered, handing Hallas to Linnea. “They must be anxious with this new motion.”

Linnea nodded, softly rocking Hallas. “We’ll be here,” she said, then looked at Brynja. “Want to help me gather our clothes into that chest?”

Brynja’s enthusiastic “Yes!” drew a smile from Eryndil, who lingered by the door. “I’ll go with Celenneth,” the elf said, “in case the horses need extra calm.”

The companionway leading to the hold was lit by gently glowing lanterns that swung with the ship’s roll. The hold itself was large and well-ventilated, the stable area segregated by sturdy wooden partitions. Nimroch and Eorlin stood in separate stalls, their ears twitching at the unfamiliar creaks. The muted glow of sunset through narrow portholes cast shifting shadows on the straw-covered floor.

Celenneth approached Nimroch, speaking quietly. “Hey, boy,” she murmured, reaching out to stroke his muzzle. “How are you faring?”

The stallion snorted, shifting weight as though to say, “This is most peculiar, but I trust you.” Celenneth patted his flank, relief in her eyes. Eorlin, calmer by nature, let out a soft nicker, pressing her nose against Eryndil’s palm when the elf approached.

“It’s all new,” Eryndil said, brushing stray hay from Eorlin’s mane, “but they’re managing well.”

Celenneth offered the horses a bit of feed, softly reassuring them they’d be taken care of. The subdued light and gentle rocking gave a strange lull to the scene, a reminder that beneath the ship’s decks, life continued—animal and human—bound by shared trust.

When they returned topside, the sky was painted in dramatic pinks and oranges, the sun sinking low. Linnea, with Hallas securely cradled, and Brynja perched on a seat near the stern, watched the horizon shift colors. The synergy of the moment touched Celenneth’s heart: a sense of peace in the midst of this great, unknown adventure.

They retreated to their shared cabin, where the cradle offered Hallas a gentle sway. Brynja was fast asleep the moment Linnea tucked her into a bunk, the day’s wonders capturing her dreams. Eryndil settled into a corner, rolling out her bedding, while Celenneth and Linnea carefully arranged themselves in the close quarters. Despite the limited space, the cabin felt welcoming—reminding them all of the vow to face life as a family.

Outside, the faint strains of Elven singing drifted through the porthole, the deck’s lanterns casting soft glows on the waves. Linnea tucked a loose blanket around Brynja, then sank beside Celenneth. Eryndil, half-propped on one elbow, gave them a gentle smile in the dim lamplight. None of them spoke—no words needed. Even Hallas stirred only once, lulled by the rhythmic rolling of the Eärlindë.
