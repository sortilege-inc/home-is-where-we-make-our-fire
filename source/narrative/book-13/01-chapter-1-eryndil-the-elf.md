# Chapter 1: Eryndil, the Elf

*Súlimë 12, 2971*

The months had drifted by in a gentle rhythm, the world beyond the cabin shifting from winter's hush to the quiet green of spring. Within the Angle, the small home Celenneth and Linnea had built was full now with the sounds of two children—Brynja, five years old and forever curious, and the infant Hallas, three months into the world and beginning to discover his own voice. The afternoons carried the scent of warming earth: Linnea coaxed seedlings from the thawed soil, Celenneth mended tools and tack, and Brynja tended the baby in the way only a child with a heart that size could—singing him nonsense songs, holding his small hand with solemn pride. For a season it had seemed the world's greater troubles could not find them here.

But peace rarely holds unchanged, and fate seldom knocks before it enters.

It was early morning when Celenneth first marked the figure on the horizon. She stood finishing her tea at the window while Linnea hung fresh linen outside; and beyond the budding branches a lone traveler came on with measured steps, tall and lithe, wrapped in a cloak of deep blue that caught the light in slow ripples, like waves on a far shore.

Her heart gave a curious flutter. This was no wanderer of mortal lands. The bearing was fluid and sure, steeped in an elegance that spoke of centuries and not decades—one of the Elves, and not of the woodland kind but of Lindon, a people shaped by western seas and long memory. As the visitor neared, Celenneth glimpsed sea-grey eyes beneath the hood, and silver-streaked hair strung with small shell-like beads that shimmered when they moved.

Linnea stepped back inside, wiping her hands, her brow lifting in question.

"Someone comes," Celenneth said, low. "An Elf of Lindon, I think. Not from these parts—she carries herself as one who has journeyed far. And I think she comes for me."

Linnea drew to the window, thoughtful, unafraid. They both knew Celenneth's past held more than the simple tale of a ranger who had settled down. Her hand brushed Celenneth's. "Then let's meet her."

They stepped out into the warming day, Brynja trailing behind, Linnea balancing Hallas on her hip. The Elf halted gracefully and drew back her hood, revealing a face sharp and yet kind, timeless and strong, her eyes the grey of a distant storm at sea. She wore fitted leather chased with patterned sweeps, and at her collar a silver clasp shaped like a star.

"I am Eryndil of Lindon," she said, her voice steady and resonant as far-off tides. "I come bearing counsel from Elrond of Rivendell. I journeyed east to seek his wisdom, and he sent me here—to you, Celenneth. He spoke of your bloodline, and of a part you might play in a matter of great weight." From beneath the blue cloak she drew a shard of adamant, carved with fine symbols that glowed faintly with a soft, oceanic light. "There is a place long lost, west of these lands, hidden by time and tide. This relic may open its truth—but only if one of Númenórean blood stands beside me. Elrond believes that one is you."

Celenneth felt the ground shift beneath her. She had come here to find peace, and yet destiny had found her out again. "I understand what you ask," she said, her voice steady though her heart was not. "But I can't answer in haste. My family is here—my whole life. I must speak with my wife, and then with you. This isn't a thing I decide alone."

Eryndil inclined her head. "I will wait beneath the birches. Come to me when you are ready."

That night, once the children slept—Brynja curled protectively around Hallas's cradle—Celenneth and Linnea sat at their table in the hearth-glow, fingers laced.

"She needs me," Celenneth said softly. "But what she asks feels enormous. I left the wandering life behind. Here we found peace."

"And the past still lives in you," Linnea answered, her eyes not wavering. "If this relic could turn fate itself, how do we ignore it? I fear staying more than going—fear we'd miss something larger than ourselves. If you walk this road, I walk it beside you. The children too. We chose this life together; we'll meet what comes together."

They sat a long while, the fire and the children's breathing steadying them both. Slowly the resolve settled. "We'll go," Celenneth said at last. "But not tomorrow. We prepare, we pack what we can carry, we see the children safe—and we close this chapter of our life here with care."

At dawn they found Eryndil among the birches, patient as the tide. "We'll go," Celenneth told her. "But as a family. Brynja, Hallas, Linnea, and I—we won't be parted. We'll need time to ready ourselves."

Respect moved in the sea-grey eyes. "Then I shall wait, and help where I may. The road is long, but shared burdens grow lighter."

There remained the relic's riddle—its glowing marks unlike any craft Celenneth or Linnea had seen. For that there was one nearby who might help: Almedra, the lore-keeper. Celenneth had sat at her hearth once before, the autumn past, and left in her keeping the silver-bound Lorebook of Arnenir carried out of the ice; and word had reached the Angle over the winter that old Berthor, Almedra's husband and fellow keeper of tales, had died as the snows lay deepest. Celenneth went softly, then, as one approaching a fresh grief.

The willow-shrouded cottage was quieter than she remembered it, the second chair by the fire empty. Almedra rose on her carved stick, thinner than before, her eyes older than her years—and yet at the sight of Celenneth, and then of the tall silver-braided stranger at her side, something in her lifted from its mourning.

"Almedra," Celenneth said gently, "this is Eryndil of Lindon. She bears a relic of Númenor's making, and we've come for your lore, if you can spare it." She paused. "I was grieved to hear of Berthor. He was kind to us."

The old woman's mouth pressed thin, and she nodded. "He passed as the snows were deepest. I've carried on alone." Then her gaze went to Eryndil, and wonder eclipsed the grief—for never in her long life of reading of Elves had she looked one in the face. "You are… of the Firstborn," she breathed. It was not a question.

"I am, lore-keeper." Eryndil inclined her head. "And it is a solemn thing to meet one whose memory guards the tapestry of mortal lives. You have my sorrow for your loss."

Celenneth drew the adamant into the lamplight, careful of its glow. Almedra leaned close, fascination overtaking her reserve, tracing with her eyes the swirls of waves and stars and ships carved into its surface. "Adamant," she murmured. "Harder than diamond, and rare. The craft is of the old tales—Númenor, and ships crossing the wide seas, and gifts between friends." Her voice steadied as it found familiar ground. She reached past her grief to the shelf where the Lorebook of Arnenir now sat among her own, and to a slim, worn notebook beside it—Berthor's hand, tidy and delicate on every page. "He copied these from an old source," she said, stopping at a set of verses. "They speak of a threshold in the West—a place where land and sea meet, where something long overlooked may be revealed under starlight. Take them. For old friendship, and for the hope that your road brings light to dark places."

Celenneth bowed her thanks; Linnea took the old woman's hand a moment, wordless. As they left, Almedra stood in her doorway on her staff, the dusk gathering, and called after them softly: "May your journey bring meaning—and your return, peace."

Eryndil looked back and smiled, and their steps carried them away into the twilight, guided now by new fragments of lore and the memory of a kind woman who had never thought to meet an Elf—and, in doing so, had helped shape a fate that might reach far beyond her quiet corner of the world.[^m-eryndil]
