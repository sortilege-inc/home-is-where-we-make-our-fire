# Chapter 3: A Shelter in a Storm

*Víressë 2, 2971*

The morning after Bree was crisp with lingering dew, but a thread of warmth ran through it. Celenneth guided Nimroch at a steady westward pace, Linnea and Eryndil close behind—Brynja in the saddle before Linnea on Eorlin, Hallas dozing in a wrap against Celenneth's chest. The road stretched ahead in long, gentle curves, trampled earth and loose gravel worn by years of travelers.

For all the easy warmth, Bree still sat heavy behind them. Celenneth watched Linnea let out a slow breath and set the old resentments down, one mile at a time.

"Are the people in Bree still angry with us?" Brynja asked.

Linnea tucked a strand of hair behind the girl's ear. "Some, maybe. Not all. We met kindness too. That's how towns are, love—some will judge you for who you are, and others will open their arms."

Eryndil said nothing, her sea-grey eyes combing the horizon, the dappled mare Silmë matching Nimroch's stride. She seemed to sense the family needed a measure of quiet after the upheaval, and gave it to them.

They passed rolling fields dotted with early wildflowers, and midday came and went in peace. But as the sun tipped toward late afternoon the clouds thickened to a heavy grey mass, the breeze sharpened, and the light drained too fast from the day.

"Storm?" Linnea asked, frowning.

"It looks that way," Celenneth said. "We'd best find shelter."

It was no gentle spring shower that broke over them but a driving downpour, roaring across the sky, churning the road to soup. In moments the world shrank to a murky veil of rain, and the path turned slick beneath the horses' hooves. Hallas's fussing rose to an anxious wail; Brynja pressed her face to Linnea's shoulder, tears mixing with rain. "Mama, I'm cold," she whispered.

They needed shelter, and soon—the baby could not stay long in this. Celenneth strained through the murk and caught it: a faint orange glow, lamplight behind a window. "There's a house ahead!" she called over the wind.

Step by careful step they climbed a rain-treacherous incline to a small farmhouse, and Celenneth swung down and rapped harder than she meant on the door. It cracked open on a middle-aged woman, worry etched on her face; she took in Celenneth's drenched state, the children, the slender Elf behind them—and compassion won out over caution. "Oh, my goodness—come in, come in, you'll catch your deaths!"

The warmth hit them like an embrace. "Luthan!" the woman called over her shoulder. "Blankets—and stoke the fire!" A broad-shouldered man appeared, his eyes widening at Eryndil before softening at the sight of a trembling child. "You're safe here," the woman said. "I'm Martha. This is my husband, Luthan. I'll not turn away a family in need."

Celenneth peeled back the soaked wrap to check Hallas's face; his cries were already easing. "I'm Celenneth. My wife, Linnea. Our children, Brynja and Hallas. And this is Eryndil of Lindon." An odd hush met the Elf's name—an Elf beneath one's roof was no small thing—but Martha only cleared her throat. "An elf's no more welcome or unwelcome than any other soul soaked to the skin," she declared, practical to the bone. "Let's get you dry."

Luthan built the fire to a roar. Warmth flooded the modest room, and the color came back into Hallas's cheeks. Linnea sank onto the couch with Brynja, tears of exhaustion and relief in her eyes. When Martha set about stew, Eryndil quietly offered to help—"I've some small skill cooking on the road"—and followed her to the little kitchen, to Luthan's frank delight at an Elf chopping his vegetables.

By the time the bowls were filled a second storm raged outside, thunder booming, rain hammering the roof—but within, the travelers had found a small island of warmth. Martha and Luthan had farmed here twenty years and rarely hosted a soul; they had no children of their own, though half a dozen distant nieces and nephews called them godparents. "Spring storms come fierce in these border-lands," Martha said, "though rarely this early. You were lucky to find us."

They laid out blankets by the hearth, and Martha would not hear otherwise. Brynja's breathing steadied into sleep; Hallas stirred once and settled to a whispered lullaby. Eryndil spread a bedroll in the far corner, as if ready to rise at a word. In that snug farmhouse, cocooned against the storm, the memory of Bree's anger loosened its grip.

They woke to a hush; the storm had blown out before dawn, and the land shimmered under a watery sky. Martha pressed them to linger till midday, until the road dried and the children might have a gentler start, and Celenneth—weighing the long errand ahead against a kindness freely given—let herself accept it. They passed a leisurely morning, Luthan and Eryndil at the stewpot again, Brynja hopping through the puddles in the yard.

When at last they made ready to leave, Brynja ran and hugged Martha about the legs, and the woman bent to ruffle her hair, her voice thick. "You be safe." While she was distracted with the goodbyes, Celenneth drew from her pouch a small Númenórean brooch, worked with swirling wave-patterns, and set it quietly on the mantel just inside the door—no word said. She would rather they found it later, a thanks past speaking.

"We owe you much," she told Luthan, her voice catching.

"You owe us nothing," he said firmly. "We only did what decent folk do."

They rode away with the sun climbing and the road glistening, and when Celenneth looked back once, Martha and Luthan stood waving, their house a small beacon of warmth amid the wide, rain-soaked fields. The storm had tested them; it had also left a reminder to carry west—that compassion could be found in the least expected places.[^m-shelter]
