# Chapter 4: Briars

*Víressë 10, 2971*

After some days the land changed, gentle farmland giving way to rolling slopes. The company skirted the fringes of the Barrow-downs—an old country spoken of in hushed voices, its grass-grown mounds and lingering shadows—and though the sky stayed clear, a tension crept back into the air, as if the breeze carried the echo of some older dark.

On a mild afternoon, with Brynja singing a nonsense tune to amuse Hallas, Eryndil reined in without warning and lifted a hand. Celenneth followed her gaze to a snarl of brambles sprawled across a bend in the path—thorns twisted netlike over one another, faintly shimmering with a sickly sheen, and swaying, almost, in a wind that was not blowing.

"These are tainted," Eryndil said quietly. "By the old darkness that lingers near the Downs, perhaps."

"They block the road entirely." Celenneth kept Hallas well back. "Is there a way around?"

"There may be. But left unchecked, brambles like these spread."

They would not leave a growing danger for the next travelers to blunder into. "Fire," Eryndil said, when Celenneth asked how. "What is tainted so recoils from flame." So they gathered what dry wood they could—scarce after the storm—and Eryndil advanced with a torch. Each step seemed to stir the vines. When the flame at last licked the thorns, a hiss went up like a thousand serpents spitting at once; the fire caught, the tangle writhed, and a cloud of acrid black smoke rolled skyward. They fell back from it, and stayed only long enough to be sure the fire held, until the twisted vines collapsed to cinders and the taint was gone.

Brynja peered out from behind Linnea's arm, eyes watering. "Are there more of those?"

"There could be," Linnea admitted gently. "But we know how to handle them now."

They pressed on, watchful. The next morning came clear, the Downs a line of silent mounds to the north—and by late morning Linnea, adjusting Brynja's saddle-blanket, spotted scraps of cloth snagged on the low rocks, and a faint ruddy stain. Blood, and recent. Eryndil crouched over the shallow drag-marks. "Someone came through not long ago—wounded, and half-carried."

Hallas chose that moment to wake and wail, and the sound carried in the stillness. "They'll know we're coming," Eryndil murmured. Celenneth nodded. "Then better we come openly." She loosened her sword in its sheath but did not draw.

The trail led into a shallow ravine, and there they found them: a man and a young woman, weary and taut with fear. "Stay back," the woman warned, though she bore no weapon.

Linnea raised an open hand. "We saw blood on the road. We came to see if you needed help." The man's leg was bound in bloodied cloth, and he leaned hard on his companion. "Something attacked us," he ground out. "Small, twisted—goblin-like, but worse. Fast. It took a bite from my leg and vanished." He and Eryndil traded a look; a tainted thing, then, of the Downs.

Suspicion warred with desperation, but the sight of the baby in Linnea's arms softened the woman's resolve, and she gave a short nod. Linnea knelt to the work—"My name's Linnea; what's yours?"—and the man managed, through his teeth, "Torric." His companion was Sera. Linnea unwound the cloth: the wound was deep and ragged, hot at the edges. She cleansed it, ground fresh herbs to a poultice, and bound the leg anew. "It's bad, but not fatal if he rests. No more dragging him about—he needs days off that leg."

Sera's rigid posture eased at last. "Thank you. We didn't think anyone would stop."

Then a ragged shriek carried from beyond the ravine's lip—no traveler's cry but the guttural rasp of something tainted and hungry. "It's back," Sera hissed, pulling Torric behind a rock.

Celenneth signed for Linnea to stay with the children and the wounded. "We'll be back." Eryndil moved like a wisp of smoke, and together they slipped beyond the bend. Linnea, Brynja, and the strangers could only wait and listen—the snarling, the clash of steel—until the sounds died into an eerie stillness, and the two returned, blades in hand, grim but unhurt.

"It won't trouble anyone again," Celenneth said simply.

Whatever new fear the couple had held of the Elf was gone, replaced by plain gratitude. "We owe you," Sera murmured. "If there's anything we can do—"

"Only rest, and travel safe from here," Linnea said, adjusting the bandage a last time. "Not at night, especially near these lands. The shadows here are treacherous." And so they saw Torric and Sera settled with water and supplies, and rode on, the specter of the Barrow-downs receding behind them—one more step in a journey guided by courage, unity, and small acts of kindness.[^r-briars]
